Abstract

The final stages of cosmic reionisation (EndEoR) are expected to be strongly regulated by the residual neutral hydrogen in the already ionised regions of the Universe. Its presence limits the mean distance that ionising photons can travel and hence, the extent of the regions that sources of ionising photons can affect. The structures containing most of this residual neutral hydrogen are typically unresolved in large-scale simulations of reionisation. Here, we investigate and compare a range of approaches for including the effect of these small-scale absorbers, also known as Lyman limit systems (LLS), in such simulations. We evaluate the impact of these different approaches on the reionisation history, the evolution of the ultraviolet background, and its fluctuations. We also compare to observational results on the distribution of Lyman-Œ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ± opacity towards the EndEoR and the measured mean free path of ionising photons. We further consider their effect on the 21-cm power spectrum. We find that although each of the different approaches can match some of the observed probes of the final stages of reionisation, only the use of a redshift-dependent and position-dependent LLS model is able to reproduce all of them. We therefore recommend that large-scale reionisation simulations, which aim to describe both the state of the ionised and neutral intergalactic medium use such an approach, although the other, simpler approaches are applicable depending on the science goal of the simulation.

1 Introduction

The Epoch of Reionisation (EoR) was the period in the first billion years of the Universe during which the neutral hydrogen (H‚Äâi¬†) present in the intergalactic medium (IGM) was ionised by ultraviolet (UV) radiation. The ionising radiation is thought to originate from the first stars, galaxies, and accreting black holes, which formed due to the gravitational collapse of baryons into the dark matter halos. This phase transition spans a large range of physical scales. In addition to the cosmological properties of the large-scale IGM, the EoR is also defined by the astrophysical properties of galaxy formation and evolution (see Pritchard & Loeb, 2012; Dayal & Ferrara, 2018, for a review).

The scattering causes polarization of the CMB on scales larger than the horizon size and suppresses temperature anisotropies on scales smaller than the horizon size. Another key probe comes from the scattering of Lyman-Œ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ± (Ly-Œ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±, rest-frame wavelength of 1216 √Ö), photons passing through an ionised region, by residual neutral hydrogen along a line-of-sight (LOS). Notable observations include the LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†equivalent width (Mason et¬†al., 2018, 2019; Hayes & Scarlata, 2023; Hoag et¬†al., 2019; Bolan et¬†al., 2022; Bruton et¬†al., 2023; Morishita et¬†al., 2023; Jones et¬†al., 2023), the LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†dark pixel fraction (McGreer et¬†al., 2015; Jin et¬†al., 2023) as well as the decreasing fraction of LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†emitters among Lyman break galaxies (Ouchi et¬†al., 2010; Endsley & Stark, 2022) can be utilised to place constraints on the global neutral fraction of hydrogen at a given redshift (see the top panel of Fig.¬†1 in grey).

Particularly, spectra of Quasi-Stellar Objects (QSO or quasars) at high-zùëßzitalic_z have been found to contain a wealth of information about the state of the IGM. Observations of the quasar damping wing (Gunn & Peterson, 1965) in high-zùëßzitalic_z quasar spectra indicate the scattering of Ly-Œ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ± photons by neutral hydrogen (H‚Äâi) and can be used to estimate its fraction as a function of redshift (e.g. Mortlock et¬†al., 2011; Greig et¬†al., 2017; Ba√±ados et¬†al., 2018; Davies et¬†al., 2018; Greig et¬†al., 2019; Wang et¬†al., 2020; Yang et¬†al., 2020; Greig et¬†al., 2022). Therefore, QSOs are an excellent probe of the end stages of reionisation (hereafter EndEoR) (see Davies et¬†al., 2024, for a comprehensive review). How the EndEoR occurred is essential for understanding the ionising budget of the universe (Cain et¬†al., 2023), and the role of the last islands of neutral hydrogen (Giri et¬†al., 2024). While observations from Becker et¬†al. (2001); Fan et¬†al. (2006) initially indicated the endpoint of reionisation to be around z‚àº6similar-toùëß6z\sim 6italic_z ‚àº 6, the LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†opacities such as those derived from QSO spectra in Bosman et¬†al. (2022) are difficult to reproduce with reionisation simulations, requiring an extended reionisation down to z‚âà5.3ùëß5.3z\approx 5.3italic_z ‚âà 5.3 (see, e.g. Kulkarni et¬†al., 2019; Keating et¬†al., 2020).

Moreover, once a region of the IGM is re-ionised, its internal state depends on the ionised hydrogen (H‚Äâii¬†) recombination rate against the ionisation rate set by the photon density or Ultraviolet Background (UVB) in the ionised IGM. How the mean UVB within such ionised areas changed over time depends on the growth and merging of such ionised structures. We will refer to the mean UVB value in the later stages of the EoR and post-EoR as the UV Background. Measurements of this quantity towards the EndEoR exist (Calverley et¬†al., 2011; Wyithe & Bolton, 2011; Gaikwad et¬†al., 2023) but most simulations struggle to reproduce this number, typically reporting UVB values higher than the measurements (e.g. Bolton & Haehnelt, 2007; Finlator et¬†al., 2009; Aubert et¬†al., 2015; Dixon et¬†al., 2016; Shukla et¬†al., 2016; Ocvirk et¬†al., 2020). The value of the UVB depends on the mean free path (MFP) of ionising (Lyman continuum) photons, which is the mean distance an ionising photon travels before being absorbed (e.g. Fan et¬†al., 2024). The MFP can be set by the absorption of ionising photons by neutral regions at the edge of the ionised bubble or by absorptions within the ionised regions. Absorption within the H‚Äâii¬†bubble could occur due to residual neutral hydrogen or due to the presence of discrete dense systems with an optical depth to ionising photons of œÑ>1ùúè1\tau>1italic_œÑ > 1, referred to as Lyman Limit Systems (LLS, see e.g. Alvarez & Abel, 2012; McQuinn et¬†al., 2011; Shukla et¬†al., 2016; D‚ÄôAloisio et¬†al., 2020; Theuns & Chan, 2024; Fan et¬†al., 2024). The latter case is dominant in the post-reionisation Universe as fully neutral islands have disappeared. The MFP value towards the EndEoR has been measured by Becker et¬†al. (2021); Zhu et¬†al. (2023); Gaikwad et¬†al. (2023) and found to be significantly smaller compared to the inferred value from post-EoR observations in Worseck et¬†al. (2014) (hereafter W14). Therefore, the evolution of the MFP of ionising photons is particularly sensitive to the final stages of reionisation and vice versa, while its value is influenced by the presence of LLS.

Modelling the EndEoR has not received much attention in the literature, partly because the required simulation techniques are non-trivial but also because the prospective probe of the 21-cm signal from the IGM will be weaker. In reionisation simulations, the opacity of the IGM for ionising photons becomes essential, and there exists quite a bit of uncertainty about this quantity. For example, the excursion set approach used in 21CMFAST (Mesinger & Furlanetto, 2007) is not applicable once most of the IGM has reionised. This limitation of the approach had triggered the development of ISLANDFAST (Xu et¬†al., 2016). This code begins reionisation of the IGM using the same method as in 21CMFAST and later switches to another formalism developed in Xu et¬†al. (2014) to follow the evolution of neutral hydrogen islands.

In this work, we use the state-of-the-art C2Ray simulation framework that does not have this limitation as it numerically solves the three-dimensional radiative transfer equation. The main objective of this work is centred on studying the final stages of reionisation. Notably, we investigate the role of unresolved LLS, which we will also refer to as small-scale absorbers, in large-scale reionisation simulations. We construct and compare various approaches to modelling the effect of these small-scale absorbers to examine how their implementation impacts the properties of reionisation. To achieve this, we model multiple observables of reionisation, from probing the state of the ionised IGM in quasars to studying the neutral islands with the 21-cm signal from the IGM during the EndEoR.

The paper is organised as follows. Sec.¬†2 describes the simulations used within this study, focusing on the LLS implementations necessary for our analysis. In Sec.¬†3, we examine the role of the small-scale absorbers on the global parameters of reionisation and their effect on the statistics of the UVB background. In Sec.¬†4, we examine the observables at the EndEoR, such as the effective LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†optical depth, the mean free path of ionising photons, as well as the effect on the neutral islands at the end of reionisation through the lens of the 21 cm power spectrum. We summarise our findings and elaborate on future improvements in Sec.¬†5. Unless stated otherwise, we address comoving megaparsec as Mpc.

In Fig.¬†7, we present the 21 cm power spectrum, both at a fixed redshift of 6 (upper panel) and as the closest possible global neutral fraction of around 9¬†% (lower panel). Therefore, by allowing the photons to freely propagate across the simulations and instead adjusting the recombination factor, the resulting neutral islands at the end of reionisation are mainly larger in size and group at the furthest voids of the simulation, resulting in more power in large scales. The eLLS and pLLS models look largely consistent with one another, as their main difference is centred on how the small-scale absorbers are modelled within the ionised regions. Compared to the remaining models, these diffuse barrier models produce neutral islands which are smaller in size and more scattered across the simulation due to the fluctuations introduced by the LLS.

5 Conclusion and discussion

This body of work examines the end stages of the Epoch of Reionisation (EndEoR) using a set of 3D radiative transfer simulations. The final stages of reionisation are particularly difficult to model numerically, due to the significant amount of ionising sources and the complex topology of its ionised overlapping structures. Particularly, modelling the UVB within such simulations often overestimates the measurements reported by Calverley et¬†al. (2011). Despite this, the EndEoR is a treasure trove of information about the process of reionisation, and the first stars and galaxies. Utilising information from high redshift quasars, the reported low values of the mean free path of ionising photons at a redshift of 6 (Becker et¬†al., 2021; Zhu et¬†al., 2023) as well as higher values of the effective LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†opacity along the line-of-sight (Bosman, 2021) indicate at an extended reionisation period (Keating et¬†al., 2018; Kulkarni et¬†al., 2019). In addition, the large Gunn‚ÄìPeterson trough reported by Becker et¬†al. (2015) hints at the presence of islands of neutral hydrogen, which can be utilised to study reionisation through the 21 cm signal (Giri et¬†al., 2019).

This work aims to explore the role of small-scale optically thick self-shielded systems, which are often not included in large-scale simulations of reionisation. Referred to as Lyman Limit systems, these systems are often associated with hydrogen present in the outskirts of galaxies and/or in the filaments between them (Theuns & Chan, 2024; Fan et¬†al., 2024). LLS have an effect on the observables of reionisation, such as the effective LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†opacity, the mean free path of ionising (Lyman continuum) photons, as well as the 21 cm signal from the intergalactic medium. We investigate several methods to model these small-scale absorbers with varying assumptions (see Section¬†2.2 and Table¬†1). All four C2-Ray models are identical in set-up save for the modelling of the LLS, described below:

  1. 1.

    In the r40 model ionising photons are cut off at a fixed distance of 40 cMpc for all redshifts. This can be seen as a model which only considers the large-scale effect of the LLS on reionisation.

  2. 2.

    The LLS40 model uses an additional subgrid opacity to impede the propagation of ionising photons. Following the definition of the mean free path of ionising photons, at 40 cMpc the cumulative additional opacity is equal to unity. Hence, this barrier is uniformly distributed and porous, as roughly 37¬†% of the photons (at the Lyman limit) can permeate past the boundary condition. Unlike the r40 model, the LLS40 will also impact the ionisation rate within the H‚Äâii¬†regions.

  3. 3.

    The eLLS model uses the same additional opacity approach as LLS40 but evolves the value of the mean free path according to the fit from W14, see eq.¬†3. It thus captures the likely redshift dependence of the number of LLS within the simulation, although calibrated on post-EoR observables.

  4. 4.

    In the pLLS we use the same MFP fit as in eLLS but distribute the additional opacity according to the a recipe based on the cross-section of dark matter halos, rather than uniformly (see Shukla et¬†al., 2016, for a description). This model captures both the likely evolution of the number of LLS as well as the probable connection between LLS and collapsed structures.

  5. 5.

    Lastly, model C2 attempts to model the presence of small-scale absorbers by boosting the global recombination rate by a factor of two. We include this method, as it is an often-used solution within the community.

The conclusions of our study can be formulated as follows:

  1. 1.

    We find that by including the presence of LLS in our simulations, we can naturally reproduce delayed reionisation histories. Including redshift and/or density dependence in our LLS models further extends the EoR. Moreover, we quantify how the role of clumping in our C2 model results in an overshooting of the UVB as well as how the choice of a cut-off barrier for the r40 case can produce nonphysical features in our observables. Conversely, the diffuse barrier models LLS40, eLLS, pLLS are more consistent with the evolution of the UVB reported by high-resolution small-scale simulations of reionisation such as THESAN (Garaldi et¬†al., 2022) and CODA III (Lewis et¬†al., 2022).

  2. 2.

    By conducting a statistical analysis of the UVB for our set of models in Sec.¬†3.2, we highlight that the role of Lyman Limit Systems and how they are modelled will affect reionisation globally as well as locally. Models such as r40, LLS40, which only model the impact of the value of the MFP on ionising photons, affect the UVB on the larges-scales (captured by the right side of the PDFs in Fig.¬†2) but not the small-scales, where LLS are not directly modelled, and the ionisation rate is higher. Including the redshift evolution of the LLS in eLLS marginally improves on this. Crucially, the density and redshift-dependent pLLS model, which models the LLS on the cell level, results in small-scale fluctuation in the ionised regions, resulting in a distinct log-normal shape of the PDF.

We can summarise that the properties of the Lyman Limit systems affect the global parameters of reionisation. In the last section of the paper, we study whether these findings are consistent with observations at the EndEoR.

  1. 3.

    We conduct mock observations of the effective LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†optical depth (œÑeffsubscriptùúèeff\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}italic_œÑ start_POSTSUBSCRIPT roman_eff end_POSTSUBSCRIPT) and compare the features of our results against the measurements from the QSO sample from B22. While the eLLS,C2 models marginally reproduce the features of the observable, only the pLLS model can match the redshift evolution of the effective LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†optical depth (Œ≤=13.3¬±0.6ùõΩplus-or-minus13.30.6\beta=13.3\pm 0.6italic_Œ≤ = 13.3 ¬± 0.6) from the measurements (Œ≤=13.7¬±1.3ùõΩplus-or-minus13.71.3\beta=13.7\pm 1.3italic_Œ≤ = 13.7 ¬± 1.3).

  2. 4.

    We further our study focusing on direct observations of the mean free path of ionising photons, which as reported by Becker et¬†al. (2021) and Gaikwad et¬†al. (2023) is particularly low at a redshift of 6 and another indicator of a delayed reionisation. In Section 6 we sample lines of sight from our simulations and construct a PDF of the free paths of ionising photons at a redshift of 6 or a fixed neutral fraction of 15¬†% (see Fig.¬†5). We find that models such r40 and C2 overshoot the value of the mean free path as their implementations and cannot induce the required opacity to limit Lyman limit photons. And while all diffuse barrier models (LLS40, eLLS, pLLS) reproduce low values of the MFP at a redshift of 6 ŒªMFP‚âà25subscriptùúÜMFP25\lambda_{\mathrm{MFP}}\approx 25italic_Œª start_POSTSUBSCRIPT roman_MFP end_POSTSUBSCRIPT ‚âà 25 cMpc, their distributions differ massively due to the nature of their implementations.   3. 5.

    Lastly, we probe the effect of Lyman Limit Systems on the neutral medium during EndEoR by examining the 21 cm signal, particularly the 21 cm power spectrum as shown in Fig.¬†7. At redshift 6, we find differences in amplitude by an order of magnitude between models such r40 and pLLS, which can be attributed to the delay of the reionisation process discussed in 1. Note, the flat shape of the r40 is consistent with the discussion in Georgiev et¬†al. (2022). When comparing the 21 cm power spectra at a fixed neutral fraction of 9¬†%, we can further peer into key differences between models. We find that the clumping model has a higher large-scale amplitude of the 21 cm signal, indicative of larger neutral islands. Since the clumping model has no imposed MFP, the majority of neutral islands trace the furthest voids from the clusters of ionised sources. And while the LLS40 model imposes a barrier of 40 cMpc, its permissivity allows for ionising photons to traverse past it, resulting in a similar effect on the neutral islands. Conversely, the r40 is seen to have power across all kùëòkitalic_k-scales, because most neutral islands will be located in regions 40 cMpc away from ionising sources.

    Lastly, both eLLS and pLLS have a similar shape and amplitude. This occurs because the H‚Äâi¬†islands are less sensitive to the modelling differences of the small-scale absorbers within the ionised bubbles, but rather on the large-scale mean free path imposed by the LLS and its redshift evolution.

In summary, we find that modelling the LLS during the epoch of reionisation has a crucial role in regulating the duration of reionisation as well its observables such as the UVB background, LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†effective optical depth, mean free path of ionising photons as well as the shape of neutral islands detentions via the 21 cm signal. In concordance with Georgiev et¬†al. Meanwhile, the additional clumping added in the C2 allows the model to marginally reproduce some of the features from the œÑeffsubscriptùúèeff\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}italic_œÑ start_POSTSUBSCRIPT roman_eff end_POSTSUBSCRIPT observations. However, it is insufficient in reducing the average distance of UV photons, resulting in higher values of the UVB and MFP at redshift 6, compared to observations. This also impacts the 21-cm power spectrum, which when compared to all models at a fixed neutral fraction, has more power on low-kùëòkitalic_k scales. Introducing a uniform opacity diffuse barrier like in LLS40 resolves many of the aforementioned problems, but is not fully consistent with observations of the evolution of the LyŒ±ùõº\alphaitalic_Œ±¬†optical depth observations. Lastly, the evolving diffuse barrier model eLLS improves on the latter and reproduces the observables adequately, but it is only when we account for the density-dependence of the absorbers such as in pLLS is sufficient to reproduce the measurements of œÑeffsubscriptùúèeff\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}italic_œÑ start_POSTSUBSCRIPT roman_eff end_POSTSUBSCRIPT and the MFP.
