Abstract

Machine learning methods for estimating heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE) facilitate large-scale personalized decision-making across various domains such as healthcare, policy making, education, and more. Current machine learning approaches for HTE require access to substantial amounts of data per treatment, and the high costs associated with interventions makes centrally collecting so much data for each intervention a formidable challenge. To overcome this obstacle, in this work, we propose a novel framework for collaborative learning of HTE estimators across institutions via Federated Learning. We show that even under a diversity of interventions and subject populations across clients, one can jointly learn a common feature representation, while concurrently and privately learning the specific predictive functions for outcomes under distinct interventions across institutions. Our framework and the associated algorithm are based on this insight, and leverage tabular transformers to map multiple input data to feature representations which are then used for outcome prediction via multi-task learning. We also propose a novel way of federated training of personalised transformers that can work with heterogeneous input feature spaces. Experimental results on real-world clinical trial data demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

1 Introduction

Identifying if and how an intervention affects an outcome is a fundamental problem that finds applications across many disciplines, like healthcare, social sciences, public policy etc. Common use cases include assessing the impact of a drug on disease progression, evaluating the impact of interventions aimed at reducing crime rate, estimating the impact of offers on a customerâ€™s purchase decision and so forth. While the overall effectiveness of an intervention on a population can be measured, interventions often have disparate effects on individuals, hence determining the degree to which diverse individuals respond to interventions, aka, individual treatment effects (ITE) or heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE) estimation, is an important problem.

While, over the last decade, several data-driven machine learning approaches have been extensively utilised for this task, the success of these methods depend on availability of large amounts of good quality dataÂ (39), which is undesirable due to the high costs of the interventions. However, there is much data available when considering the aggregate information from disparate trials (and on different subpopulations) across different institutions. Thus methods that can allow these institutions to collaborate without sacrificing the privacy of their data, such that each location can benefit by improving their own estimators, are greatly desirable.

Federated Learning (FL) is a distributed learning paradigm that involves learning across numerous siloed organizations (or end devices) called clients, each retaining local data access, with the help of a central server that coordinates learning process without needing data access. Federated Learning has shown benefits in many supervised, self-supervised and unsupervised settings under the non-IID data distribution across the clients. But the HTE estimation problem imposes certain unique challenges in FL that have remained largely unexplored. These challenges include - i) heterogeneity in the feature and outcome space arising due to each client (institution) recording its own local data using different features and outcomes via different measurements, ii) selection bias which arises because the individuals that are given a particular intervention are not selected randomly but according to an existing selection process, and iii) need for counterfactual estimation. Since each individual is given exactly one treatment only the factual outcome is observed, necessitating the estimation of the counterfactual outcome(s). An overview of a typical setting is given in FigureÂ 1 where each location corresponding to an institution has different features and different treatments. Therefore, our solution to the problem of federated learning for HTE estimation requires an approach that not only builds personalize models for each local institution but can also maximally utilize the data similarities across the said institutions.

In this work, we propose an end-to-end framework that can help with all the above mentioned challenges by utilising a transformer based shared framework for learning a common feature representation space and using local multi-task learning to predict the outcomes for interventions being considered locally at each institution. The framework can accommodate both non-identical feature sets and diverse treatments across sites. We cater to such heterogeneous data by devising a novel way of collaborating through attention matrices of the transformers by jointly learning the global attention matrices which are then used to obtain the local attention matrices for personalisation. Also, we utilize the multi-task learning framework to share the task similarities within each institution that might be administering more than one intervention. Though this approach is general and can be used for HTE estimation in various application domains, this paper focuses on HTE estimation in healthcare.

3 Experiments

In this section, we conduct experimental evaluations of our method and compare it with various baseline approaches. Because of the inherent complexity of the problem, assessing the algorithmâ€™s performance on real-world datasets poses significant challenges as ground truth for the counterfactuals is typically unavailable. We will specify below the metrics that we use for testing the methods as well as the dataset it was tested on.

3.1 Experiment Setting

The primary goal of our method is to enable efficient decentralized training of the estimators for potential outcomes which finds use-cases in settings where different institutions are administering different interventions but have limited local data for training the estimator models, and carefully collaborating across institutions to learn personalized models for each of them provides better models. Since clinical trials can be risky to patients, each hospital typically has a small number of patients participating in a specific trial, and these trials are unique to each hospital.

We collect data from three randomized clinical trials for intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) therapy development: ATACH2 (NCT01176565), MISTIE3 (NCT01827046) and ERICH (NCT01202864). 4 Related Work

This section provides a brief overview of the most relevant prior work in the fields of machine learning for ITE estimation, and federated learning.

HTE Estimation

The methods for HTE estimation can broadly be divided into two categories - ones that predict the outcomes for different interventions, factual and counterfactual, and use the difference between them to obtain the ITE, and the other methods that try to directly model the ITE. Amongst the methods that predict the outcomes for each intervention separately, some methods use the intervention given or not as a binary feature along with the co-variates to create a set of independent variables (ğ’³,ğ’¯)ğ’³ğ’¯(\textbf{$\mathcal{X}$},\textbf{$\mathcal{T}$}) that are fed to the model to predict the Î¼1mâ€‹(x)superscriptsubscriptğœ‡1ğ‘šğ‘¥\mu_{1}^{m}(x) and Î¼0mâ€‹(x)superscriptsubscriptğœ‡0ğ‘šğ‘¥\mu_{0}^{m}(x) like the methods using regression treesÂ (5), random forestsÂ (21), non-parametric methodsÂ (11), and Bayesian methodsÂ (17). Due to selection bias, conventional supervised learning methods that use intervention as a feature might not be directly applicable to the task of estimation. Therefore, most works focus on building two separate models one for each potential outcome in the Neyman-Rubin framework. The earlier versions of such methods used techniques like regression based modelingÂ (8), trees, random forestsÂ (4; 15), and othersÂ (27; 20). Then recently, the focus has shifted on using neural networks where the most common approach is to segregate the estimation into learning a representation from the input and then building predictive models on top of the representationÂ (12; 3; 18; 29; 33; 2; 41; 7; 16; 38; 16). The second set of methods that focus on learning the treatment effects directly includeÂ (36; 28; 39).

Federated Learning

Initially presented as the FedAvg algorithm in the pioneering study byÂ (24), FL has since undergone numerous modifications tailored to address distinct challenges. These adaptations include both global solutions as well as personalized solutions that cater to the non-IID data across clients in a better way. Some key works that obtain global solutions for FL includeÂ (10; 19; 40; 1; 9) and that for personalised FL includeÂ (22; 34; 23; 14). Hiwever, it is only very recently that the problem of heterogeneous feature spaces has been explored in the FL setting.Â (35) use clustering to figure out similar clients and exchange knowledge within the cluster whereasÂ (30) use learnt prototypes to align the feature spaces across the clients.

5 Conclusion

This paper introduces a novel framework for heterogeneous treatment effects estimation in a federated manner and shows that collaborative learning via federated mechanisms across different institutions helps in learning better estimators. Although this paper focuses on a healthcare context with binary treatments within each institution, the framework proposed herein can be extended to accommodate multiple treatments and is applicable to various other domains as well. The insights from this work show that the method can provide much more utility particularly for the institutions which have very little local data. In the future, our objective is to expand this solution to incorporate zero or few-shot inference capabilities, enabling us to assess the effectiveness and efficacy of a completely new treatment for a specific population. We also aim to provide the privacy guarantees on the information loss while sharing the model parameters between the institutions specifically for applications where data privacy is of utmost importance.
