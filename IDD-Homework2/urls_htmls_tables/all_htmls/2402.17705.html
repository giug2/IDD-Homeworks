Abstract

Machine learning methods for estimating heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE) facilitate large-scale personalized decision-making across various domains such as healthcare, policy making, education, and more. Current machine learning approaches for HTE require access to substantial amounts of data per treatment, and the high costs associated with interventions makes centrally collecting so much data for each intervention a formidable challenge. To overcome this obstacle, in this work, we propose a novel framework for collaborative learning of HTE estimators across institutions via Federated Learning. We show that even under a diversity of interventions and subject populations across clients, one can jointly learn a common feature representation, while concurrently and privately learning the specific predictive functions for outcomes under distinct interventions across institutions. Our framework and the associated algorithm are based on this insight, and leverage tabular transformers to map multiple input data to feature representations which are then used for outcome prediction via multi-task learning. We also propose a novel way of federated training of personalised transformers that can work with heterogeneous input feature spaces. Experimental results on real-world clinical trial data demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

1 Introduction

Identifying if and how an intervention affects an outcome is a fundamental problem that finds applications across many disciplines, like healthcare, social sciences, public policy etc. Common use cases include assessing the impact of a drug on disease progression, evaluating the impact of interventions aimed at reducing crime rate, estimating the impact of offers on a customer’s purchase decision and so forth. While the overall effectiveness of an intervention on a population can be measured, interventions often have disparate effects on individuals, hence determining the degree to which diverse individuals respond to interventions, aka, individual treatment effects (ITE) or heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE) estimation, is an important problem.

While, over the last decade, several data-driven machine learning approaches have been extensively utilised for this task, the success of these methods depend on availability of large amounts of good quality data (39), which is undesirable due to the high costs of the interventions. However, there is much data available when considering the aggregate information from disparate trials (and on different subpopulations) across different institutions. Thus methods that can allow these institutions to collaborate without sacrificing the privacy of their data, such that each location can benefit by improving their own estimators, are greatly desirable.

Federated Learning (FL) is a distributed learning paradigm that involves learning across numerous siloed organizations (or end devices) called clients, each retaining local data access, with the help of a central server that coordinates learning process without needing data access. Federated Learning has shown benefits in many supervised, self-supervised and unsupervised settings under the non-IID data distribution across the clients. But the HTE estimation problem imposes certain unique challenges in FL that have remained largely unexplored. These challenges include - i) heterogeneity in the feature and outcome space arising due to each client (institution) recording its own local data using different features and outcomes via different measurements, ii) selection bias which arises because the individuals that are given a particular intervention are not selected randomly but according to an existing selection process, and iii) need for counterfactual estimation. Since each individual is given exactly one treatment only the factual outcome is observed, necessitating the estimation of the counterfactual outcome(s). An overview of a typical setting is given in Figure 1 where each location corresponding to an institution has different features and different treatments. Therefore, our solution to the problem of federated learning for HTE estimation requires an approach that not only builds personalize models for each local institution but can also maximally utilize the data similarities across the said institutions.

In this work, we propose an end-to-end framework that can help with all the above mentioned challenges by utilising a transformer based shared framework for learning a common feature representation space and using local multi-task learning to predict the outcomes for interventions being considered locally at each institution. The framework can accommodate both non-identical feature sets and diverse treatments across sites. We cater to such heterogeneous data by devising a novel way of collaborating through attention matrices of the transformers by jointly learning the global attention matrices which are then used to obtain the local attention matrices for personalisation. Also, we utilize the multi-task learning framework to share the task similarities within each institution that might be administering more than one intervention. Though this approach is general and can be used for HTE estimation in various application domains, this paper focuses on HTE estimation in healthcare.

Modeling Φ(.)\Phi(.)

Owing to the exceptional demonstrated capability of transformers in learning effective representations and their ability to work with inputs of different lengths, it makes them a natural fit for utilization as the core representation learning module. This modules obtains the representations, 𝒵𝒵\mathcal{Z}, from the input space 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X}. Since the pre-intervention covariates are captured in a tabular data format, we use transformers specially designed for tabular data structures ((42; 37)) that perform a vectorization of each row in the dataset before passing it to the transformer. A transformer heavily relies on the concept of attention mechanisms encoded in the attention matrix which allows the model to focus on different parts of the inputs for making predictions. Further, it uses multiple attention heads to allow it to attend to different parts of the input sequence simultaneously. After self-attention, the context-aware representations are passed through feed-forward neural networks to capture higher-level features. The feed-forward networks typically consist of two linear transformations separated by a non-linear activation function like ReLU.

In scenarios where each client has identical data co-variates, 𝒳msuperscript𝒳𝑚\textbf{$\mathcal{X}$}^{m}’s, the entire transformer can be learnt via the de-facto FedAvg (24) algorithm, that requires each client to learn a local model Φm(.)\Phi^{m}(.) which is periodically shared with the server where all the client models are aggregated to obtain a global model. This global model is then shared with all the clients to continue next round of training. Learning common representations for centralized HTE estimation problem have also been previously explored under the name of balanced representations ((32; 33; 13)) but we work in a distributed setting.

Modeling gm(.)g^{m}(.) and him(.)h_{i}^{m}(.)

With the goal of sharing learning between the two potential outcomes on each client, the function gm(.)g^{m}(.) is based on cross-stitch networks (25). A cross-stitch or a cross-talk architecture has been in use for multi-task learning for efficiently sharing the parameters between various tasks. A cross-talk architecture has a separate network for each task, with information flow between parallel layers in the task networks, where the amount of flow is determined by a set of learnable parameters, much like the attention mechanism. Previously, the works by (13), and (6) have showed that considering each potential outcome learning as a different task in multi-task learning framework can be useful but they use a different multi-task learning model.

Specifically, after obtaining the representations 𝒵=Φ​(𝒳)𝒵Φ𝒳\mathcal{Z}=\Phi(\textbf{$\mathcal{X}$}) from the shared transformer model, we pass them through the gm(.)g^{m}(.) specific to each client but common for different potential outcomes. Finally, we train a single-hidden layer MLP with a non-linear activation separately for each potential outcome, him(.)h_{i}^{m}(.), which are used for the estimation of the two client-specific potential outcomes.

3 Experiments

In this section, we conduct experimental evaluations of our method and compare it with various baseline approaches. Because of the inherent complexity of the problem, assessing the algorithm’s performance on real-world datasets poses significant challenges as ground truth for the counterfactuals is typically unavailable. We will specify below the metrics that we use for testing the methods as well as the dataset it was tested on.

3.1 Experiment Setting

The primary goal of our method is to enable efficient decentralized training of the estimators for potential outcomes which finds use-cases in settings where different institutions are administering different interventions but have limited local data for training the estimator models, and carefully collaborating across institutions to learn personalized models for each of them provides better models. Since clinical trials can be risky to patients, each hospital typically has a small number of patients participating in a specific trial, and these trials are unique to each hospital.

3.2 Dataset Description

We collect data from three randomized clinical trials for intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) therapy development: ATACH2 (NCT01176565), MISTIE3 (NCT01827046) and ERICH (NCT01202864). Each hospital location has a set of patient-level pre-treatment measurements that are considered the co-variates 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} (such as demographics and clinical presentation of the ICH), a binary treatment variable to indicate if the patient is under the active treatment or standard of care, and the outcomes measured as the modified Rankin Score (mRS) representing patient’s severity. Regarding the treatment, ATACH2 tests for medical intervention (𝒯1=1superscript𝒯11\textbf{$\mathcal{T}$}^{1}=1), MISTIE3 tests for surgical intervention (𝒯2=1superscript𝒯21\textbf{$\mathcal{T}$}^{2}=1), each of which is being administered on a different hospital location. All three trials contains standard-of-care therapy (𝒯m=0,∀m=1,2,3formulae-sequencesuperscript𝒯𝑚0for-all𝑚123\textbf{$\mathcal{T}$}^{m}=0,\forall m=1,2,3).

3.3 Baselines

We consider the popular CATE estimation methods based on TNet, TARNet and FlexTENet architectures as our baselines. Since these methods are designed to work in centralized settings, we show the results with these methods when each client uses the specific method to obtain the estimates. For fair comparison we show the performance of our method in a similar setting as well and call it local training. The performance of the proposed framework in the federated setting is shown as the "Ours" method. Moreover, we create a federated version of the FlexTENet based method by training local FlexTENet architecture models on the hospitals and periodically aggregating them to construct a global model which is shared by all the hospitals, this method is denoted as FlexTENet-FL in the results.

4 Related Work

This section provides a brief overview of the most relevant prior work in the fields of machine learning for ITE estimation, and federated learning.

HTE Estimation

The methods for HTE estimation can broadly be divided into two categories - ones that predict the outcomes for different interventions, factual and counterfactual, and use the difference between them to obtain the ITE, and the other methods that try to directly model the ITE. Amongst the methods that predict the outcomes for each intervention separately, some methods use the intervention given or not as a binary feature along with the co-variates to create a set of independent variables (𝒳,𝒯)𝒳𝒯(\textbf{$\mathcal{X}$},\textbf{$\mathcal{T}$}) that are fed to the model to predict the μ1m​(x)superscriptsubscript𝜇1𝑚𝑥\mu_{1}^{m}(x) and μ0m​(x)superscriptsubscript𝜇0𝑚𝑥\mu_{0}^{m}(x) like the methods using regression trees (5), random forests (21), non-parametric methods (11), and Bayesian methods (17). Due to selection bias, conventional supervised learning methods that use intervention as a feature might not be directly applicable to the task of estimation. Therefore, most works focus on building two separate models one for each potential outcome in the Neyman-Rubin framework. The earlier versions of such methods used techniques like regression based modeling (8), trees, random forests (4; 15), and others (27; 20). Then recently, the focus has shifted on using neural networks where the most common approach is to segregate the estimation into learning a representation from the input and then building predictive models on top of the representation (12; 3; 18; 29; 33; 2; 41; 7; 16; 38; 16). The second set of methods that focus on learning the treatment effects directly include (36; 28; 39).

Federated Learning

Initially presented as the FedAvg algorithm in the pioneering study by (24), FL has since undergone numerous modifications tailored to address distinct challenges. These adaptations include both global solutions as well as personalized solutions that cater to the non-IID data across clients in a better way. Some key works that obtain global solutions for FL include (10; 19; 40; 1; 9) and that for personalised FL include (22; 34; 23; 14). Hiwever, it is only very recently that the problem of heterogeneous feature spaces has been explored in the FL setting. (35) use clustering to figure out similar clients and exchange knowledge within the cluster whereas (30) use learnt prototypes to align the feature spaces across the clients.

5 Conclusion

This paper introduces a novel framework for heterogeneous treatment effects estimation in a federated manner and shows that collaborative learning via federated mechanisms across different institutions helps in learning better estimators. Although this paper focuses on a healthcare context with binary treatments within each institution, the framework proposed herein can be extended to accommodate multiple treatments and is applicable to various other domains as well. The insights from this work show that the method can provide much more utility particularly for the institutions which have very little local data. In the future, our objective is to expand this solution to incorporate zero or few-shot inference capabilities, enabling us to assess the effectiveness and efficacy of a completely new treatment for a specific population. We also aim to provide the privacy guarantees on the information loss while sharing the model parameters between the institutions specifically for applications where data privacy is of utmost importance.
