Abstract

With the maturity achieved by deep learning techniques, intelligent systems that can assist physicians in the daily interpretation of clinical images can play a very important role. In addition, quantum techniques applied to deep learning can enhance this performance, and federated learning techniques can realize privacy-friendly collaborative learning among different participants, solving privacy issues due to the use of sensitive data and reducing the number of data to be collected for each individual participant. We present in this study a hybrid quantum neural network that can be used to quantify non-alcoholic liver steatosis and could be useful in the diagnostic process to determine a liver‚Äôs suitability for transplantation; at the same time, we propose a federated learning approach based on a classical deep learning solution to solve the same problem, but using a reduced data set in each part. The liver steatosis image classification accuracy of the hybrid quantum neural network, the hybrid quantum ResNet model, consisted of 555 qubits and more than 100100100 variational gates, reaches 97%percent9797\%, which is 1.8%percent1.81.8\% higher than its classical counterpart, ResNet, although the number of weights in the hybrid quantum model is 1.751.751.75 times less than in the classical one. Crucially, that even with a reduced dataset (trimmed by more than half), our hybrid approach consistently outperformed its classical counterpart, indicating superior generalization and less potential for overfitting in medical applications. In addition, a federated approach with multiple clients, up to 323232, despite the lower accuracy, but still higher than 90%percent9090\%, would allow using, for each participant, a very small dataset, i.e., up to one-thirtieth. Our work, based over real-word clinical data can be regarded as a scalable and collaborative starting point, could thus fulfill the need for an effective and reliable computer-assisted system that facilitates the daily diagnostic work of the clinical pathologist.

I Introduction

In recent years, the application of Convolutional Neural Networks¬†[1] (CNNs) has showed excellent results in a wide range of fields related to pattern recognition, from image processing to document classification. CNNs consist of two blocks, the convolutional block used to extract image features that is connected to a block consisting of one or more fully connected layers, typical of neural networks¬†[2].

In the evolving landscape of machine learning, Quantum Machine Learning (QML) has emerged as a promising avenue, intertwining quantum computing principles with classical machine learning techniques. Incorporating quantum layers, such as Hybrid Quantum-Classical Neural Networks (HQNNs)¬†[3, 4], between the convolutional and fully connected layers of classical neural networks can offer substantial enhancements. Integrating such quantum layers can potentially improve accuracy, even with reduced dataset sizes, due to the ability of quantum computations to capture intricate data correlations¬†[5, 6, 7].

Using these algorithms, it is possible to surpass the accuracy levels achieved by a human expert. However, training them requires a substantial amount of data collection. In cases where this data involves sensitive information, privacy, security, and data integrity become significant concerns that need to be addressed.

The federated learning (FL) remedies these issues. FL, is a machine learning setting where many clients collaboratively train a model under the orchestration of a central server, while keeping the training data decentralized. It is potentially usable in any field where it is important to share knowledge without sharing data (e.g., when it is necessary to comply with the GDPR¬†[8], such as for the analysis of healthcare data or to keep customer data confidential).

This system also complies with the requirements of the Council Recommendation on Artificial Intelligence¬†[9] regarding robustness, safety and protection, and with the ‚ÄúProposals for an Italian Strategy for Artificial Intelligence‚Äù¬†[10], for ethics, reliability and protection of the consumer-user.

This machine learning system is also extremely current, facilitates compliance with the principle of accountability imposed on the data controller by the GDPR and allows to comply with the requirements of the Italian Data protection Authority in the Decalogue for the creation of national health services through Artificial Intelligence systems¬†[11], because it solves the problems linked to the use of large health databases without data sharing.

The European Union is about to adopt the world‚Äôs first law regulating artificial intelligence (AI Law)¬†[12]. By the principle on which it is based, FL should be included in limited risk systems according to the future European law on AI. Once the legal principle for the usability of the data has been identified, FL overcomes any legal problem related to the definition of privacy relationships between the participants in a multicentric analysis activity.

In this study we address the issue of the presence of hepatic steatosis in order to determine whether a liver is a viable candidate for transplantation. To obtain a robust model, we created a dataset of 440044004400 samples equally distributed among the 4 stages of steatosis (00, 111, 222, 333) and then divided it into two categories, that of transplantable livers where a low level of steatosis (00 or 111) was present and that of non-transplantable.

The objective, which was completely achieved, was to propose models that could have higher levels of accuracy than the expert user, i.e. greater than 90%percent9090\%, and with an rate of false negative, intended as a non-transplantable sample recognised as transplantable, lower than 5%percent55\%.

As a first technique we used a classical CNN, especcially ResNet, and obtained a recognition level of over 95%percent9595\%, we then created a HQNN whose accuracy is in the same range or higher but which, on a smaller dataset, has less deterioration in accuracy, with an improvement in performance of up to more than one percentage point.

Indeed both of the proposed networks have been also trained on a reduced dataset, down to 40%percent4040\% of the initial dataset, always achieving results in excess of 94%percent9494\%, thus resulting in stable and robust solutions.

Unfortunally even in the case with the smallest dataset, the number of samples to be collected is high, i.e.: 500500500, so we used a federated approach where multiple clients, up to 323232, collaborate to train the classic centralised CNN developed in the first case study.

This federated approach achieves similar performance to the centralised case by using a small number of samples, a minimum of 125125125 per client, and ensuring data privacy by satisfying the GDPR.

In light of these results, we believe that the use of HQNNs, together with the federated approach, may be the model of choice in the future for all problems where privacy or data scarcity is an issue.

We will continue the study initiated by scaling the solution by identifying the exact staging of steatosis and applying a quantum federated learning algorithm.

Methods

I.1 Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)

Steatosis can be defined as an abnormal accumulation of fat within the liver cell and can occur in a various liver diseases and its clinical implications generally correlate with its extent. Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is a chronic disorder of relative recent identification but in considerable and continuous epidemiological growth. The afflicted population is estimated about 1 billion people worldwide¬†[13]. NAFLD ranges from simple steatosis in the absence of excessive alcohol intake to non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) with or without cirrhosis¬†[14].

The natural history of NAFLD progresses from steatosis, steatohepatitis, fibrosis, cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma¬†[14]. Approximately 30%percent3030\% of the individuals with NAFLD shows histologic progression: 15%percent1515\%‚Äì20%percent2020\% of these individuals may develop cirrhosis, while another 30%percent3030\%‚Äì40%percent4040\% may suffer liver-related morbidity and mortality¬†[15]. Currently, cirrhosis due to NASH is the second leading indication for liver transplantation in the United States¬†[16]. Hepatic steatosis is a pathogenic, potentially reversible condition, even in serious cases: it is therefore important to identify the condition and assess its severity¬†[17].

Histologically, steatosis is evaluated in paraffin-embedded sections stained with hematoxylin and eosin (HE) and is visible as white droplets in the cytoplasm of hepatocytes. The fat dissolves during histological processing and droplets appear as gaps in the tissue. Droplets of fat can be distinguished from other empty spaces, such as vessels or tissue artifacts, by their size and rounded shape (Fig¬†1 (a)).

Liver biopsy and histological classification is the gold standard to diagnose hepatic steatosis. It also allows the identification of other histological features such as steatosis zonality, droplet size (macrovesicular vs. microvesicular), inflammation, cellular injury, iron overload and fibrosis staging. Kleiner et al.¬†[18] proposed a histological scoring system for NAFLD, the NAFLD Activity Score (NAS), to include a wider spectrum of NAFLD that includes isolated steatosis and not only steatohepatitis. The authors analyzed degree of inter‚Äêobserver and intra‚Äêobserver agreement between and within pathologists. Semi-quantitative evaluation of steatosis shows an inter-rater k values of 0.790.790.79, while semi-quantitative evaluation of steatosis intra‚Äêrater agreement is 0.830.830.83 (with k=1ùëò1k=1 for perfect agreement and values of k near 0 for random agreement).

Liver steatosis has emerged as the single most important variable determining graft function after transplantation¬†[19] and can be considered one of the key parameters in the decision on the suitability of a liver transplant¬†[20, 21]. In Brunt et al. classification and in Kleiner et al. classification, steatosis was classified from grade 00 to grade 333¬†[22, 18]. The extent of steatosis is commonly evaluated and reported semi-quantitatively (Table¬†1).

Taking into account that the global prevalence of NAFLD is between 24%‚àí25%percent24percent2524\%-25\% with some regional differences (the highest rates is reported in South America and the Middle East, followed by Asia, USA and Europe)¬†[23], it is not surprising that steatosis represents the most frequent liver morphological alteration in the population of potential transplantable liver donors. A model such as the one proposed, even in its current formulation, could therefore be considered a rapid diagnostic system that can be used in a very important decision-making step in liver transplantation.

I.2 Quantum machine learning

QML is a burgeoning intersection of quantum mechanics and classical machine learning techniques. Drawing inspiration from quantum mechanics, QML leverages unique quantum phenomena such as superposition and entanglement, potentially offering exponential speedups in specific computational tasks¬†[24, 5, 25, 26]. By operating within an exponentially large search space, these algorithms can achieve enhanced efficiency in pattern recognition and prediction¬†[27, 28, 29, 30].

Despite its immense potential, QML is not without challenges. The sensitive nature of quantum computations demands rigorous error correction and makes them vulnerable to external disturbances¬†[31]. However, recent advancements in quantum and quantum-inspired computing have shown promise in refining and enhancing classical machine learning processes¬†[32, 33, 34, 35, 36, 37, 38]. In particular, HQNNs have garnered attention for their applications across industries¬†[39, 40, 41], including healthcare and pharmaceuticals¬†[42, 43, 44, 45, 46]. Further, HQNNs have exhibited faster convergence to optimal solutions and superior accuracy, especially with small datasets, compared to their classical counterparts¬†[42, 47]. Moreover, when it comes to image classification, the use of transfer learning, hybrid quantum ResNets has shown remarkable potential¬†[48]. These HQNNs exhibit faster convergence rates and superior accuracy, particularly with smaller datasets, compared to classical models¬†[47].

These innovations emphasize the potential of HQNNs over purely classical methods. With this arsenal of quantum tools, we are better equipped to address intricate medical challenges, such as the task of steatosis liver image classification. Such quantum-enhanced methodologies may revolutionize diagnostics and therapeutic strategies in the near future¬†[49].

Federated learning

Federated learning¬†[50] is a machine learning technique to achieve privacy-preserving collaborative learning among different parties. Compared with traditional centralized and distributed learning, which require data to be collected in a single compute node the former and in a server to parallelize over multiple nodes the computation and thus speed up the training phase the latter, in federated learning only locally trained models are exchanged, without sharing the data with the consequence of guaranteeing privacy.

In addition, we can improve data security and privacy by using privacy-enhancing technologies (PET)¬†[51], which are developed to meet the basic principles of data protection, minimizing the use of personal data and maximizing data security.

The two most widely used techniques are Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE)¬†[52] and the Secure Multi-Party computation (SMPC)¬†[53]. FHE is a key-based encryption in which the encryption process preserves algebraic operations, in this technique a third party can calculate on the data without knowing it. In addition to depending on a trusted third party, they do not provide perfect security and therefore could represent a lack of security when used in FL. To solve this problem, Liang at all. have proposed a symmetric quantum version of FHE¬†[54] that guarantees perfect privacy by making such an encryption technique safe. The SMPC technique is a cryptographic protocol that distributes a computation across multiple parties where no individual party can see the other parties data. This approach does not need a trusted third party to see the data and in addition, the data are encrypted in-use because they are divided and distributed among the players during calculation, which makes it secure against quantum attacks (for now). On the other hand there is an high communication costs between players because the computational overhead due the use of random numbers which must be generated in order to ensure the security of the computation.

Wireless Communication

With¬†[55] the high communication costs between players. Ways to overcome this and be explore in future work is chip-to-chip wireless communication can be used to improve the efficiency and performance of federated learning. Chip-to-chip communication can be used to speed up the communication between the devices and the central server. This is because chip-to-chip wireless communication is typically much faster that traditional wired communication. Additionally, chip-to-chip wireless communication can reduce the power consumption of the devices. This is because chip-to-chip wireless communication is typically more energy-efficient than traditional wired communication. Universal Chiplet Interconnect Express is a new interconnect standard that is designed to enable high-performance, low-power communication between chiplets. It has the potential to revolutionise the way that federated learning systems are implemented. UCIe¬†[56] can be used to connect chiplets that responsible for different parts of the federated learning process, such as data aggregation, model training, and inference. This can help to improve the performance and scalability of federated learning systems. One of the challenges of federated learning is that it can be slow to train a model, especially if there is a lot of data or if the devices are not very powerful. UCIe can help to address this challenge by providing a high-speed, low-latency interconnect between devices. This can allow the device to train the model more quickly and efficiently.

Results

I.3 Dataset

Histological bioptic liver specimens were extracted from the anonymous teaching archive of the Institute of Pathological Anatomy of the University Clinical Department of Medical Surgical and Health Sciences, University of Trieste. Liver biopsy was fixed in 10%percent1010\% formalin, embedded in paraffin and stained with hematoxylin-eosin. Specimens already classified as NAFLD were rechecked and restaged by two experienced pathologists (FZ and DB) before being part of the dataset.

The dataset consists of 414141 jpeg2000 images of liver tissue digitized with D-Sight Menarini¬Æ microscope. Smaller images of [1024x1024] pixel size have been created from each of these 414141 jpeg2000 images (Fig.¬†1 (b))

We created a balanced dataset containing 110011001100 images for each stage of the disease, then in the case study we divided the images in the dataset into two classes, the one representing livers suitable (i.e.: samples labeled 00 and 111) for transplantation and those not suitable (i.e.: samples labeled 222 and 333) while maintaining a balanced dataset.

I.4 Hybrid quantum image classification

We address the classification of liver steatosis types by harnessing the capabilities of transfer learning. Transfer learning, a potent strategy in neural network training, allows expertise gained from one problem to bolster performance on another, related problem¬†[57].

For our classification task, we deploy the ResNet (Residual Neural Network) model, pretrained on the expansive ImageNet dataset¬†[58, 59]. Training deep neural networks is often plagued by the vanishing gradient issue, but ResNet effectively navigates this with its innovative residual blocks. These blocks enable deeper layers to retain essential input data information by directly passing inputs to subsequent layers within the block. Owing to this design, ResNet has cemented its reputation as a formidable tool for image classification tasks. Specifically, we employ ResNet18 via PyTorch¬†[60], with ‚Äú18‚Äù indicating the network‚Äôs depth in terms of layers.

As illustrated in Fig.¬†1(d), subsequent to the ResNet architecture, we add a fully-connected layer. Each neuron in this layer aligns with a distinct class in our binary classification objective.

Our proposed hybrid quantum model called hybrid quantum ResNet, inspired by¬†[48] is depicted in Fig.¬†1(c). Upon the completion of processing through ResNet, the hybrid architecture includes both classical and quantum layers. Given that the output feature map from ResNet encompasses 100010001000 distinct features, the subsequent fully-connected layers incorporates these 100010001000 input neurons, producing 100100100 output features.

Following the classical processing, these output features are channeled into the quantum domain via the Quantum Depth-Infused (QDI) layer¬†[42]. Remarkably, the QDI structure efficiently processes numerous features 100100100 in our model with the utilization of a mere 555 qubits. This efficiency proves crucial, especially in the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era.

The architecture of the QDI layer begins with several variational layers, succeeded by 202020 data re-uploading layers, each embedded with a variational layer. The total number of variational gates is more than 100100100. Subsequent to these, measurements are taken from all qubits, converting the quantum data into a classical vector. This vector is then fed into a final classical fully-connected layer, which discerns and predicts the corresponding output class.

The whole dataset contains 440044004400 images and, to speed up the training process without losing the prediction quality of our model, we cropped the images to size 258√ó258258258258\times 258.

Hybrid and classical neural networks were trained for 303030 epochs with Adam¬†[61, 62]. Moreover, we optimized the hyperparameters of our models using the Optuna optimizer¬†[63]. For our loss computation, we adopt the cross-entropy formula:

I.5 Federated learning for medical privacy

This section describes the architecture and the strategy as well as the dataset used in the federated learning solution.

For implementing the federated learning solution, we use the Flower framework¬†[64] which is open source and provides support for extending FL implementations to mobile and wireless clients, with heterogeneous compute, memory, and network resources. In addition it is released under Apache 2.0 license, making it a perfect candidate for integrating a quantum machine learning library.

The proposed architecture is of the horizontal federated learning type, in which each client trains its own model on its own device and then sends its parameters to the server, which after aggregating them sends a new model to the clients. The aggregation strategy used is the vanilla federated average¬†[50] strategy, in which all clients were used in both the training and validation phases.

The data set was balanced where the number of samples available for each client was the same and equally divided between the two classes, and no samples were used by two different clients.

In all simulations, the common model to be trained is the same as in the centralised case and in order to avoid overfitting in the training phase in each client the number of training epochs was less than those used in the centralised case. Specifically, 15 rounds were carried out at server level and 1 epoch locally.

As in the centralised case, we performed cross-validation by dividing the dataset into 222 parts: training and testing in a ratio of 888 to 222, resulting in 555 models.

We performed two types of simulation. The first one involved the use of almost the entire dataset, i.e. 400040004000 samples, increasing the number of clients, i.e. 444, 888, 161616 and 323232, collaborating in the training. Thus, in the 444-client case, each client used a quarter of the samples used in the centralised case, i.e.: 100010001000, while each doubling of the number of clients corresponded to a halving of the available samples. As shown in Fig.¬†3, in all the cases the accuracy is higher than 90%percent9090\% also in the 323232-client case where each client is using only 125 samples as dataset.

The second one involved reducing the dataset available in the clients, i.e. from 100010001000 representing the entire dataset to 50 samples representing one twentieth of it, leaving the number of clients unchanged. As shown in Fig.¬†3, we can obtain results above 90%percent9090\% with only 125125125 samples in each client in the case of a large number of clients, i.e.: 323232, and in all likelihood even with only 100 samples by increasing the number of clients.

In addition, by increasing the dataset by a small amount, up to 250 samples, even just collaboration among a few clients, e.g.: 4, allows for accuracy rates comparable to those of the experienced pathologist, representing valuable technical support that can be used by physicians at the time of diagnosis.

Instead, the use of a very small dataset, i.e.: 50, does not allow to obtain neither acceptable nor stable results in fact turn out to be less than 60%percent6060\% and to deteriorate as the number of clients involved in the training increases.

Discussion

Although noninvasive systems for diagnosis and staging of NALFD are being studied and tested¬†[65], the visual analysis of histological sections on liver tissue samples obtained by biopsy or resection is still considered the gold standard. The histological analysis of steatosis is performed routinely to diagnose NAFLD or to decide about the suitability of liver grafts for transplantation. In addition, because NALFD can be associated with other diseases by increasing their risk¬†[66], liver histological analysis plays a very important role in research and clinical settings.

There are previous works that used deep learning and NAFLD histology images, but these studies have taken into account animal tissues¬†[67, 68]. Animal models of liver disease are required, but they cannot reproduce the complexity of the human liver. Tissue samples analysis from diagnostic biopsies can be more difficult compared to animal biopsies performed during controlled experiments. Indeed, in human tissue there are many etiopathogenic factors (e.g. nutrition, drugs, viral infections) present in different combination, which are difficult to quantify.

Our work is original for a number of reasons. First, the possibility of automating the classification of NALFD by means of a reliable automated system that we have based on human tissue images and real clinical data. This work, which can be regarded as a scalable and collaborative starting point, could thus fulfill the need for an effective and reliable computer-assisted system that facilitates the daily diagnostic work of the clinical pathologist.

In this paper, we present a HQNN, hybrid quantum ResNet, and its classical counterpart, ResNet, to determine the presence of a steatosis rate greater than or less than 33%percent3333\%. The presence of mild steatosis is a particularly important parameter that must be considered in liver transplantation. There is general agreement that mild steatosis (<30%) causes minor graft injury¬†[69].

Both models are robust, as they were trained on a dataset of 400040004000 samples, and resulted in high classification accuracy, consistently above 95%percent9595\%, higher than that of an experienced pathologist. In addition, the level of false negatives, defined as nontransplantable samples recognized as transplantable, also has a rate comparable to that of an experienced pathologist, which is <5%.

The hybrid quantum model is a ResNet with the addition of a QDI layer, in which there are 555 qubits and over 100100100 variational gates. The HQNN achieved an accuracy of 97%percent9797\% with less than 3%percent33\% false negatives, which is very important for medical purposes. The accuracy of the classical analogue of this model is 1.8%percent1.81.8\% less, although the number of weights in the classical model is 1.751.751.75 times greater than in the hybrid one.

More accurate classification predictions even on a reduced dataset of a hybrid model indicate its better generalization ability and less overfitting compared to its classical counterpart.

A further innovation is the use of a federated learning approach based on our classical ResNet model in order to enable collaboration among different clients (up to 323232) by obtaining results comparable to those of an experienced pathologist (>90%) using a reduced dataset (up to 125125125 samples) while ensuring the privacy and security of the dataset itself. In our federated learning case, datasets in different clients were labeled by the same group of experts. In a real-world scenario, each client will have its own group of experts labeling the dataset reducing the possibility of error due to possible misclassification provided by a single group of experts resulting in a likely increase in the level of accuracy of the system.

Our model seems to exceed the results of an expert pathologist, but it still relies on data labeled by the expert pathologist himself, so it is currently still dependent on an external actor. To overcome this problem, the model can be updated in the future by relying on a new dataset labeled ‚Äúground truth‚Äù that can be created by the user(s) after clinical and instrumental feedback on the correctness of the diagnosis and classification.

Future works

Our federated model relies on conditions such as a balanced database among clients and no client failures situation not likely in the case of its use in production. To make our model more robust and fault-tolerant, we began a study based on a decentralized aggregation approach and using unbalanced datasets.

In addition, the promising results of the hybrid quantum solution and the improvements in the field of encryption due to quantum techniques as well as the lack of a reliable quantum federated learning framework lead us to focus our future studies on creating a masked quantum federated learning framework where quantum PET techniques are combined with a quantum federated learning framework.

Optical interconnections for federated learning is an exciting direction for this work as it can be a bridge between classical/quantum interconnect using wireless technology. Wavelength division multiplexing (WDM)¬†[70] networks can improve the performance and scalability of federated networks. WDM networks can be used to connect the devices in a federated network and to provide high-bandwidth, low-latency communication between devices. This can improve the performance of federated learning applications, which require fast communication between the devices and the central server.

Wireless interconnects provide an energy-efficient, scalable, and unified solution for interconnecting heterogeneous multi-chip systems with multicore processors, and in-package memory modules. Multi gigabits-per-second wireless interconnects through on-chip antennas are a reality in the backdrop of 5G communication and Internet-of-Things. Using a combination of omnidirectional and directional wireless links to provide interference-free communication among all the chips. Using directional dual-band on-chip antennas for a wireless interconnection architecture that establishes direct wireless links between processor and memory banks.

Wireless links operating at radio frequencies, establish uniform interconnection between chips operating at any voltage/frequency or communication protocols where each processing chip and memory module in the system is equipped with at least one wireless transceiver to enable inter-chip communication. In the long run, this could enable a quantum architecture enabled quantum coherent network to take advantage of quantum computers.
