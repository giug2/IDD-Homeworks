Engagement, Content Quality and Ideology over Time on the Facebook URL Dataset

Authors Emma Fraxanet 1, Fabrizio Germano 2, 3, Andreas Kaltenbrunner 4,5,1, Vicen√ß G√≥mez 1
Abstract

Unpacking the relationship between the ideology of social media users and their online news consumption offers critical insight into the feedback loop between users‚Äô engagement behavior and the recommender systems‚Äô content provision. However, disentangling inherent user behavior from platform-induced influences poses significant challenges, particularly when working with datasets covering limited time periods. In this study, we conduct both aggregate and longitudinal analyses using the Facebook Privacy-Protected Full URLs Dataset, examining user engagement metrics related to news URLs in the U.S. from January 2017 to December 2020. By incorporating the ideological alignment and quality of news sources, along with users‚Äô political preferences, we construct weighted averages of ideology and quality of news consumption for liberal, conservative, and moderate audiences. This allows us to track the evolution of (i) the ideological gap between liberals and conservatives and (ii) the average quality of each group‚Äôs news consumption. These metrics are linked to broader phenomena such as polarization and misinformation. We identify two significant shifts in trends for both metrics, each coinciding with changes in user engagement. Interestingly, during both inflection points, the ideological gap widens and news quality declines; however, engagement increases after the first one and decreases after the second. Finally, we contextualize these changes by discussing their potential relation to two major updates to Facebook‚Äôs News Feed algorithm.

Introduction

Previous research has hinted at a correlation between the quality of news sources, user ideology leanings, and online engagement. Studies have suggested that individuals tend to gravitate towards news sources that align with their political beliefs, particularly with less moderate content correlating with lower-quality news sources and with conservative audiences exhibiting higher levels of ideological segregation¬†(Gonz√°lez-Bail√≥n et¬†al. 2023; Bakshy, Messing, and Adamic 2015; Robertson et¬†al. 2023; Heseltine 2023).

However, most of these results are obtained for a specific moment in the lifetime of a platform, while the platform is typically undergoing changes in its algorithmic features, UX design, privacy settings, or moderation strategies. Therefore, while these studies are very informative on the behavior of different segments of the population in the online realm, they usually cannot address the interplay of these behaviors and the characteristics of the platform. Understanding whether the behaviors are stable through time or, on the other hand, subject to both exogenous and endogenous changes, is of relevance for the promotion of healthier online spaces.

In this paper, we approach this problem by presenting an aggregate as well as a longitudinal engagement analysis exploiting the temporal characteristics of the Facebook URL Dataset. Our primary contributions include the characterization of different types of engagement (clicks, likes, shares, comments, etc.) in relation to user ideology and domain ideology and quality. Furthermore, we develop a metric to assess partisan bias in the consumption patterns of users, with a focus on the ideological gap between news consumed by conservatives and liberals, which serves as a proxy for polarization in information consumption. Additionally, we perform a detailed study of changes in the quality of news consumption and its relation to user and news outlet ideology.

We observe notable changes in engagement trends and abrupt shifts in their dynamic patterns, which cannot be trivially explained by simple changes in user behavior. Lastly, we contextualize these changes within possible algorithmic adjustments (Mosseri 2018; Hagey and Horwitz 2021; Narayanan 2023).

Related Literature

We first present the relevant literature on user behavior online, ideological leaning, and news consumption. Then, we discuss the dataset we used and previous research conducted with it.

Social media audiences and engagement

A recent series of papers centered on the 2020 US Election and Facebook¬†(Guess et¬†al. 2023a; Nyhan et¬†al. 2023; Gonz√°lez-Bail√≥n et¬†al. 2023; Guess et¬†al. 2023b) examine social media engagement on Facebook and Instagram and present notable findings about the effects of algorithmic features on user experience and political polarization¬†(Garcia 2023).

While these studies do not offer straightforward solutions to the current challenges, they pave the way for new methodologies to incorporate algorithmic effects into the analysis of social media phenomena. For instance, Guess et¬†al. (2023a) assesses the consequences of disabling all feed ranking calculations, revealing that chronological order increases political content exposure to untrustworthy sources while decreasing user engagement and satisfaction. Similarly, Nyhan et¬†al. (2023) concludes that decreasing exposure to like-minded sources does not seem to offer a simple solution for polarization issues.

In this work, we find several results that agree with these studies, for example, highly segregated and asymmetric partisan content diets¬†(Nyhan et¬†al. 2023; Gonz√°lez-Bail√≥n et¬†al. 2023), and evidence that re-shares ‚Äîthe feature facilitating virality‚Äî may not be the only factor generating misinformation and polarization¬†(Guess et¬†al. 2023b). However, Gonz√°lez-Bail√≥n et¬†al. (2023) is performed on selected treatment groups of users who gave consent for the study. This makes it difficult to generalize the observed effects to the content shared and posted by users who are outside of the studied samples and thus subject to the standard features of the platform. Moreover, another constraint inherent in organized experiments, in general, is their reliance on static data or shorter time frames, which usually cover a few months in extent.

Outside of the studies mentioned above, research surrounding algorithmic amplification of social phenomena is also relevant to our work. Chavalarias, Bouchaud, and Panahi (2024) study a recommender system with an evolving sharing network and its interaction with opinion dynamics and individual biases. They find that recommender systems tend to maximize user engagement, leading to overexposure to toxic content and polarization.

Finally, the results of Gonz√°lez-Bail√≥n et¬†al. (2022) and Huszar et¬†al. (2022) support the hypothesis that conservative-leaning content is amplified on Twitter. Regarding the assessment of partisan audience bias in social media, Robertson et¬†al. (2018, 2023) have conducted audits and investigations on user exposure and interactions with content on Google Search, while Bakshy, Messing, and Adamic (2015) used US Facebook data on interactions with shared news and friend networks. Contrarily to Gonz√°lez-Bail√≥n et¬†al. (2023), both concluded that individual choices played a stronger role in increasing like-minded content, as well as limiting cross-cutting exposure, than algorithmic ranking.

The Facebook privacy protected full URLs dataset

The Facebook Privacy Protected Full URLs Dataset¬†(Messing et¬†al. 2020) is a large-scale collection of public posts containing URLs and user interaction data on the Facebook platform. To be included in the dataset, a URL needs to be posted (or reshared) a specific number of times¬†111According to¬†Messing et¬†al. (2020), this minimum is around 100 times, with some variability due to the injected noise to minimize information leakage.. The dataset contains information (interaction counts) at the URL-action level, broken down by month and audience demographics (country, age, and gender). For the US, these counts are also stratified by the estimated ideological leaning of the users, called Political Page Affinity or PPA, which classifies the audience into five different buckets, scaled from ‚àí22-2- 2 (most liberal) to +22+2+ 2 (most conservative), with an additional bin (unknown). This metric, based on the model described in Barber√° (2015), is computed internally at Facebook.

This dataset has been protected using differential privacy¬†(Messing et¬†al. 2020; Evans and King 2023). Specifically, the counts included in each URL have been perturbed with zero-centered Gaussian noise whose variance depends on the type of interaction. This protects individual privacy by making it hard to trace actions back to specific URLs and users.

Evans and King (2023) show that ignoring such differential privacy protections when applying inference methods to this dataset can lead to unpredictable biased results. They provide corrections in feasible scenarios, especially for linear models. For more complex models that involve, e.g., weighted average metrics, there are no available corrections in closed form. For situations involving a noisy denominator, such as the ones in this work, Evans and King (2021) and Buntain et¬†al. (2023) show that for high enough signal-to-noise ratio estimates of the denominator, it is possible to ensure reliable metrics.

Researchers have used the Facebook Privacy Protected Full URLs Dataset for various applications. Our work, akin to Guess et¬†al. (2021) and Bailey, Gregersen, and Roesner (2021), provides a descriptive static analysis of exposure and sharing patterns of news URLs in the US. We reveal findings that agree with the aforementioned previous literature, such as conservative skewed consumption of low-quality news. Interestingly, Bailey, Gregersen, and Roesner (2021) also find no difference between ideological leaning when clicking on a URL after exposure, while there is a difference in sharing.

When it comes to studying potential platform strategies, Thero and Vincent (2022) and Vincent, Thero, and Shabayek (2022) investigate engagement changes in posts from groups and pages tagged as ‚Äúrepeat offenders‚Äù of misinformation, noting the limited long-term effectiveness of such measures. Bandy and Diakopoulos (2023) characterize Facebook‚Äôs news exposure during the 2020 US election. They show that Facebook amplified low-quality publishers and that their efforts to improve the news ecosystem quality by tweaking their News Feed Algorithm had a generalized effect on most publishers, disregarding their quality.

Finally, other studies leverage the dataset as a tool for information extraction (Allen, Watts, and Rand 2024; Buntain et¬†al. 2023; Barnab√≤ et¬†al. 2022, 2023; Heuer and Glassman 2022).

The work closest to ours is Buntain et¬†al. (2023), where the authors propose a metric for obtaining robust ideology scores for URLs and domains based on exposure and engagement. Unlike Buntain et¬†al. (2023), we consider the temporal dimension and analyze the patterns of ideological segregation in news consumption and information quality from engagement and ideology scores.

Data collection and preprocessing

For each URL, we extract the counts of user engagement actions. The data is segmented by month, covering the period from January 2017 to December 2020¬†222Access to this data and posterior analysis is conducted on the Facebook Open Research and Transparency platform and allowed through the Social Science One (SS1) approval process.. We focus on URLs shared primarily in the US (i.e., the most-seen country of the URL is the US) and extract engagement counts from users located in the US only.

We only extract counts for URLs that are not older than one year (i.e. that were not posted on Facebook for the first time more than one year previous to the measurement). This is a very loosely restricting threshold to avoid extracting intractable amounts of data, given that (we hypothesize) the inclusion of URLs in the dataset is cumulative: Facebook includes new URLs in the dataset every month (if they meet the minimum shares threshold) but does not remove them. This is the case for, at least, the time interval of 2017‚Äì2019. We empirically chose the one-year threshold after observing that the engagement of a URL typically declines, on average, by two orders of magnitude within three months of its initial posting.

We define passive engagement or consumption (used interchangeably) as the absolute counts of views and clicks. Conversely, we define active engagement as the likes, shares, comments, and emoji reactions (angers, hahas, wows, loves and sorrys). We also consider the sum of all active engagements as an additional metric. We aggregate the data in terms of gender and age of users, but use the dataset bucket counts for the month and user PPA, including politically undefined users.

Aggregate Analysis

To understand and characterize user behavior in the platform concerning their consumption and interaction with posts that contain URLs, we first provide an aggregate analysis comprising the four years of data. We want to understand how the categories of users, in terms of ideological leaning, differ in their engagement patterns. Therefore, after the description of the aggregate methods and before the description of other methods, we present the aggregate findings. This allows a more nuanced and comprehensive analysis in the following sections.

Aggregating engagement with news-related URLs

We compute the distribution of engagement counts over each user PPA category. We do not normalize the counts of engagement per capita because we do not have information on the unique users who performed those actions. The methods to obtain confidence intervals for these sums are detailed in the Appendix, section A2.

To understand the patterns in news consumption for different user categories in terms of ideology and quality of the domains contained in the URLs, we discretize both dimensions into bins and represent the number of clicks in that ideology or quality level for each category of user in the form of a heatmap. We use clicks because it is a form of passive engagement that is a more reliable indicator of consumption of the information contained in the URLs, since a view could merely account for a loaded and quickly scrolled-over post in a user‚Äôs feed. However, we studied the same patterns explained below with several engagement metrics (views, clicks, shares, likes, and comments) and found no relevant differences between them.

We normalize the heatmaps by column, obtaining the probability of the consumption of a specific domain level conditioned on a user‚Äôs membership in a given category. A high value of SNR ensures that the noise is sufficiently low, preventing inflation of the metric or pathological outcomes such as having a zero in the denominator. Engagement patterns by user ideology

When examining patterns of engagement, we observe interesting trends across different types of interactions and ideological groups. From Figure¬†1A, we find a slightly higher number of views for moderate and strong liberals, which could imply that either (I) they are more active in passively consuming URL-containing posts or (II) there are more of them on Facebook (if we hypothesize that the passive post consumption capacity of each group is independent of their ideological leaning). For clicks, the other type of passive engagement, the pattern shifts slightly to a U-shape, with center users having the lowest number of clicks. As for the active engagement counts, there is a clear U-shaped pattern for hahas, likes, comments, and angers (increasingly pronounced in that same order). The distribution for shares and other reactions (loves, sorrys and wows) has a flatter shape, or at least more similar to views (where moderate liberals have larger numbers). Longitudinal Analysis

In this section, we present the methods, metrics, and results of our longitudinal analysis, focusing on engagement and consumption of news-related URLs over a four-year period.

Engagement timeline and breakpoint detection

Figure¬†2 presents examples of time series for view counts (top) and aggregated active engagement counts (middle). To analyze these series, we first identify relevant inflection points by applying segmented linear regression¬†(Muggeo 2003; Jekel and Venter 2019) to each engagement metric individually. Segmented linear regression models the data as a piecewise linear trend with changes at distinct breakpoints.

For most individual engagement metrics the most natural number of breakpoints identified is two, indicating the presence of two distinct shifts: the first around the end of year 2017, where engagement transitions from a steep decline to a moderate increase, and the second occurring after the first quarter of 2020, where the reverse pattern is observed. These shifts are marked by shaded regions in Figure¬†2 (see Appendix, section A5 for more details) while the dashed lines indicate dates for which actual changes in the Facebook ranking algorithm have been reported (Mosseri 2018; Hagey and Horwitz 2021; Narayanan 2023).

Although all individual engagement metrics exhibit similar trends, we find notable differences between some of them. For example, after the second trend shift, the absolute sum of shares declines (orange line, bottom plot in Figure¬†2), aligning with the absolute sum of comments (red line in the same plot). This is consistent with a reported decrease in the weight for shares affecting the feed ranking in 2020. We further elaborate this in the Discussion section.

Content Quality metrics

For our analysis, we compute the previous metrics for each month and consider their evolution during the four years in the dataset. We then approach the study of the quality of news consumption in two separate ways.

First, we present a combined longitudinal study of both content quality and ideological leaning. Based on our prior aggregate analysis that shows differences in the news diets of conservative and liberal users in terms of quality, we calculate the average content quality for each ideological class (i.e., conservatives, liberals, centrists, and undefined), weighted by engagement metrics such as shares, likes, comments, and clicks. This calculation follows the same methodology as the ideology-weighted average of equation¬†(1). Additionally, we compute the weighted standard deviation, similarly to equation¬†(2). The evolution of both metrics over time provides further insight into the dynamics within each group.

Second, we study the evolution of news consumption through time in terms of quality so we can assess the distribution of engagement for different quality levels per month. In this case, we consider all users (with defined or undefined PPA).

Findings regarding the ideology gap

The aggregate analysis (see Figure¬†1) has already shown that news consumption varies across user categories based on ideological leanings. Consequently, a gap between the average consumption across user classes is expected, and this is indeed observed. Discussion and Conclusions

Summary of findings

We conducted an exploratory aggregate and longitudinal analysis of a large-scale dataset of URLs shared and accessed on Facebook in 2017-2021. We investigated the patterns and shifts of news exposure and engagement of Facebook users over the period. Importantly, the data also allowed us to track the quality of the news sources accessed as well as the ideological leaning of both the sources and the users.

The aggregate analysis shows U-shaped patterns of engagement that indicate more extreme users are more likely to engage actively with content, particularly through comments, likes, and expressions of anger. The correlation between ideology strength and participation online that generates these U-shaped patterns was already found in Kalogeropoulos et¬†al. (2017) for shares and comments, having comments a higher association between the two variables. Additionally, we find that news diets of users, and engagement with such news, are biased toward their ideological leaning. However, these biases are not symmetric. Agreeing with found trends of asymmetric polarization in congressional media engagement over time, based almost exclusively on a growing engagement extremity by Republicans in Congress (Heseltine 2023), we find a strong bimodal pattern for conservative users comprising a moderate and a more extreme set of sources. Liberals, on the other hand, have a shifted unimodal pattern. In terms of quality, we also find that conservative users, in the majority, are the users who consume content from lower-quality domains. This agrees with previous literature (Guess et¬†al. 2021; Bailey, Gregersen, and Roesner 2021).

The longitudinal analysis, on the other hand, reveals two distinct inflection points in engagement trends: an upward trend commencing in 2018 and a downward trend starting in 2020. The initial upward trend is accompanied by a moderate increase in the ideological gap and a rise in the prevalence of lower-quality sources, which is followed by a gradual decline until early 2020. Conversely, the second change in the engagement trend is accompanied by a sizable increase in the ideological gap and prevalence of lower-quality sources followed by a moderate decrease later in 2020. Additionally, the two previous points also exhibit sudden declines in the proportion of engagement directed to low-quality sources, which could be attributed to the downgrading of some of these sources in the posts clicked. Below, we connect these observations with their potential relation to the News Feed algorithm changes.

Contextualization of our results with the Facebook News Feed algorithm

A major update in Facebook‚Äôs News Feed algorithm occurred at the start of 2018 (Mosseri 2018; Hagey and Horwitz 2021; Narayanan 2023). This update had the purpose of overturning an observed downward trend in user engagement. It introduced the metric of Meaningful Social Interactions (MSI‚Äôs) and rewarded posts that were likely to trigger certain types of engagement (especially comments and reshares, but also reactions such as haha‚Äôs, wow‚Äôs or angers). A second update, somewhat less documented, seems to have occurred at the beginning of 2020 (Hagey and Horwitz 2021; Narayanan 2023). The purpose seems to have been not so much to boost engagement as to limit the spread of particularly toxic, divisive or low-quality posts, through limiting long chains of reshares. Among other things, it apparently took away the boost given to reshares, while keeping it high for comments (Hagey and Horwitz 2021; Narayanan 2023).

A natural question that arises is whether the changes in engagement observed in the longitudinal analysis are related to the changes in the News Feed algorithm mentioned above, and, eventually, what the underlying mechanism(s) may be. Matias (2023) and Narayanan (2023) emphasize the importance of asking this kind of question.

Consider the 2018 update. It increased the weights attached to specific types of engagement (comments, shares, reactions etc.) with the stated objective of increasing overall engagement (‚Äúmeaningful social interactions‚Äù) (Mosseri 2018; Hagey and Horwitz 2021). At the same time, however, as our longitudinal analysis suggests, the increase in engagement came with increased ideological segregation of news consumed by liberals and conservatives and a decrease in information quality. According to Simchon, Brady, and Van¬†Bavel (2022), whose results indicate that for political contexts polarized language is associated with higher engagement in social media, this increase would make sense.

In terms of quality, we believe that a possible explanation for this, based on the aggregate analysis, comes from the fact that more extreme users consume more extreme content, and, particularly for conservative users, also of lower quality. This, combined with the U-shaped patterns of engagement, suggests that more extreme users may be more likely to engage with comments, likes or angers than moderate ones. The reasoning is that an algorithmic update that boosts the weights on, say, comments that are strongly U-shaped, makes posts that are likely to generate comments more visible to users. But because more extreme users are more likely to comment, and such users tend to consume more extreme and (in part) also lower quality content, the algorithmic boost will tend to make more extreme and lower quality content more visible. The latter will in turn be more likely to be commented on, making it even more visible and so on. Related to these questions, Germano, G√≥mez, and Sobbrio (2022) study such feedback loops occurring in response to algorithmic changes and Chavalarias, Bouchaud, and Panahi (2024) also study associated network effects. Going through the feedback loop between algorithm and user engagement, applying the above logic to an update such as the MSI update of 2018, may well trigger increased engagement but also more polarization (because of the more oppositely extreme content being consumed on both sides) and more low-quality information or misinformation (because of the lower quality content being consumed, especially on the conservative side).

On the other hand, the 2020 update seems more difficult to reconcile with the observed patterns, since, unlike the 2018 one, it did not boost the weights on engagement but rather reduced them, especially for the reshares (Hagey and Horwitz 2021; Narayanan 2023). We observe reduced engagement but also an increased ideology gap and a stronger prevalence of low-quality sources. One point that may be worth emphasizing is that reshares have a visibly less U-shaped pattern than comments, whose weight remained high in the news feed ranking algorithm. It is therefore not inconceivable that a reduction in the weight of reshares (not really U-shaped) while maintaining the weights on comments high (strongly U-shaped) may make comments relatively more important and may therefore boost those undesired effects in terms of consumption of more extreme and low-quality sources. Clearly, the precise role of the differences in weights for different engagement patterns and their relation to engagement as well as segregation of news consumption and diffusion of low-quality news and/or misinformation is something that requires access to more detailed information and seems worth exploring further.

Limitations

In our analysis, we present the findings derived from interactions with news-related URLs on Facebook spanning from 2017 to 2021 by US users. However, it is crucial to note that our conclusions are not intended to be universally applicable to all Facebook posts or online behavior in general.

It is also important to acknowledge the potential selection bias inherent in the dataset, which consists only of publicly shared URLs that have garnered significant interaction. This bias may skew towards viral posts and a particular subset of users who engage with such content. Also, since we do not have access to individual-level data we may be missing the effects of small segments of the population that may contribute to spreading low-quality information through reshares, which was what motivated the reduction of the reshare weights.

The fact that we use domain-level ideology and quality scores is another limitation given that it is possible to have heterogeneity of URLs within a single source. In terms of ideology, it has been shown that the average partisanship of source audiences is a relative measure of partisanship (Green et¬†al. 2023; Gonz√°lez-Bail√≥n et¬†al. 2023; Buntain et¬†al. 2023), not an absolute one, particularly for moderate sources (i.e. there are different ways to reach a moderate average, which differ on what is the overlap of the consumed particular news pieces by each ideological group). If we assume that the heterogeneity within domains remains stable over time, at least for the majority of the domains, the effect of these biased scores on our weighted ideology averages should remain constant over time as well. Considering that the problem is that we might be categorizing bimodal distributions of URLs as a moderate domain, this may result in a smaller perceived ideological gap than actually exists, which would just strengthen our conclusions. Moreover, this reasoning likely explains the high weighted standard deviations observed in Figure 3.

Additionally, three significant confounders‚Äî the COVID-19 pandemic, beginning in March 2020, the US 2018 Midterm elections in November 2018, and the US Presidential elections in November 2020‚Äî could potentially influence our results. While we acknowledge their presence, their exact impact remains difficult to quantify within the scope of this study.

Ethical considerations

Access to the dataset is governed by an application process and formal agreement, facilitated through Facebook‚Äôs servers to ensure user privacy. While this protects user data, it poses challenges for replication by other researchers and even ourselves, particularly if access is revoked or the dataset undergoes changes.

The dataset‚Äôs limitations prevent us from disentangling the influence of algorithmic recommendations in user news feeds from the impact of their social network, considering both friend connections and pages. Friendship networks, and their degrees of homophily, play a role in the spreading dynamics of reshares in contrast to comments or likes. This complexity adds a layer of uncertainty to our analysis.

Finally, the opacity surrounding platform features, such as alterations in the weighting of engagement metrics in the recommendation pipeline and their role in the overall News Feed algorithm, poses a significant challenge. This limits our study to a descriptive and exploratory analysis, leaving many questions regarding the platform‚Äôs mechanisms as open questions or mild hypotheses.

We explicitly discourage the use of our research to draw potentially negative conclusions about subsets of users regarding their ideology.

Future work and directions

There are many more questions that this particular dataset alone cannot answer, but would be of great interest to pursue (e.g. specific information on the timing and nature of key changes in the News Feed algorithm). We hope that our initial longitudinal analysis provides a foundation for continued investigations.

It is not within the scope of this work to disentangle the correlation of the studied behaviors with user demographics such as age or gender, which we believe could be a highly interesting topic for future research. Additionally, our study is focused solely on US data due to availability constraints. Expanding this analysis to other countries with different political systems and cultural backgrounds would be a valuable direction for further research.

A5. Breakpoint detection

To automatically detect the breakpoints or inflection points in the engagement timelines, we fit continuous piece-wise linear functions to our data with the implementation of (Jekel and Venter 2019), which combines it with global optimization to find the best locations for the inflection points with a given number of segments to fit.

Our approach is the following: we fit the data of each metric for a range of number of segments and compute the R-squared value of each fit. The quality of the fit increases with the number of segments since more segments will always allow a better fit, therefore using the elbow method we heuristically choose a number that explains most of the variation without over-fitting. Then, rounding the inflection points found in this continuous space to the closest month for a discrete interval while considering the standard deviation of the parameters, we determine which are the ‚Äùinflection point‚Äù periods.
