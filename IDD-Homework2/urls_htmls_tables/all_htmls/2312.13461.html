Abstract

In the expanding realm of machine learning (ML) within edge computing, the efficient exchange of information in federated learning (FL) environments is paramount. FL‚Äôs decentralized nature often leads to significant communication bottlenecks, particularly in settings where resources are limited. Traditional data compression techniques, such as quantization and pruning, provide partial solutions but can compromise model performance or necessitate costly retraining. Our paper addresses this issue through FedSZ, a novel lossy compression-based FL framework. FedSZ is designed to minimize the size of local model updates without impacting model performance. Our framework features a compression pipeline integrating data partitioning, lossy and lossless model parameters, metadata compression, and efficient serialization. We conduct a thorough evaluation of FedSZ utilizing a variety of lossy compressors, among which SZ2 emerged as the most effective, consistently performing well across diverse neural network architectures, including AlexNet, MobileNetV2, and ResNet50, and datasets such as CIFAR-10, Caltech101, and FMNIST. A relative error bound of 1E-2 balances compression and data integrity, achieving compression ratios ranging from 5.55‚Äì12.61√ó5.55\mbox{--}12.61\times. Furthermore, we observed that the runtime overhead introduced by FedSZ is minimal, at less than 4.7%percent4.74.7\%, compared to a significant reduction in network transfer times, which we noted to exceed 13.3√ó13.3\times reduction or saving of over 100100100s in edge networks operating at 10Mbps. Our findings firmly establish the efficacy of FedSZ, offering valuable insights for achieving an optimal balance between communication efficiency and model performance in FL settings, particularly in edge computing environments.

I Introduction

Federated learning (FL), a decentralized machine learning (ML) paradigm, has found applications in a plethora of fields, including image classification and generative text models on mobile phones, real-time decision-making on edge devices, large-scale anomaly detection, and personalization in healthcare¬†[1]. However, FL‚Äôs broad adoption and effectiveness face challenges due to the high complexity of models, substantial computational demands and ever-increasing scale of the system. Modern FL systems (such as FedAvg¬†[2]) often require processing and training models, each with billions of parameters, while communicating with thousands of distributed clients, bringing a heavy computational and communication overhead.

Communication and I/O become a significant bottleneck with many clients to achieve high scalability and robustness for FL¬†[3, 4, 5]. For instance, an autonomous vehicle can generate up to 1 GB/s of sensor or image data for on-device training and validation¬†[6]. If a 10GB model is connected to a FL system via a mobile network with a bandwidth of 10Mbps, one client update to the server would take approximately 150 minutes. With several other computational tasks happening and an autonomous vehicle being battery-constrained, this is a significant amount of time and energy spent on communication alone. As a result, strategies to cut data-related communication challenges are necessary to enhance the scalability and robustness of FL¬†[7].

Although error-bounded lossy compression (EBLC)¬†[8, 9, 10] has been widely used to significantly reduce the grand volumes of data generated by high-performance computing (HPC) simulations, whether and how the EBLC can be used to mitigate the communication cost for FL significantly is still an open burning question. Moreover, introducing EBLC produces many new challenges for FL: (1) The FL environment fundamentally differs from HPC. In the area of HPC, the compute or storage nodes are generally under centralized management in a supercomputer, where the related resources are relatively stable. However, in FL, the distributed clients could be any device (e.g., Raspberry Pi, ESP8266, NVIDIA Jetson) on the wide area network (WAN), projecting a fairly non-deterministic environment with diverse communication bandwidths and node compute power, so the lossy compression method has to consider data fidelity, network latency, and device processing power. (2) Scientific datasets are generally much more compressible than the parameter datasets generated by FL clients. The key reason is that the scientific datasets generally correspond to a certain status of a physical, chemical or biological phenomenon. In comparison, the FL parameter data are often irregular/spiky, to be shown in Section V-A. Which compression method best fits the FL parameter data reduction use case is a non-trivial problem to resolve.

In this paper, we address several key questions to confirm our hypothesis that EBLC significantly cuts communication costs and overall wall-clock time while preserving FL model accuracy very well.

  1. 1.

  3. 3.

    Lossy compression, while reducing data size, introduces both computational overhead and data error. Is the runtime and error cost incurred by these factors outweighed by the benefits of reduced communication time and bandwidth usage?

II Background and Motivation

FL is an ML framework designed to leverage distributed edge devices across a wide-area network (WAN) while maintaining data privacy¬†[5]. Unlike traditional models, FL significantly reduces data volume and privacy concerns. In FL, edge clients perform local computations, lessening data transferred to the server, which cuts bandwidth consumption, latency, and storage needs¬†[1]. Additionally, it enhances privacy since raw data remains on local devices, reducing the risk of data leaks¬†[12].

However, FL framework still faces challenges, particularly data transfer bottlenecks, necessitating the exploration of data reduction methods like lossy compression to alleviate long I/O times for clients¬†[7, 5]. This section explores lossy compression‚Äôs role in improving I/O efficiency in FL workflows.

II-A Error-bounded Lossy Compression

Lossy compression techniques explicitly designed for floating-point data like SZ2¬†[13], SZ3¬†[8, 9], SZx¬†[14], and ZFP¬†[10] enable significant reductions in storage and transmission costs while preserving the accuracy needed for analysis. Below, we summarize four state-of-the-art error-bounded lossy compressors with fundamentally different designs and implementations. The four compressors are also outstanding representatives for three cutting-edge classic lossy compression models: prediction-based model, bit-wise encoding model, and transform-based model, respectively.

  • ‚Ä¢

    SZ2¬†[13] operates on a prediction-based model. It processes datasets in small multi-dimensional blocks, applying hybrid prediction using Lorenzo and linear regression. Post-prediction, SZ2 quantizes prediction errors and compresses the resultant integers using Huffman encoding and Zstd, ensuring efficient data reduction.

  • ‚Ä¢

    SZ3¬†[8, 9] enhances the prediction model of SZ2 with multi-dimensional dynamic spline interpolation, followed by quantization, Huffman encoding, and Zstd. This approach, not requiring storage of linear regression coefficients, offers improved compression ratios, especially beneficial for higher error bounds.

  • ‚Ä¢

    SZx¬†[14], designed for speed, adopts a bit-wise-operation-based encoding model. It segments data into consecutive blocks, determining if each can be represented as a constant block within a given error bound. Non-constant blocks undergo bit-wise truncation. This method‚Äôs simplicity ensures rapid compression and decompression.

  • ‚Ä¢

    ZFP¬†[10], diverging in approach, employs a transform-based model. It applies a custom orthogonal transform to data blocks, encoding the transformed coefficients with specialized bitplane encoders. ZFP‚Äôs method offers high compression ratios and speeds, benefiting from its optimized transform and encoding strategies.

II-C Federated Learning Data Management

FL involves collaboratively training a model across decentralized edge devices while keeping data localized. This preserves privacy but imposes statistical and systems-related challenges compared to centralized data settings¬†[15]. Statistically, FL must address non-IID (independent and identically distributed) data distributions across devices. This can hamper model convergence and accuracy compared to centralized datasets¬†[15]. Devices also have unbalanced and sparse data, which can skew training. A core constraint is the massive volume of data generated at the network edge. Sensors, multimedia, logs, and other sources produce enormous and rapidly growing data on resource-limited devices. Transmitting all this raw data to central servers is infeasible due to device capabilities, latency, and privacy risks. Thus, data must be locally stored and used for model training. However, edge devices have limited storage, bounded by megabytes to gigabytes. This severely restricts the volume of accumulated data for local learning. Smaller local datasets degrade model accuracy and training convergence compared to centralized settings.

III Related Works

In this section, we discuss three aspects of related work. We identify the gap our solution, FedSZ, aims to address while foreshadowing the advantages of our methodology.

III-A Impact of Lossy Compression on Data Transfer

Error-bounded lossy compression (EBLC) has been widely explored to improve data transfer performance significantly in many scenarios. For example, Liang et al.¬†[16] disclosed that various supercomputers may project different I/O bandwidths, such that selecting appropriate lossy compressors is critical to maximizing the overall compression-based I/O performance. Specifically, Liang et al. characterized the I/O bandwidth on multiple supercomputers and developed an adaptive lossy compression framework combining SZ and ZFP to maximize the I/O performance. Liu et al.¬†[17] developed Ocelet, which can leverage parallel EBLC to accelerate the data transfer performance between Globus endpoints on a wide area network (WAN). Over 90% of the transfer time can be reduced by Ocelet. Compared with the above existing use cases, using lossy compression in FL faces more challenges (such as more diverse/dynamic bandwidth between clients and servers and the parameter data being much more challenging to compress than scientific datasets).

III-B FL Communication Cost Reduction

Various approaches have emerged in the quest to mitigate communication costs in FL, each with its unique set of drawbacks. Kang and Ahn¬†[18] offer a method involving partial model structures sent to clients. However, this technique highly depends on specific neural network architectures and client resources, limiting its generalizability. Yang et al.¬†[19] propose RingFed, a decentralized aggregation method that, while practical, adds complexity and necessitates reliable local network conditions, potentially restricting its applicability in diverse settings. Wu et al.¬†[20] focuses on reducing the indexing overhead in sparse model updates with SmartIdx. This solution performs well for inherently sparse models but may not be universally applicable. These approaches, though innovative, often excel in specialized circumstances‚Äîbe it model architecture, network topology, or update sparsity. Our work aims to offer a more universally applicable solution by integrating lossy compression to balance communication efficiency and model performance.

III-C Compression of Machine Learning Models

Several techniques have been proposed for compressing deep neural networks (DNNs), including lossless and lossy methods. Lossless compression approaches like LEO use linear programming and rectified linear units to encode network weights but have low compression ratios and high compression time overheads¬†[21]. Lossy DNN compression techniques such as DeepSZ and Delta-DNN apply pruning to sparsify networks and quantization to shrink weight parameters but may degrade model accuracy and require costly retraining to recover performance¬†[22, 23]. Universal randomized lattice quantization has been proposed to compress DNNs by quantizing weight vectors, offering universal near-optimal performance regardless of weight statistics but with limitations in codebook size¬†[24]. Therefore, it is necessary to explore lossy compression‚Äôs effects on model performance to improve the compression time overhead and compression ratios while maintaining high levels of accuracy.

V FedSZ: A Federated Lossy Compression Scheme

Our main contribution in this paper is FedSZ: a widely applicable compression scheme for FL client-server communications. Our technique is summarized as follows: (i) partitioning the model state dictionary into lossy and lossless segments, (ii) parallel lossy and lossless compression of the partitions, and (iii) communication of the bitstream for decompression at the receiving host. This method is illustrated in Figure 1, which involves the compression stage (as shown in Figure 1(a)) and decompression stage (as shown in Figure 1(b)). The following text details the different considerations of our design and our new strategies.

V-A Characterizing Model State Data

First, we characterize the FL model parameters in this subsection, which is critical to understanding the challenge and effectiveness of error-bounded lossy compression (EBLC) when applied to them. We compare the value variation of FL model parameters versus the classic scientific simulation data in Figure 2, where the relative data index means the 1D data index in the specific snippet or slice. The figure illustrates that the FL model parameters are very spiky, while the classic simulation datasets exhibit much higher smoothness. This is because the scientific simulation data are often used to convey specific physical or chemical phenomena, as demonstrated in Figure 2 (c) and (d). As such, a serious question arises: Can the EBLCs still work effectively on FL model parameters? Moreover, which compressor is the best choice? We answer them in our study.

V-B Overview of FedSZ Algorithm

FedSZ is designed based on essential takeaways learned during careful analysis and experiments. Specifically, we learn that the optimal compression scheme given the design in Figure 1 needs to include three critical strategies: (i) partition the state dictionary into lossy and lossless parts, (ii) use SZ2 to lossy compress and blosc-lz to lossless compress, (iii) dump to a bitstream and communicate the model to the server. Design details are described in the following Algorithm 1.

V-C Partitioning the State Dictionary

Our FedSZ algorithm employs a partitioning strategy segregating the state dictionary into the components that we can compress without sacrificing accuracy and the parts that require lossless compression to maintain the integrity of the model state (as shown in line 4 of the algorithm 1). The complexities in efficiently compressing DNNs necessitate a method for partitioning the model‚Äôs state dictionary into lossy and lossless compressible segments (see line 4-8). In a DNN, maintaining the state for training and evaluation involves various data types, including parameter tensors, running means, and bottlenecks. PyTorch provides methods like named_parameters() and state_dict() to access this information, which is indispensable for accurately loading models. While named_parameters() yields only the weights and biases, the state_dict() captures the complete state of the model, both trainable and non-trainable. However, indiscriminate lossy compression of the entire state dictionary risks significant degradation in model accuracy, which has been verified in our experiments. Our observation is also consistent with DeepSZ¬†[22], which aims to save storage space using lossy compression. As such, we adopt an adaptive/selective compression strategy to retain essential model metadata.

V-D Exploring the Most Effective Lossy Compression Method

In our exploration of determining which lossy compressor we should use, we compare SZ2 (v1.12.5), SZ3 (v3.1.7), SZx (v1.0.0), and ZFP (v1.0.0), all error-bounded floating-point lossy compressors with different characteristics as described in Section II-A. To decide which EBLC to use, we carefully investigate (i) the distribution of the data to compress, (ii) the impact on inference accuracy, and (iii) the performance of the compressors. All performance data here are computed on Argonne‚Äôs Swing cluster, each node with 8√ó\timesNVIDIA A100 40GB GPUs and 2√ó\timesAMD EPYC 7742 64-Core Processors with 128 cores.

V-D1 Relative Error Bounding

Selecting an appropriate error bounding mode is a nuanced task that directly correlates with the statistical characteristics of the data to be compressed¬†[25]. Our study focuses on the weight distributions of three distinct models: AlexNet, MobileNetV2, and ResNet50, as visualized in Figure 3. While each model‚Äôs weight distribution is between -1 and 1, they exhibit different dynamic ranges and clustering behaviors around zero.

Given these variations in weight distributions, a fixed error bound could either be overly conservative for some data segments or too liberal for others, thereby not exploiting the compression potential to its fullest. This is where the utility of relative error bounds becomes apparent. A relative error bound adapts to the local properties of the data by being a multiple of the dynamic range, ensuring that the error introduced during compression is proportional to the data‚Äôs actual value. Consequently, we opt for relative error bounding modes for SZ2, SZ3, and SZx. For ZFP, it does not offer a direct relative error mode, so we select the closest analogous option, which is fixed precision mode, where the number of uncompressed bits is fixed. This choice allows us to harness the benefits of adaptive error control, accommodating the different dynamic ranges observed across different models.

V-D2 Inference Accuracy Comparison

The measure of the efficacy of a lossy compression algorithm in an FL setting such as FedSZ is not just compression performance but also its impact on the model‚Äôs accuracy and convergence behavior. Achieving a high compression ratio is of little benefit if it comes at the cost of a model that fails to converge to a satisfactory level of accuracy. Therefore, in addition to comparing raw compression metrics, we also closely examine how each compressor influences the model‚Äôs accuracy throughout training epochs. We run the AlexNet model on the CIFAR-10 dataset for ten epochs to conduct this evaluation, applying each candidate compressor. The observed trends in inference accuracy for these compressors are depicted in Figure 4.

As illustrated, the accuracy trajectories for most compressors are closely aligned, indicating a minimal difference in compressor impact, except for SZx, which exhibits a marked accuracy deviation due to its block mean storage. Our findings affirm that a nuanced balance between compression performance and accuracy impact is critical for the practical applicability of the chosen lossy compressor.

V-D3 Performance Characteristics

We summarize the comparison of lossy compressors in the context of FedSZ in Table I through which we find that SZ2 is the optimal compressor. This table summarizes critical performance metrics‚Äîmean throughput, runtime, compression ratio, and Top-1 Accuracy‚Äîacross multiple error bounds. Our primary focus is on compressors that achieve high compression ratios and maintain high accuracy. In this regard, SZ2 stands out. It outperforms SZ3 and ZFP in compression ratios by 14.6% and 170.3%, respectively. Notably, this comes at the relatively modest cost of an additional 0.62‚Äãs0.62s0.62$\mathrm{s}$ in runtime when compared to ZFP, only 3.1%percent3.13.1\% more in total training time. In FL training when network I/O can often become the bottleneck, especially with large ML models, a higher compression ratio is often more valuable than marginal gains in runtime¬†[26]. Therefore, the modest increase in SZ2‚Äôs runtime is a justifiable tradeoff for its superior compression ratio.

We analyze the critical reasons as follows. Note that the FL model parameters are always flattened to 1D data arrays with spiky data changes (as demonstrated in Section 2), so we have to perform 1D compression by these lossy compressors. ZFP was optimized particularly for multi-dimensional datasets but may perform poorly on 1D spiky datasets, as disclosed by prior literature¬†[27, 28, 29]. Thus, it suffers relatively low compression ratios on FL model parameters. SZx¬†[14] adopts relatively simple compression operations (such as marking constant blocks and using bit-wise operations) as it was designed primarily to get high compression/decompression speed, which, however, may cause low compression ratios and reconstructed data qualities inevitably. SZ2 and SZ3 should exhibit similar compression ratios because they both gracefully degrade to using Lorenzo+quantization essentially when the data exhibit significant variations, according to their hybrid compression design¬†[13, 8]. In comparison, SZ3 features a more sophisticated predictor selection policy, leading to lower compression throughput. All in all, SZ2 exhibits the best tradeoff (compression speed, ratio and quality), which has been verified by our characterization (as shown in Table V).

Furthermore, our selection of SZ2 as the preferred compressor aligns coherently with the design criteria and feasibility region outlined in formulation (2). Here, we are guided by the dual objectives of achieving a low runtime and a high compression ratio, all within a predefined feasibility region. The empirical data solidifies SZ2 as the most suitable lossy compressor for FedSZ, effectively balancing performance metrics with the practical constraints of an FL environment.

V-E Exploring Bestfit Lossless Compression for Metadata and Non-weight Parameters

In the FedSZ algorithm, we use lossless compressors to compress our models‚Äô metadata and non-weight parameters (‚àº0.5‚ÄãMBsimilar-toabsent0.5MB\sim 0.5$\mathrm{M}\mathrm{B}$). Similar to the lossy case, we evaluate several state-of-the-art lossless compressors according to our compressor score. The considered compressors include blosc-lz (v1.21.3)¬†[30], zlib (v1.2.13)¬†[31], zstd (v1.5.5)¬†[32], gzip (Python v3.11.4)¬†[33], and xz (v0.5.0)¬†[34]. Since lossless compressors do not introduce errors, we do not need to observe any impacts on inference accuracy. VI Methodology for Evaluating FedSZ

With the design of FedSZ documented above, we now detail our evaluation methodology.

VI-A Federated Learning Platform: APPFL with FedAvg

We implement FedSZ in an open-source Python package APPFL¬†[11] for the development and evaluation of privacy-preserving FL (PPFL) algorithms. APPFL offers modular APIs for implementing essential components such as learning algorithms, privacy mechanisms, communication protocols, models, and data handling. The package utilizes gRPC for cross-platform communication and employs Message Passing Interface (MPI) for parallelism, enabling scalable simulations. It is compatible with PyTorch, allowing for the integration of custom neural network models. APPFL has been successfully used to train models across various decentralized datasets, including biomedical images¬†[35] and electric grid¬†[36, 37].

In FL, the package provides the capability to train a global model by aggregating updates from client models operating on decentralized data. Various algorithms can be employed, each with different privacy, efficiency, and robustness trade-offs. For the scope of this study, we focus on Federated Averaging (FedAvg)¬†[2], a well-established algorithm that performs local Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) on client devices and averages these local models to update a global one. The choice of FedAvg is deliberate; its simplicity, scalability, and robust performance make it compatible with compression techniques, thus serving our study‚Äôs objectives effectively. This choice aims to deliver insights that are relevant to our specific study on compression and applicable to the broader field of FL. In summary, the APPFL package and our selection of FedAvg form a methodological foundation that aligns with real-world scenarios, striking a balance between efficiency, privacy, and performance.

VI-B Models and Datasets for Training

The primary aim of FedSZ is to optimize communication efficiency without sacrificing model accuracy or imposing a significant runtime overhead. To rigorously evaluate our framework, we chose DNNs that vary significantly in terms of parameter count, model size, percentage of data that we lossy compress, and computational complexity (FLOPs). The characteristics of these models are summarized in Table III.

Selecting a diverse set of models ensures that our results are not specific to any particular architecture, making the insights gained from this study more broadly applicable. For instance, MobileNet-V2, with its relatively fewer parameters and FLOPs, represents edge cases where the device capabilities might be limited. In contrast, ResNet101 offers a more complex architecture suitable for resource-rich environments, and AlexNet serves as a middle-ground model.

Similarly, we chose three well-established image classification datasets to maintain comparable tasks across different models, as detailed in Table IV.

The choice of CIFAR-10, Fashion-MNIST, and Caltech101 serves multiple purposes. CIFAR-10 and Fashion-MNIST are more straightforward datasets, often used for benchmarking. They allow us to assess how well FedSZ performs under less demanding conditions. Caltech101, with its higher input dimensions and more classes, poses a more challenging problem, testing the robustness and scalability of our framework. These datasets provide a comprehensive test bed for evaluating FedSZ across various scenarios.

VI-C Simulating Network Bandwidth

To evaluate FedSZ‚Äôs performance in varying network conditions, we emulated different bandwidths using MPI for communication between nodes. Although our system inherently has network throughput, we wanted to test how FedSZ would perform under constrained bandwidth scenarios, which are common in real-world FL deployments. After obtaining the native MPI bandwidth, we implemented a bandwidth scaling technique using sleep commands to artificially delay the transmission of messages proportional to our desired bandwidth. By incorporating these controlled sleep delays into the communication protocol, we could simulate the low-bandwidth environments typical of edge devices (e.g., 10Mbps¬†[44]) compared to the data center setting. This methodology allows us to robustly evaluate the performance gains offered by FedSZ‚Äôs compression techniques under various networking conditions.

VII-A Compression Impacts on Inference Accuracy

In our study, we explored the trade-off between compression rate and model accuracy by utilizing FedSZ with SZ2 and blosc-lz compressors across a span of SZ2‚Äôs relative (REL) error bounds from 10‚àí5superscript10510^{-5} to 10‚àí1superscript10110^{-1}. Our findings, illustrated in Figure 5, reveal a clear threshold at an error bound of 10‚àí2superscript10210^{-2} beyond which model accuracy sharply declines due to the magnitude of the error as shown in Figure 3. This decline indicates a boundary where a loss in model utility offsets the benefits of higher compression (and thus reduced communication costs). However, for error bounds less than or equal to 10‚àí2superscript10210^{-2}, we observed that the inference accuracy remained within 0.5% of the uncompressed model‚Äôs performance. At some error bounds, there is a deviation from this 0.5%. However, this is due to the stochastic nature of the training and validation. This is a crucial observation, underscoring that substantial data compression can be achieved without affecting the model‚Äôs efficacy.

Moreover, the compression ratios presented in Table V further corroborate our recommendation of 10‚àí2superscript10210^{-2} as an optimal REL. For instance, at a REL of 10‚àí2superscript10210^{-2}, AlexNet achieved a compression ratio of 12.61√ó\times on CIFAR-10, a significant gain without damaging model accuracy. Similar trends were observed for MobileNetV2 and ResNet50 across all datasets. The consistency in these trends across various model architectures and datasets suggests that a REL of 10‚àí2superscript10210^{-2} could be a generalized optimal setting in a wide range of FL applications.

By integrating this extensive analysis, we establish that the choice of the REL error bound is a critical factor in achieving an effective balance between compression efficiency and model accuracy in the context of FedSZ. Therefore, based on our results, a REL of 10‚àí2superscript10210^{-2} is recommended as it offers a judicious mix of communication efficiency and model performance, making it well-suited for diverse FL environments.

VII-C Scalability of FedSZ

To test whether FedSZ scales effectively with the number of clients or computing nodes. We evaluated the scalability of FedSZ using up to 128 CPU cores, increasing the core count in powers of 2 while simulating a network bandwidth of 10Mbps to accentuate the advantages of compression in FedSZ.

In the context of weak scaling, we kept the number of clients per core constant while increasing the total number of cores. FedSZ demonstrated effective weak scaling, as evidenced by the near-linear increase in communication time as the number of cores and clients increased, shown in Figure¬†8(a). In our evaluation, FedSZ exhibited a recalculated weak scaling speedup ranging from 0.36 at 128 MPI processes to a peak of 1.64 with 8 MPI processes, indicating moderate adaptability to an increasing client count. This result is noteworthy because it indicates that the system can handle many clients without significant performance drops. FedSZ also showed robust, strong scaling characteristics as shown in Figure 8(b). For strong scaling, the framework achieved a recalculated speedup as high as 7.51 at 128 MPI processes, demonstrating effective utilization of additional computational resources for a fixed number of clients.

Simulating a constrained network environment of 10Mbps demonstrated the efficacy of FedSZ‚Äôs compression algorithms. Under these conditions, the framework was able to maintain or even improve the model‚Äôs accuracy while significantly reducing the communication overhead. The advantage of using compression became increasingly evident as the number of cores (and thus, the number of clients) increased. This suggests that FedSZ‚Äôs compression techniques can offer substantial benefits in low-bandwidth scenarios, where communication costs are often bottlenecks.

VII-D Differential Privacy of Lossy Compression

An intriguing aspect of FedSZ‚Äôs lossy compression is the distribution of errors induced by the varying REL error bounds. Our experiments have shown that the error distributions for REL error bounds of 0.5, 0.1, and 0.05 exhibit characteristics similar to a Laplacian distribution, as evidenced by the histograms in Figures 9.

Differential Privacy (DP) often leverages noise addition mechanisms like Laplace noise to ensure that individual data points cannot be easily identified from aggregate statistics. It is worth noting that the resemblance to Laplacian noise does not guarantee DP, as the formal guarantee depends on specific mathematical conditions related to the data sensitivity and the noise scale. However, the observed characteristics in our study make this an avenue worth exploring. Given the critical importance of data privacy in FL, this warrants further investigation and could add another layer of utility to FedSZ‚Äôs already promising performance.

VIII Discussion and Future Work

VIII-A Future Directions

This study presents a robust compression algorithm for reducing the communication overhead of client-server interactions for FL. Future studies we plan beyond the scope of this study are the following: (i) Hyperparameter Optimization In this study, we observed that the relative error bound plays a critical role in balancing compression and accuracy. Future work would delve into how other hyperparameters might be tuned to mitigate the accuracy loss introduced by compression, thereby leading to more optimal model performance. (ii) Differential Privacy A by-product of lossy compression is the introduction of noise into the data. This opens up the possibility that the noise might offer some level of DP, a concept crucial for ensuring data privacy in FL. Future studies could examine the relationship between the noise generated by lossy compression and its impact on DP guarantees. These areas all merit subsequent exploration to increase the merits of including lossy compression in this technology.
