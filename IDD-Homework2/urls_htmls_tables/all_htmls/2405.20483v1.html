Hiding Your Awful Online Choices Made More Efficient and Secure: A New Privacy-Aware Recommender System

Shibam Mukherjee 1122    Roman Walch 1133    Fredrik Meisingseth 11   
Elisabeth Lex 11    Christian Rechberger 11
Abstract

Recommender systems are an integral part of online platforms that recommend new content to users with similar interests. However, they demand a considerable amount of user activity data where, if the data is not adequately protected, constitute a critical threat to the user privacy. Privacy-aware recommender systems enable protection of such sensitive user data while still maintaining a similar recommendation accuracy compared to the traditional non-private recommender systems. However, at present, the current privacy-aware recommender systems suffer from a significant trade-off between privacy and computational efficiency. For instance, it is well known that architectures that rely purely on cryptographic primitives offer the most robust privacy guarantees, however, they suffer from substantial computational and network overhead. Thus, it is crucial to improve this trade-off for better performance. This paper presents a novel privacy-aware recommender system that combines privacy-aware machine learning algorithms for practical scalability and efficiency with cryptographic primitives like Homomorphic Encryption and Multi-Party Computation - without assumptions like trusted-party or secure hardware - for solid privacy guarantees. Experiments on standard benchmark datasets show that our approach results in time and memory gains by three orders of magnitude compared to using cryptographic primitives in a standalone for constructing a privacy-aware recommender system. Furthermore, for the first time our method makes it feasible to compute private recommendations for datasets containing 100 million entries, even on memory-constrained low-power SOC (System on Chip) devices.

1 Introduction

Recommender System (RecSys) have become integral parts of our online world, helping users navigate the vast information in the online space by providing them with the most suitable choices. Currently, all major web-based industries, including online health, finance, or entertainment platforms use RecSys to provide a better user experience. Due to the substantial trade-off between privacy and performance, currently most of the RecSys research primarily focuses on building practical algorithms and relevant datasets for increased accuracy and efficiency, thus guaranteeing privacy mostly during data transmission due to its negligible impact on performance.

In addition to not protecting the data-owner’s data (when outsourced to a cloud), such RecSys also inherently does not protect the user query and response from the cloud. For example, if the cloud knows the final response, it can also manipulate it. In the past several years, many works have suggested creating various Privacy-Aware Recommender System (PrivRecSys) approaches that improve the privacy-performance trade-off by adopting trust-based security models, such as non-colluding parties [EVTL12, WPLX19, ZHT16, WCKM09, SLD22, KKN+18], secure hardware [AAS+20], among others. Unfortunately, such security assumptions do not guarantee user data protection against colluding parties or attacks against secure hardwares [NBB20], thus leaking the user information to the cloud. Related works like [WHL22] take a different approach by using Order Preserving Searchable Encryption (OPSE) with R-trees structure to construct an efficient PrivRecSys without trust assumptions, however, they do not take into account information leakage through access pattern [LCNL22, MT19]. Recent work by Chen et al. SANNS [CCD+20] proposes a PrivRecSys construction by solely relying on privacy-preserving cryptographic primitives with strong security assumptions like use of no trusted-party or secure hardware, in addition to providing resistance against access pattern analysis. However, due to the use of privacy-preserving cryptographic primitives like Homomorphic Encryption (HE) and Multi Party Computation (MPC), their proposal is computationally more expensive compared to the other approaches with weaker security assumptions. Refer to Table 1 for an overview comparing different PrivRecSys approaches from the last years. Notably, even though crucial, in the previous literature, protection towards the data-owner’s data (Recommendation Dataset (RecDS)) when outsourced to a recommendation cloud was found missing. Previous works like SANNS assumed that the data-owner and the recommendation cloud are one single entity which might not always be the case.

1.1 Main Contributions

First Scalable Private Implementation. For the first time, to the best of our knowledge, our work demonstrates a truly scalable PrivRecSys, capable of generating private recommendations from datasets with 100 million entries, pushing the limits of dataset size by an order of magnitude in comparison to the earlier state-of-the-art trust based or trust less approaches.

Improved Time, Memory and Privacy. As a direct consequence of combining privacy-aware Machine Learning (ML) with cryptographic primitives like HE and MPC, our experiments show a time and memory improvement by three orders of magnitude when computing a single recommendation compared to using only cryptographic primitives. Additionally, our new approach enables the data-owner to protect its RecDS containing historic user data (like ratings) and other Personal Identifiable Information (PII) from the cloud. This allows more user privacy, especially to the vulnerable groups.

Portability and Efficiency. For the first time, to the best of our knowledge, we present a PrivRecSys capable of generating private recommendations for a dataset with 100 million entries under 18 seconds on a standard consumer device, suitable for batched private recommendations. Moreover, we also demonstrate the efficiency of our PrivRecSys by hosting it on a low-power SOC device.

Until our work, SANNS [CCD+20] was the only state-of-the-art PrivRecSys requiring no trust assumptions. However, as far as we are aware, there is no public implementation of SANNS at the time of writing this paper. Thus, to further expedite the open-source research in the direction of efficient PrivRecSys implementations, we are making our implementation publicly available.111https://extgit.iaik.tugraz.at/krypto/privacyawarerecsys

1.2 Preliminaries

Homomorphic Encryption. HE [BV11, GSW13] enables a server to privately compute on the client’s encrypted data without having to decrypt it first. HE natively allows only basic arithmetic operations (addition and multiplication), but one can construct and evaluate any other function using them. The native operations of HE are fast, however, when evaluating other functions like comparison, MPC outperforms even the most optimized HE [CGGI20] implementation by several orders of magnitude. More specifically, HE can be used for comparisons only when it is instantiated over Boolean values.

Multi-Party Computation. MPC allows multiple parties to jointly compute a function over their inputs while keeping their inputs private. This work uses Garbled Circuit (GC) [Yao86] as the MPC protocol where the client and the recommendation cloud are the two parties. Secret sharing is another important MPC technique that enables splitting up a secret among multiple parties such that a single secret share reveals nothing about the original secret.

Private Information Retrieval. By observing the read access pattern of a client request, a malicious cloud can infer sensitive information about the client’s encrypted data stored on the cloud[LCNL22, MT19]. Private Information Retrieval (PIR) [CG97, CMO00] protects against such attacks by making read operation indistinguishable from each other, thus hiding the access pattern. This work uses a trivial MPC-based PIR approach discussed later.

2 Related Works

One can divide the problem of constructing a PrivRecSys into two main sub parts, namely, data protection and private computation.

Data Protection. Besides the recommendation algorithm, the RecDS plays a crucial role determining the recommendation quality and thus it is in the best interest of any data-owner to protect it. In previous works like SANNS [CCD+20], the authors assumed that the data-owner and the cloud are one entity, however, in our work, we want to argue that for many data-owners, especially small to medium sized, it is infeasible to develop and deploy in-house PrivRecSys. Thus, a majority of them will rely on third-party clouds, requiring them to forfeit their RecDS to the cloud, potentially containing sensitive user/client information. One trivial approach to hide the RecDS is to store it encrypted on the cloud [WPLX19, KKN+18, SLD22, WCKM09, ZHT16, AAS+20]. However, most of these proposals assume a trust-based model with non-colluding parties or use of secure hardwares like Intel SGX. Interestingly, we observed that one can also partially hide the RecDS information from the cloud by using Collaborative Filtering (CF) [HKR00, MLSK23], which is a nearest neighbor algorithm that leverages similarity metrics like cosine similarity to find the k𝑘kitalic_k most similar neighbors (neighbor-based CF) for a requesting client and recommend the items from these neighbors to the client. This implies that given a RecDS, one can use CF to pre-compute the recommendations for all the possible items present in the RecDS that a client has not seen yet (potential future recommendations) and store them in a smaller and more private reduced-dataset. One can consider the reduced-dataset as a Recommendation Model (RecModel) because whenever a client requests for a recommendation, the cloud uses this to generate recommendations. Additionally, when constructing the RecModel with CF algorithm, one gets the flexibility of choosing similar neighbor such that the vulnerable neighbors (users) are never used to generate recommendations. Such a protection, despite being crucial, was found missing in the previous PrivRecSys literature.

Private Computation. Another major challenge of constructing a cloud-based PrivRecSys is to compute recommendations privately without revealing any intermediate or final results, including results inferred from exploits like access pattern analysis. Approaches like [ZHT16, WPLX19] do not protect against access pattern analysis, whereas [WHL22, KKN+18, SLD22, AAS+20] provide such a protection but only with trust assumptions. Purely relying on cryptographic primitives, SANNS [CCD+20] is the only PrivRecSys protecting against access pattern analysis without a trusted party. However, due to the use of expensive privacy-preserving cryptographic primitives, namely HE and MPC, SANNS comes with a higher computational and communication overhead in comparison to the other trust based approaches.

3 Private Recommender System

We begin by describing the threat model (Section 3.1) of our approach, followed by a high level description (Section 3.2) of our protocol. Finally, a more detailed description of our private recommender system is provided in Section 3.3 Section 3.4.

3.1 Threat Model

Client. Our model protects the client query and the recommendations both from the data-owner and the cloud without any trust assumptions, including protection against access pattern analysis. For generating the RecModel and providing the optional recommendation feedbacks, the client has to forfeit its sensitive historic/feedback data (PII) like personal preference (ratings) and usage patterns to the data-owner.222Section 5 explores possible strategies to minimize the historic preference leakage. Furthermore, when generating the RecModel s, a vulnerable client can request the data-owner for special privacy protection against malicious client and cloud collaboration (discussed next) by reducing or abstaining from the vulnerable client’s RecModel contribution as a neighbour.

Data-Owner. The data-owner is interested in protecting its RecDS and the RecModel s both from the malicious cloud and the clients. First, the full RecDS is never exposed, neither to the cloud nor to the clients, instead secret shared RecModel s are provided to the cloud and their respective clients. If a malicious client and the cloud collaborate to combine their secret shares, they reveal only the RecModel generated for that particular malicious client. This substantially limits the information leakage about the full RecDS and if the RecModel s are generated with care, even with several malicious clients collaborating with the cloud, the full RecDS cannot be leaked.333Alternatively, one can also use matrix factorization to hide the RecModel, however, it requires user interaction and thus was not considered in our use-case. Furthermore, this strategy also protects against malicious clients (without cloud collaboration) relying on query based reconstruction attack, by limiting the client to be able to reconstruct only its own RecModel. In Section 3.3, we discuss some of the strategies to generate RecModel while minimizing RecDS leakage.

Cloud. The cloud is interested to protect its proprietary recommendation algorithms from the data-owner and the clients. Due to use of privacy preserving primitives, similar to SANNS, the cloud’s internal proprietary algorithms are never exposed.

3.2 High Level Protocol

The collaborative filter algorithm (1) (Figure 1) takes the RecDS as an input and generates a RecModel (2) for each requesting client. The RecModel is pre-processed (3) into stash, cluster and group sets (discussed later) which are then secret shared (4) between the client and the cloud. Similar to SANNS, we use privacy-preserving cryptographic primitives like HE (6) and MPC (7) to guarantee data privacy during all the interactions (5,8,10) between the client and the cloud. The client query (5), intermediate results (8) and the final results (10) are homomorphically encrypted by the client with its secret key, allowing the cloud to compute both on the encrypted client query and the intermediate data before sending the final encrypted results back to the client. MPC, more specifically GC, enables the client and the cloud to perform private comparisons required to generate the recommendations. Moreover, all the intermediate results are additively secret shared between the client and the server, guaranteeing privacy through information-theoretic security when the client decrypts its homomorphically encrypted intermediate results. PIR (9) guarantees that a client can privately retrieve any intermediate information from the cloud without revealing its choice, thus protecting from attacks like access pattern analysis. We propose to construct a PIR using MPC-friendly ciphers (low multiplicative complexity) like Kreyvium [CCF+16] and LowMC [ARS+15]. The security guarantees of the PIR comes from the security of cipher and GC in general. Lastly, after the client has received its recommendation, it can send an optional feedback (11) to the data-owner to improve the recommendation over the time.

3.3 Data-Owner Processing

Generating Recommendation Model. (1,2) We use collaborative filtering (CF) [HKR00] to generate the RecModel s, in particular, we employ a recently proposed adaptation of CF called ReuseKNN [MLSK23], which includes a novel neighborhood selection mechanism to identify a smaller set (RecModel) of k𝑘kitalic_k similar neighbors whose ratings can be reused for many different recommendation requests where only this set is exposed or needs to be protected. For n𝑛nitalic_n users (targetuser) present in the RecDS, we generate n𝑛nitalic_n RecModel s using the ReuseKNN algorithm. During the recommendation process, the cloud selects the appropriate RecModel for the requesting client (targetuser) to generate accurate recommendations. When generating the RecModel, there are two main steps in the ReuseKNN algorithm. The first step computes the rating similarity between the targetuser and the neighbors present in the RecDS using some similarity metric like cosine similarity. The second step estimates a neighbor’s reusability score for the targetuser. Please note that Muellner et al. [MLSK23] describe several strategies to estimate a neighbor’s reusability. In our work, we leverage their personalized neighborhood reuse strategy (Gain) that computes, for how many ratings provided by the targetuser, could a neighbor have covered in the previous recommendation queries. Each neighbor is assigned a final score based on the combined weighted similarity and reusability score. For every item in the RecDS that has not been rated by the targetuser we choose the top-k𝑘kitalic_k neighbors who have rated that item and choose their ratings to construct the RecModel. Pre-Processing Recommendation Model. (3) Once the RecModel is generated by the data-owner, it needs to be pre-processed for the cloud to generate efficient client recommendations. However, unlike SANNS, instead of pre-processing the RecDS, we pre-process the RecModel s, making the overall PrivRecSys even faster and more scalable than SANNS. Using the K-Means clustering algorithm, the data-owner clusters the RecModel items into clusters. For fair representation, depending on the cluster size, either the clusters are clustered into groups or the items contained in the clusters are moved to a stash set. After the pre-processing phase, the data-owner gets two data sub-sets (stash and cluster) for each RecModel. We refer the reader to SANNS [CCD+20] for more details regarding the pre-processing phase.

Encrypting Sets. (4) After preprocessing, once the stash and cluster sets are generated, they must be secret shared between the client and the cloud such that the sets are not exposed to either party without an active collaboration. Using a randomly generated client key, the cloud generates a random stream using the stream cipher Kreyvium and sends it to the data-owner. This random stream is then used to mask (encrypt) the pre-processed sets before sending them to the client. The cloud masks the random client key with its primary secret key and sends the masked key to the client.

3.4 Cloud Processing

Anonymous PrivRecSys. (5) Every client has a masked (encrypted) sets which must be unmasked (decrypted) before it can be used as an input to the PrivRecSys algorithm. In order to achieve this the client and the cloud jointly unmask the sets using Kreyvium in GC with their respective secret keys before continuing with the HE KNN computation of the client’s query.

Remark 3.1.

Instead of using KNN, one can choose any other algorithm including neural networks to generate better recommendations. Nonetheless, similar to SANNS, we want to emphasis that KNN is the simplest algorithm that can be efficiently computed with HE and thus any other algorithm will simply demonstrate a different accuracy-performance trade-off. Since we primarily demonstrate the benefits of combing privacy-aware ML with privacy preserving primitives, exhibiting such trade-off for different algorithms is out of scope for this work. Furthermore, we conjecture that irrespective of the algorithm used, the privacy protection should stay the same if the privacy preserving primitives are used correctly.

KNN Comparison. (8) After the distance computation, the client and the cloud combine their secret shared distances using GC and compare them to find the top-k𝑘kitalic_k items with the least distance (most similar to the client query). If the output of the GC is the final result (when using stash set), the client learns the result in plain, otherwise (when using cluster sets), the result is again secret shared between the client and the cloud as an intermediate result.

Private Information Retrieval. (9) When the KNN computation is performed on the cluster sets, the client learns the top-k𝑘kitalic_k clusters. However, knowing the top-k𝑘kitalic_k cluster serves no benefit as the client is interested in the items contained inside top-k𝑘kitalic_k clusters. In order to retrieve those items privately from the cloud without revealing the cluster identity, the client uses PIR. SANNS proposes to use the Functional Secret Sharing (FSS) based Oblivious Read Only Memory (OROM) called Floram [DS17] for PIR. We alternatively opt for a trivial approach by constructing a PIR with MPC. After privately retrieving the items contained in the top-k𝑘kitalic_k cluster, the client and the cloud perform the same computation as they did to find the stash top-k𝑘kitalic_k items. For more details on the cloud processing, we refer reader to SANNS [CCD+20].

Improving PrivRecSys. (11) Improving the recommendation quality over time is key to any successful RecSys. In our approach, after a client has viewed its recommendation, it can send a feedback to the data-owner who updates the RecDS and the RecModel for that client (targetuser) and sends the new secret shared pre-processed sets to the cloud.

4 Experiments

Environment. We used two different environments to host our PrivRecSys cloud. For the standard CPU benchmarking, we use Intel i5-8250U, 24 GB of RAM running Ubuntu 20.04.3. The System on Chips (SOC) benchmarking uses Intel Celeron N4100 with 4 GB of RAM running Ubuntu 20.04.3. We perform the network benchmarks on LAN settings and provide traffic analysis for the same to estimate on other network conditions.

Datasets. We use two standard benchmarking datasets, namely, the MovieLens555https://grouplens.org/datasets/movielens (100k, 1M, 10M and 20M) where we have up to 20 million ratings with 27278 movies rated by 138493 users and the Netflix666https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/netflix-inc/netflix-prize-data 100M dataset containing 100 million ratings with 17770 movies rated by 480189 users. Each dataset contains userid, movieid and ratings attributes where the ratings range between 1 and 5 with an interval of 0.5 and 1 for the MovieLens and Netflix dataset respectively.

Accuracy. We achieve a 9-out-of-10 correct KNN recommendation accuracy777The accuracy of a private algorithm should match to that of a plain non-private algorithm as similar computations are performed in both approaches without loss in accuracy. We emphasize that the slight drop in accuracy, discussed above, is due to the stash and cluster optimization, similar to SANNS, leading to a runtime/memory-accuracy trade-off., similar to SANNS, for all the datasets. In comparison to the prior time-memory-accuracy trade-offs discussed in SANNS, ReuseKNN provides a broader and more flexible trade-off between time-memory-accuracy and privacy. For example, when decreasing the k𝑘kitalic_k-nearest neighbour size for each item from 3 to 2, the RecModel size reduces by 33%. Due to the linear time and memory complexity of our PrivRecSys with respect to the RecModel size, we witness a similar decrease both in time and memory requirement, however, the accuracy drops to 7-out-of-10 as a trade-off.

Time-Memory-Privacy Improvement. Here we present the performance comparison when our PrivRecSys runs with/without the proposed optimization. For MovieLens 100k and 1 million datasets, we improve by an order of magnitude (except memory for 100K), whereas, for 10M and 20M datasets, it improves by two orders of magnitude for both time and memory. Lastly, when using the ReuseKNN optimization for the Netflix 100M dataset, we witness three orders of magnitude improvement compared to not using the ReuseKNN optimization. Refer to Table 2 for more details. The ReuseKNN optimization enables the data-owner to reduce the average user data exposure by up to 93% as a part of the privacy-accuracy trade-off. We measure this amount by calculating the average difference in the number of items rated by a neighbour used (exposed contribution) for generating targetuser recommendations. By tweaking the neighbour contribution when generating the RecModel s, our approach enables us to protect the special vulnerable users.

Standard and SOC Benchmark. Using our SOC device as a cloud, processing a single PrivRecSys query with the 10M MovieLens dataset takes under a minute. Similarly, for the 100M Netflix dataset, a private recommendation is generated in around 100 seconds, further demonstrating the efficiency of our proposal even on power constrained device. Remark 4.1.

At the time of writing this paper, the implementation of SANNS was not publicly available, due to which, any direct comparison between SANNS and our work is not possible. Moreover, due to our work solely relying on privacy preserving cryptographic primitives when generating recommendations, we limit ourselves from comparing with other proposals relying on non-rigorous privacy techniques like trusted party, secure hardware, among others, as they naturally enjoy a better efficiency when considering a much weaker privacy assumption.

5 Further Discussion

Our approach assumes that the data-owner owns the RecDS and wants to protect it. However, protecting the privacy of the users who are providing their data to the data-owner remains an open problem, especially, when the data-owner cannot be trusted. Several works [ZC15, MDC16, CWZ+21] have discussed numerous techniques to privately perform CF, however, all of them rely on trusted-party or active user communication, in addition to having scalability issues, leaking recommendation models, among others. Trivially, one can also use MPC as a solution but we conjecture that such an approach is not scalable due to the involved computation and communication overhead. Another standard technique is data perturbation [PD03], where user data is protected by adding some structured noise. The unrated value 2222 is placed in the middle to reduce the overall impact on the accuracy when perturbing the ratings. For perturbation, the noise was drawn from a zero-centered (μ=0𝜇0\mu=0italic_μ = 0) Laplace distribution Lap(μ,b𝜇𝑏\mu,bitalic_μ , italic_b) (rounded into the rating domain), where b=0.7𝑏0.7b=0.7italic_b = 0.7 was found to be the most suitable in our use-case.888We naively selected a small b𝑏bitalic_b and raised it until the accuracy suffered. Unfortunately, the above privacy guarantees are based on heuristics and thus a shift towards a more rigorous privacy guarantee like Differential Privacy (DP) [DMNS06], especially the notion of Local Differential Privacy (LDP) [CJK+18], actively used in the industry [BEM+17, DKY17, SJ16, app17] and research [BBH+22, CWZ+21] would be desirable. We leave the incorporation of DP for future work.

6 Conclusion

Using cryptographic primitives for constructing PrivRecSys was so far discouraging due to the high cost and little flexibility regarding time-memory-accuracy-privacy trade-offs. With this work, we proposed a significantly more scalable and efficient PrivRecSys by combining cryptographic primitives with machine learning algorithms for the first time. We also demonstrated that adopting our new approach provides improved privacy, especially, for vulnerable users who may require additional protection. Moreover, our proposed model allows us to describe a more realistic scenario where not only the client query and recommendation is protected from the cloud but also the proprietary RecDS of the data-owner is protected both from the client and the cloud. Finally, we release our solution as open-source with the intention of faster adoption and integration of PrivRecSys into the existing non-private recommendation system infrastructure for more secure and private access to news, social media, academic papers, or entertainment artifacts.
