Towards more sustainable enterprise data and application management with cross silo Federated Learning and Analytics

Hongliu CAO Corresponding author, email: caohongliu@gmail.com
Abstract

To comply with new legal requirements and policies committed to privacy protection, more and more companies start to deploy cross-silo Federated Learning at global scale, where several clients/silos collaboratively train a global model under the coordination of a central server. Instead of data sharing and transmission, clients train models using their private local data and exchange model updates. However, there is little understanding of the carbon emission impact of cross silo Federated Learning due to the lack of related works. In this study, we first analyze the sustainability aspect of cross-silo Federated Learning, across the AI product life cycle instead of focusing only on the model training, with the comparison to the centralized method. A more holistic quantitative cost and CO2 emission estimation method for real world cross-silo Federated Learning setting is proposed. Secondly, we propose a novel data and application management system using cross silo Federated Learning and analytics to make IT companies more sustainable and cost effective.

Keywords:
Sustainable AI, Carbon emission estimation, Cross silo Federated Learning, Cross silo Federated Analytics

1 Introduction

The Information and Communication Technology (ICT) sector has seen massive and accelerating growth in the last decades, and it is now responsible for a significant portion of the global CO2 emissions as shown in Figure 1 [1]. According to recent studies, the ICT sector’s carbon footprint has grown quickly from around 1.5% to around 3.5% of total CO2 emissions. This growth is expected to be even faster in the future as more and more people rely on technology and Artifical Intenlligence (AI) solutions for their daily lives [1, 2]. Hence, there is an increasing need to have solutions in making ICT sector more sustainable.

Nowadays, compute, data storage and network are three main compositions of ICT companies’ CO2 emission and cost. It’s been discovered by [3] that over half of the information stored by businesses has no value to daily operations. Additionally, over a third of data is obsolete or redundant, and therefore is known to be completely unusable [3]. With the surging popularity of Large Language Models such as ChatGPT, more and more people are becoming aware of AI, and many companies are investing more in AI research and development. However, in the race to develop AI products, a lot of companies are ignoring the sustainability aspect. Training GPT-3 with 175 billion parameters consumed 1287 MWh of electricity, and resulted in carbon emissions of around 500 metric tons of CO2 [4, 5]. However, data and model training are just two example parts of the AI product life cycle, there are also a lot of CO2 emitted by data management, model inference, model management, etc.

Federated Learning is a distributed learning framework known for its privacy-preserving ability, where several clients collaboratively train a global model under the coordination of a central server. Instead of data transmission, clients train models using their private local data and upload model updates. When participated clients are small, distributed entities with relatively small amount of local data, it is called cross-device Federated Learning. In cross-silo Federated Learning, the clients are companies or organizations: small number of participants each with large amount of local data [6]. In the state-of-the-art studies, most of them focus on Federated Learning combating Non-IID data and trustworthy Federated Learning. A few existing solutions studied the energy efficient training strategies for Federated Learning [7, 8, 9]. However, they mainly focus on reducing the energy cost of model training only for cross-device Federated Learning and lack of sustainability comparison with the centralized solution.

Cross-silo Federated Learning is now starting to be deployed at a global scale by more companies as businesses need to comply with new legal requirements and policies committed to privacy protection [9]. However, the worrying situation is that we currently have little to no understanding of its impact on carbon emissions. In this work, we focus on how companies can reduce their carbon footprint by leveraging cross-silo Federated Learning, where few existing solution is found in the literature. The main contributions of this work include:

  1. 1.

    A novel framework for the analysis of energy and carbon footprints in Cross-silo FL is proposed: studying the sustainability aspect of cross-silo Federated Learning, across the AI product life cycle instead of focusing only on the model training in the literature, with the comparison to the centralized method.

  2. 2.

    Analytical Carbon Footprint analysis for cross-silo Federated Learning: We provide a more holistic quantitative cost and CO2 emission estimation method for cross-silo Federated Learning, including data storage, data transmission, model training, communication, etc.

  3. 3.

    Real-world experiments: cross-silo Federated Learning cost and carbon footprint analysis with multiple real-world industrial scenarios on real hardware, network and security settings of Federated Learning (instead of in a simulated environment) on the Cloud are reported and analyzed

  4. 4.

    A new data and application management system based on Cross-silo Federated Learning and Federated Analytics is proposed to reduce the data redundancy, model redundancy, application redundancy from the company while enhancing data privacy.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: the background and related works are introduced in Section II. In Section III, the novel framework for the analysis of energy and carbon footprints in Cross-silo FL is introduced. We describe the experiments comparing the CO2 emission and cost of cross silo Federated Learning with Centralized Learning in Section IV and the proposed sustainable data and application management system in V. Finally, the conclusion and future works are given in Section VI.

2 Related Works

Despite fast development and impressive results of deep learning methods, the privacy and environmental concerns induced by the training and inference of deep learning models have also been raised in recent years. In response, alternatives to Centralized Learning such as Federated Learning and Federated Analytics have emerged. Federated Learning enables collaborative learning in a distributed way under the coordination of a central server. The term federated analytics was first coined by Google in 2020 to represent “collaborative data science without data collection,  re-using the Federated Learning infrastructure but without the learning part” [10, 11]. Both Federated Learning and Federated Analytics enhance the data privacy by avoiding data collection (putting datasets together) for learning/analysis. While most studies focus on the privacy perspective, Federated Learning and Federated Analytics are also potentially more sustainable than Centralized Learning by avoiding data transfer in the big data era as shown in Figure 2.

Few state of the art work put the focus on the sustainability perspective of Federated Learning. Recently, Savazzi et al [12] initialized an energy and carbon footprint analysis of distributed and Federated Learning. They quantify both the energy footprints and the carbon equivalent emissions for vanilla FL methods and consensus-based fully decentralized approaches. Similarly in the work of [9], the authors offered the first-ever systematic study of the carbon footprint of Federated Learning with a rigorous model to quantify the carbon footprint, facilitating the investigation of the relationship between FL design and carbon emissions. However, there are several common limitations of the existing solutions:

  • •

    Most existing state of the art studies only focus on the sustainability study on cross-device Federated Learning, ignoring the cross silo Federated Learning.

  • •

    Existing solutions focus only on the training energy cost comparison instead of the entire AI product life cycle.

  • •

    Most studies are based on simulated Federated Learning environment instead of on real world settings.

In this work, we propose solutions to deal with all these limitations to provide a more holistic analysis of the carbon footprint of cross-silo Federated Learning in the real world environment.

3 Quantifying CO2 estimations

3.1 The real world Federated Learning environment

In this work, the real world setting of cross silo Federated Learning environment is constructed on Azure Cloud with the collaboration with Microsoft team as shown in Figure 3. The Microsoft team has released and improved the usage of the Federated Learning Cloud setting with multiple scenarios (the code can be found here: https://github.com/Azure-Samples/azure-ml-federated-learning). From Figure 3, it can be seen that each data silo is accessed only by the corresponding compute allocated to this silo for the local training while the Orchestrator has no access to data silos. The role of the Orchestrator (the central server) is to aggregate the weights of local models and update the global model following a strategy such as FedAVG [13, 14]. There is one data storage which is shared by the Orchestrator and local computes to exchange the model weights, where each local compute save the weights to this data storage after local training and the Orchestrator aggregate the local models’ weights and save the global model weights also to this storage.

3.2 CO2 estimation on the Cloud

As the real world setting of cross silo Federated Learning environment is on the Cloud, we focus mainly on measuring the CO2 estimation for Cloud providers in this work. The Software Carbon Intensity (SCI), as defined by the Green Software Foundation, is a rate of carbon emissions per one functional unit or R [15]. The SCI value measurement equation of a software system is:

S​C​I=((E×I)+M)​ per R𝑆𝐶𝐼𝐸𝐼𝑀 per RSCI=((E\times I)+M)\text{ per R} (1)

where E is the energy consumption (kilowatt hours) for different components of the software boundary over a given time period, including CPU/GPUs at different percentages of utilisation, data storage, memory allocation, data transferred over a network, etc. I is measured by grams of carbon dioxide equivalent per kilowatt-hour of electricity (gCO2eq/kWh), which represents the Location-based marginal carbon emissions for the grid that powers the datacenter. M is embodied carbon emission related to the creation, usage, and disposal of a hardware device; R is the functional unit [16].

In the AI project life cycle for a company, the first step is to identify the relevant datasets for the given task once the problem is defined. In Centralized Learning setting, the following step is to request access to the datasets and transfer them to the team’s own storage account, while there is no extra data copy, transfer and storage in the Federated Learning setting. The second step is data processing, model training and testing where the main CO2 emission comes from the compute instances or clusters. In this step, cross-silo Federated Learning as distributed computing method usually has more compute instances/clusters than Centralized Learning. The final step is the model deployment and monitoring, which is the same for both Centralized Learning and Federated Learning.

There are several existing framework to measure the CO2 emission of the data centers such as Etsy’s Cloud Jewels model (cloud energy conversion factors) [17]. Cloud Carbon Footprint (CCF) approach builds upon Etsy’s Cloud Jewels model for estimating CO2e emissions for cloud compute and storage services, with the addition of networking and memory usage. Based on the CCF framework:

  • •

    Storage: 0.65 Watt-Hours per Terabyte-Hour for HDD. 1.2 Watt-Hours per Terabyte-Hour for SSD.

  • •

    Networking: CCF takes into account only the data exchanged between different geographical data centers and use the smallest coefficient available to date: 0.001 kWh/Gb for data transfer. However, based on [18, 19] the average electricity intensity of transmitting data through the Internet (measured as kilowatt-hours per gigabyte [kWh/GB] is: 0.06.

  • •

    Memory: CCF takes the average of both different estimations and uses 0.000392 Kilowatt Hour / Gigabyte Hour for memory energy consumption.

  • •

    Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE): PUE is a score of how energy efficient a data center is, which needs to be multiplied with estimated kilowatt hours for compute, storage and networking. Based on CCF, the PUEs for different Cloud providers are: AWS: 1.135, GCP: 1.1, Azure: 1.185

  • •

    Carbon Intensity (CI): Convert estimated kilowatt hours for usage of a given cloud provider to estimated CO2e based on the cloud provider data center region that each service is running in. Google has published the grid carbon intensity for their GCP regions. For AWS and Azure, CCF generally uses carbonfootprint.com’s country specific grid emissions factors report [20]. For most of Europe, EEA emissions factors are used [21].

To be noted that most cloud provider storage, database services and node pools automatically replicate your data as well as any associated compute and memory resources to achieve adequate durability and availability for data stores and to ensure better redundancy in the case of service outages. For example, Azure Storage always stores multiple copies of your data so that it’s protected from planned and unplanned events, including transient hardware failures, network or power outages, and massive natural disasters 111https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/storage/common/storage-redundancy. Locally redundant storage (LRS) copies your data synchronously three times within a single physical location in the primary region. Zone-redundant storage (ZRS) copies your data synchronously across three Azure availability zones in the primary region. Data redundancy needs to be considered when measuring the CO2 emission for data transfer and data storage.

In terms of the energy consumption by computing such as CPUs or GPUs, we follow the example provided by Microsoft in [22] using the thermal design power (TDP) of the hardware components to estimate the power consumption as shown in Equation 2:

4 Experiments

The main focus of this work is to study the CO2 emission of cross silo Federated Learning compared to Centralized Learning. As both learning methods have similar embodied emission, similar CO2 emission for deployment and monitoring, we concentrate on the CO2 emission comparison in terms of data storage, data transfer and model training.

4.1 Datasets and experimental protocols

In terms of datasets and experimental protocols, we follow the benchmarking study from Microsoft using the same datasets and experimental protocols (as shown in Figure 5) 222https://github.com/Azure-Samples/azure-ml-federated-learning/blob/main/docs/concepts/benchmarking.md. In this benchmark, they studied the training overhead, model performance and training scalability of the real world cross silo Federated Learning setting on Azure Cloud. Their key findings of the benchmark indicate that model performance is boosted with cross silo Federated Learning comparing to a single model with partial data. They also show that the model performance with cross silo Federated Learning is comparable to Centralized Learning (a single model trained on all data). In this work, we reuse their data, model and experimental protocol, with the focus on comparing the CO2 emission and cost between cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning.

4.2 Experimental results

The Microsoft benchmark showed that the model performance with cross silo Federated Learning is comparable to Centralized Learning (a single model trained on all data). In this work, we put focus on comparing the CO2 emission and cost between cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning reusing their data and experimental protocol. To test the scalability of our implementation, we artificially replicated the datasets the same way as in the Microsoft benchmark. We compare cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning using 3 scales of datasets: small size dataset with around 1.2 GB data; medium size dataset with around 12 GB data; large size dataset with around 120 GB data. These dataset sizes should cover most classic Machine Learning use cases in companies.

Firstly, the comparison between cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning in terms of model training including the CPU/GPU usage, memory usage, disk usage and extra communication (for Federated Learning only) is studied. From the results in Figure 6, it can be seen that cross silo Federated Learning experiments have much less wall clock time across all dataset sizes due to the distributed training. For both small and medium sized datasets, cross silo Federated Learning has slightly more CO2 emission and cost than Centralized Learning because it has extra communication rounds. However, for large datasize, Centralized Learning has much more CO2 emission and cost than cross silo Federated Learning because of larger compute clusters allocated (due to too large training datasize) 333 To be noted that simple compute cluster selection method is used based on the datasize, which can be further improved.

Secondly, the comparison between cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning in terms of model training, data storage (extra cost of Centralized Learning) and data transfer (extra cost of Centralized Learning) altogether is shown in Figure 7. The blue color represents the CO2/cost of model training while the orange color represents the CO2/cost of data storage and transfer. It can be told that the results are different from the ones using model training only (which is the case on the literature of cross device Federated Learning). When combing model training with data transfer and storage, Centralized Learning has more CO2 emission and much more cost than cross silo Federated Learning across all training dataset sizes.

In summary, the model training energy and cost between cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning is similar. But the extra data transfer and data storage caused by Centralized Learning can lead to a lot of unnecessary CO2 emissions, which is often ignored in the state-of-the-art analysis.

5 Proposed Data and application management system

From the experiments and analysis in the previous section, we can see the advantages of using cross silo Federated Learning. Hence, we discuss the possibility of leveraging Cross-Silo Federated Learning and Federated Analytics to reduce data redundancy, model redundancy, and application redundancy for companies while enhancing data privacy in this section, so that businesses can streamline their operations and make their solutions more cost effective and environmentally friendly.

The proposed data and application management system is shown in Figure 8. Data are important assets for technology companies nowdays. Assume a company has various of data silos owned by different business units across different regions. For each application or product, to make enterprise data and app management more sustainable, instead of requesting the data copy, the application/product owner can:

  1. 1.

    Frame the use case in details in text description and send the request to data owners

  2. 2.

    Data owners analyze the request and compare with historical requests (e.g. using Large Language Models): If the application/product is new: approve the request and assign an appropriate compute cluster (so that we can reduce the waste of choosing wrong compute clusters) to the application/product owner with read access to the requested data. If the application/product exist already, share the contact of duplicated application/product owners to avoid application/product redundancy.

  3. 3.

    The application/product owner uses cross silo Federated Learning or Federated Analytics for the development.

6 Conclusion

This work develops a novel framework for the analysis of energy and carbon footprints in cross silo Federated Learning across the AI product life cycle instead of focusing only on the model training in the literature. This is the first study from Company data and product management perspective, with the comparison to the centralized method, with experiments on real Federated Learning setting on Azure Cloud, with the objective to help companies to be more cost effective and more environmentally friendly. The main conclusions from the experiments are: the model training energy and cost between cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning is similar. But the extra data transfer and data storage caused by Centralized Learning can lead to a lot of unnecessary CO2 emissions, which is often ignored in the state-of-the-art analysis. Based on the experimental analysis, we also propose a cross-silo Federated Learning/Analytics based framework for companies to reduce data redundancy, model redundancy, and application redundancy.

There are several limitations of this study. Firstly, the performance comparison between cross silo Federated Learning and Centralized Learning is not holistic. In the Microsoft benchmark, datasets used are Independent and Identically Distributed (IID), which are not naturally cross silo datasets. Some real world cross silo datasets may be non-IID, which can make the Federated Learning training longer with worse performance. There is few state of the art solutions trying to deal with non-IID silos efficiently. Secondly, this work focuses on horizontal Federated Learning instead of vertical Federated Learning. However, the experimental results of vertical Federated Learning should be similar while some studies showing that vertical Federated Learning can perform better than Centralized Learning [23, 24, 25, 26]. Apart from Federated Learning, there are also other ways to learn a model collaboratively without data sharing which can perform better than Centralized Learning [27, 28]. Thirdly, the real world experiments do not include Federated Analytics (collaborative data science without model learning) use cases, which should be included in future works.
