Augmenting Team Diversity and Performance by Enabling Agency and Fairness Criteria in Recommendation Algorithms

Diego Gomez-Zara dgomezara@nd.edu 0000-0002-4609-6293 University of Notre DameNotre DameINUSA ,  Victoria Kam Northwestern UniversityEvanstonILUSA ,  Charles Chiang University of Notre DameNotre DameINUSA ,  Leslie DeChurch Northwestern UniversityEvanstonILUSA  and  Noshir Contractor Northwestern UniversityEvanstonILUSA
(2018)
Abstract.

In this study, we examined the impact of recommendation systems’ algorithms on individuals’ collaborator choices when forming teams. Different algorithmic designs can lead individuals to select one collaborator over another, thereby shaping their teams’ composition, dynamics, and performance. To test this hypothesis, we conducted a 2×2222\times 22 × 2 between-subject laboratory experiment with 332 participants who assembled teams using a recommendation system. We tested four algorithms that controlled the participants’ agency to choose collaborators and the inclusion of fairness criteria. Our results show that participants assigned by an algorithm to work in highly diverse teams struggled to work with different and unfamiliar individuals, while participants enabled by an algorithm to choose collaborators without fairness criteria formed homogenous teams without the necessary skills. In contrast, combining users’ agency and fairness criteria in an algorithm enhanced teams’ performance and composition. This study breaks new ground by providing insights into how algorithms can augment team formation.

1. Introduction

In the modern workplace, algorithms are increasingly reshaping how workers connect, interact, and collaborate with others (Polzer, 2022; Kellogg et al., 2020; Laapotti and Raappana, 2022; Jain et al., 2021; Faraj et al., 2018). These computational artifacts can prompt workers to find new collaborators, consider different individuals, or prioritize existing relationships (De-Arteaga et al., 2019; Gómez-Zará et al., 2020; Helberger et al., 2018). Algorithm interventions, also referred to as “algorithmic nudges” (Kellogg et al., 2020; Raveendhran and Fast, 2021), have profound repercussions on team composition, collective decision-making, and organizational values. While HCI researchers have extensively studied and built socio-technical systems that form specific kinds of teams (Valentine et al., 2017; Salehi et al., 2017; Hastings et al., 2018; Salehi and Bernstein, 2018), the impact of algorithms on the emergence, organization, dynamics, and outcomes of workers’ collaborations remains not fully explored (Danaher et al., 2017).

Recent studies have highlighted concerning trends in algorithmic interventions, such as the exacerbation of biases, reduced visibility for disadvantaged groups, and the lack of contestability and users’ control (Glaser et al., 2021; De-Arteaga et al., 2019; Obermeyer et al., 2019). Different algorithmic designs have been tested to understand the consequences of using algorithms to assemble teams (Gómez-Zará et al., 2020; Barnabò et al., 2019). On the one hand, algorithms can assign different members to work together based on organizational criteria, such as maximizing teams’ competence or diversity. However, under these circumstances, team members are less likely to engage with their team and be motivated to work with teammates who differ in demographic or functional attributes (Dobbin and Kalev, 2016). Most people are reluctant to be assigned to teams because they may feel threatened by unfamiliar people or people from other groups (Kaiser et al., 2013; Dover et al., 2016), avoid interacting with others with different opinions or qualities (Liao and Fu, 2013), and rebel against rules threatening their autonomy (Dobbin and Kalev, 2016). On the other hand, algorithms can allow individuals to choose their collaborators, increasing their commitment and satisfaction with the assembled team (Chapman et al., 2006; Jahanbakhsh et al., 2017). However, research shows that people are likely to choose others they already know, share similar characteristics, or are familiar with (Gómez-Zará et al., 2019; Piezunka and Dahlander, 2015; Hinds et al., 2000), consequently making biased team selection decisions. Assessing potential collaborators is difficult since skills, points of view, or knowledge characteristics are intangible, leading people to judge others based on first impressions, stereotypes, social networks, and demographics (Contractor, 2013).

To address these concerns, advancements in machine learning have enabled socio-technical systems to augment individuals’ decisions for altruistic and beneficial goals (Heer, 2019; Zheng et al., 2017). Leveraging users’ agency with artificial intelligence can help users engage with their choices and find more appropriate solutions (Kamar et al., 2012). For example, experiments conducted at several Fortune 100 companies showed that Microsoft Copilot (an assistant AI tool) increased 26% the number of completed tasks among developers and helped less experienced developers to learn faster (Cui et al., 2024). Moreover, augmenting users’ decisions with algorithms brings an opportunity to incorporate fairness criteria in individuals’ decisions and mitigate bias and systemic inequalities (Kusner and Loftus, 2020; Wang et al., 2023). In another example, LinkedIn researchers implemented a fair re-ranking approach to ensure a balanced gender representation in candidate searches, resulting in a threefold increase in the number of recommended candidates from the underrepresented gender (Geyik et al., 2019). As these new combinations between human decisions and artificial intelligence continue growing, we ask: Can algorithmic interventions augment users’ collaborator choices while mitigating discrimination biases? We seek to further explore the effects of these advancements by examining how different algorithmic designs affect group organization and emerging collaborations (Polzer, 2022).

To address this research question, we conducted a 2×2222\times 22 × 2 between-subject laboratory experiment examining the impact of recommendation systems’ algorithms on individuals’ teammate choices when choosing collaborators and assembling teams. We posit that enabling both agency and fairness criteria in these algorithms can help individuals assemble diverse teams more effectively. Our study involved over 320 participants who used a recommendation system designed for this study. We tested four algorithms that controlled participants’ agency to choose collaborators and the inclusion of fairness criteria in those decisions:

  1. (1)

    an algorithm assigning participants to teams randomly (i.e., random teams),

  2. (2)

    an algorithm assigning participants to the most diverse team combinations possible (i.e., algorithmically-diverse teams)

  3. (3)

    an algorithm recommending collaborators based on participants’ teammate preferences without fairness criteria (i.e., self-assembled teams), and

  4. (4)

    an algorithm recommending collaborators based on participants’ teammate preferences while incorporating fairness criteria based on demographic and expertise attributes (i.e., fairness-aware teams).

Participants were randomly assigned to one of these experimental conditions and assembled teams of size four to work on a creativity task requiring them to ideate, generate, discuss, and implement ideas. We analyzed how these algorithms influenced the final team composition, dynamics, and performance through a combination of participants’ survey responses, trace data, teams’ task results, and conversation transcripts.

Our findings show that enabling fairness criteria or agency separately did not help participants succeed in this task. While the participants in the algorithmically-diverse teams had low performance since they struggled to work with different and unfamiliar teammates, participants in the self-assembled teams performed poorly since they formed homogenous teams without the necessary skills to solve the task. In contrast, the algorithm that combined agency and fairness criteria allowed participants to assemble diverse teams, enabled them to perform the best and reduced their perceived demographic differences. Although these teams were not the most diverse, allowing participants to choose from a recommended list of diverse collaborators helped them assemble teams with the necessary diversity to establish rapport and effective communication, which ultimately contributed to their success in the task. These findings suggest a “Goldilocks” effect of these algorithms on participants’ collaborations: forcing too much diversity negatively affected teams’ functioning, whereas allowing them to choose without encouraging diversity did not enable teams to thrive.

Our contributions are twofold. First, this study extends insights into algorithmic management in organizations by examining the causal mechanisms that enable more efficient team formation under different algorithmic interventions. Second, we present a comprehensive laboratory experiment that investigates the effects of these algorithmic interventions, from the team formation stages until the task conclusion. We also provide a publicly available, de-identified dataset at to facilitate reproducibility and further analysis. Our research has significant implications for the design of socio-technical systems that can foster diverse and effective collaborations, taking into account users’ agency and artificial intelligence capabilities to unite individuals from different backgrounds and identities.

2. Literature Review

2.1. Algorithms in Organizations

Among multiple definitions in the literature, Kellogg et al. (Kellogg et al., 2020) defined algorithms as “computer-programmed procedures to transform input data into a desired output,” encompassing their materiality, functions, and purpose. Algorithms are intricately connected with their creators’ values and ethics, the completeness and representativeness of the data, stakeholders’ rights and outcomes, and embedded goals (Kitchin, 2017; Martin, 2019). Previous research has highlighted algorithms as computational tools that improve individuals’ decision-making processes (Shim et al., 2002; Bonczek et al., 1979; Dawes, 1979). Workers can use algorithms to complement and augment their decisions, evaluating which outcomes and provided information should be considered to make decisions (Allen and Choudhury, 2022). For example, Valentine et al. (Valentine et al., 2017) introduced ‘flash organizations’ that facilitated organizing crowd-workers on temporal organizational structures based on their expertise and availability. The ‘DreamTeam‘ (Zhou et al., 2018) system helped team members to iterate their roles, structures, and hierarchies in multiple iterations assisted by multi-armed banded algorithms, which searched for better combinations. Lastly, Dow et al. (Dow et al., 2012) employed a system that shepherded crowd workers using real-time expert feedback, which helped them produce better results.

Furthermore, algorithms can automatize these analytical steps to provide faster, more accurate, and more precise results. Driven by the increasing complexity of work and data, organizations and individuals are required to employ algorithms to agglomerate information and make faster decisions (Huang et al., 2023; von Krogh, 2018). Their inclusion also enables workers to conduct analyses and make conclusions based on empirical data, reducing arbitrary decisions (Dane and Pratt, 2007). From the organizations’ perspective, algorithms allow managers, engineers, and employers to standardize steps (Lillrank, 2003), embed values and norms (Martin, 2019), and provide specific directions that workers must follow (Kellogg et al., 2020; Meijerink and Bondarouk, 2023).

Based on Taylorism approaches, algorithms can increase organizations’ value by directing, controlling, and optimizing workers’ actions (Kittur et al., 2013; Kellogg et al., 2020; Noponen et al., 2023). Some algorithmic interventions in organizational settings include inventory management (Sadeghi et al., 2014), scheduling (Ruiz-Torres et al., 2019), public administration (Meijer et al., 2021), transportation (Munkres, 1957), and job assignments (Vilà and Pereira, 2014; Valentine et al., 2017). Although employers and algorithm designers may have benevolent intentions when creating and implementing algorithms, these technological components can harm workers, acting in opposition to their values or interests. Rather than complementing or augmenting individuals’ decisions, algorithms can be used to substitute their decisions, threatening their autonomy and expertise (Parker and Grote, 2022; Raghu et al., 2019). As such, previous research has documented several issues and concerns regarding algorithmic structures, such as bias (Kordzadeh and Ghasemaghaei, 2022), surveillance (Newlands, 2021; Jarrahi et al., 2021), power imbalance (Stefanija, 2023), aversion (Burton et al., 2020), and lack of transparency (Burrell, 2016; Rahman, 2021).

Much of the empirical research on algorithms in organizational contexts is built on gig work platforms. Their dominance in multiple markets has motivated many scholars to understand how workers perform, manage themselves, and deal with the algorithms behind them (Alkhatib et al., 2017; Caza et al., 2022; Kittur et al., 2013). Based on centralized architectures, algorithms can act as organizational brokers and mediate services, coordination, and interactions between workers and customers (Jarrahi et al., 2020; Vallas and Schor, 2020; Glavin et al., 2021). As these systems exemplify, algorithms can orchestrate workers’ actions to minimize their collective efforts, leading to precarious work and isolation (Muntaner, 2018; Glavin et al., 2021).

Despite extensive research and reviews on algorithms in organizations, much of the existing literature primarily concentrates on the immediate interactions between individuals and algorithms, as well as on how algorithms assign individuals to groups (Polzer, 2022; Gómez-Zará et al., 2020). Yet, how algorithms shape individuals’ interactions and collective decisions has not been fully explored. On the one hand, most systems have mainly focused on architectures centralized by algorithms rather than configurations that enable users to choose their groups. On the other hand, most of the literature addressing how individuals collectively organize by their own originates from social media studies (Milan, 2015; Danaher et al., 2017). These studies wield a strong influence over collective action, polarization, and online collective intelligence, as they primarily investigate how users see and interact with one another due to algorithmic recommendations.

2.2. Human Agency in Algorithmic Settings

The pervasiveness of algorithms has increased critical discussions on how individuals maintain their ability to exercise judgment and make decisions when using socio-technical systems. The rising inclusion of advanced algorithms in processes historically driven by humans (e.g., collaboration, coordination, idea generation, creative work) is challenging human ownership, autonomy, control, and authorship rights (Anderson, 2023; Evans et al., 2023). Individuals’ agency—the extent to which humans are proactively engaged in their own development and can exercise a measure of control over their thoughts, decisions, and actions (Bandura, 1986)—is continuously contested by the design and restrictions imposed by algorithms. Exercising agency has effects on individuals’ perceived self-efficacy, which is the extent to which people believe that they can master a particular skill or complete a specific goal (Bandura, 1997). It not only affects their behavior but also impacts their goals, aspirations, outcome expectations, affection proclivities, and perception of impediments and opportunities (Bandura, 2000). Therefore, providing control and contestability to users is necessary as algorithms can perpetuate bias, errors, and opaque decisions (Lyons et al., 2021).

In HCI studies, providing agency to users encompasses several benefits such as independence, personal and causal involvement, and exercise of their personal choices (Bennett et al., 2023). To enable human agency in algorithmic processes, socio-technical systems can provide a “choice architecture” in which users can explore, review, and choose options (Chen et al., 2013; Jameson et al., 2015). The most exemplary cases are recommendation systems, which are highly employed in search engines, e-commerce, job recruitment, entertainment, and social platforms. Far from being exempt from issues, recommendation systems can induce users’ bias by reinforcing likely options or by the order of the recommendations (De-Arteaga et al., 2019; Geyik et al., 2018).

2.3. Team Diversity

Previous research has increasingly examined the impact of diversity in teams and organizations. Researchers have described team diversity as a “double-edged sword” (Horwitz and Horwitz, 2007), offering mixed, and even contradictory, results on the effects that it could bring (Bell et al., 2011; O’Reilly et al., 1998). On one hand, combining team members with different characteristics and attitudes can provide potential benefits to enhance their processes and deliverables (Mathieu et al., 2017; Williams and O’Reilly, 1998). On the other hand, diversity can cause coordination problems and conflicts in a group due to differences in training and knowledge. It can elicit inter-bias among members (i.e., “us-them” distinction) (Tropp, 2012), leading to a lack of cohesion, communication, and trust (Harrison et al., 1998; Lau and Murnighan, 2005).

To disentangle these diversity effects, Harrison et al. (Harrison et al., 1998) defined diversity based on demographic (i.e., surface-level) and attitudinal (i.e., deep-level) attributes that members provide to their group. At the surface level, previous research shows that demographic diversity—the differences of team members in gender, race, or age—can positively influence team performance. Cultivating demographic diversity in teams can bring different traits, points of view, and experiences (van Knippenberg et al., 2004; Page, 2019). Some examples are gender diversity, which promotes productivity in software development teams (Vasilescu et al., 2015), teams’ collective intelligence (Woolley et al., 2015), and innovation in R&D groups (Diaz-Garcia Cristina et al., 2020).

Previous studies have also shown that teams with high levels of deep-level diversity—that is, team members with different expertise, careers, and backgrounds—can deliver more original and creative outcomes. Promoting deep-level diversity can enhance creativity because it expands the breadth of information, knowledge, ideas, and perspectives within a team (Bell et al., 2011). It also encourages divergent thinking, greater scope of skills, and idea recombination (Uzzi et al., 2013). As a result, teams built on deep-level diversity are more likely to solve complex problems that require creativity and innovation than homogeneous groups (Lungeanu and Contractor, 2014; Page, 2019). Overall, the interplay of surface-level and deep-level diversity plays a role in how team members’ differences leverage their work and performance (van Knippenberg et al., 2004).

Despite the well-reported benefits of diversity in teams, the vast majority of individuals struggle to choose to collaborate with others who are different (Aiello et al., 2012; McPherson et al., 2001). Homophily, the tendency to seek out similar individuals, often leads people to collaborate with those they already know or who share similarities. This natural tendency occurs to reduce the uncertainty of future collaborations and potential conflicts with unknown individuals (Hinds et al., 2000; Twyman et al., 2022). Therefore, allowing individuals to choose their collaborators without any incentives or restrictions will likely end in homogenous teams (Ertug et al., 2022). To address the lack of diversity in teams, organizations and managers opt for recruiting members who can increase units’ demographics and backgrounds. Yet, workers’ attitudes and behaviors toward diversity are hard to change (Chang et al., 2019; Dobbin and Kalev, 2016; Noon, 2018). Most people are reluctant to be assigned to teams with people who are different from them because they may feel threatened by unfamiliar people or by people from other groups (Dover et al., 2016; Kaiser et al., 2013), avoid interacting with others who have different opinions or qualities (Liao and Fu, 2013), and rebel against orders that threaten their autonomy (Dobbin and Kalev, 2016).

Could algorithms encourage individuals to collaborate with others who are different? Twyman et al. (Twyman et al., 2022) conducted a field study in which participants used a team formation recommendation system to search for and invite collaborators. Their findings revealed that candidates without prior collaborations particularly benefited from these systems, as their likelihood of being invited significantly increased when they appeared in searchers’ top-10 recommendations. Similarly, Geyik et al. (Geyik et al., 2019) introduced fairness criteria adjustments to LinkedIn’s recommendation algorithms, enhancing the visibility of job seekers from underrepresented groups. Together, these studies suggest that integrating agency and fairness criteria into team formation algorithms can influence individuals’ collaborator choices, prompting them to select others who enhance the diversity of their teams. Based on this, we propose the following hypotheses:

  • •

    H1: Allowing individuals to choose their collaborators (i.e., agency) without any fairness guidance will negatively influence their team performance.

  • •

    H2: Assigning individuals to work in highly diverse teams will negatively influence their team performance.

  • •

    H3: Allowing individuals to choose their collaborators while incorporating fairness criteria into the recommendation algorithm will positively influence team performance.

3. Methodology

To test our hypotheses, we conducted a 2 (agency: included vs. not included) ×\times× 2 (fairness criteria: included vs. not included) between-subject experiment. We designed a recommendation system and its algorithms for this study. We pre-registered the study before conducting it 111https://osf.io/dgqvw?view_only=e6617fae4e3847c78a4ba5d8cc614ad7. Northwestern University’s Institutional Review Board overview and approved this study (IRB Protocol Number # STU00212865). Data collection occurred between July 2021 and November 2021. 3.1. Participants

We used GPower 3.1 (Faul et al., 2009) to calculate the sample size of our experiment. We selected the F-test (ANOVA fixed-effects, one-way) using a minimum detectable effect size of 0.3, a significance of 95% (α=0.05𝛼0.05\alpha=0.05italic_α = 0.05), and a power of 80%. This calculation provided us with a minimum of 19 teams in each condition. These numbers are similar to the numbers presented in Lykourentzou et al.’s experiment (Lykourentzou et al., 2017), which conducted a three-condition experiment with 16 teams in each condition. In that study, random teams averaged a score of 3.0 in their works, the average groups scored 3.5, and the best groups averaged a score of 4.0.

We recruited participants through Northwestern Kellogg’s Behavioral Research Lab and prescreened them to verify their qualifications for this study. The requirements were being 18 or older, residing in the United States, and being a full-time employee or student. The participant pool included the institution’s students, staff, faculty, and residents of the local area. More than 600 participants answered a battery of demographic, educational, and social network questionnaires. The goal was to conduct experimental sessions with a specific team formation algorithm. To ensure a balanced proportion of demographics in each experimental session, we used stratified sampling to assign participants to each experimental session. Based on the participants’ availability, we scheduled 13 sessions to run these team formation sessions, which ranged from 20 to 50 participants per session.

After the prescreening and session assignment, we invited approximately 500 subjects who qualified to participate in this study via email. The invitation email contained a brief introduction to the study, the payment information, and a confirmation link to the electronic consent form. The final sample included 386 participants who accepted to be part of the study. From this sample, 197 participants were students and 189 were community members (university employees and local residents). Regarding gender, 124 participants reported being male, 252 female, and 10 non-binary. The sample counted 194 White, 123 Asian, 41 African American, and one American Indian participant. Twenty participants identified themselves as multiple races. Regarding their country of origin, 314 identified with the US and 72 with another country. Lastly, 35 participants identified themselves as Hispanic or Latino. We compensated each participant 40 USD; each session took about 120 minutes. We also announced that the top-performing team of this study will receive an additional 50 USD gift card per member. Because of the COVID-19 pandemic restrictions, we conducted these sessions online by hosting meetings on Zoom. These virtual meetings allowed participants to use their voices and see their faces, emulating face-to-face interactions. We did not control participants’ location given the COVID-19 pandemic lockdowns.

3.2. Procedure

For each experimental session, we invited participants to Zoom meetings and asked them to keep their cameras off and microphones muted. Once the session started, we described the purpose of the study and asked participants to create public profiles on the recommender system to assemble their teams. While creating their profiles, participants had 15 minutes to complete a survey that included questions relating to their demographic information (i.e., gender, age, race, nationality, and ethnicity) and six project skills related to the task.

After all participants created their profiles and filled out the initial survey, we introduced them to the tasks they had to accomplish in their teams. Each team had to complete a 20-minute creativity task adapted from Klonek et al. (Klonek et al., 2021), which required them to develop volunteer recruitment materials for a fictitious healthcare organization. The campaign’s purported goal was to recruit volunteer drivers to transport cancer patients from their homes to the hospitals. As specified by McGrath’s circumplex (McGrath, 1984), we chose a creativity task since it provides open-ended exercises without correct answers, benefits from different points of view, does not require specific training, and requires participants to collaborate to provide a deliverable. All participants received background information about the organization and its need for volunteers during the explanation. Based on this task, participants had to answer the following questions in the initial survey using a 5-item Likert scale ranging from “Not at all skilled” to “Extremely skilled”: “Managing campaigns,” “Coordinating people,” “Designing visual graphics, “Recruiting volunteers,” “Writing reports, articles, or papers,” and “Presenting reports and new ideas.” We explained the task after the initial survey to avoid priming the participants.

Based on the 2×2222\times 22 × 2 experimental design, we randomly assign sessions to one of the following experimental conditions:

  • •

    Random teams (i.e. no agency and no fairness criteria included): The team formation recommendation system assigned participants to teams randomly.

  • •

    Algorithmically-diverse teams (i.e., no agency, fairness criteria included): The recommendation system assigned participants to teams using a multi-objective genetic algorithm (Gómez-Zará et al., 2022) that maximized teams’ surface-level diversity (i.e., participants’ age, gender, and race) and deep-level diversity based on the project skills required to solve the experiment’s task. The system notified participants afterward.

  • •

    Self-assembled teams (i.e., agency included, no fairness criteria): The recommendation system allowed participants to search for teammates and choose with whom they wanted to work. Participants could search for others based on skills, characteristics, and social connections. The system provided a ranked list based on the users’ search criteria.

  • •

    Fairness-aware teams (i.e., agency and fairness criteria included): The recommendation system allowed participants to search for teammates and choose with whom they wanted to work. Unlike the third condition, the algorithm re-ranked the teammate recommendations based on the fairness criteria described in the second condition. Teammate candidates who contributed more surface-level and deep-level diversity to the searcher participant’s team were placed in higher positions on the list.

To avoid team differences based on the number of members, we studied teams of the same size. We set four as the required team size to guarantee enough variance in each team’s attributes. Furthermore, participants did not know in which condition they were assigned to avoid introducing bias in the team formation process. Because the number of participants in some sessions was not a multiple of four, some participants had to work alone. For the agency conditions, the recommendation system randomly assigned members without a team to teams that required more members.

Once participants formed their teams, they had three minutes to review their teammates’ profiles on the recommendation system, which included their names and bios. Participants then completed a brief post-assembly survey evaluating the recommendation system. Following that survey, we moved participants into Zoom breakout rooms with their teams for 20 minutes. We also moved solo participants to breakout rooms, so that they could work alone. Participants had to activate their cameras and microphones in their breakrooms. Each breakout room had a researcher team’s connected computer to record the team’s discussion. Each team received a Google Slide deck to create their recruitment materials. Alerts were sent to all breakout rooms five minutes before the end of the task. Team participants were encouraged to discuss and solve the problem as a group, while solo participants worked in silence.

After participants completed their team task, we closed the Zoom breakout rooms and asked them to turn off their cameras and microphones. We sent participants a personal link for a final survey on Qualtrics. This final survey aimed to understand participants’ perceptions of the various team processes, dynamics, and experiences working with their teammates. On average, participants required 20 minutes to complete this final survey. Once the session was finished, we sent participants a brief debrief explaining the experimental conditions, the condition they were assigned, and the design decision. Participants received their compensation after their session was completed. We gave a bonus to the team with the highest performance score after we completed the data collection.

3.3. Details of the recommendation system and its algorithms

In this subsection, we provide details of the recommendation system and algorithms employed in this study. The research team developed this system to facilitate data collection and the proposed experimental conditions. The system was implemented using Django and Python. We created accounts for each one of the participants, and they logged in using their web browsers.

3.3.1. Profiles and the initial survey.

At the beginning of each experimental session, we asked participants to populate a profile on the recommendation system. They completed a survey to provide demographic information, competence, personality traits, leadership, creativity, psychological collectivism, and social skills to feed the information necessary to create the system’s teammate recommendations. We asked them to identify other participants they knew, previously collaborated with, and enjoyed socializing. Participants’ responses were kept confidential; they were, however, used by the recommendation system to provide suggested teammates based on search queries discussed next.

3.3.2. Settings for the non-agency conditions.

After completing the initial survey, participants in the non-agency conditions saw the teams that the recommendation system assigned for them. In the case of the random teams, the recommendation system used a random function from the Python package “numpy” to assign participants to teams. In the case of the algorithmically-diverse teams, the recommendation system used a genetic algorithm that maximized a two-objective optimization problem (Gómez-Zará et al., 2022): maximizing teams’ surface-level diversity and teams’ deep-level diversity. This algorithm started creating random team combinations and searched for better combinations by swapping members. If the resulting team combination has higher diversity scores than the previous ones, the new combination will be kept, and the previous ones will be deleted. This process was done twenty times and created a Pareto front, maximizing both surface-level and deep-level diversity scores. We employed the elbow method to select one team combination from the Pareto Front and assign participants to teams. The resultant teams had higher diversity scores than the initial team combination solution. Once the recommendation team assigned participants to teams, we asked participants to review their assigned teams and their teammates’ profiles.

3.3.3. Settings for the agency conditions.

After completing the initial survey, the recommender system prompted the participants a search interface to find potential collaborators. This interface consisted of a search query to indicate teammate preferences based on the attributes collected in the initial survey. Some search criteria examples were finding people with high expertise, similar or different characteristics, or people with creativity skills. In each search query, participants had to select at least two criteria and rate the importance of these criteria using a 7-point Likert scale, ranging from “Not important at all” (-3), to “Don’t care” (0), to “Yes, for sure.” (+3). 3.5.3. Team Dynamics

In the final survey, participants answered several questions about their team processes. Participants answered sociometric questions to identify which team members they frequently communicated with during the task. They also had to indicate which team members were leaders, followers, and with whom was easy to work. From these socio-metric questions, we computed teams’ communication, leadership, followership, and likable networks. We computed the number of times that participants mentioned other ones (i.e., outdegree) and the number of times that they were mentioned by other participants (i.e., indegree).

To measure team dynamics, we used the taxonomy of Marks et al. Finally, to assess how much diversity participants perceived in their teams, they evaluated how different their team members were compared to themselves using Harrison et al. 3.5.4. Text Analytics

We transcribed teams’ conversations from the Zoom recordings using Otter.Ai. Research assistants revised and edited the transcripts to fix errors and assign voices to participants. All transcripts were coded for participant, timestamp, and dialogue. We analyzed teams’ transcripts using LIWC-22, which connects important psychological constructs and theories with words and phrases (Boyd, R. L., Ashokkumar, A., Seraj, S., Pennebaker, J. W., [n. d.]). We calculate the percentage of words in the transcripts that fall into each of the predefined categories. LIWC-22 has 96 predefined subcategories, and we reduced the total category set to 42 subcategories, focusing on function-, drives-, cognition-, social-, states-, perception-, and time-oriented categories.

We also incorporated sentiment analysis features to understand how the tone and positivity of team members’ conversations influence team dynamics. We used VADER (“Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner”), a rule-based sentiment analysis tool widely used to analyze social media text (Hutto and Gilbert, 2014). It can generalize favorably to many other domains (Chandralekha et al., 2023). We computed the maximum, minimum, average, and standard deviation of the compound sentiment scores for each participant’s conversations as well as for each team’s conversations. The VADER compound score ranges from -1 to 1, with a score closer to 1 indicating a more positive sentiment and a score closer to -1 suggesting a more negative emotion.

We recruited ten undergraduate students with majors in marketing and communication to evaluate teams’ projects. We adapted Lykourentzou et al. (Lykourentzou et al., 2017)’s evaluation scale to ask graders to rate teams’ submissions based on their originality and uniqueness on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from “Not at all” to “Very much.” These judges worked alone and independently. The scale’s items showed high consistency (Cronbach α=.96𝛼.96\alpha=.96italic_α = .96) as we calculated the mean of two dimensions’ scores to determine the team performance score.

3.6. Statistical analysis

We conducted our statistical tests using R 3.4.0. Given the multiple individual- and team-level variables, we first conducted ANOVA tests to see if there were statistically significant differences across the conditions. Then, we conducted Tukey HSD tests to compare the means of the different conditions. We standardized the independent variables before the analysis and checked that the variables followed a normal distribution.

In the case of text analytics, we assumed that the variables were not normally distributed and performed the Kruskal Wallis Test to compare the text metric differences across conditions. We employed Dunn’s test using the “FSA” package (Ogle et al., 2023) to perform multiple comparisons and adjusted the p-values using the Benjamini-Hochberg method.

To analyze the participants’ collaborator choices in the agency conditions, we employed mixed-effects logistic regression models to assess which factors likely explained these choices (Gómez-Zará et al., 2022). We included random effects for participants as they could have generated multiple search queries. The model’s observations were the recommendations generated by the agency algorithms, while the dependent variable was whether a recommendation was selected by the searcher or not. For the fixed effects, we included “recommendation’s rank” to check if the recommendation’s order increased the likelihood of being selected. We also added a “same gender” variable to check whether gender homophily was a factor that explained participants’ collaborator choices. We added the “recommendation’s diversity score,” which was calculated for all the generated recommendations, to check whether the differences between the recommended collaborator and the searcher’s team made a difference. We treated including the diversity score to the algorithm as a treatment variable: 0 in the self-assembled teams’ condition since the algorithm only used the fit score to generate the recommendations, and 1 in the fairness-aware teams’ condition as the algorithm used the fit and diversity scores. Lastly, we included an interaction term between the treatment and diversity score to check if, in fact, participants selected diverse collaborators in the fairness-aware condition. We checked the normality assumptions to run this model, and that multicollinearity was not an issue in this model by verifying that the Variation Inflation Factors (VIF) scores of the independent variables were all below 10.

Lastly, we employed a mediation analysis among the condition variables, team dynamics, and team performance. We operationalized the conditions based on whether the algorithm allowed participants’ agency and included fairness criteria. We used an interaction term to represent when the algorithm combined both components. This model provided insights into how the effects of team formation algorithms on teams’ outcomes and processes. We used the package “lavaan” (Rosseel, 2012) for this analysis.

5. Discussion

In this study, we empirically examined the impact of algorithmic interventions on team composition, dynamics, and performance. While previous research has demonstrated how algorithms can influence individuals’ decisions, satisfaction, and autonomy, their effects at the collective level have been less explored and require further investigation. Through a comprehensive laboratory experiment involving over 320 participants, our study reveals that algorithms can profoundly shape how teams are organized and performed. These findings lead to several key conclusions.

On the one hand, the findings suggest that algorithms allowing users to choose their collaborators without fairness criteria lead them into homogenous teams without the necessary skills to succeed (H1). Self-assembled teams scored the lowest in surface-level diversity, which is consistent with previous research indicating that individuals are less inclined to seek diversity when they can freely decide with whom to collaborate (Dobbin and Kalev, 2016; McPherson et al., 2001). Consequently, these teams’ low diversity levels affected their originality and creativity capabilities, becoming the least suitable teams to perform this task. Therefore, allowing agency requires guidelines to avoid homophily and segregation among teams.

On the other hand, algorithms assigning individuals to work in highly diverse teams without providing agency hurt their team dynamics and performance (H2). Specifically, too much diversity made it difficult for members to communicate during the task (Dinsbach et al., 2007). Their considerable differences may have exacerbated the adverse effects of diversity, hindering mutual trust and understanding among members (Bell et al., 2011). One possible explanation of this result is the resistance to being assigned to a highly diverse team (Dobbin and Kalev, 2016). Unlike random teams, forcing individuals to work with others who are diverse can exacerbate their judgments and lack of communication among members (Shemla et al., 2016). In sum, team diversity seems more beneficial when members can exercise certain levels of control during the team formation process. As such, these findings suggest a “Goldilocks” effect of these algorithms on participants’ collaborations: forcing too much diversity negatively affected teams’ functioning, whereas allowing them to choose collaborators without encouraging diversity did not enable teams to thrive.

In contrast, the algorithm that combined agency and fairness criteria positively impacted team composition, dynamics, and performance (H3). Participants from different backgrounds were more visible and more frequently selected when the algorithm included fairness criteria in participants’ collaboration searches. Although the resultant diversity levels were not significantly higher than those of self-assembled teams, they were probably high enough to enable them to work together effectively and benefit from diversity effects. As a result, enabling agency and fairness criteria benefited participants’ collaboration decisions at the critical stage of forming a team, creating a chain of effects later on.

This work shed light on developing recommendation algorithms that assist individuals toward collective goals rather than individual ones. Currently, many algorithms are primarily designed to optimize individual searches and decisions. Unlike those, the tested algorithm that incorporated fairness criteria and enabled collective participation resulted in augmenting participants’ decisions for their social good. Moreover, implementing algorithms with pre-coded rules facilitated a better understanding of what individuals would do later. Organizations should consider how desired values can be coded in these algorithms’ behaviors, such as diverse collaborations. A crucial aspect of this experiment was that participants were unaware of these algorithmic interventions. Keeping these algorithms’ rules and processes opaque to participants reinforced the fact that algorithms can control and direct individuals in particular directions, affecting their opportunities, relationships, and belonging (Kellogg et al., 2020).

5.1. Implications

The primary theoretical implications of this study lie in establishing causal relationships among individuals’ agency, fairness criteria, and team dynamics. Our findings reveal that the presence and absence of participants’ agency in these algorithms triggered different negative effects. On the one hand, individuals’ agency drives them toward individualistic goals. Homophily was the most evident consequence of allowing agency in this case. Algorithms used in socio-technical systems, such as those studied here, have impactful implications not only at the individual level but also in shaping collective dynamics, as similarity and like-minded communities often form by algorithms reinforcing users’ social preferences (Pariser, 2011). Future HCI research should further explore the consequences of agency in socio-technical systems, as it becomes more necessary and critical in the new AI era (Berente et al., 2021; Bennett et al., 2023).

On the other hand, restricting users’ agency also had negative effects on individuals’ collective dynamics. Our study found that participants were more aware of their demographic and cognitive differences when they were assigned to teams by the algorithms. Allowing users to choose collaborators can reduce these perceived differences by giving them a sense of control, evaluation, and reflection on their collaborations (Shemla et al., 2016). As such, future HCI studies should explore how algorithms—and artificial intelligence, broadly speaking—can enhance users’ agency to foster more commitment and satisfaction with collective goals. Our findings indicate that implementing fairness criteria in the algorithms was more effective when individuals could also exercise their agency. While there are several mechanisms to assemble teams, this study highlights the critical role of individuals’ agency in enabling team diversity to emerge.

Finally, the practical implications of this study focus on the algorithm designs that HCI practitioners, organizations, and team builders should adopt to foster collective organization. The study offers an extended perspective on how augmented intelligence can support collective goals by guiding or influencing users’ choices for good (Heer, 2019; Gómez-Zará et al., 2020). However, while this approach has potential benefits, manipulating recommendations risks shepherding users toward specific combinations that may not align with their preferences (Abbass and Hunjet, 2021). Future socio-technical systems, particularly social recommender systems or social media platforms, must find a balance between employing algorithmic interventions, providing users with transparent explanations of these recommendations, and establishing mechanisms for users to contest and explore alternative solutions. As more complex algorithms continue dominating the technological landscape, it will be crucial for algorithms’ designers and developers to audit their opaque architectures and provide transparency and explainability for the final users (Berente et al., 2021).

5.2. Limitations and Future Work

It is important to acknowledge the limitations of this study. First, teams assembled in laboratory experiments do not have a past or a future. A 20-minute creativity task would need to be revised to observe more conclusive and generalizable patterns. Moreover, diverse teams may require more time and mentoring to work well. Future research should examine more types of tasks and long-term teams in organizational settings assembled by the team formation strategies employed in this study. Second, the experimental design’s choices may have introduced bias. For example, we conducted several experimental sessions (with 30 participants on average) rather than one large-scale session (100 participants or more) for each experimental condition. We conducted small sessions to give participants enough time to review other teammates’ profiles and recommendations. Furthermore, participants formed these teams sequentially: two participants would agree to form a team, and then they could invite more people to join. The order and current status of a team could have influenced participants’ teammate decisions. This design allowed us to study the decisions at the dyadic level. Moreover, participants saw their real names on the platform, which induced gender bias. We decided to provide the participants with a setting close to reality, where names, faces, and other aspects are overt in 1-on-1 interactions.

Another crucial aspect of this experiment was that participants were unaware of these interventions. The system did not explain how the team formation algorithm worked to avoid inducing any bias in their decisions. Despite this decision, the manipulation checks showed that participants noticed these interventions after using the system. Moreover, rather than conducting this experiment on traditional online recruitment platforms (e.g., Amazon MTurk or Prolific), we decided to recruit participants from a local area. The main reason was to recruit participants who shared context (i.e., being from the same institution or city), which has implications for their diversity. Furthermore, recruiting participants from those online platforms would have prevented participants from perceiving their differences at the surface level and deep level, as required by this experiment. Future research should conduct larger-scale sessions with different kinds of participants, including those from online recruitment platforms. Lastly, the recommendation system used for this experiment did not show participants’ survey responses or reviews. The information provided could be limited compared to what individuals are used to see in real-world contexts (e.g., references, resumes). As such, participants in the experiment could have decided based exclusively on names and the rankings provided by the algorithms. Future research should test more realistic platforms and examine how additional information on recommender systems can influence individuals’ choices.

6. Conclusion

This study contributes to our understanding of algorithms in organizational contexts by examining the effects of agency and fairness criteria on team composition, processes, and performance. The results suggest that incorporating fairness criteria alone was insufficient to ensure team success, as was allowing participants to freely choose their collaborators. Instead, a minor intervention in the recommender system’s algorithm, which was resorting collaborator recommendations based on fairness criteria, enabled participants to form teams with higher diversity levels and a better balance between experts and novices compared to other algorithms. As a result, those participants performed better and were able to communicate more frequently with their teammates. We hope these findings can guide the design of improved social recommendation systems, helping organizations and HCI practitioners nudge individuals into forming more effective and diverse teams.
