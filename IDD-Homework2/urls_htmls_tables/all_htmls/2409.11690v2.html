Abstract.

The ID-free recommendation paradigm has been proposed to address the limitation that traditional recommender systems struggle to model cold-start users or items with new IDs. Despite its effectiveness, this study uncovers that ID-free recommender systems are vulnerable to the proposed Text Simulation attack (TextSimu) which aims to promote specific target items. As a novel type of text poisoning attack, TextSimu exploits large language models (LLM) to alter the textual information of target items by simulating the characteristics of popular items. It operates effectively in both black-box and white-box settings, utilizing two key components: a unified popularity extraction module, which captures the essential characteristics of popular items, and an N-persona consistency simulation strategy, which creates multiple personas to collaboratively synthesize refined promotional textual descriptions for target items by simulating the popular items. To withstand TextSimu-like attacks, we further explore the detection approach for identifying LLM-generated promotional text. Extensive experiments conducted on three datasets demonstrate that TextSimu poses a more significant threat than existing poisoning attacks, while our defense method can detect malicious text of target items generated by TextSimu. By identifying the vulnerability, we aim to advance the development of more robust ID-free recommender systems.

1. Introduction

Traditional recommendation paradigms convert all users/items into ID embeddings and match these embeddings to capture user preferences and recommend suitable items from a vast pool (Guo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2022). Although such ID-based recommendations show promise, they struggle with the unavoidable cold start problem (Wei et al., 2021a). When a new item/user is introduced, the recommender system must assign a unique new ID to this item/user, necessitating continuous retraining of the system to accommodate it. Additionally, the newly trained corresponding ID embeddings, which lack sufficient historical information, cannot precisely encode the users/items, leading to degraded recommendation (Yuan et al., 2023b). Thus, the reliance on IDs significantly hinders the effectiveness of recommendation tasks, particularly when dealing with cold-start items.

To overcome the limitations imposed by IDs in recommender systems, an advanced ID-free recommendation paradigm has been proposed and proven effective in addressing the cold start problem (Hou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023). Figure 1 illustrates the difference between ID-based and ID-free recommendations. In ID-based recommendations, a unique ID number is assigned to each user or item, and every ID has its corresponding embedding stored in the look-up table. In contrast, ID-free recommender systems leverage the powerful semantic understanding capabilities of language models (LMs) (Zhao et al., 2023) to encode the text information of each user and item into corresponding representations. These text representations can establish semantic relationships between newly added users or items and existing ones, which effectively eliminates the dependence on ID embeddings and mitigates the cold start problem.

While ID-free recommendation offers a new paradigm for modern recommender systems, its robustness against the poisoning attacks (Wang et al., 2024) aimed at promoting individual target items remains underexplored. In ID-based recommendation, attackers can deceive the system into recommending low-quality target items to more users (Chen et al., 2022; Huang and Li, 2023a; Huang et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2020), severely undermining the utility of recommender systems. If such vulnerabilities exist in ID-free recommender systems, the consequences could be serious once it is widely adopted. To address this risk, we initially explored the susceptibility of these systems to two types of attacks: injection poisoning attacks (Wang et al., 2024), which involves injecting forged user profiles with malicious interactions into recommender systems, and text poisoning attacks (Li et al., 2018), typically used in the natural language processing (NLP) domain, which subtly modifies the textual information of target items. Through experiments (refer to Section 2 for details), we found that both attack types have a limited impact on ID-free recommender systems. Injection poisoning attacks, while more effective, require prohibitive costs due to the need to inject a substantial number of malicious profiles (over 0.1% of the total user base). Conversely, traditional text poisoning attacks, despite their low cost, proved ineffective as minor text modifications cannot significantly impact the recommendation model’s encoding process.

Although ID-free recommender systems appear almost immune to existing attacks (large-scale injections are actually infeasible due to limited attack budgets and the increased difficulty to evade defense systems), in this paper, we reveal a critical vulnerability in ID-free recommender systems by demonstrating how these systems can be disrupted by a novel cost-effective attack. As shown before, traditional text poisoning attacks are often limited by their minor modifications, leading to ineffectiveness. However, in recommender systems, some attackers have the ability to control item text, allowing for more flexible and extensive modifications. Building on this premise, we introduce a special type of text poisoning attack called Text Simulation attack (TextSimu), which utilizes benign-looking simulation prompts to manipulate large language models (LLMs) into rewriting target item texts in a manner that mimics popular items. By aligning with the characteristics of popular items, the generated text can be favored by the recommendation model and appeal to more users. Specifically, TextSimu integrates a unified popularity extraction component to extract keywords from multiple popular items and incorporate them into the prompt instruction, ensuring global popular preferences are reflected rather than preventing reliance on a single popular item. Additionally, given that a single prompt may potentially result in an output with unstable quality, we developed the N-persona consistency simulation strategy, which involves creating multiple personas that each independently crafts malicious texts. These personas then collaborate and refine outputs to ensure consistency. The final output is synthesized based on the most consistent characteristics across the generated texts for the specific target item.

Furthermore, while the proposed attack is designed for black-box settings, to assess the theoretical upper bound of TextSimu’s threat, we extend it to a white-box attack scenario, assuming access to the LLM and the ID-Free recommender system. In this white-box context, we employ an adaptive fine-tuning method to train the victim LLM as a poisoning text generator. This method can be seamlessly combined with the N-persona consistency simulation strategy, enhancing the potency of the text simulation attack.

Lastly, to mitigate the harm caused by TextSimu and similar attacks, we correspondingly propose a defense method named RewriteDetection. The core idea of RewriteDetection is to segment item text into two parts and use LLMs to predict the content of the remaining part. By comparing the differences between the ground-truth content and the predicted content, including semantic and recommendation effects, we can identify whether the text is LLM-generated and possesses malicious promotional capabilities. This approach effectively filters out malicious items, achieving the goal of maintaining the integrity of the recommender system.

Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

  • •

    We investigate the vulnerability of ID-free recommender systems to poisoning attacks, a previously underexplored topic, despite the effectiveness of ID-free recommendations.

  • •

    We unveil a potential text simulation poisoning attack model, bulit on a unified popularity extraction component and N-persona consistency simulation strategy. This model demonstrates how LLMs can inadvertently become tools for malicious attackers.

  • •

    We propose a detection method to protect systems from the malicious promotion of individual target items, countering our proposed and similar poisoning attacks.

  • •

    Extensive experiments on several datasets illustrate that our proposed attack method poses a greater threat than existing attack methods, and our defense method can effectively detect malicious target items.

2.2. Experiments and Findings

2.2.1. Impact of Injection Poisoning Attacks

We first initiated a series of experiments targeting injection poisoning attacks on ID-free recommender systems, which involves injecting a set of malicious users with well-designed interactions to boost the visibility of target items within the Beauty dataset. Our focus was on two prevalent injection attack methods: Random-Attack (Lam and Riedl, 2004a), which involves malicious users selecting random items and target items to interact with, and Bandwagon-Attack (Turk and Bilge, 2019), which increases the visibility of target items by associating them with popular items instead of randomly selected ones.

Subsequently, we assessed the impact of these poisoning attacks on two widely-used ID-free recommendation algorithms: MoRec (Yuan et al., 2023b) and UnisRec (Hou et al., 2022). The chosen target items is nearly with zero opportunities to be recommended. As depicted in Figure 2, the threat level of injection poisoning attacks, quantified by Hit Ratio@50, escalates with the increase in the number of malicious users. A higher injection ratio of malicious users significantly disrupts the recommendation outcomes. However, it is crucial to highlight that even a 0.1% presence of malicious users indicates an impractically high budget for the attackers (Huang and Li, 2023b). This implies that achieving an effective attack requires substantial resources from the attackers.

2.2.2. Impact of Text Poisoning Attacks

The heavy reliance of ID-free recommender systems on textual information prompted us to investigate text poisoning attacks, focusing on text modifications to influence model recommendations. To evaluate the effectiveness of traditional text poisoning attacks transferred in the recommendation domain, we employed two well-known techniques: TextBugger (Li et al., 2018) and TextFooler (Jin et al., 2020), which were used to alter the textual content of target items to enhance their visibility within ID-free recommender systems.

As shown in Figure 2, text poisoning attacks had minimal impact on recommendation outcomes, with Hit Ratio@50 values not exceeding 0.1%. This suggests that text poisoning methods effective in NLP contexts do not adapt well to the recommendation domain. We attribute this to the fact that traditional text poisoning attacks are primarily designed for traditional NLP tasks such as text classification (Kowsari et al., 2019). These attacks typically involve minor modifications to characters or words, directly converting correct tokens into incorrect or unknown tokens. While such small modifications are highly effective for NLP tasks, the additional recommendation encoding process in ID-free recommender systems tends to overlook these minor textual changes, rendering the modifications ineffective.

Discussion. Injection poisoning attacks can greatly increase the visibility of target items, but they are expensive to execute. Conversely, text poisoning attacks are less costly since they only require text modifications, but are almost ineffective in promotion. However, there is a key difference between traditional text poisoning attacks in NLP and those in the recommendation domain. In NLP tasks, attackers make subtle changes to the text to avoid detection, while in the recommender system domain, the text of an item is often controlled by the merchant (attacker), allowing for more significant modifications. This indicates that in recommender systems, there is greater flexibility in how the text of target items can be manipulated. Following this idea, the next section will explore the possibility of implementing a novel text poisoning attack that is cost-effective while achieving powerful attack effectiveness simultaneously.

3.2. Naive LLM-Powered Poisoning Attack

A straightforward way for attackers to achieve cost-effective and powerful malicious promotion is to rewrite the original textual information of target items in an appealing style using expert domain knowledge. However, hiring experts increases the attack budget, which contradicts our goal of cost-effectiveness. Instead, advanced LLMs, with their extensive open-world knowledge, can serve as a substitute for human experts. This allows us to accomplish the task conveniently without incurring additional costs for industry expertise. To illustrate this approach, we first design a simple example prompt for LLMs as follows:
Rewrite the product content so that it is more likely to be liked by users.

However, this simple prompt has two unavoidable weaknesses: (1) Since different users have diverse preferences, the simply generated textual information cannot appeal to a wide user base. (2) Additionally, to seamlessly integrate the text into systems, the generated textual information should align with the language style of the target system. LLMs, lacking this specific knowledge, cannot accurately tailor content to align with the recommendation tendency and language style of the victim system. Consequently, a simple prompt is inadequate for LLMs to generate precisely suitable textual formats for describing target items.

In the following subsections, we will introduce a novel type of text poisoning attacks, called Text Simulation attack (TextSimu), which leverages simulation thought with LLMs as a rewriting tool to adapt to different user preferences and language styles. Firstly, we will introduce the black-box TextSimu, which does not require prior knowledge of the recommender system and can be more easily applied to real-world scenarios. Subsequently, we delve into the expLoRAtion of the white-box TextSimu, which is aware of the victim LLM and recommendation model used by the system, to examine the theoretical upper limits of this attack.

3.3. TextSimu in Black-Box Setting

In the black-box setting, attackers have limited information about the recommendation tendencies and language style of the victim system, which further increases the difficulty of conducting a text poisoning attack. To circumvent this constraint, our solution extracts the text of popular items as reference content and rephrases the target items’ text to align with this reference content. In general, popular items have a higher probability of being recommended compared to other items, reflecting the concentration of user preferences. By simulating the text of popular items, the generated textual information can capture the features that appeal to most users while aligning with the system’s language style.

Based on this idea, we aim to guide LLMs to rewrite the textual information of target items by simulating popular items. First, we extract popular items from recommender systems. Using Gemma-2B111https://huggingface.co/google/gemma-1.1-2b-it, we encode the text of both the target item and the popular items into text representations. Then we calculate the cosine similarity between the target item and each popular item, selecting the one with the highest similarity as the reference item. Finally, we provide a simple prompt to the LLM based on this reference item. Here is an example:
Rewrite the ¡Original Content¿ to closely align with the ¡Reference Content¿.

The simulation prompt can be effective to some extent, but we have identified two drawbacks affecting the quality of output: (1) Feeding a single reference content into LLMs often fails to adequately uncover the universal characteristics of most popular items. This shortfall hinders the model’s ability to contextually understand and identify the most engaging aspects of the content for users. To address this, we propose a unified popularity extraction component that leverages keyword integration to capture the essential attributes of widely admired items; (2) An LLM can only generate one instance per prompt, and due to the variability of LLMs’ output, a single instance can result in instability and compromised quality. To mitigate this issue, we have developed an N-persona consistency simulation strategy, inspired by existing research that demonstrates the positive impact of persona-based customization on LLM output quality (Chan et al., 2024). An overview of the black-box TextSimu is depicted in Figure  3.

3.3.1. Unified Popularity Extraction

We use the same method to retrieve the 50 most similar popular items as reference items to extract the core unified characters of popular items. After that, we construct a graph where the nodes represent tokens from the text of popular items, and edges connect adjacent tokens. Utilizing the TextRank algorithm (Mihalcea and Tarau, 2004), we extract the most pivotal keywords from this graph. These keywords are not random but are the most recurrent terms across popular items, representing the core attributes that contribute to their popularity. We further leverage LLMs to pick out the appealing keywords and remove the general ones. By incorporating these extracted keywords into our prompts, we ensure that the generated content aligns closely with the essential elements that characterize widely favored items. For practical use, the prompts can be structured as follows:
Ensure the rewritten content naturally incorporates ¡Keywords¿.

3.3.2. N-Persona Consistency Simulation.

LLMs are tasked with generating N distinct personas, each designed to embody a unique set of preferences. This method enables each persona to generate text that encapsulates its unique characteristics, resulting in a varied collection of textual outputs. We choose to create a number of personas of sales experts to maximally elicit the knowledge of LLMs in item promotion. Inspired by the self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022b) in Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022) that conducts multiple different ways of thinking to arrive at the correct answer for a reasoning problem, these diverse outputs are then aggregated into a discussion, ultimately facilitating the creation of a cohesive and more universally appealing final text. The following sketchy prompt provides a complete demonstration of this N-persona consistency simulation strategy.

Implementing the N-persona strategy offers dual advantages. Firstly, the varied perspectives provided by multiple personas enrich the textual output, preventing monotony and enhancing the potential for effective aggregation in the discussion. Secondly, by simulating and analyzing the preferences of different personas, we can better approximate and cater to the varied tastes of a broader user base. This not only improves the relevance and appeal of the generated text but also increases its effectiveness in reaching diverse audiences, eliminating the risk of individual poor-quality results and enhancing the stability of the eventual malicious effects. 3.4. TextSimu in White-Box Setting

While TextSimu is practical from an application standpoint, it cannot leverage the intelligence an attacker might have to enhance the attack due to the black-box setting. In certain scenarios, attackers may possess unexpected capabilities, allowing them to gain comprehensive knowledge of the victim system. For instance, some systems may publicly disclose the backbone LLMs they use, enabling attackers to exploit them. In more severe cases, the attackers could be internal personnel with access to the victim recommendation model. Thus, in this section, we further explore the adaptation of TextSimu to the white-box setting to investigate the theoretical upper limit of the vulnerability of ID-free recommender systems. Specifically, we designed a fine-tuning strategy based on LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), to train the victim LLM into a malicious generator. The process of fine-tuning the victim LLM is illustrated in Figure  4, including popular item simulation and target rank promotion, to learn the system’s recommendation tendency and language style.

6. Related Work

6.1. ID-Free Recommendation

Traditional ID-based recommender systems encounter cold-start problems due to the assignment of unique ID representations to new users and items(Huang et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2021b). To address these issues, multi-modal recommender systems have utilized pre-trained language models to encode the textual information of items, transforming them into text representations (Wei et al., 2023). These enriched text representations are then integrated with ID representations for model training.

With the advent of advanced LMs, researchers have introduced ID-free recommendations to address the limitations of ID representations. This approach leverages text representations encoded by LMs, eliminating the need for item ID embeddings (Yuan et al., 2023b). For example, ZESRec (Ding et al., 2021) introduces zero-shot recommenders that utilize natural-language descriptions as continuous indices, eliminating the need for item IDs used in traditional methods. MoRec (Yuan et al., 2023b) utilizes LLM encoders to transform plain text into item representations, demonstrating performance comparable to traditional ID-based methods that need an expensive end-to-end training process. Similarly, UnisRec (Hou et al., 2022) eschews explicit item IDs, employing a lightweight item encoding architecture based on parametric whitening and a mixture-of-experts enhanced adaptor. This system incorporates two contrastive pre-training tasks to encode item description texts into transferable representations across various recommendation scenarios. Furthermore, Recformer (Li et al., 2023) represents items as key-value attribute pairs instead of item IDs, and introduces a novel bi-directional Transformer model for item representation. This model, based on a pre-training and fine-tuning framework, significantly outperforms existing ID-based methods, particularly in zero-shot and cold-start item recommendation scenarios.

6.2. Poisoning Attacks

In the past, poisoning attacks in recommendation primarily depended on heuristic manipulations of user behavior data (Lam and Riedl, 2004b). However, with the increased sophistication of recommender systems, traditional attack methods have become ineffective. Thus, researchers have developed more advanced attacks that are strategically designed to craft malicious interactions (Wang et al., 2022a, 2023b; Guo et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023a). For instance, SGLD (Li et al., 2016) generates malicious interactions within matrix factorization-based systems to effectively promote target items. Building on this, GSPAttack (Nguyen Thanh et al., 2023) exposes vulnerabilities intrinsic to graph structures, where users and items are represented as nodes connected by interaction edges. This approach optimizes malicious user-item interactions by exploiting the dynamics of graph propagation.

Vulnerabilities to poisoning attacks become more pronounced in text data-involved scenarios. These attacks demonstrate how textual modifications can significantly increase an item’s visibility, bypassing direct manipulation of user interactions. Initially, text poisoning attacks proved effective primarily in the NLP domain, leading to the emergence of numerous text poisoning attack methods (Ebrahimi et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020). In the field of recommender systems, text attacks are also gradually revealing their impact. For example, ARG (Chiang et al., 2023) utilizes a reinforcement learning framework with three auxiliary rewards and aspect generation training to craft malicious reviews that convincingly deceive review-based recommender systems. TextRecAttack (Zhang et al., 2024) underscores the security vulnerabilities of LLM-based recommender systems by targeting the manipulation of textual data associated with items. This approach employs adding, deleting, and replacing word techniques to generate malicious text aimed at inducing shifts in recommendations. ATR-2FT (Oh et al., 2024) employs text rewriting way by fine-tuning an LLM to achieve both greater attack performance and higher text rewriting quality. The fine-tuned LLM is then used as a generator to produce malicious text. It should be noted that ARG and TextRecAttack still require injecting interaction data. In contrast, our approach leverages LLMs solely to generate malicious text.

7. Conclusion

In this study, we examine the vulnerability of ID-free recommendation methods to text-poisoning attacks aimed at promoting specific items. Specifically, we introduce a potential text simulation attack approach in black-box and white-box settings, called TextSimu, which leverages LLMs with seemingly harmless prompts to generate malicious textual information. With the enhancement of proposed unified popularity extraction and N-Persona consistency simulation components, this approach can increase the visibility of target items in users’ preference lists, while effectively reducing the attack cost to almost zero. To counter this attack method, we propose a detection mechanism named RewriteDetection, which identifies malicious text from LLM-generated and malicious promotion features. Extensive experiments on three real-world datasets demonstrate both the potential threats posed by TextSimu and the effectiveness of RewriteDetection. Our future research will focus on identifying more potential threats within text-enhanced recommender systems and addressing the specific challenges associated with poisoning attacks.
