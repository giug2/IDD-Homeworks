Federated Hypergraph Learning with Hyperedge Completion

Linfeng Luo1, Fengxiao Tang1, Xiyu Liu1, Zhiqi Guo1, Zihao Qi1, Ming Zhao1
Abstract

Hypergraph neural networks enhance conventional graph neural networks by capturing high-order relationships among nodes, which proves vital in data-rich environments where interactions are not merely pairwise. As data complexity and interconnectivity grow, it is common for graph-structured data to be split and stored in a distributed manner, underscoring the necessity of federated learning on subgraphs. In this work, we propose FedHGN, a novel algorithm for federated hypergraph learning. Our algorithm utilizes subgraphs of a hypergraph stored on distributed devices to train local HGNN models in a federated mannerâ€”by collaboratively developing an effective global HGNN model through sharing model parameters while preserving client privacy. Additionally, considering that hyperedges may span multiple clients, a pre-training step is employed before the training process in which cross-client hyperedge feature gathering is performed at the central server. In this way, the missing cross-client information can be supplemented from the central server during the node feature aggregation phase. Experimental results on seven real-world datasets confirm the effectiveness of our approach and demonstrate its performance advantages over traditional federated graph learning methods.

Introduction

Hypergraphs, as a type of complex graph structure, enable the connection of multiple nodes through single hyperedges. Compared to simple graphs, hypergraphs offer advantages by capturing higher-order relationships that reflect the multi-dimensional interconnectivity present in various real-world data structures. Recently, hypergraph neural networks have gained prominence as tools for mining features and patterns on complex graph-structured data in data-rich environments (Feng etÂ al. 2019; Yadati etÂ al. 2019; Xia etÂ al. 2021). However, hypergraph works face the same significant challenges as simple graphs: the rapid growth and sheer volume of data necessitate distributed storage, while stringent data protection regulations complicate data sharing across distributed systems. This scenario calls for robust solutions that enable efficient distributed training of hypergraph neural networks across decentralized data sources.

Federated learning (FL) is developed to address the challenges of training neural networks in distributed environments, enhancing data privacy and security by allowing multiple participants to collaboratively build a shared model without sharing their local data (McMahan etÂ al. 2017). Based on federated learning, several works on federated graph learning have been proposed to effectively train graph neural networks across multiple decentralized data sources (Zhang etÂ al. 2021; Yao etÂ al. 2024; Chen etÂ al. 2021; Lei etÂ al. 2023). These efforts specifically tackle the loss of cross-subgraph information, a critical issue that often leads to incomplete node representations and potentially degraded model performance. This issue arises because each local system holds only a portion of the entire graph, resulting in lost connections between different subgraphs.

While federated learning on simple graphs has been extensively studied, the scenario changes significantly when it comes to hypergraphs, making existing methods inapplicable. Using the computer networks as a potential application scenarios of federated hypergraph learning: graph neural networks are increasingly employed in network modeling for studying and analyzing its performance (Rusek etÂ al. 2020; Ferriol-GalmÃ©s etÂ al. 2022; Yang etÂ al. 2022; Zhou etÂ al. 2023). As shown in Figure 1, in a typical network environment, multiple Internet Service Providers (ISPs) collectively provide network services to users but avoid sharing internal data due to privacy concerns. Network administrators aim to predict future network performance metrics (such as throughput and latency) or classify the operational status or types of the network devices based on network performance data. To develop a comprehensive model that can analyze the overall state of the network while adhering to privacy protection, it is essential that ISPs collaboratively participate in the model training process in a federated manner.

For scenarios including the above, where high-order relationships exist among nodes, federated graph learning is required. To be specific, existing graph neural network methods for network modeling represent network node metrics as input features and physical links as adjacency relationships within the graph. This modeling approach overlooks the communications between multiple devices in network operations, i.e., the data flows transmitted along paths planned by routers. While inadequately represented in conventional graphs, the data flow can be precisely depicted using hyperedges, thereby necessitating the adoption of hypergraph neural networks. Moreover, the inability to share data across ISPs leads to the loss of cross-client data flow information, which may result in severe performance degradation.

In this work, we propose FedHGN, a novel federated hypergraph learning algorithm to address the above challenges. We select HGNN (Feng etÂ al. 2019) as the graph mining model, considering its advantageous property of updating node features in two stages: edge feature collection and node feature aggregation. In FedHGN, independent HGNN models are deployed on each client, and the global model parameters are updated and synchronized to the clients using the FedAVG (McMahan etÂ al. 2017) algorithm. We first propose a basic version of FedHGN that trims cross-client hyperedges to minimize node relationship loss within clients while adhering to privacy regulations. Furthermore, to prevent the loss of cross-client information, an Nğ‘N-round pre-training hyperedge completion (HC) operation is introduced. In the pre-training step, clients share the processed embeddings of border nodes with the central server, thus enabling the aggregation of cross-client hyperedge features. Subsequently, the missing cross-client information is completed by using the border hyperedge features distributed by the server. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

  • â€¢

    We present FedHGN, a novel algorithm for federated hypergraph learning that jointly learns from multiple local subgraphs of a hypergraph. To our knowledge, it is the first comprehensive solution for hypergraph mining tasks on distributed subgraphs.

  • â€¢

    To address the challenge of cross-client information loss, we introduce a pre-training hyperedge completion operation. This approach gathers node features for cross-client hyperedges on the server, effectively supplementing cross-client information without sharing raw features between clients. By this way, we significantly boost the performance of FedHGN while minimizing the risk of privacy leakage.

  • â€¢

    We validate our FedHGN by using four real-world hypergraph datasets, focusing on both federated hypergraph training and the HC operation. Additionally, we compare it with state-of-the-art federated subgraph learning algorithms on three simple graph datasets. The results demonstrate that FedHGN effectively handles distributed training for higher-order graph data and performs competitively on simple graphs.

Related Work

Hypergraph Learning

The field of hypergraph learning has evolved considerably in recent years (Antelmi etÂ al. 2023). (Zhou, Huang, and SchÃ¶lkopf 2006) laid the groundwork for hypergraph learning by introducing techniques for spectral clustering, classification, and embedding to manage high-order relationships in data. Building upon this, (Feng etÂ al. 2019) extended spectral convolution to hypergraphs by proposing Hypergraph Neural Networks (HGNN), which effectively capture complex data correlations. Another contribution is HyperGCN by (Yadati etÂ al. 2019), which simplifies the learning process by approximating hyperedges with pairwise edges. Incorporating additional structural information, (Dong, Sawin, and Bengio 2020) proposed Hypergraph Neural Networks with Line Expansion (HNHN), which enhance performance through a more detailed representation of hyperedges. (Arya etÂ al. 2020) introduced HyperSAGE, an inductive framework that generalizes representation learning on hypergraphs using a two-level neural message passing strategy. The dual-channel approach to hypergraph convolution is developed by (Xia etÂ al. 2021) with Dual Channel Hypergraph Convolutional Networks (DHCN), leveraging two separate channels for node and hyperedge updates. Lastly, (Gao etÂ al. 2022) introduced HGNN+, a framework that integrates hyperedge modeling and hypergraph convolution, offering an optimized approach to hypergraph learning.

Federated Subgraph Learning

Federated subgraph learning has gained significant attention for enabling collaborative learning across distributed subgraphs while preserving data privacy and avoiding cross-client information loss. FedGNN, introduced by (Wu etÂ al. 2021), is a framework designed to preserve user privacy in recommendation systems by leveraging graph neural networks and federated learning. In another effort, (He etÂ al. 2021) developed FedGraphNN, a comprehensive benchmark system for federated learning with graph neural networks. (Zhang etÂ al. 2021) proposed FedSage, which employs GraphSage combined with federated averaging, and includes FedSage+, a variant with a missing neighbor generator to address incomplete neighbor information in federated settings. (Chen etÂ al. 2021) introduced FedGraph, which enhances graph learning capabilities through intelligent sampling and cross-client convolution operations while preserving privacy. Additionally, FedGCN, proposed by (Yao etÂ al. 2024), reduces communication overhead and improves convergence rates in federated training of graph convolutional networks by using homomorphic encryption and differential privacy techniques. Lastly, (Lei etÂ al. 2023) presented FedCog, a federated learning framework for coupled graphs that efficiently manages distributed graph data and improves node classification performance.

Despite the advancements, these methods are unsuitable for federated hypergraph learning because the higher-order relationships in hypergraphs necessitate different modeling techniques and propagation rules.

In this section, we first discuss the notations used in hypergraphs learning, then we define the problem of semi-supervised node classification on subgraphs of a hypergraph. Finally, we present a base version of our federated hypergraph learning scheme: FedHGN.

Security Analysis of Hyperedge Completion

The primary goal of federated learning is to enable distributed model training while protecting privacy. Therefore, any information sharing between clients or between clients and the server must ensure that the privacy of the raw features is not compromised. Our HC operation has good properties that avoid the use of high-cost privacy protection methods such as homomorphic encryption and differential privacy. Specifically, as illustrated in Figure 2, there are two scenarios during the HC process where privacy leakage can occur: when clients upload the embeddings to the central server, and when the central server distributes the features of border hyperedges to the clients. Results and Discussions

As shown in Table 3, the experimental results of FedHGN for semi-supervised node classification on three simple graph datasets demonstrate its optimal performance. Before incorporating the HC operation, FedHGN did not have an advantage over other methods that ignore cross-client information loss. One potential reason is that we generate hyperedges by selecting the 1-hop neighbors from the simple graph without introducing other high-order information. However, the HC operation supplements missing cross-client information, allowing FedHGN to recover and surpass the performance of the current state-of-the-art federated subgraph learning methods. The results show that FedHGN not only addresses hypergraph mining tasks where traditional methods are not applicable but also provides a superior solution for handling cross-client information loss in federated subgraph learning.

Conclusion

The increasing complexity of graph data and the trend toward distributed storage demand advanced federated hypergraph learning methods. In this work, we introduce FedHGN, a framework addressing the challenges of distributed training for higher-order graph data. Alongside deploying HGNN models locally on clients and using the FedAVG algorithm to update global parameters, our approach includes a pre-training hyperedge completion operation to mitigate cross-client information loss, thereby enhancing performance while maintaining privacy. Experimental results on real-world datasets confirm FedHGNâ€™s effectiveness and its edge over existing federated subgraph learning methods.
