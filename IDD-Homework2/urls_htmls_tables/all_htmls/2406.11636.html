Feasibility of Federated Learning from Client Databases with Different Brain Diseases and MRI Modalities

Felix Wagner 11    Wentian Xu 11    Pramit Saha 11    Ziyun Liang 11    Daniel Whitehouse 22    David Menon 22    Natalie Voets 33    J. Alison Noble 11    Konstantinos Kamnitsas 114455
Abstract

Segmentation models for brain lesions in MRI are commonly developed for a specific disease and trained on data with a predefined set of MRI modalities. Each such model cannot segment the disease using data with a different set of MRI modalities, nor can it segment any other type of disease. Moreover, this training paradigm does not allow a model to benefit from learning from heterogeneous databases that may contain scans and segmentation labels for different types of brain pathologies and diverse sets of MRI modalities. Is it feasible to use Federated Learning (FL) for training a single model on client databases that contain scans and labels of different brain pathologies and diverse sets of MRI modalities? We demonstrate promising results by combining appropriate, simple, and practical modifications to the model and training strategy: Designing a model with input channels that cover the whole set of modalities available across clients, training with random modality drop, and exploring the effects of feature normalization methods.  Evaluation on 7 brain MRI databases with 5 different diseases shows that such FL framework can train a single model that is shown to be very promising in segmenting all disease types seen during training. Importantly, it is able to segment these diseases in new databases that contain sets of modalities different from those in training clients.  These results demonstrate, for the first time, feasibility and effectiveness of using FL to train a single segmentation model on decentralised data with diverse brain diseases and MRI modalities, a necessary step towards leveraging heterogeneous real-world databases. Code will be made available at: https://github.com/FelixWag/FL-MultiDisease-MRI

1 Introduction

Brain MRI segmentation is crucial for disease analysis, diagnosis and treatment. Traditionally, brain MRI segmentation models are trained on data for a specific disease, acquired with a predefined protocol and set of MRI modalities [8, 9, 30, 28]. Models trained with this standard approach cannot segment other types of diseases, and expect the same modalities in all training and testing data. While a recent study [26] demonstrated the feasibility of training a segmentation model across multiple brain MRI databases with different diseases and sets of modalities, that study used a centralised database. It did not address the practical challenge related to the sensitive nature of medical data, which prevents data sharing between institutions and therefore the creation of an appropriately diverse centralised database due to legal and privacy concerns.

To address this, this study aims to develop a Federated Learning (FL) framework [15] for training a single model on diverse brain lesion databases, each with a different disease and set of MRI modalities. Central questions for this study are: How to make such FL training feasible? Will it be beneficial compared to training a disease-specific model? Will it result in a model that can segment new data that have different combination of modalities than the training data?

Related Work: While previous studies investigated training with heterogeneous databases [27, 22, 16], they have not studied FL using decentralised databases, nor data with diverse sets of MRI modalities. A recent study [26] investigated heterogeneous databases with diverse sets of MRI modalities in a centralized scenario. In contrast, our study focuses on the challenging setting of decentralized FL, which has not been explored yet. Some works have focused on FL of a segmentation model for a single, specific disease when a specific set of multi-modal MRI is available in each client [11, 19, 17]. Other works studied FL for learning from data with different labels in segmentation of CT [20] or classification in MRI [6]. In summary, there are works that focus on FL with multi-modal MRI and FL for heterogeneous tasks; however, the combination of both, especially with different sets of modalities per client, has never been explored.

Learning from databases with varying sets of MRI modalities relates to research on developing multi-modal models that perform well even when a modality is missing at inference time [4, 18, 21, 5, 31, 24]. This line of work focuses on learning a model for a specific disease, from data with the same set of modalities during training. While addressing missing modalities is an important capability of our method, it is not the primary topic of this research. Instead, this study concentrates on the effectiveness and practicality of learning from diverse databases with different sets of input modalities and segmentation tasks.

Contribution: This study develops a simple but practical solution for training via FL a single model that can process different sets of modalities and segment brain lesions of different types. We show this can be achieved by a standard U-net, enhanced such that input channels can receive the whole set of unique modalities available across clients and using random drop of modalities during training to prevent associations of modalities with specific pathologies. Additionally, due to the high data distribution shift across diverse databases, we present an analysis of the effects of feature normalization techniques. Experimentation on 7 brain MRI databases with 5 different pathologies and 6 MRI modalities provides the following findings:

  • •

    Feasibility: This study is the first to show feasibility of Federated training of a single segmentation model on decentralised databases with different brain diseases and sets of MRI modalities.

  • •

    Knowledge Transfer Across Pathologies: This paper demonstrates effectiveness of FL for jointly learning to segment multiple pathologies, achieving performance that compares favorably to disease-specific models.

  • •

    Segmenting new combination of modalities: This study shows that models trained with this FL paradigm can segment data from new databases with combination of modalities not seen during training.

2.2 Training a Unified Model for Multiple Segmentation Tasks

Handling Different Sets of Modalities Across Clients: Since every client comes with a different combination of MRI modalities, the challenge arises to find a single model architecture capable of processing different sets of input modalities. To address this, we augment each client’s input to cover the full set of distinct MRI modalities across all clients, and fill missing modalities m∈ℳ−ℳc𝑚ℳsubscriptℳ𝑐m\in\mathcal{M}-\mathcal{M}_{c} of each client c𝑐c with zeros. This transforms the input space of each client to 𝒳c∈ℝp×w×h×dsubscript𝒳𝑐superscriptℝ𝑝𝑤ℎ𝑑\mathcal{X}_{c}\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times w\times h\times d}. Consequently, our global model accepts the set of distinct MRI modalities as input and becomes a function f​(⋅;θ):ℝp×w×h×d→ℝw×h×d:𝑓⋅𝜃→superscriptℝ𝑝𝑤ℎ𝑑superscriptℝ𝑤ℎ𝑑f(\cdot;\theta):\mathbb{R}^{p\times w\times h\times d}\to\mathbb{R}^{w\times h\times d}, capable of processing each client’s input modality combination, ensuring compatibility across all clients.

Modality Drop for Pathology Disassociation & Missing Modalities: To develop a model that generalises to modality combinations not seen during training, we introduce modality drop, where we randomly set input modalities to zero during training. Therefore, we define a probabilistic masking function Z​(𝒳,ϕ)𝑍𝒳italic-ϕZ(\mathcal{X},\phi) where each input modality xm∈𝒳superscript𝑥𝑚𝒳x^{m}\in\mathcal{X} is set to zero with probability ϕitalic-ϕ\phi. This prevents the model from associating specific modality combinations with specific pathologies, ensuring it can accurately segment known pathologies in unseen clients with modality combinations not seen during training. Furthermore, it also enhances the model’s robustness against missing modalities during testing, as shown in our evaluation.

Feature Normalization for Heterogeneous Clients: Each client’s data contains different pathologies and modalities, which increases the difficulty of training a model due to data heterogeneity. To tackle this, we employ Batch Normalization (BN) [7], but keep client-specific BN parameters and statistics, and exclude them from the model averaging step, as proposed in [1, 12]. Our experiments show that this method proves effective in improving the performance of the training (source) databases. However, it is not clear how to apply the source-specific BN layer parameters/statistics to unseen (target) clients during inference. One way involves averaging the learned BN parameters and estimating running statistics on the target data, which has the disadvantage that target data is needed. Therefore, in our experiments, we also explore alternative feature normalization methods for this training framework with inherently high data heterogeneity. Specifically, the recently proposed Normalization Free (NF) technique [32] uses re-parameterized convolutional layers instead of explicitly normalizing statistics of feature activations. This does not require target data to estimate the target statistics, alleviating this requirement of keeping client-specific BN layers. Empirical analysis (Sec. 3) also shows that this results in methods that generalize better to unseen clients. However, it slightly decreases performance on source clients. Therefore, the choice of feature normalization method in this framework should be chosen based on the target application for the trained model (generalization to new domains or application to source domains) and whether data are expected to have been collected a-priori from the target domain for estimating BN statistics.

Algorithm: To summarise, our simple yet effective algorithm is shown in Fig. 1. Initially, the central server receives a list of available MRI modalities from each client’s dataset. Afterwards, the server initializes a model architecture that accepts the set of unique MRI modalities ℳℳ\mathcal{M} as input and the FL algorithm starts (Sec. 2.1). During training, each client’s input modalities are augmented to the whole set of unique MRI modalities and modality drop is applied.

3 Experiments & Results

3.1 Datasets & Prepocessing

We use 7 annotated brain MRI databases, each with different MRI modalities and segmentation tasks collected from different sites: ATLAS v2.0 [13] for stroke lesion, BRATS 2016 [2] for tumor, MSSEG [3] for multiple sclerosis, TBI from our site for traumatic brain injury, WMH [10] for White Matter Hyperintensity, ISLES 2015’s SISS subtask [14] for stroke lesion, and Tumor2 for tumour segmentation from our institution. The data is split into training and validation data. Tab. 1 gives an overview of the data sizes and modalities. We resample the images to 1x1x1 mm, skull-strip and apply z-score intensity normalization, and combine all lesion types across databases into a single ’lesion’ label.

3.2 Experimental Setup

Setup & Methods: We use the Residual U-Net [29] as network architecture and Adam optimizer. We linearly scale the learning rate from 0 to 10−3superscript10310^{-3} over 50 communication rounds, keep it constant until communication round 150, and then linearly decay it. We set τ=58𝜏58\tau=58 (equivalent to 1 epoch for the largest database, ATLAS), E=300𝐸300E=300 (or 300 epochs for centralised training), and use a batch size of 8. The Dice score on the validation set is reported after the last epoch. For our federated experiments, both the standard FedAvg algorithm [15] and, separately, FedBN [12] (to keep client-specific BN layers) are used. As discussed in Section 2.2, in addition to BN, our experiments investigate the impact of alternative feature normalization methods that do not rely on client-specific normalization layers: Group Norm (GN) [25], Instance Norm (IN) [23] and the NF [32] technique.

3.3 Results & Discussion

Unified Model - Improves Performance Across Databases: Results in Tab. 2 show that, when keeping client-specific BN parameters, federated joint training across diverse MRI databases with different brain diseases and different input modalities improves the model’s performance on each database, either surpassing or matching single center training. This highlights the benefits of transferring knowledge between different brain diseases, especially for databases with limited data (MSSEG, TBI, and WMH). Training with modality drop and client-specific BN layers leads to the highest overall results and improves over FedAvg using averaged BN parameters, demonstrating the benefits of client specific BN layers for source clients. Furthermore, this joint learning framework produces a single model instead of training 5 individual models, demonstrating its practicality.

Modality Drop Improves Robustness Against Missing Modalities: Our initial step towards developing a model capable of segmenting pathologies with modality combinations different from those seen during training involves robustness against missing modalities. Results in Tab. 3 show the need for modality drop to improve the model’s robustness against missing modalities. When using modality drop during training, the Dice score decreases by only 3.9 when evaluating with missing modalities compared to all modalities, versus a 21.3 decrease without modality drop. Without modality drop, the model relies on having all modalities for a specific disease present during inference. Therefore, the following experiments focus on models trained with modality drop.

Zero-Shot Generalization to Unseen Modality Combinations: Tab. 4 presents generalization results on unseen databases with modality combinations not seen during training. It is remarkable that despite not seeing any of the new client’s data, the best federated model’s average Dice score reaches only 4.1% below the upper bound of single center training on the target database and comes close to centralized joint training (for which only results with IN are shown as it achieved best performance). Traditionally, models are trained on databases specific to a disease and evaluated on unseen clients with the same disease, as we trained with ATLAS (for stroke lesion) and evaluated on ISLES, and trained with BRATS (for brain tumors) and evaluated on Tumor2, setting this as baseline. Both are only trained and evaluated on T1 since it is the only overlapping modality. Federated joint training achieves a performance gain of 49.3 on ISLES and 8.1 on Tumor2, reinforcing the benefits of joint training across multiple MRI databases compared to pathology specific models.

Additionally, modality drop proves essential to dissociate specific modality combinations with certain pathologies, as all jointly trained models with modality drop show improved average performance on segmenting pathologies with modality combinations not seen during training. For Tumor2, there is extensive improvement (both in centralised and federated settings), whereas for ISLES, the federated models performed unexpectedly slightly worse, likely due to gradient stochasticity in federated optimization.

Comparing client-specific BN layers to non-client-specific normalization approaches, the results indicate that the NF technique outperforms all other techniques. Using the average of client-specific BN layers and estimating the running statistics on the validation data gives the second-best results. However, this estimation is less practical as it requires unlabeled data from the unseen client.

Investigating the Effects of Normalization Layers on Source Clients: Tab. 2 shows the effects of feature normalization on the source databases. The findings indicate that keeping client-specific BN layers yields better performance compared to other normalization techniques on the source clients, but pose difficulties when dealing with unseen clients (as shown in Table 4). The NF method outperforms other non client-specific feature normalization layers in both source client and generalization results. Consequently, a trade-off must be made when designing a model for training with this type of heterogeneous data: either improve the source clients’ performance or prioritizing the development of a model that generalizes to unseen clients.

4 Conclusion

This study shows the feasibility, practicality and effectiveness of using FL to train a single model across decentralised brain MRI databases, each with different input modalities and segmentation tasks. Our results show that federated joint training on multiple databases results in a single model that can segment all diseases and sets of modalities seen in training clients as well as client-specific models trained for a specific set of modalities and disease. Furthermore, it shows promising generalization to unseen clients with modality combinations not seen during training, and highlight the importance of the feature normalization method’s choice to address data heterogeneity. We believe this study highlights the benefits of leveraging heterogeneous brain MRI databases for federated training and opens up future research directions in this area.
