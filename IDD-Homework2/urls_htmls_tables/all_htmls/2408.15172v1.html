X-Reflect: Cross-Reflection Prompting for Multimodal Recommendation

Hanjia Lyu1, Ryan Rossi2, Xiang Chen2, Md Mehrab Tanjim2,
Stefano Petrangeli2, Somdeb Sarkhel2, Jiebo Luo1
Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) and Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) have been shown to enhance the effectiveness of enriching item descriptions, thereby improving the accuracy of recommendation systems. However, most existing approaches either rely on text-only prompting or employ basic multimodal strategies that do not fully exploit the complementary information available from both textual and visual modalities. This paper introduces a novel framework, Cross-Reflection Prompting, termed X-Reflect, designed to address these limitations by prompting LMMs to explicitly identify and reconcile supportive and conflicting information between text and images. By capturing nuanced insights from both modalities, this approach generates more comprehensive and contextually richer item representations. Extensive experiments conducted on two widely used benchmarks demonstrate that our method outperforms existing prompting baselines in downstream recommendation accuracy. Additionally, we evaluate the generalizability of our framework across different LMM backbones and the robustness of the prompting strategies, offering insights for optimization. This work underscores the importance of integrating multimodal information and presents a novel solution for improving item understanding in multimodal recommendation systems.

Introduction

Leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) or Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) to enrich item descriptions has been shown to enhance the effectiveness of recommendation systemsÂ (Lyu etÂ al. 2024; Xi etÂ al. 2023; Li etÂ al. 2023). By generating detailed and contextually relevant descriptions, LLMs and LMMs contribute to more accurate and personalized recommendations. However, many existing methods either rely on text-only prompting strategies or use basic multimodal prompting techniques that do not fully utilize the information available from both textual and visual modalitiesÂ (Liu etÂ al. 2024c; BaltruÅ¡aitis, Ahuja, and Morency 2018).

For instance, a movie described textually as a â€œlight-hearted romantic comedyâ€ may have a poster that, with its dark tones and serious expressions, suggests underlying tension. A text-only approach might overlook these visual cues, while a simple multimodal strategy might superficially combine the text and image without resolving their differences, leading to an incomplete or misleading representation of the item. Similarly, a grocery item labeled as â€œorganic and healthyâ€ might have packaging that subtly suggests indulgence, like rich colors or gourmet styling. While neither the text nor the image alone may convey the full character of the product, analyzing them together can highlight these additional dimensions, such as the balance between health and indulgence. These examples illustrate the limitations of current approaches and the need for more sophisticated methods that can effectively integrate and interpret information across modalities.

To recommend items more effectively, a deeper understanding of these items is necessary, which cannot be achieved by neglecting the rich information provided by additional modalities, such as images. Visual content often captures aspects that text alone cannot fully convey, including aesthetic qualities, emotional tone, or specific details that may significantly influence user preferencesÂ (Huang etÂ al. 2019). Therefore, it is critical to develop methods that can seamlessly integrate and reconcile information from both text and images.

To address this need, we introduce a novel Cross-Reflection Prompting framework termed X-Reflect. This method, as illustrated in FigureÂ 1, prompts LMMs to process textual and visual information concurrently, explicitly identifying and reconciling any supportive or conflicting elements between these modalities. By capturing the nuanced information present in both text and images, cross-reflection prompting enables the generation of more diverse and contextually rich item representations.

The contributions of this paper are as follows:

  • â€¢

  • â€¢

  • â€¢

In summary, this work underscores the importance of fully exploiting multimodal information in recommendation systems and presents a novel framework that enhances item understanding through a more integrated analysis of textual and visual data.

Related Work

Multimodal Recommendation System

Traditional unimodal recommendation systems, which depend on a single data source such as textual descriptions, often encounter challenges like data sparsity, cold start problems, noise, and redundancy. To mitigate these issues, researchers have developed multimodal recommendation systems that integrate diverse types of data, including text, images, audio, and video. These systems enable a more comprehensive understanding of content, as different modalities can offer varied perspectives and insights. Moreover, when one modality suffers from poor quality or sparse data, other modalities can compensate, enhancing the systemâ€™s overall robustness and reliabilityÂ (Wei etÂ al. 2019; Lyu and Luo 2022; Wang, Chen, and Zhu 2021; Huang etÂ al. 2019; Tao etÂ al. 2020).

Our method aligns with the goals of multimodal recommendation systems by also leveraging multiple data modalities, specifically text and images, to enrich item descriptions. However, unlike traditional multimodal approaches that may focus on designing advanced neural architectures for integrating data from different modalities, our framework introduces a novel cross-reflection prompting strategy to augment the input data.

Prompting Strategies for Recommendation

In recent years, text-only prompting strategies have gained attention for their effectiveness in recommendation systems. For example, Xi etÂ al. (2023) introduced a method that leverages the reasoning and factual knowledge of LLMs to enhance item recommendations. Lyu etÂ al. (2024) proposed four distinct prompting strategies designed to augment item descriptions using LLMs. While these methods have shown promise, they primarily focus on textual data and do not fully explore the potential of integrating visual information.

There has been limited exploration of multimodal prompting strategies in the context of recommendation. One notable exception is Rec-GPT4VÂ (Liu etÂ al. 2024c), which introduced a reasoning scheme called Visual-Summary Thought (VST). This method leverages large vision-language models (LVLMs) for multimodal recommendation by using user history as in-context user preferences and generating item image summaries to enhance the recommendation process.

Our work builds upon these existing prompting strategies. While Rec-GPT4V focuses on summarizing visual information to aid in recommendations, our cross-reflection prompting framework goes a step further by explicitly examining the relationship between textual and visual data. By doing so, our method captures nuanced information between modalities, leading to more informed and contextually rich item representations that significantly improve recommendation performance.

Cross-Reflection Prompting

In many cases, the text and image associated with a product may be consistent with each other and do not exhibit obvious contradictions, but subtle differences and complementary details can emerge when they are examined together.

This paper aims to design a framework that can more effectively leverage Large Multimodal Models to enrich item descriptions by fully exploiting information from both textual and visual modalities. To achieve this, we propose a novel approach called Cross-Reflection Prompting, referred to as X-Reflect.

The core idea of cross-reflection prompting is to provide LMMs with both textual and visual information as context, while explicitly instructing the models to identify and reconcile whether the textual and visual information supports or contradicts each other. The responses generated by the LMMs are then converted into embeddings, which serve as input item embeddings for the subsequent recommendation module, as illustrated in FigureÂ 2.

We present two distinct strategies for implementing cross-reflection prompting: separate prompting and combined prompting.

Experiment Setup

Datasets

We use two widely recognized recommendation benchmarks: MovieLens-1MÂ (Harper and Konstan 2015) and Amazon-SoftwareÂ (Ni, Li, and McAuley 2019). The dataset statistics are provided in TableÂ 1.

  • â€¢

    MovieLens-1M: This dataset contains anonymous user ratings collected from MovieLens. The movie poster URLs are obtained from the movielens-poster repository.111https://github.com/babu-thomas/movielens-posters

  • â€¢

    Amazon-Software: The original dataset includes product reviews and metadata from Amazon between May 1996 and October 2018. The product metadata comprises details such as color, size, and package type, along with product images captured after users received the products. Additional metadata includes bullet-point descriptions under the product title, technical details, and tables of similar products. For our experiments, we focus on the Software category within the Amazon dataset.

Following the approach of He etÂ al. (2017), we convert the rating data into implicit feedback, indicating whether a user has interacted with a corresponding item. In the Amazon-Software dataset, the original item descriptions are provided. However, the original MovieLens-1M data only include movie titles and genres. Therefore, we use the item descriptions for movies generated by GPT-3 from Lyu etÂ al. (2024). For both datasets, we retain users who have rated at least five items and items that have been rated by at least five unique users.

Evaluation Protocols

We follow the evaluation methodology outlined by Wei etÂ al. (2019). For MovieLens-1M, we split user interactions into training, validation, and test sets using an 8:1:1 ratio, while for Amazon-Software, we use a 6:2:2 ratio to ensure at least one interaction per user in the validation and test sets. Negative training samples are created by pairing users with items without recorded interactions (i.e., pseudo-negative samples). For validation and test sets, we pair each observed user-item interaction with 1,000 items the user has not interacted with. There is no overlap between negative samples in the training set and unobserved user-item pairs in the validation and test sets, ensuring the independence of evaluation data. We evaluate top-K recommendations using metrics such as Precision@K, Recall@K, and NDCG@K, where Kğ¾Kitalic_K is set to 10. The reported results are averaged across five different test set splits.

For fair comparison, we use the same language model across all prompting strategies. For X-Reflect and other multimodal prompting baselines, we use GPT-4VÂ (OpenAI 2023) (gpt-4-vision-preview variant). To test the effectiveness and generalizability of our framework, we also evaluate it using two open-source LMMs: LLaVA-1.5Â (Liu etÂ al. 2024a) (llava-1.5-7b-hf), an enhanced version of LLaVaÂ (Liu etÂ al. 2024b), and InternLM-XComposer2Â (Dong etÂ al. 2024) (internlm-xcomposer2-vl-7b), a vision-language large model based on InternLM2Â (Cai etÂ al. 2024). Implementation Details

Recommendation Module

Following Lyu etÂ al. (2024), we evaluate different prompting baselines using a two-tower modelÂ (Wang, Zhu, and He 2021), designed to predict the likelihood of a user interacting with an item. The model consists of two towers that process user and item features into dense embeddings. The interaction likelihood is computed using the dot product of these embeddings. We use Sentence-BERTÂ (Reimers and Gurevych 2019) to generate textual embeddings from responses, specifically using the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model variant. These item embeddings serve as input to the item tower. The text encoder remains frozen to minimize computational costs. For the user tower, we employ an embedding table to convert user IDs into latent representations, with the output dimension set to 128 for both datasets.

Results

Comparison with Baseline Prompting Strategies

TableÂ 2 presents the average recommendation performance of X-Reflect compared to various baseline approaches. Several key observations can be made:

First, prompting methods that integrate both text and image generally outperform those relying solely on text. However, on the Amazon-Software dataset, the text-only prompting strategy LLM-RecÂ (Lyu etÂ al. 2024) shows notably strong performance. The specific LLM-Rec variant implemented in this work involves instructing the LLM to describe the item with a focus on recommendation purposes. Upon closer examination, it appears that the original Amazon-Software descriptions, which contain highly technical details, may not generalize well. The responses generated through LLM-Rec, which tend to be less technical, likely contribute to its superior performance.

Second, among the prompting methods that combine text and image, those incorporating cross-reflection consistently outperform those that do not take cross-reflection into account.

Third, similar to the findings in LLM-Rec, our method, which leverages augmented information from the cross-reflection prompting components, achieves superior performance compared to all other baselines.

Top-1 Recommendation Performance

TableÂ 3 displays the recommendation performance for Top-1 recommendations, measured by Precision@1 and Recall@1. These metrics assess the quality of recommendations when only one item is suggested. Since only one item is recommended, NDCG@1 simplifies to Precision@1. X-Reflect demonstrates strong performance in Top-1 recommendations, achieving the highest Precision@1 and Recall@1 on the Amazon-Software dataset, and the highest Recall@1 with the second-highest Precision@1 on MovieLens-1M. KARÂ (Xi etÂ al. 2023) achieves the highest Precision@1 on MovieLens-1M, likely because it uses open-world knowledge to enrich item descriptions. For movies, unique characteristics that are better represented as knowledge may allow KAR to precisely augment special information, making it particularly effective in Top-1 recommendations. However, as seen in the Top-10 recommendation results, KARâ€™s performance diminishes, potentially due to its limited ability to enrich general information effectively.

Generalizability of the Proposed Framework

To assess the generalizability of our framework, we replace the GPT-4V backbone with two other widely used LMMsâ€“InternLM and LLaVAâ€“while keeping the experimental settings consistent across all baselines. TablesÂ 5 and 4 show the corresponding recommendation performance. With LLaVA as the backbone, X-Reflect continues to outperform other prompting baselines. When InternLM is used as the backbone, our method remains one of the top-performing frameworks, achieving the highest Precision@10 across both datasets and excelling on the Amazon-Software dataset.

Further Discussion on Augmented Content

To better understand the impact of cross-reflection, we compute the cosine similarity between the embeddings of the original text description and the image description, as well as the responses generated through text-only prompting (LLM-Rec), multimodal prompting (CoT), and cross-reflection (separate) multimodal prompting.

For each item in the MovieLens-1M dataset, we convert its text description and image description into embeddings, defining the cosine similarity between these two embeddings as reference similarity. A higher value indicates greater semantic similarity between the text and image descriptions, serving as a proxy for how much additional information the image description contains compared to the original text description.

Similarly, we compute the cosine similarity between the text description embeddings and the responses generated through text-only prompting, multimodal prompting, and cross-reflection multimodal prompting. We then compare these cosine similarities with the reference similarity. The results are presented in FigureÂ 3.

In each subplot, the x-axis represents the cosine similarity between the embeddings of the text and image descriptions, while the y-axis shows the cosine similarity between the embeddings of the text description and the responses generated through the other three prompting baselines.

The key finding is that cross-reflection multimodal prompting generates semantically more diverse information compared to other prompting strategies and image descriptions. This aligns with our earlier discussion, where we note that visual information from item images contains additional details not present in the original text description. However, directly prompting LMMs to describe an image may not elicit a comprehensive understanding of the item.

Even combining text and image in the prompt is not sufficient; explicit instruction is necessary to direct LMMs to focus on non-overlapping information across modalities. Although not identical, a similar pattern has been observed in multi-input image promptingÂ (Zhang etÂ al. 2024), where explicit instructions to focus on similarities and differences among multiple image inputs improve multimodal reasoning in LMMs.

It is important to note that the utility of augmented information in downstream recommendation tasks is still under investigation. While this experiment demonstrates that semanticallt more diverse information often leads to better performance, this is not an absolute rule. As seen in the experiments, generating less granular responses can sometimes also enhance performance in downstream tasks. This remains an ongoing area of research in the recommendation domain, and we plan to explore this further in future work.
