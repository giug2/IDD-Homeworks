Abstract.

Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown significant potential in enhancing recommender systems. However, addressing the cold-start recommendation problem, where users lack historical data, remains a considerable challenge. In this paper, we introduce KALM4Rec (Keyword-driven Retrieval-Augmented Large Language Models for Cold-start User Recommendations), a novel framework specifically designed to tackle this problem by requiring only a few input keywords from users in a practical scenario of cold-start user recommendations. KALM4Rec operates in two main stages: candidates retrieval and LLM-based candidates re-ranking. In the first stage, keyword-driven retrieval models are used to identify potential candidates, addressing LLMs‚Äô limitations in processing extensive tokens and reducing the risk of generating misleading information. In the second stage, we employ LLMs with various prompting strategies, including zero-shot and few-shot techniques, to re-rank these candidates by integrating multiple examples directly into the LLM prompts. Our extensive evaluation, using Yelp restaurant data from three English-speaking cities and TripAdvisor hotel data, demonstrates that KALM4Rec excels in improving recommendation quality across the two domains. The framework‚Äôs adaptability to different domains highlights its potential for widespread applications. By integrating in-context instructions with LLMs, KALM4Rec notably enhances the performance of cold-start recommender systems, offering a novel approach to exploring solutions in this field. 1. Introduction

Recommender systems play a pivotal role in assisting users in navigating the vast number of available choices in the digital world. However, a significant and ongoing challenge in this field is addressing the issue of cold-start users. These are users who are new to the platform and therefore have little to no interaction history. The lack of data makes it difficult for the system to generate accurate and personalized recommendations. Conventional Collaborative Filtering (CF) such as NCF(He et¬†al., 2017), BPR (Rendle et¬†al., 2009)and LightGCN (He et¬†al., 2020) or generative methods (Liang et¬†al., 2018; Wang et¬†al., 2023c; Liu et¬†al., 2023b) struggle to suggest relevant items effectively for these new users due to the lack of detailed preference information. While user-user content-based algorithms offer a solution by relying on user features to find similar users and recommend positively interacted items, this approach raises privacy concerns. Overall, there remains a significant gap in research addressing the needs of cold-start users, highlighting the necessity for further exploration in this area.

Meanwhile, Large Language Models (LLMs) have garnered considerable interest for their potential to enhance recommender systems, showcasing significant proficiency in natural language understanding, generation, and complex reasoning. Leveraging their robust architecture and extensive training on vast datasets, several studies employ LLMs to comprehend and address user requirements throughout the recommendation process (Gao et¬†al., 2023; Wang et¬†al., 2023b; Li et¬†al., 2024; Wu et¬†al., 2023). Moreover, researchers have explored LLMs‚Äô potential to alleviate the cold-start recommendation problem through in-context learning (He et¬†al., 2023). With LLMs‚Äô adaptability to new tasks, prompts are often tailored to incorporate user and item information, facilitating effective adaptation and performance enhancement (Sanner et¬†al., 2023; Wang et¬†al., 2024b).

Challenge. Despite their excellent capacities, existing LLMs suffer from several limitations when applied to recommender systems, especially for cold-start scenarios. For one, lacking comprehensive knowledge in a specific domain may result in non-factual outputs. While fine-tuning smaller LLMs could potentially reduce such hallucinations, this solution is often impractical due to the significant resources required (Mialon et¬†al., 2023). Furthermore, directly incorporating user/item information can be costly in terms of token usage and restricts input length due to limited context length.

Approach. For recommender systems in the various domains such as restaurant, user reviews offer valuable insights into their opinions about businesses. Modeling these personal reviews is beneficial for comprehending users‚Äô preferences and capturing the characteristics of restaurants (Vu et¬†al., 2020). For cold-start users who lack historical data, a practical strategy is to ask them to provide a few keywords describing their preferences. This approach helps create an initial user profile with minimal effort and without compromising their privacy. Furthermore, using keywords instead of entire reviews to prompt LLMs can significantly enhance the efficiency and accuracy of the recommendations while minimizing token usage and computational costs.

To this end, we present a novel framework called Keyword-Driven Retrieval-Augmented Large Language Models for Cold-Start User Recommendations (KALM4REC). Our framework is built upon two essential components centered around keywords: candidates retrieval focusing on retrieving relevant items, and LLM-ranker which leverages LLM to re-rank the retrieved candidates (illustrated in Figure 1). User reviews reflect user preferences but often contain noise, potentially leading to inaccurate representations of user preferences. By exclusively leveraging extracted keywords, we retain the contextual essence of the reviews while still describing the user profile well. Utilizing keywords also allows us to optimize the prompts by reducing noise, thereby enhancing their effectiveness while still ensuring sufficient information of users and restaurants in LLMs, effectively addressing cold-start issues. Essentially, KALM4REC, grounded in sets of keywords, aims to narrow down the candidate set relevant to predefined cold-start preferences. The LLM utilizes its contextual understanding and reasoning capabilities to generate a ranked list of items for recommendations. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

  • ‚Ä¢

    To the best of our knowledge, KALM4REC stands as the pioneering framework to employ keyword as the focal point of both the candidate retrieval and LLM-based re-ranking modules in recommender system. By prioritizing keywords, our augmented LLMs framework effectively addresses the significant challenge of hallucinated outcome and optimizing prompt design, resulting in promising performance for cold-start users recommendation.

  • ‚Ä¢

    We propose approaches that take advantages of the extracted keywords to obtain most suitable candidates for cold-start users. Furthermore, we designed prompting templates to enable LLMs work efficiently for re-ranking tasks.

  • ‚Ä¢

    Finally, we conduct comprehensive experiments to validate and demonstrate the effectiveness of KALM4REC, compared to previous baseline methods for the cold-start user recommendation challenge, using two different domains including restaurant (Yelp) and hotel (Tripadvisor).

2. Related works

Cold-start Recommendations. A recommendation system that aims to provide suggestions to new users with little or no historical data is known as a cold-start recommendation system. These systems play a critical role in businesses and services that require recommendations for new users or products. Various strategies have been proposed to tackle the challenge of generating accurate recommendations in such scenarios (Guo et¬†al., 2020; Bianchi et¬†al., 2017; Gao et¬†al., 2018; Camacho and Alves-Souza, 2018). Collaborative filtering (CF) (He et¬†al., 2017; Wang et¬†al., 2019a) has traditionally been effective in recommendation systems but struggles with the cold-start problem. To address this challenge, some approaches have integrated data augmentation techniques into CF models, incorporating user and item information to improve recommendations (Bianchi et¬†al., 2017; Gao et¬†al., 2018). While methods such as (Wei et¬†al., 2020; Anwar et¬†al., 2022) extend CF techniques, others like (Son and Kim, 2017) combine collaborative filtering with content-based methods to mitigate the cold-start issue. Deep learning techniques have also been employed to create representations or embeddings that capture the latent features of users and items, aiding in cold-start scenarios (Ying et¬†al., 2018; Volkovs et¬†al., 2017; Zhao et¬†al., 2022; Wang et¬†al., 2019b). DropoutNet (Volkovs et¬†al., 2017) directly addresses cold-start issues by avoiding the need to aggregate all content-based information into the objective function. (Zhao et¬†al., 2022) employs latent variables to develop a probabilistic approach for learning distributions over side information, helping to ‚Äùwarm up‚Äù cold item embeddings. Additionally, graph-based techniques (Ying et¬†al., 2018) leverage user-item interaction graphs to infer user preferences, while (Wang et¬†al., 2019b) uses knowledge graphs as a source of side information to support recommendations. Some methods generate warm embeddings for cold-start items based on their features and ID embeddings, utilizing meta-networks (Zhu et¬†al., 2021). Moreover, (Zhou et¬†al., 2023) explores contrastive learning with CF-based modules to create content-based collaborative embeddings using information from warm-start items to address the cold-start issue. Although these methods show improvements in cold-start problems, they rely heavily on auxiliary information which usually causes privacy concerns.

LLMs for Recommendations.

The impressive capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in natural language understanding, generation, and complex reasoning have inspired innovative approaches in recommendation systems (Fan et¬†al., 2023; Geng et¬†al., 2022; Wang et¬†al., 2024a, 2023b; Li et¬†al., 2024; Wu et¬†al., 2023; Dai et¬†al., 2023; Wang et¬†al., 2023a). Among these, the ‚ÄùPretrain, Personalized Prompt, and Predict Paradigm‚Äù (P5) (Geng et¬†al., 2022; Xu et¬†al., 2024) stands out as a groundbreaking approach, harnessing the inherent flexibility of the language to tackle diverse recommendation tasks with remarkable success. Rather than fine-tuning models (Lyu et¬†al., 2024; Geng et¬†al., 2022; Cui et¬†al., 2022), some methods use LLMs in a zero-shot setting to recommend items directly, demonstrating promising results (Liu et¬†al., 2023a; Zhang et¬†al., 2023; Kang et¬†al., 2023; Wang and Lim, 2023; Sun et¬†al., 2024). To address the issue of hallucinations when being used for direct recommend ations, LLMs can be employed as re-rankers within recommendation systems(Hou et¬†al., 2024; Dai et¬†al., 2023). For example, Dai et¬†al. (2023) found that GPT could outperform weak baselines when re-ranking a list of candidate items. A notable example of LLMs‚Äô potential is highlighted by Hou et¬†al. (2024), which used LLMs as zero-shot rankers. They transformed the recommendation process into a conditional ranking challenge based on sequential interaction histories and carefully crafted prompt templates. This approach not only demonstrates LLMs‚Äô ability to deliver contextually accurate recommendations but also indicate that by enhancing retrieval modules, LLMs can provide a richer and more responsive user experience. Furthermore, Sanner et¬†al. (2023); Wang et¬†al. (2024b) explore the integration of user and item information into prompts to overcome cold-start issues, while He et¬†al. (2023) shows that in-context learning with LLMs can effectively address these challenges without needing extra data. Despite these advances, there remains a significant gap: no research has yet investigated the combination of keyword extraction with LLMs to boost recommendations for cold-start users. Our study aims to bridge this gap by introducing a novel approach that blends keyword extraction with LLMs to enhance recommendation accuracy in cold-start scenarios.

3. Proposed Framework

In this section, we present the details of our keyword-driven framework, KALM4REC (Figure 2), designed to tackle the cold-start recommendation problem. KALM4REC unfolds in two primary stages: (1) Candidates retrieval, where meaningful noun phrases are extracted from collected reviews to form word sets representing users and targeted items, and subsequently utilized for queries from cold-start users to obtain potential candidates. This phase employs our retrieval models, leveraging a collaborative filtering mechanism with keywords at its core to rank potential candidates; and (2) LLM-based candidates re-ranking, where an LLM is employed as a ranker to re-rank the retrieved candidates.

3.2. Candidates Retrieval

In this stage, we present methodologies for retrieving potential candidates that include Content-based Recommendation by Keywords (CBR) and Message Passing on Graph (MPG). For CBR, we utilize deep neural networks to learn the interaction function from sets of keywords representing users and items. To accommodate the arbitrary order of keywords within sets, we adopt a pooling layer based on an attention mechanism. For MPG, we construct a heterogeneous graph comprising nodes representing keywords and items, with two types of edges defined, and introduce a scheme for estimating scores of the connections between keywords and items.

4.1. Experimental Settings

Dataset. The experiments utilize two datasets: the Yelp.com dataset (Vu et¬†al., 2020) and the TripAdvisor dataset (Li et¬†al., 2013). The Yelp.com dataset features over 67,000 restaurant reviews across three English-speaking cities. The TripAdvisor dataset consists of 878,561 reviews from 4333 hotels crawled from TripAdvisor.com across 17 cities. The dataset is partitioned into training and testing sets. Since we deal with the problem of cold-start user recommendation, we split the dataset so that users in the test set do not have any reviews in the train set. Dataset statistics are provided in Table 1.

Keyword processing. We use Spacy222https://spacy.io/ to extract keywords from reviews. Specifically, we keep consecutive words having targeted part-of-speech tags as keywords. The targeted part-of-speech tags include ‚ÄòADJ‚Äô, ‚ÄòNOUN‚Äô, ‚ÄòPROPN‚Äô, and ‚ÄòVERB‚Äô . These keywords encompass different word categories, describing the aspects mentioned in reviews. RQ4. Do LLMs suffer from biases while ranking? We adopt the insights from (Hou et¬†al., 2024) that suggest several factors can affect the ranking performance of LLMs. To explore potential biases in LLM ranking, specifically on Gemini Pro, we conducted experiments focusing on various aspects. First, we examined how the order of keywords in the prompt affects performance. We shuffled the keywords for each testing user and reported the average performance of re-ranking. As shown in Figure 7a, the performance of re-ranking with shuffled keywords varies, indicating that keyword order influences LLMs. Notably, performance declines when keywords are not sorted by importance, suggesting that LLMs better understand user preferences when keywords are arranged according to their relevance. This effect is especially pronounced in Edinburgh, where performance with unordered keywords is even worse than with MPG.

Additionally, we investigated the impact of candidate order on LLMs. The results, shown in Figure 7b, reveal that Gemini Pro is sensitive to the order of candidates in the prompt. Shuffling the candidate order leads to a significant drop in baseline performance at P@3. However, LLMs‚Äô re-ranking performance shows notable improvement. This suggests that even if the list of candidates is shuffled, LLMs can still recommend suitable items if ground truth items are presented. While the re-ranking performance with shuffled candidates is relatively stable, it remains lower compared to re-ranked MPG. This indicates that integrating efficient retrieval models with LLMs enhances overall performance, as the order of candidates by retrieval positively influences the results.

Next, we assessed LLM performance under the assumption that LLMs can recommend suitable items even if ground truth items are positioned in less favorable locations within the candidate list. We replaced all ground truth items in the candidate list at positions 5, 10, 15, and at the end, ensuring that each list contained no more than four ground truth items to avoid overlap. Results shown in Figure 7c indicate that LLMs are effective at identifying ground truth items even in challenging scenarios, demonstrating relatively stable performance across different settings. However, with the order provided by the retrieval module, KALM4REC achieved the best results highlighting the efficiency of our framework.

Finally, we explore how item popularity affects LLM recommendations. While item popularity cannot be measured directly, we use the normalized interaction count from the training set to represent it. Figure 7d illustrates the relationship between the average popularity score of items and their position in the re-ranked list (e.g., positions 0, 4, 9, 14). We find that highly popular items are often recommended at the top of the re-ranked list, whereas items with lower popularity tend to appear in lower positions. This suggests that popular items have a higher chance of being ranked favorably.

5. Discussion and Conclusion

In this work, we investigate the idea of augmenting LLMs for cold-start user recommendations with keywords extracted from user reviews. For retrieving potential candidates, we present CBR and MPG, keyword-based methods, which outperform conventional approaches. We then employ LLMs to re-rank the obtained candidates using designed prompting strategies that incorporate keywords to represent users and items. Comprehensive experiments indicate that KALM4REC is capable of handling cold-start user scenarios effectively. Additionally, our framework shows the potential of integrating different retrieval and language models to achieve promising performance in the future for multiple domains. Several factors may affect the performance of our framework. For instance, while the constructed graph in MPG is effective, the connections between keyword nodes should also be considered, as similar keywords may need to pass information to each other before reaching the restaurant nodes. However, implementing this could be computationally costly when the number of keywords is large. Additionally, the number of keywords introduced into the prompt may be reduced by increasing the number of example introductions, as shown in the experiments. For future work, we plan to develop technical approaches to enhance the robustness of each stage in our framework, leveraging the insights drawn from our comprehensive set of experiments.
