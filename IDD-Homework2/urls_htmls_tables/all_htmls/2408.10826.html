NeuLite: Memory-Efficient Federated Learning via Elastic Progressive Training

Yebo Wu, Li Li, Chunlin Tian, Dubing Chen, Chengzhong Xu Corresponding author.
Abstract

Federated Learning (FL) emerges as a new learning paradigm that enables multiple devices to collaboratively train a shared model while preserving data privacy. However, intensive memory footprint during the training process severely bottlenecks the deployment of FL on resource-constrained devices in real-world cases. In this paper, we propose NeuLite, a framework that breaks the memory wall through elastic progressive training. Unlike traditional FL, which updates the full model during the whole training procedure, NeuLite divides the model into blocks and conducts the training process in a progressive manner. Except for the progressive training paradigm, NeuLite further features the following two key components to guide the training process: 1) curriculum mentor and 2) training harmonizer. Specifically, the Curriculum Mentor devises curriculum-aware training losses for each block, assisting them in learning the expected feature representation and mitigating the loss of valuable information. Additionally, the Training Harmonizer develops a parameter co-adaptation training paradigm to break the information isolation across blocks from both forward and backward propagation. Furthermore, it constructs output modules for each block to strengthen model parameter co-adaptation. Extensive experiments are conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of NeuLite across both simulation and hardware testbeds. The results demonstrate that NeuLite effectively reduces peak memory usage by up to 50.4%. It also enhances model performance by up to 84.2% and accelerates the training process by up to 1.9×\times.

Introduction

Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al. 2017) enables multiple devices to collaboratively train a shared model while preserving data privacy. Instead of gathering the raw data and conducting centralized training on the cloud, FL directly conducts local training on the participating devices and then aggregates the local updates (Tian et al. 2022; Wang et al. 2023; Li et al. 2022; Fu et al. 2022). One fundamental and prevailing challenge that hinders the deployment of FL in real-world cases is the memory wall. During the training process, all the intermediate activations, model weights, and optimizer status are mandated to be retained in the memory, which causes an intensive memory footprint. For instance, training a MobileNet_v2 on ImageNet with a batch size of 128 consumes 13.5 GB of memory. However, the available RAM for off-the-shelf mobile devices only ranges from 4 to 16 GB (Wu et al. 2024). Moreover, to retrieve high analysis capability, the model architectures are becoming deeper and wider, further increasing the memory footprint. This means that, due to memory limitations, many low-end devices cannot even afford to support the local training process, and the private data on them cannot benefit the convergence of the global model well.

Recently, several approaches have been proposed to resolve the resource limitation for FL, which can be mainly divided into the following two categories: 1) model-heterogeneous training  (Cho et al. 2022; Zhang, Wu, and Yuan 2022) and 2) partial training (Wang et al. 2024; Yang et al. 2022). Model-heterogeneous training customizes local models according to the memory budget of each participating device and aggregates different local models through knowledge distillation. However, the distillation process requires a high-quality public dataset, which is usually impractical for FL due to privacy concerns. Partial training tailors the global model through width scaling or depth scaling based on the resource constraints of the participating devices and then allocates the sub-models accordingly. Specifically, width scaling (Diao, Ding, and Tarokh 2020) yields sub-models of varying complexity by adjusting the number of channels in the convolutional layers. However, this strategy can compromise the model architecture, leading to performance degradation. While depth scaling (Kim et al. 2022) customizes sub-models with varying depths to address memory constraints. Meanwhile, this mechanism limits the complexity of the global model that can be trained.

To this end, we propose NeuLite, a novel framework that effectively breaks the memory wall for FL via elastic progressive training. The design of NeuLite is guided by the observation that different layers of the global model exhibit varying convergence rates. The initial layers stabilize quickly as they are responsible for extracting generic features, while the deep layers take more training rounds to retrieve task-specific features. Thus, updating the full model throughout training leads to unnecessarily high memory usage. Based on this observation, NeuLite first divides the global model into multiple blocks corresponding to different training stages, with each block containing a specified number of layers. It first trains the first block, safely freezes it after convergence, and then triggers the training of the next one. This process iterates until the full model is effectively trained. In this way, the memory space required for storing intermediate activations and gradients of the frozen layers can be well released. Thus, more devices can meet the memory requirement and contribute to the global model with their private data. However, designing such a progressive training paradigm faces the following two critical challenges guiding the design of the corresponding core approaches.

•Challenge 1: Information Loss in Each Stage. Training each block individually, freezing them after convergence, and then triggering the training of the next one can deteriorate model performance. This is because each block, due to its limited fitting capacity and shortsightedness, tends to learn the features most beneficial to its current stage, leading to the loss of valuable information. This issue is especially concerning in the earlier blocks, which typically have the lowest complexity and are thus less capable of maintaining critical information. As a result, the subsequent blocks experience accuracy stagnation due to the incomplete information provided by these earlier blocks. This cumulative information loss hampers the overall model performance. To address this challenge, we introduce the Curriculum Mentor, which designs curriculum-aware training losses for each block based on the information bottleneck theory, mitigating the valuable information loss during each training stage.

•Challenge 2: Information Isolation Across Blocks. Despite the effectiveness of the Curriculum Mentor in mitigating information loss, this training paradigm still faces the challenge of information isolation across blocks. This issue arises because, on the one hand, later blocks initiate training only after the preceding blocks are frozen, preventing them from adjusting to the evolving features and updates of earlier blocks. On the other hand, since each block is updated independently, there is a lack of gradient information flow between blocks, leading to fragmented learning and misaligned gradients. To address this, we introduce the Training Harmonizer, which establishes a parameter co-adaptation training paradigm for each block to break information isolation. Specifically, it involves performing model growth in each round to empower later blocks to capture the update trajectories of preceding blocks, enhancing forward propagation interaction. Additionally, it concurrently trains each block alongside the last few layers of the preceding block to break gradient isolation, thereby improving backward propagation interaction. Furthermore, corresponding output modules are designed for each block to strengthen overall information interaction and parameter co-adaptation.

We conduct extensive experiments using four typical DNN models and one Transformer-like model across five different datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that NeuLite effectively reduces the peak memory footprint by up to 50.4%, improves the model accuracy by up to 84.2%, and simultaneously speeds up the training process up to 1.9×\times. To the best of our knowledge, NeuLite is the first work that extensively explores the efficient implementation of progressive training to tackle memory limitations in FL. The main contributions are summarized as follows:

  • •

    We propose NeuLite, an efficient progressive training framework to address memory limitations in FL while ensuring both model performance and training efficiency.

  • •

    In NeuLite, we first propose a novel progressive training paradigm for FL. After that, we design two core components, the Curriculum Mentor and the Training Harmonizer, to guide the overall training procedure.

  • •

    Extensive experiments on representative models and datasets are conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of NeuLite with both simulation and hardware testbeds.

Related Work

Resource-Constrained FL. Many approaches have been developed to address the memory constraints in FL, primarily categorized into model-heterogeneous training and partial training (Wu et al. 2024; Yebo et al. 2024). Model-heterogeneous training involves deploying different models on the central server and participating devices, using knowledge distillation for model aggregation. For example, FedMD (Li and Wang 2019) enables clients to share logits on a public dataset to facilitate information transfer. However, this strategy typically requires a high-quality public dataset, which is not feasible for FL due to privacy concerns. Partial training involves applying width or depth scaling on the global model to generate models of varying complexity. For width scaling, methods like Federated Dropout (Caldas et al. 2018b) scale the number of channels in the convolutional layers to match device memory capacities. However, this approach compromises the model architecture, leading to performance degradation. For depth scaling, such as in DepthFL (Kim et al. 2022), local models of different depths are tailored according to device memory capacities. However, the complexity of the global model that can be trained is constrained by the device with the maximum memory capacity. In contrast, NeuLite trains the global model in a progressive manner, effectively reducing peak memory usage.

Progressive Training. ProgFed (Wang et al. 2022) is the most closely related work to ours. It divides the model into blocks and then trains the first block. After a fixed interval, the second block is concatenated and trained together with the first. This process iterates until the full model size is reached. However, several key differences distinguish our work from ProgFed: 1) Training Paradigm: Unlike ProgFed, which does not employ block freezing and uses fixed intervals for model growth, NeuLite reduces peak memory usage by intelligently freezing blocks and enhances block interaction by growing the model with each round. 2) Training Loss: NeuLite tailors curriculum-aware training losses for each block to mitigate the loss of valuable information, while ProgFed relies solely on cross-entropy. 3) Training Objective: ProgFed targets reducing communication and computation overhead, while NeuLite is specifically crafted to overcome memory constraints in FL.

Method: NeuLite

The proposed approach, NeuLite, is composed of two components-Curriculum Mentor and Training Harmonizer, as elaborated in the following subsections. Prior to that, we first present the high-level ideas and the progressive training paradigm of NeuLite.

Despite achieving memory reduction, conducting progressive training in a straightforward way faces the following two critical issues that severely degrade the model performance. Challenge 1: Information Loss in Each Stage. When training each block individually, its limited capacity and shortsightedness lead to losing valuable information. How can we tailor different training strategies for each block to mitigate information loss at each stage? Challenge 2: Information Isolation Across Blocks. Training each block individually to convergence before model growth hinders information interaction across blocks in both forward and backward propagation. How can we break information isolation to enhance overall model performance?

Curriculum-Aware Training Loss

For challenge 1, NeuLite introduces the Curriculum Mentor, which develops the curriculum-aware training loss for each block, assisting them in learning the expected feature representation and mitigating valuable information loss during each training stage.

However, designing such a technique is not trivial due to the limited control over which features each block learns or ignores. To gain deeper insights, we leverage the information bottleneck (IB) theory (Tishby, Pereira, and Bialek 2000) to analyze this problem. IB seeks to find an optimal intermediate representation Z𝑍Z of given input X𝑋X and output Y𝑌Y that preserves task-relevant information from the input while eliminating task-irrelevant details. The objective centers on minimizing the following:

I​(X;Z)−β​I​(Y;Z)𝐼𝑋𝑍𝛽𝐼𝑌𝑍I(X;Z)-\beta I(Y;Z) (2)

where β>0𝛽0\beta>0 serves as a hyperparameter that balances the importance of the two components, while I𝐼I denotes mutual information. For an L𝐿L-layer neural network, each layer compresses the data and generates different intermediate representations Z1,Z2,…​ZLsubscript𝑍1subscript𝑍2…subscript𝑍𝐿Z_{1},Z_{2},...Z_{L}. This process can be modeled as a Markov chain (Yu et al. 2023), satisfying the following inequality:

I​(Y;X)≥I​(Y;Z1)≥⋯≥I​(Y;ZL)𝐼𝑌𝑋𝐼𝑌subscript𝑍1⋯𝐼𝑌subscript𝑍𝐿I(Y;X)\geq I(Y;Z_{1})\geq\cdots\geq I(Y;Z_{L}) (3)

As indicated by Eq. (3), with each layer’s data processing, there is a gradual loss of task-relevant information from the input. Therefore, we can incorporate mutual information into the training objective to minimize task-relevant information loss. However, due to the high dimensionality of the activations (Sakamoto and Sato 2024), directly computing mutual information faces the challenge of high computation complexity. Therefore, we employ the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC) bottleneck (Ma, Lewis, and Kleijn 2020) to estimate it. The HSIC (Wang, Dai, and Liu 2021) is a statistical measure that quantifies the dependence between variables. In this context, the HSIC bottleneck estimates feature relevance by maximizing the dependence between the input and learned representations while minimizing the influence of irrelevant factors. Specifically, we utilize n​H​S​I​C​(X;Z)𝑛𝐻𝑆𝐼𝐶𝑋𝑍nHSIC(X;Z) to estimate I​(X;Z)𝐼𝑋𝑍I(X;Z) and n​H​S​I​C​(Y;Z)𝑛𝐻𝑆𝐼𝐶𝑌𝑍nHSIC(Y;Z) to estimate I​(Y;Z)𝐼𝑌𝑍I(Y;Z), where nHSIC is defined by the Hilbert Schmidt norm of the normalized cross-covariance operator.

To illustrate the information loss caused by the progressive training paradigm, we conduct experiments with ResNet18 on CIFAR10. We compute n​H​S​I​C​(X;Z)𝑛𝐻𝑆𝐼𝐶𝑋𝑍nHSIC(X;Z) and n​H​S​I​C​(Y;Z)𝑛𝐻𝑆𝐼𝐶𝑌𝑍nHSIC(Y;Z) for the output of each block under two training paradigms: end-to-end (E2E) and progressive training (PT). A larger n​H​S​I​C​(Y;Z)𝑛𝐻𝑆𝐼𝐶𝑌𝑍nHSIC(Y;Z) indicates that Z𝑍Z contains more critical features about the labels, while a larger n​H​S​I​C​(X;Z)𝑛𝐻𝑆𝐼𝐶𝑋𝑍nHSIC(X;Z) signifies that Z𝑍Z retains more input information. Parameter Co-Adaptation Training Paradigm

For challenge 2, NeuLite designs the Training Harmonizer, which develops a parameter co-adaptation training paradigm to break information isolation across blocks in both forward and backward propagation. Additionally, it customizes output modules for each block to enhance the co-adaptation of model parameters.

In this training paradigm, subsequent blocks commence training only after the earlier blocks have fully converged and been frozen. This sequential training paradigm introduces a delay that hinders later blocks from promptly adjusting to the evolving features and updates of earlier blocks, interrupting the effective flow of information during forward propagation. The delayed interaction leads to sub-optimal feature learning and performance degradation. To address this issue, the Training Harmonizer employs a dynamic strategy, performing model growth after each training round rather than waiting for convergence. It retrains the first block after completing the final block’s training, allowing subsequent blocks to align with the update trajectories of preceding blocks in real time. This strategy fosters cohesive forward propagation and enhances the model’s adaptability, leading to improved performance and robustness.

Moreover, since gradients are confined within each block during updates, this also hinders information interaction during backward propagation, resulting in gradient isolation (Guo et al. 2023). This isolation disrupts the model’s ability to capture dependencies between blocks, leading to fragmented learning and misaligned gradients, which increases the risk of converging to sub-optimal solutions and weakens the model’s overall coherence. To address this issue, the Training Harmonizer concurrently trains the subsequent layers of the previous block alongside the current block to break gradient isolation, as shown in Fig. 4. We denote these layers of block t𝑡t for client n𝑛n, which are employed to break the gradient isolation, as Ln,tbsubscript𝐿𝑛subscript𝑡𝑏L_{n,t_{b}}. This strategy allows for better synchronization and communication between blocks during backward propagation, thus improving overall model performance.

Although these two aspects achieve some effectiveness, their gains come from later blocks continuously correcting the training bias of preceding blocks. Specifically, the earlier blocks update based on their perspective, ignoring the existence of later blocks. This short-sighted behavior leads to biased update directions. The later blocks then correct this bias, akin to “better late than never”. Based on this observation, we address the issue by constructing output modules for each block. To minimize the additional overhead, we replace each subsequent block with a basic layer, such as a convolutional layer, to form the output module. These output modules enable earlier blocks to recognize the existence of subsequent blocks, akin to “having an anchor to windward.” For example, in Fig. 4, when training the first block, we replace the following three blocks with three basic layers and assemble them with a fully connected layer into the output module θO​psubscript𝜃𝑂𝑝\theta_{Op}. Evaluation

Experiment Setup

Models and Datasets. We evaluate the effectiveness of NeuLite using four conventional CNN models commonly deployed in mobile devices: ResNet18, ResNet34, VGG11_bn, and SqueezeNet, as well as the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al. 2020). Additionally, we conduct evaluations under both IID and Non-IID scenarios across five representative datasets: CIFAR10, CINIC10, CIFAR100, Mini-ImageNet (Vinyals et al. 2016), and the large-scale dataset FEMNIST (Caldas et al. 2018a). The Non-IID setup is established based on Dirichlet distribution, with a concentration parameter set to α=1𝛼1\alpha=1.

Default Settings. We perform experiments utilizing a combination of simulation and hardware testbeds to evaluate the effectiveness of NeuLite. We establish an on-device FL system with 100 diverse mobile devices. In each training round, the server randomly selects 10% devices based on their available memory to participate in the training process. During the local training process, SGD is employed as the optimizer with a weight decay of 5e-4, and the local epoch is set to 5. To ensure fair comparisons, we randomly allocate available memory to each device according to the profile results of hardware configurations.

Baselines. The following baselines are adopted for evaluation purposes: 1) AllSmall (Wu et al. 2024) serves as a naive baseline, scaling the channels of the global model based on the minimum device memory to obtain a simple global model, thereby involving devices with varying memory capacities. 2) ExclusiveFL (Wu et al. 2024) exclusively permits devices with sufficient memory for training the global model to engage in the training process. 3) DepthFL (Kim et al. 2022) conducts depth scaling on the global model and assigns sub-models of varying depths to devices depending on their memory capacity. 4) HeteroFL (Diao, Ding, and Tarokh 2020) accommodates different memory constraints by statically scaling the number of channels in convolutional layers. 5) FedRolex (Alam et al. 2022) dynamically performs width scaling on the global model through a sliding window. 6) TiFL (Chai et al. 2020) categorizes devices by their training duration, selecting devices from specific tiers for each round. 7) Oort (Lai et al. 2021) enhances time-to-accuracy by considering both data and system heterogeneity.

End-to-End Evaluation

We evaluate the effectiveness of NeuLite across four primary dimensions, i.e., overall performance, performance across training tasks of varying complexities, compatibility with large-scale datasets and Transformer-based models.

Overall Performance.

Table 1 presents benchmark evaluation results of NeuLite across three end-to-end training models on different datasets under Non-IID settings, demonstrating NeuLite’s superior performance across diverse experimental setups. For the CIFAR10 dataset, NeuLite exhibits accuracy enhancements of 10.9%, 12.5%, and 8.4% across the three models compared to AllSmall. This disparity arises because the global model of AllSmall is determined by the device with minimal memory capacity, resulting in limited feature extraction capability. In comparison to ExclusiveFL, which restricts participation to high-memory devices, resulting in a participation rate of only 11% to 22%, NeuLite demonstrates improvements of 3.6%, 8.3%, and 17.4%, respectively, by effectively leveraging data from low-memory devices. NeuLite outperforms DepthFL with improvements of 15.3%, 12.6%, and 8.3% on the three models. This outcome arises partly because of DepthFL’s ineffective utilization of data from low-memory devices, evidenced by participation rates of 43%, 43%, and 39% across three models, and the imbalance in parameter training caused by only a subset of devices training the later blocks of the global model. While width scaling methods, such as HeteroFL and FedRolex, achieve a 100% participation rate, they compromise the model architecture, especially for models like SqueezeNet with fewer channels. Thus, compared to these methods, NeuLite achieves performance gains ranging from 3.7% to 41.2%. Moreover, both TiFL and Oort, with participation rates of only 11% to 22%, experience accuracy reductions ranging from 4.5% to 16.2%.

NeuLite consistently outperforms its counterparts when examining the CINIC10 dataset, with performance improvements ranging from 3.1% to 41.1%. It also excels on the more challenging CIFAR100 dataset, enhancing accuracy by 7.7% to 33.8%. These significant performance gains can be attributed to two critical factors: 1) NeuLite is an inclusive framework that effectively leverages data from all devices. 2) NeuLite incorporates two core components, enabling the effective execution of the progressive training paradigm.

Tasks of Varying Complexities.

We employ the ResNet series models to assess the robustness of NeuLite across training tasks of different complexities. Table 2 compares the performance of these strategies on ResNet18 and ResNet34 training tasks. NeuLite demonstrates outstanding performance across both tasks. As the complexity of the training task increases, training ResNet34 results in a high memory footprint. The memory wall leads to methods like ExclusiveFL, TiFL, and Oort being unable to function in such scenarios because of no device with enough memory to train the full model. In contrast, NeuLite effectively mitigates peak memory usage by strategically partitioning the model into different blocks and training them progressively, improving accuracy by up to 84.2% and reducing peak memory usage by up to 47%.

Compatibility with Large-Scale Datasets.

To demonstrate the effectiveness of NeuLite on large-scale datasets, we utilize the FEMNIST dataset, consisting of 805,263 images, to train a ResNet18 under Non-IID settings. We employ the same memory settings as in Table 1 and evaluate the performance of NeuLite across different device scales ({120, 240, 500}). Additionally, we present the convergence curve of FedAvg with a device scale of 120 as the baseline. The experimental results are depicted in Fig. 5(a), where the X-axis represents the sampling training rounds and the Y-axis denotes the testing accuracy. We observe that NeuLite effectively converges across different device scales, achieving a 3% accuracy improvement compared to FedAvg.

Compatibility with Transformer-Based Models.

Furthermore, we perform experiments on Mini-ImageNet using the ViT with 12 Transformer encoders as the global model to demonstrate the adaptability of NeuLite to mainstream Transformer-based models. We treat each encoder as a basic layer, dividing the ViT into three blocks, each comprising four encoders. Memory Efficiency.

To demonstrate the memory efficiency of NeuLite on real-world devices, we perform experiments with two models (ResNet18 and ResNet34) on CIFAR10 using a Jetson TX2. Training Efficiency.

NeuLite is also a training efficiency framework where the time required to train each block is significantly reduced compared to full model training, as shown in Fig. 7. For instance, when training ResNet18 on TX2 (CIFAR10), training each block can accelerate the time per round by 1.841.841.84 to 2.31×2.31\times compared to training the full model. Ablation Study

We further conduct a breakdown analysis of the benefit brought by each technique, i.e., curriculum-aware training loss and parameter co-adaptation training paradigm. We perform experiments with ResNet18 on CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 datasets under both IID and Non-IID scenarios. The results are illustrated in Fig. 8. We observe that both curriculum-aware training loss and parameter co-adaptation training paradigm have non-trivial contributions to performance improvement, particularly the parameter co-adaptation training paradigm. For example, as depicted in Fig. 8(b), for the CIFAR100 (IID), the accuracy declines by 1.2% without the curriculum-aware training loss and significantly drops by 9.0% without the parameter co-adaptation training paradigm. However, there is still an accuracy improvement of 19% and 11.2% over FedAvg, respectively. These results confirm the effectiveness of these techniques and highlight the efficacy of progressive training.

Conclusion

This paper introduces NeuLite, a framework that addresses the memory constraints in FL through elastic progressive training. NeuLite first divides the global model into blocks and trains each block in a progressive manner. Furthermore, NeuLite incorporates two core components to guide the training process: Curriculum Mentor and Training Harmonizer. Specifically, the Curriculum Mentor devises curriculum-aware training losses for each block, mitigating the loss of valuable information. The Training Harmonizer develops a parameter co-adaptation training paradigm to break the information isolation across blocks and constructs output modules for each block to strengthen model parameter co-adaptation. Extensive experiments and ablation studies demonstrate NeuLite’s superiority in addressing memory constraints in FL.
