Are Synthetic Data Useful for Egocentric Hand-Object Interaction Detection? An Investigation and the HOI-Synth Domain Adaptation Benchmark

Rosario Leonardi1, Antonino Furnari1,2, Francesco Ragusa1,2, Giovanni Maria Farinella1,2
1FPV@IPLab - University of Catania, Italy
2Next Vision s.r.l. - Spinoff of the University of Catania, Italy
Abstract

In this study, we investigate the effectiveness of synthetic data in enhancing hand-object interaction detection within the egocentric vision domain. We introduce a simulator able to generate synthetic images of hand-object interactions automatically labeled with hand-object contact states, bounding boxes, and pixel-wise segmentation masks. Through comprehensive experiments and comparative analyses on three egocentric datasets, VISOR, EgoHOS, and ENIGMA-51, we demonstrate that the use of synthetic data and domain adaptation techniques allows for comparable performance to conventional supervised methods while requiring annotations on only a fraction of the real data. When tested with in-domain synthetic data generated from 3D models of real target environments and objects, our best models show consistent performance improvements with respect to standard fully supervised approaches based on labeled real data only. Our study also sets a new benchmark of domain adaptation for egocentric hand-object interaction detection (HOI-Synth) and provides baseline results to encourage the community to engage in this challenging task. We release the generated data, code, and the simulator at the following link: https://iplab.dmi.unict.it/HOI-Synth/.

1 Introduction

Understanding how humans interact with the surrounding objects from egocentric images is a fundamental challenge in computer vision, with applications in diverse domains in which wearable devices, such as smart glasses, can assist the user in object manipulation tasks. Examples of such domains include collaborative robotics [17, 5], industrial behavior understanding [56, 49], human-computer interaction [39], and healthcare [1]. Previous works investigated the task of understanding human-object interactions from an egocentric perspective in different forms, including action recognition [11], object state-change detection [21], and hand-object interaction forecasting [36]. A line of work developed around the goal of identifying the actively manipulated object, the presence of hands, and the contact state between hands and objects [58, 49, 31, 12], which is generally referred to as Hand-Object Interaction (HOI) detection. Despite the progress in model design granted by the availability of egocentric benchmarks such as EPIC-KITCHENS [10, 11] and VISOR [12], performance in real application scenarios is closely tied to the availability of large amounts of annotated real-world and domain-specific data [49]. In addition, the need for spatial and interaction annotations makes acquiring and labeling such data an expensive and time-consuming process. Driven by the aforementioned motivations, in this paper, we aim to answer the following question:

Can synthetic data generated at scale and automatically labeled improve hand-object interaction detection, reducing the need for labeled real data?

Many studies explored the use of synthetic data for training models intended for real-world applications [30, 55, 69, 24]. However, the exploitation of synthetic data is currently underexplored in egocentric vision in general and hand-object interaction detection in particular. To support our investigation, we developed a simulator, based on the Unity engine111https://unity.com/, able to generate variegated synthetic data of hand-object interactions automatically labeled for the considered task. Our simulator generates synthetic data that mimic real-world hand-object interactions by combining the Habitat-Matterport 3D (HM3D) dataset [51] (to include real-world environments) and DexGraspNet [67] (to model realistic hand grasps). The generated data are automatically labeled with 2D bounding boxes, instance segmentation masks, depth maps, and hand-object interactions (Figure 1-top). We use our simulator to generate synthetic images that complement three SOTA datasets of hand-object manipulations: EPIC-KITCHENS VISOR [12], a popular dataset of egocentric videos of subjects interacting with objects in kitchens; Ego-HOS [72], including egocentric images of hand-object interactions annotated with pixel-wise masks, contact states and contact boundaries; ENIGMA-51 [50], a dataset of industrial object manipulation. Collectively, the real and generated egocentric data define a new benchmark dataset, which we term HOI-Synth.
We hence study a set of domain adaptation scenarios in which hand-object interaction algorithms are trained in the presence of automatically labeled synthetic data, unlabeled real data (which can be easily acquired and requires no annotations), and, optionally, different amounts of labeled real data (Figure 1-bottom).

Extensive experiments with different state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods on the proposed HOI-Synth dataset highlight that i) even with a simple pre-training approach, the use of synthetic egocentric data can effectively reduce the need for labeled real data at training time, ii) existing domain adaptation approaches for object detection [62, 37, 34, 20], repurposed for the hand-object interaction task, deliver promising results in the contexts of unsupervised and semi-supervised domain adaptation, iii) in-domain synthetic data depicting the same environment and objects present in the target real data systematically improve the performance of hand-object interaction detection models. Our results show that, when using labeled synthetic data and only 25%percent2525\% of real labeled data, we obtain improvements in Hand+Object mAP over baselines trained on real data only of +7.66%percent7.66+7.66\% on the EPIC-KITCHENS VISOR dataset, +3.43%percent3.43+3.43\% on Ego-HOS, and +7.65%percent7.65+7.65\% on ENIGMA-51 with in-domain synthetic data.

In sum, the contributions of this work are the following: 1) We propose a systematic investigation of the egocentric HOI task to assess whether the generation and use of automatically labeled synthetic data can reduce the need for labeled real data in hand-object interaction detection; 2) We introduce a new simulator able to generate images of realistic and diverse egocentric hand-object interactions automatically labeled; 3) We propose HOI-Synth, a new benchmark dataset for unsupervised and semi-supervised domain adaptation useful to study hand-object interaction detection in egocentric vision. Together with the dataset, we include baseline results showing the potential of synthetic data in this domain. To enable future research on this topic, we release our simulator, the generated data, and all the codes required to reproduce the results at the following link: https://iplab.dmi.unict.it/HOI-Synth/.

2 Related Work

Hand-Object Interaction Detection The authors of [58] were among the first to frame hand-object interaction detection as the task of detecting hands, inferring contact states, and detecting manipulated objects, considered both egocentric and third-person vision images in which hands and objects are clearly visible. This task formulation was later extended in [7] adding object and hands segmentation, secondary objects, and grasp type prediction. Similar investigations have been also performed considering purely egocentric vision scenarios. The authors of [38] proposed an architecture leveraging specific egocentric cues such as hand poses and object masks. The authors of [49] defined Egocentric Human-Object Interaction (EHOI) detection as the task of identifying manipulated objects and predicting interaction verbs for each of them. Hand-object interaction detection has also been considered in an industrial scenario in which manipulated object classes are known beforehand [31, 50]. Other investigations considered different tasks related to hand-object interaction understanding such as State Change Object Interaction Detection [21] or active object detection [19]. The aforementioned previous investigation assumed different task formulations, which makes it hard to compare methods and assess progress. Recently, the authors of [12] provided a task formulation termed Hand-Object Segmentation (HOS) together with the EPIC-KITCHENS VISOR dataset, thus setting a standard benchmark for hand-object interaction detection in egocentric vision. HOS consists in estimating the contact relation between the hands and the objects and segmenting them given a single RGB frame. A similar formulation was proposed in [72] together with the EgoHOS dataset. In this paper, we adopt the HOS formulation and the baseline model of [12] to perform experiments on three datasets designed for hand-object interaction detection: VISOR [12], EgoHOS [72], and ENIGMA-51 [50].

Simulators for Synthetic Visual Data Generation Previous works introduced simulators to generate synthetic data mimicking the behavior of agents (e.g., cars, robots) in the real world. CARLA [15] is an open-source simulator for urban driving, developed to support the prototyping, training, and testing of autonomous driving models that can control speed, acceleration, weather, pedestrians, and street signs. Several virtual platforms have been released specifically for embodied AI agents. Gibson [68] and iGibson [32] allow the design of perceptual agents, virtualizing real spaces and embodying agents with space and physics constraints. Habitat [40, 60] is a simulation platform designed to support the training of virtual robots that can interact with objects and 3D environments. Omniverse [43] is a platform that allows the replication of real environments for synthetic data generation. Isaac Sim [44], belonging to the Omniverse suite, allows the design, training, and optimization of AI-based robots for several tasks using synthetic data. AI2-THOR [29] is a framework for visual AI research consisting of several 3D indoor scenes where AI agents can navigate and interact with objects. Thanks to the advances in rendering engines, game engines such as Grand Theft Auto (GTA) V222https://www.rockstargames.com/it/gta-v have also been exploited for synthetic data generation for pedestrian detection and tracking in urban scenarios [18, 14]. Other works proposed simulators specifically crafted to represent human agents moving in the scene and interacting with objects. Among these, the authors of [45] proposed a simulator based on Unity3D to generate synthetic data for indoor image-based localization in the cultural heritage domain. In [48], GTA V was used to generate third person and egocentric images for safety monitoring in construction sites. Other works repurposed simulators initially designed for robotics to tackle egocentric vision tasks [23]. In this work, we provide a new simulator designed to generate high-quality RGB images of fine-grained egocentric human-object interactions. We leverage 3D models of real environments and objects, as well as pre-optimized grasp poses to provide realistic visual examples.

Synthetic Data for Hand-Object Interaction Understanding Previous studies tackled the problem of generating and automatically labeling synthetic data to tackle egocentric hand-object interaction understanding tasks. The authors of [22] presented the ObMan dataset to study 3D hand-object interactions. The dataset is composed of 20K synthetic images of hand-object interactions with backgrounds randomly selected from LSUN [71] and ImageNet [52]. The authors of [25] proposed AffordPose, a large-scale dataset of synthetic hand-object interactions to study hand-object affordance understanding and affordance-oriented hand-object interaction generation. The authors of [63] presented FLEX, a new framework to generate synthetic grasping from third and first person views. The authors of [70] proposed an approach based on diffusion models to create realistic hand-object interactions which allowed to study how and where hands touch the objects. Differently from these works, we leverage synthetic data generated with the proposed simulator to study the problem of detecting hand-object interactions from egocentric images. Akin to our investigation, the work of [31] generates synthetic images of hand-object interactions using the Blender 3D rendering software to study domain-specific object and hand-object interaction detection in an industrial scenario. Our investigation aims to study the potential of synthetic images for hand-object interaction detection from egocentric images considering a domain adaptation setting and both a general, domain-agnostic scenario in which synthetic data are not coupled with the target real dataset and an “in-domain” scenario in which synthetic data accurately replicate the real environments in which the system should work at test time.

Domain Adaptation Domain Adaptation (DA) techniques have gained a lot of attention in the past years [9, 53, 2, 73]. These methods rely on different strategies to reduce the domain gap between a source and a target domain, such as adversarial training [20, 64], transfer learning [20, 64] and pseudo-labeling [62, 37, 34, 3, 13]. Domain adaptation has been extensively studied in egocentric vision for different tasks [8, 47, 41, 42, 33]. While domain adaptation approaches have been previously applied to the object detection task [46], our investigation is the first to explicitly consider the problem of hand-object interaction detection leveraging from synthetic data.

3 The HOI-Synth Benchmark

In this section, we describe the HOI-Synth benchmark to study domain adaptation for hand-object interaction detection. As part of the benchmark, we release our hand-object interaction simulator and the synthetic data generated to complement three popular state-of-the-art egocentric datasets for hand-object interaction detection.

3.1 Hand-Object Interaction (HOI) Simulator

Figure 2 shows a scheme of the developed simulator. The simulator leverages the Unity Perception package [66], a toolkit designed for generating large-scale synthetic datasets. This package provides tools to manage the labeling process and to setup the virtual scene, allows the randomization of light intensity, the color of light sources, object textures, etc., and supports the inclusion of visual effects like motion blur or noise to improve the realism and diversity of the scene.

We used the HM3D dataset [51] to get a large amount of 3D indoor environments. This dataset contains 1K 3D reconstructions of real-world indoor environments, including homes, commercial stores, and civic spaces. To the best of our knowledge, this represents the first attempt to generate synthetic data of HOIs considering such a large number of environments. To generate realistic human-object interactions, we use DexGraspNet [67], a collection of 1.32 million robotic grasps involving 5,355 different objects generated with an accelerated differentiable force closure estimator, and SyntheticHumans [65], a package for the generation of rigged human 3D models. This package allows to customize different parameters of the generated human, such as ethnicity, clothing, age, height, weight, and sex.

For each generation iteration, an environment is randomly selected from the pool of 3D models and placed in the virtual unity scene (Figure 2-top-left). We generate a navmesh denoting all positions in the 3D model in which human agents can be placed. We hence generate a human model, place it at a random navmesh position, and randomize the pose of the hands and arms. Then, we randomly choose one object-grasp pair from DexGraspNet, attach the object to the human model, and fit the hand pose to the hand of the generated human (see Figure 2-bottom-left). It should be noted that DexGraspNet contains untextured and convex-decomposed versions of 3D models from the ShapeNet [6, 54], YCB [4], Big-BIRD [59], Grasp [26], KIT [27] and Google’s Scanned Objects [16]. We recover original textured objects from such datasets to obtain a more realistic hand-object interaction. We also convert the hand pose included in DexGraspNet from the ShadowHand format [57] to the SyntheticHumans conventions. Finally, we place a virtual camera at human eye level to capture the scene from the first person point of view (Figure 2-center). For each generated interaction, the Perception package automatically annotates the bounding boxes and the segmentation masks of the hands and interacted objects. We extend Perception to also provide annotations for the hand contact state, and the hand-object relations as shown in Figure 2-right. The supplementary material reports additional details on the simulator and visual examples of the generated data.

3.2 Datasets

The HOI-Synth benchmark dataset is obtained by extending three sets of egocentric images to study hand-object interaction detection, EPIC-KITCHENS VISOR [12], Ego-HOS [72], and ENIGMA-51 [50], with automatically labeled data generated through our simulator.

EPIC-KITCHENS VISOR [12] It contains 36 hours of videos from EPIC-KITCHENS 100 [11], including 272K manual semantic masks, 257 object classes, and 9.9M interpolated dense semantic masks. Along with these data, the authors release annotations for the HOS task, including 32,857 training images and 42,787 hand-object relations. We complement this dataset with 30,259 synthetic images.

Ego-HOS [72] It is composed of 11,243 egocentric images of hand-object interactions sparsely sampled from videos belonging to EGO4D [21], EPIC-KITCHENS [10], THU-READ [61], and other egocentric videos where people play escape rooms. The dataset has been annotated with per-pixel masks and hand-object relations, which allow us to study the considered HOS task. We complement this dataset with 8,107 synthetic images.

ENIGMA-51 [50] It is an egocentric dataset of subjects following instructions to repair electrical boards in an industrial laboratory. The dataset contains 12,597 interaction frames, 17,363 hand annotations, and 9,342 annotations of objects, with instance segmentation masks for the hands and objects. The dataset also provides 3D models of the manipulated objects and the industrial laboratory. We complement this dataset with two sets of synthetic images: an in-domain and an out-of-domain sets. The in-domain set is generated using the 3D models of the environment and objects provided by the authors, thus obtaining synthetic images aligned to the real data. The out-of-domain set contains images of hand-object interactions in generic environments and with generic objects. Figure 3 reports examples of images from these two sets. The ENIGMA-51 portion of HOI-Synth allows to study the ability of hand-object interaction detection models to generalize to a domain-specific scenario in the presence of generic and domain-specific synthetic data. Table 1 reports statistics of the training section of the HOI-Synth benchmark dataset, including the number of real and synthetic images, annotated hands, HOIs, and objects. We use the official validation and test sets of the respective datasets for evaluation.

4 Experimental Analysis and Results

We use VISOR HOS [12] as a baseline for our experiments. This method is based on the PointRend [28] instance segmentation network with the addition of three modules to detect the hand side, contact state (“contact” or “no contact”), and an offset vector that links the hand to the interacted object. We consider five different approaches to hand-object segmentation based on VISOR HOS:

Synthetic-Only This approach consists of training the VISOR HOS model using only synthetic data and testing it directly on real data. Experiments with this approach aim to assess whether synthetic data can entirely replace real data.

Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) This approach replicate the VISOR HOS architecture within the Adaptive Teacher unsupervised domain adaptation approach proposed in [34]. While Adaptive Teacher was originally designed to tackle cross-domain object detection, we adapted it to perform hand-object interaction detection by adding modules to estimate hand side, contact state, offset vector, and segmentation masks. The model is hence trained using labeled synthetic data and unlabeled real data following an unsupervised domain adaptation scheme. This approach aims to assess whether current domain adaptation techniques in conjunction with high-quality labeled synthetic data allow to avoid labeling real data.

Real-Only This approach consists of training the VISOR HOS model on labeled real data only. We experiment with different amounts of labeled real data to assess how much performance depends on the scale of training data when synthetic images are not available. This method provides baseline performance and corresponds to the standard fully supervised setup.

Synthetic + Real This approach consists in pre-training the VISOR HOS model on labeled synthetic data and fine-tune it on labeled real data. Also in this case, we experiment with different amounts of labeled real data. These experiments are aimed to assess the potential of labeled synthetic data to reduce the amount of real data required for training.

Semi-Supervised Domain Adaptation (SSDA) This approach consists of training the Adaptive Teacher model with labeled synthetic data, unlabeled real data, and a set of labeled real data. We experiment with the 100% of unlabeled real data and different proportions of labeled real data (i.e., 10%, 25%, 50% and 100%). To allow the Adaptive Teacher model to work in a semi-supervised regime, we merge the set of labeled synthetic data with the labeled real data. These experiments aim to assess whether semi-supervised domain adaptation techniques allow to reduce the fraction of real data that should be labeled to obtain reasonable performance.

5 Conclusion

We investigated the usefulness of synthetic data in improving performance and reducing the need for real labeled images in hand-object interaction detection. To support our analysis, we proposed a simulator capable of generating high-quality synthetic hand-object interaction images, which builds on recent advances in synthetic data generation and grasp analysis. We hence introduced the HOI-Synth benchmark dataset, which includes real data from three existing datasets, VISOR, Ego-HOS, and ENIGMA-51, together with synthetic data generated and automatically labeled through our proposed simulator. The results show that synthetic data can significantly reduce the need for real labeled data to address the hand-object interaction task by adopting state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques. We release the generated data, code, and simulator at the following link: https://iplab.dmi.unict.it/HOI-Synth/.

Appendix A Implementation Details

The implementation details are reported in the following. First, we provide information about evaluation measures in Section A.1. Then, we detail the employed domain adaptation methods in Section A.2. Finally, we delve into training details in Section A.3.

A.1 Evaluation Measures

The performance of the different methods is evaluated using COCO Mask AP [35] as in [12]. Specifically, we report detailed performance on the following predicted elements: (1) AP Hand: A​P50:95𝐴subscript𝑃:5095AP_{50:95} of the hand category; (2) AP Object: A​P50:95𝐴subscript𝑃:5095AP_{50:95} of the in-contact object category; (3) mAP Hand + Contact: m​A​P50:95𝑚𝐴subscript𝑃:5095mAP_{50:95} of in-contact hand and not in-contact hand categories; (4) mAP Hand + Side: m​A​P50:95𝑚𝐴subscript𝑃:5095mAP_{50:95} of the right hand and left hand categories; and (5) AP Hand + Object: A​P50:95𝐴subscript𝑃:5095AP_{50:95} of the hand-object category. Refer to [12] for additional information about the evaluation process.

A.2 Domain Adaptation Methods

We adapted four state-of-the-art semi-supervised domain adaptation methods for the considered HOS task. All considered domain adaptation methods are based on the teacher-student paradigm. Original methods have been adapted adding modules to estimate the hand side, contact state, offset vector, and segmentation masks and in [12]. The considered methods are reported in the following.

Mean Teacher (MT) [62]. Mean Teacher is a semi-supervised learning technique based on the Teacher-Student paradigm. In this approach, both the student and teacher models process the same input applying different noise augmentation. During training, the weights are updated via gradient descent for the student’s model, whereas the teacher weights are updated using an exponential moving average (EMA) of the student network’s weights. We extend this method with additional modules for the HOS task.

Mean Teacher + Adversarial Loss (MT+GRL). Following the idea of [34], we combine the Mean Teacher with Gradient Reverse Layer [20] (GRL) in order to align feature representations between source and target domains. We extend this method with additional modules for the HOS task.

Unbiased Teacher (UT) [37]. In this work, the authors introduced a new training procedure composed of two steps. The first step is called Burn-In, where a standard object detector is trained with labeled data, and the second step is called Teacher-Student Mutual Learning, where the trained object detector is duplicated into a student and a frozen teacher model. The new unlabeled input images are passed to both models using different augmentation techniques. The teacher will produce pseudo-labels used to train the student model. We extend this method with additional modules for the HOS task.

Adaptive Teacher (AT) [34]. This method was originally designed to tackle cross-domain object detection. It combines the previously discussed techniques, i.e., GRL, Augmentation, and EMA. We extend this method with additional modules for the HOS task.

A.3 Training details

For consistency across the different approaches, we adopted the ResNet-101-FPN architecture as the backbone for our models. Tables 6, 7, and 8 show the hyperparameters used for training on EPIC-KITCHENS VISOR, Ego-HOS, and ENIGMA-51, respectively. These include learning rates, batch sizes, training iterations, batch sizes of the unlabeled images used in the domain adaptation approaches, and burn-up iterations. These values have been selected to optimize models’ performance across the different datasets. Our models have been trained using four A30 NVIDIA GPUs and utilized the SGD optimizer.

Appendix B Hand-object interaction simulator: additional features

The developed simulator based on the Unity engine555https://unity.com/ can generate variegated synthetic data of hand-object interactions automatically labeled for the considered task. The generated data are automatically labeled with 2D bounding boxes, instance segmentation masks, depth maps, and hand-object interactions. Our simulator also allows us to further randomize the number and the position of virtual cameras, in order to increase the variability of the generated data and to consider even third-person points of view (see Figure 5). It is also able to generate synthetic video shots and additional labels such as depth maps (see Figure 6), 3D bounding boxes, and 3D poses. While this work focused on the exploitation of the RGB modality, as in the standard HOS task, future works may also take advantage of the rich set of modalities and 3D annotations provided by our simulator.
