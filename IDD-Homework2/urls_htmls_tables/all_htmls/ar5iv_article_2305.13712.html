Abstract

This paper investigates the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in the context of understanding their own knowledge and measuring their uncertainty. We argue this is an important feature for mitigating hallucinations. Specifically, we focus on addressing known-unknown questions, characterized by high uncertainty due to the absence of definitive answers. To facilitate our study, we collect a dataset with new K nown- U nknown Q uestions (KUQ) and propose a novel categorization scheme to elucidate the sources of uncertainty. Subsequently, we assess the LLMs’ ability to differentiate between known and unknown questions and classify them accordingly. Moreover, we evaluate the quality of their answers in an Open-Ended QA setting. To quantify the uncertainty expressed in the answers, we create a semantic evaluation method that measures the model’s accuracy in expressing uncertainty between known vs unknown questions.

1 Introduction

\say To know what you know and to know what you do not know, that is true knowledge — The Analects of Confucius

Recently, Language Models have increased their parameters size from a few millions in 2018, such as ElMo (94M) Peters et al. ( 2018 ) or BERT (Large - 340M) Devlin et al. ( 2018 ) ; to hundreds of billions, like GPT-3 (175B) Brown et al. ( 2020 ) or Megatron-Turing NLG (530B) Smith et al. ( 2022 ) . As these models have grown in size, their capabilities have correspondingly expanded. They have demonstrated to acquire reasoning capabilities Wei et al. ( 2022 ) , solve mathematical problems, coding problems Chen et al. ( 2021 ) , and more.

However, as models continue to grow in size, their underlying learning mechanisms and their level of comprehension remain largely unclear. A concern with LLMs is their tendency to confidently produce inaccurate responses and to provide responses to questions without hesitation. In cognitive psychology, metacognition is defined as the awareness and thoughts of one’s own thought process. Do LLMs know what they know? And more importantly, are they aware of what they do not know? This is an important question to understand the certainty of their statements or prevent such language models from making up facts.

We divide knowledge following the quadrant of knowledge popularized by former US Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld, Table 1 . In this classification, Known-Unknowns are especially relevant. They are mainly represented by questions that do not have a definitive answer. The answers to such questions are often subjective and may vary depending on the individual’s perspective or knowledge. Some questions may even be unanswerable due to a lack of information or the inherent complexity of the topic. As a result, the answers to such questions are considered to have high uncertainty levels. For example, If the Universe started at the Big Bang, what existed before then? . Our goal is to understand how language models deal with these kinds of uncertain questions.

Previous works have explored how to correctly extract knowledge from a language model Jiang et al. ( 2020 ) . They have also studied if they can self-evaluate the correctness of their responses Kadavath et al. ( 2022 ) . And they have tried to understand the expressions of confidence or uncertainty Zhou et al. ( 2023a ) .

In this paper, we introduce a set of K nown- U nknown Q uestions (KUQ), which can later be used to study how language models understand such questions. These questions are expected to have highly uncertain answers. We evaluate the knowledge of current LLMs on these questions, introduce a new categorization of unknown questions and explore the uncertainty of their answers.

In particular, the paper presents the following contributions: (1) New set of Known-Unknown questions: KUQ . This paper introduces a novel dataset of known unknown questions, which we can formulate but do not have a clear answer to. Additionally, it also introduces a categorization for the unknown questions. We divided them into sub-categories according to their source of uncertainty and assigned each question the corresponding category. (2) Exploration of current large language models’ behaviors on known unknowns questions . We have explored the comprehension of LLMs for unknown questions and compared them to known questions in the Eli5 dataset. (3) Answer Uncertainty Disparity : We evaluate the uncertainty disparity of LLMs when answering questions with high uncertainty compared to the answers provided when answering known questions.

After the evaluation, the main insights from this work are summarized in the following points:

  • •

    In classifying known-vs-unknown questions, LLMs still have room for improvement. The smaller models and open-source models achieved nearly random performance. Results are presented in Section 5.1

  • •

    When classifying the unknown categories, some categories are easier to identify than others. For instance, Future Unknowns contain linguistic features that tell them apart. While models achieve also good results. More detail in Section 5.2 .

  • •

    Humans achieve similar or slightly better performance than the models in question classification. However, the rationales generated by the models may improve human results when the generated text is not misleading. More details are in Section 5.3 .

  • •

    In terms of uncertainty disparity, our observations indicate a minimal gap in the expression of answer uncertainty between known and unknown questions, with only marginal distinctions observed among the models. Although there is a slight improvement when the model is provided with the solution, it still falls short of expressing it in the correct questions. An evaluation of answer uncertainty can be found in Section 5.4

2 Related Work

Understanding Large Language Models.

There have been several works trying to measure and understand the real capabilities of Large Language Models. Different benchmarks have arisen from such studies, some for specific tasks like summarization Zhang et al. ( 2023 ) or planning Valmeekam et al. ( 2022 ) ; or others more comprehensive like Big-Bench Srivastava et al. ( 2022 ) , which presents 204 tasks to test the modern capabilities of language models.

Text Uncertainty.

Modeling (un)certainty has been a persistent challenge for the linguistics community. The expression of certainty plays a fundamental role in Question-Answering, as it requires knowledge about one’s own knowledge and the ability to define the level of confidence in one’s response. Several studies have examined various approaches to expressing certainty, including those presented in Kuhn et al. ( 2023 ) , Szarvas et al. ( 2012 ) , and Farkas et al. ( 2010 ) . Given the many factors that contribute to the overall level of text uncertainty, it is essential to measure multiple aspects in order to gauge overall uncertainty, as demonstrated in Pei and Jurgens ( 2021 ) . In the domain of Large Language Models, some other works have explored how they behave when expressing uncertainty Zhou et al. ( 2023b ) and how they can learn to express uncertainty in words Lin et al. ( 2022 ) .

Uncertainty in Language Models.

The study of hallucinations and robustness, as well as possible calibration methods, has led to several works measuring uncertainty predictions on language models. In Xiao and Wang ( 2021 ) they show prediction uncertainty correlates with hallucination probability and Xiao et al. ( 2022 ) does uncertainty quantification to minimize the calibration error.

Investigating what Language Models know.

Since the beginning of the first pre-trained language models, some researchers have studied what information is stored in their weights and how we can extract that knowledge Jiang et al. ( 2020 ) . More recently, another work has explored if LLMs are able to evaluate the validity of their claims Kadavath et al. ( 2022 ) . The question of what a model should know has also been recently explored in computer vision systems Sharifi Noorian et al. ( 2022 ) , with a human-in-the-loop process to investigate what the models really know and what they should know.

Similar data.

In terms of similar data with unknown questions, the closest available dataset is the known_unknown questions section in the Big-Bench benchmark Srivastava et al. ( 2022 ) . But it only contains 46 samples. A similar but yet very different is SQuAD 2.0 Rajpurkar et al. ( 2018 ) , which modified the original questions to convert them into a set of questions that cannot be answered with the given context but differ from our questions that are meant to not have answers in general.

3 Data collection

For the purpose of this study, we have found a very limited number of datasets available with known-unknown questions. We consider known-unknown questions, those for which we do not have a clear answer. For example, Are there other forms of intelligent life in the universe? Which year will the next financial crisis arrive? or What was Napoleon eating when he decided to get married? We consider the 46 known-unknown questions included in the Big-Bench Srivastava et al. ( 2022 ) benchmark not sufficient for a correct evaluation. Thus, we have decided to generate additional samples.

In this work, we have collected questions from different sources and generated a new dataset with K nown- U nknown Q uestions: KUQ . In Table 2 , we show the number of questions generated per source. In the following section, we describe how the questions were generated.

3.1 Data sources

The unknown questions are extracted from 3 different sources:

Crowd-source : We have used a crowd-source service to ask people to provide known-unknown questions. To achieve this, they were explained what a known-unknown question is and provided a category from Table 3 with a set of examples. They were asked to generate a question and explain the reason why its answer is unknown.

Large Language Model : After some initial tests, we have seen that generative models can help generate some unknown questions with a wide distribution of topics. We have used an LLM to generate a wide range of new questions without answers. We have specifically used the GPT-4 model OpenAI ( 2023 ) in this case. These questions have been filtered and confirmed unknown as described later. The corresponding prompt and generation process can be found in Appendix B.1 .

Web : We have identified some websites that contain unsolved questions or problems, crawled the questions, and filtered them, both automatically and manually. The source websites are, a Wikipedia page with the List of Unsolved Problems and another website with impossible questions to answer.

In the filtering stage, we employed Sentence-BERT Reimers and Gurevych ( 2019 ) to automatically filter each question source using a similarity threshold of 0.925, which was determined empirically and found to effectively remove questions that were deemed too similar. The questions crawled from the web were also filtered manually, removing those considered irrelevant or unworthy. In addition, we have implemented a second filtering stage in a crowd-sourcing platform to extract the categories later explained in Section 4.2 . To do so, people were presented with one question and their suggested category, they had to decide if the question belonged to that category and or if they thought another category was more suitable. They were also allowed to select if the question was known and therefore removed from the final dataset.

The set of known questions has been retrieved from a currently existing dataset. In particular, we have considered Eli5 Fan et al. ( 2019 ) , a dataset with questions retrieved from a Reddit thread. The questions from this dataset are selected with the BM25 algorithm Trotman et al. ( 2014 ) taking the first non-repeated match from each unknown questions. By doing so, our objective is to choose the closest known question to its unknown counterpart in linguistic terms.

4 Tasks

In this work, we have explored and analyzed a set of tasks relevant to known-unknown questions and we present them in this section. The goal of these tasks is to analyze how current LLMs deal with highly uncertain questions. In the experiment section, we will further develop the tests carried out and present the results.

4.1 Known vs Unknown: What’s known?

Research Question: Do Language Models distinguish between what’s known or unknown?

In our research, we aim to replicate the awareness capability of humans in distinguishing between known and unknown questions. As humans can discern whether we have the ability to answer a question or if the question is a known topic. Hence, we propose a binary classification model for known and unknown questions.

4.2 Multi-class Classification: Exploring the unknown

Research Question: Do Language Models know the categories for a question to be unknown?

Diving into the unknown questions, we have identified a series of reasons why a question may be unknown. We have categorized and presented them in Table 3 , including a definition of the category and some sample questions.

Some of these questions have been studied separately in some previous works. For example, in ControversialQA Wang et al. ( 2023 ) , they present a dataset with 10k controversial questions. And (QA) 2 Kim et al. ( 2022 ) presents another study on questions with questionable assumptions.

Given this, we consider this to be one of the first times to introduce a fine-grained classification of unknown questions. The question categories in our new dataset KUQ have been agreed upon and confirmed by 3 crowd-source workers.

Creating a classification system is important for identifying different types of uncertainty in unknown questions. This classification may help models improve their answers by understanding the sources of uncertainty. It also enables the division of tasks based on different categories. For example, for controversial questions, the classification system helps identify conflicting views, while for underspecified questions, it helps explain why they lack clarity. Developing such a classification system can greatly enhance the effectiveness and reliability of models when dealing with unknown questions.

4.3 Open Domain Question-Answering: Generative text Generation

Research Question: How do Language Models deal with the uncertainty of known-unknown questions?

The development of generative language models has facilitated the task of open domain question-answering, and as a result, they have been successfully applied in various datasets, such as Eli5 Fan et al. ( 2019 ) , for generating comprehensive and elaborate longer responses.

In this particular scenario, the task at hand is inherently more complex because unknown questions lack a definitive solution. Generative Language Models have demonstrated high levels of confidence in generating responses, as they are designed to produce an output for every input. Consequently, these models may exhibit overconfidence by generating responses that are unwarranted or inaccurate, commonly referred to as "hallucinations" Ji et al. ( 2023 ) , which produce nonsensical or unfaithful text.

This is why the dataset may become especially relevant for identifying when the model is showing over-confidence in their responses. We expect a higher proportion of words expressing uncertainty in the unknown questions.

5 Experimental Results

This section presents the experiments of our study. For the experiments, we have used, different LLM models, mainly available through the OpenAI API: text-davinci-003 , GPT-3.5-turbo , GPT-4 ; or open-source models such as Alpaca Taori et al. 5.2 Multi-class Classification: Exploring the unknown

To further evaluate the performance of Large Language Models in classification, we conducted a similar set of experiments on the categories classification. As in the previous experiments, models were presented with a subsample of known-unknown questions and they were expected to provide the correct category. The models were given the different categories, their definitions, and a representative example as in Table 3 . Similarly, we performed the 1-shot experiment, few-shot, and self-ask. The used prompts can be found in Appendix B.3 .

The results of these experiments are shown in Table 5 . And the accuracy divided by category is shown in Figure 1 .

The outcomes of this experiment revealed the varying levels of accuracy achieved by the different models in categorizing the questions. While GPT-4 performed higher across different question types, they all exhibited notable weaknesses in classifying certain categories of questions. The highest accuracies were achieved in Future Unknowns and the Controversial/Debatable questions. The Future Unknowns usually contain linguistic features that can make them easier to be identified. Additionally, we observe the self-ask experiments provide lower results than even zero-shot. We hypothesize this might be due to the overconfidence of the models when generating the answers. We investigate the uncertainty of the answers in the following section.

Our findings suggest that while LLMs possess the capability to classify questions based on their underlying category, further research is necessary to improve their accuracy and generalization across different types of questions.

5.3 Open-ended Question-Answering: Generative text Generation

In this section, we explore the models’ answers in a generative scenario. In particular, we use gpt-3.5-turbo We first evaluate the humans’ perception of the models’ generated answers in both the known vs unknown and the categories explanation. Later, we explore their answers uncertainty.

Rationales: Known vs Unknown Perception.

This experiment aims to explore the impact of answer rationales generated by a language model on human perception of the corresponding question. Specifically, we investigate the extent to which individuals’ perception of known vs unknown may be influenced by the output of language models. This can explain how a user may see their decision-making process influenced particularly in cases where the correctness of the answers is uncertain. The models still try to provide an answer which may not be entirely correct but it is sufficient to the eyes of the user.

We set up an experiment where the LLM ( GPT-3.5-turbo) is asked to generate (1) a rationale for the question being known or unknown, and (2) a rationale provided the question is known/unknown. The users are then asked to answer if the question is known or unknown given [1] the question only, [2] the first rationale, and [3] the second rationale.

In a randomly selected subset of the questions, we observe users’ accuracy goes from 0.74 initially, to 0.705 when they are shown the first rationale, to 0.87 when they see the second rationale. In Figure 2 , we show how users change their perception of the question.

With these results, we can claim the rationales generated by the LLMs do not provide the correct information by themselves. In fact, they make people drop their accuracy. We also observe the users are influenced by the rationale provided, even when the models are wrong about it.

Rationales: Category Selection.

In this experiment, we want to understand how the model explanations help users understand the category assignment.

We have shown users the category assigned by the model, along with their explanation. As explained previously, the accuracy achieved by the model is around ≈ 0.66 absent 0.66 \approx 0.66 for the best models, while our test users reach an accuracy of 0.67 in general. This accuracy remains similar when they read the model explanation. However, for the cases where they perceive the explanation is useful or correct, their accuracy goes up to 0.83.

Figure 3 plots a histogram of how the users perceive the model rationale and their assignation being correct. As they perceive the rationale as being correct, they also achieve higher accuracy.

6 Conclusion

In this work, we have explored how known-unknown questions are tackled by LLMs. These questions are interesting because they contain high uncertainty and highly confident answers should not be expected. We have introduced a new classification of unknown questions which provides different reasons for questions to be unknown. And we have introduced 3 tasks for the newly introduced set of known-unknown questions. (1) Differentiating between known and unknown questions. This is a relevant task because if done effectively, it may allow the models to calibrate their responses (2) Classifying the questions in their correct categories. Similarly, a correct classification can let the model tune their answers. (3) Evaluating the answers. Here, we have introduced a semantic evaluation to measure the uncertainty of the models when answering questions, which may help protect the models from expressing overconfidence.

Some potential research directions may be new methods that improve the classification problems. Furthermore, measuring model uncertainty is an open question in its black-box and white-box approaches. In this direction, it is interesting to study if LLMs can express their own uncertainty probabilities and find calibration methods that may help improve the uncertainty evaluation.

Limitations

It is important to acknowledge a limitation of our evaluation approach, which employs a semantic evaluation to quantify the uncertainty expressed in the answers of Large Language Models (LLMs). While this method provides a useful measure, it may not capture the full accuracy of the model’s understanding compared to a learned metric. A learned metric could potentially leverage contextual information and nuances specific to the model’s training data, resulting in a more precise assessment of uncertainty.
