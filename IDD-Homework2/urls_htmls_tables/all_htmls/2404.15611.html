PoisonedFL: Model Poisoning Attacks to Federated Learning via Multi-Round Consistency

Yueqi Xie HKUST yxieay@connect.ust.hk ,  Minghong Fang Duke University minghong.fang@duke.edu  and  Neil Zhenqiang Gong Duke University neil.gong@duke.edu
Abstract.

Model poisoning attacks are critical security threats to Federated Learning (FL). Existing model poisoning attacks suffer from two key limitations: 1) they achieve suboptimal effectiveness when defenses are deployed, and/or 2) they require knowledge of the model updates or local training data on genuine clients. In this work, we make a key observation that their suboptimal effectiveness arises from only leveraging model-update consistency among malicious clients within individual training rounds, making the attack effect self-cancel across training rounds. In light of this observation, we propose PoisonedFL, which enforces multi-round consistency among the malicious clients’ model updates while not requiring any knowledge about the genuine clients. Our empirical evaluation on five benchmark datasets shows that PoisonedFL breaks eight state-of-the-art defenses and outperforms seven existing model poisoning attacks. Moreover, we also explore new defenses that are tailored to PoisonedFL, but our results show that we can still adapt PoisonedFL to break them. Our study shows that FL systems are considerably less robust than previously thought, underlining the urgency for the development of new defense mechanisms.

1. Introduction

Federated Learning (FL) enables clients–such as smartphones, self-driving cars, and IoT devices–to collaboratively train a model without sharing their raw local training data with a central server (Konečnỳ et al., 2016; McMahan et al., 2017). Typically, FL involves multiple training rounds, each of which consists of three key steps: 1) the server sends the current global model to the clients or a subset of them; 2) a client trains a local model based on the current global model and its local training data, and sends the model update (i.e., the difference between local model and global model) to the server; and 3) the server aggregates the clients’ model updates according to an aggregation rule and adds the aggregated model update to the global model. FL has wide applications across diverse domains, including healthcare (mel, [n. d.]; Kairouz et al., 2021; Rieke et al., 2020) and finance (web, [n. d.]; Yang et al., 2019).

However, due to the distributed nature, FL is fundamentally vulnerable to model poisoning attacks (Baruch et al., 2019; Cao and Gong, 2022; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021). In particular, an attacker can control some malicious clients and send carefully crafted malicious model updates to the server to poison the global model. The malicious clients could be compromised genuine clients or injected fake clients. The poisoned global model misclassifies many inputs indiscriminately, i.e., it has a large testing error rate (also known as untargeted model poisoning attacks).

Existing model poisoning attacks suffer from two key limitations. First, they (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021; Cao and Gong, 2022) only leverage in-round consistency, i.e., they send the same or similar malicious model updates in a single training round to amplify the attack effect, but neglect multi-round consistency. For instance, Fang attack (Fang et al., 2020) crafts the malicious model updates in a given training round to maximally deviate the global model in that round, while MPAF (Cao and Gong, 2022) sets the malicious model updates in a training round as the magnified difference between the current global model and a random model. Such a strategy limits the attack effectiveness when defenses are deployed. This is because defenses weaken the attack effect in every training round by filtering and clipping the malicious model updates; and the weakened attack effects further self-cancel across training rounds due to the lack of multi-round consistency, as shown by our measurement results in Section 4. Second, most existing attacks (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021) require knowledge of genuine clients’ model updates or local training data. They often assume compromising a significant number of genuine clients to obtain such knowledge, which is notably impractical. These attacks with suboptimal effectiveness and/or impractical assumptions give a false sense of FL security.

Our work:  In this work, we propose PoisonedFL, a novel model poisoning attack to FL, which overcomes the above limitations of existing attacks. Specifically, to enhance attack effectiveness, PoisonedFL leverages two key components: 1) multi-round consistency, and 2) dynamic attack magnitude adjustment. The first component ensures that even if the attack effect is weakened in individual training rounds by defenses, the cumulative attack effect over multiple training rounds remains undeterred. The second component aims to prevent the malicious model updates from being entirely filtered out in individual training rounds by defenses. Moreover, PoisonedFL does not require genuine clients’ information, and is agnostic to the defenses deployed by the server.

For the first component, our key observation is that a model, when updated substantially in a random direction, exhibits significantly degraded accuracy (see Fig. 10 in Appendix). Based on this observation, we formulate an all-round optimization problem that facilitates multi-round consistency. Specifically, the objective of our optimization problem is to maximize the magnitude of the total aggregated model updates in a random update direction, which is characterized by a random sign vector 𝒔𝒔\bm{s}. Each dimension of the sign vector 𝒔𝒔\bm{s} is either +1 or -1 uniformly at random, meaning that the total aggregated model updates increase or decrease the corresponding parameter of the global model. With such total aggregated model updates, the final global model is substantially moved along the random update direction, leading to a large testing error rate. Note that our objective stands substantially distinct from those of existing attacks, as it enforces multi-round consistency without self-cancellation. However, it is challenging to solve the all-round optimization problem in a given round because the model updates in future training rounds are not available. To address the challenge, we transform the optimization problem as a per-round one, which aims to find malicious model updates on the malicious clients to maximize the magnitude of the aggregated model update along the update direction 𝒔𝒔\bm{s} in each round. In particular, our malicious model update is a dimension-wise product of the sign vector 𝒔𝒔\bm{s} and a magnitude vector, a dimension of which indicates the magnitude of the corresponding dimension of the malicious model update.

For the second component, we propose to dynamically adjust the magnitude vector in each training round based on the attack results in the past rounds, making our attack effective against the unknown defense that the server may have deployed. Our key idea is to find a magnitude vector that has a large magnitude while avoiding the malicious model update being filtered out by the unknown defense. On one hand, based on the global models and malicious model updates in the previous training rounds, we estimate the model updates of genuine clients to determine an unit magnitude vector. The magnitude vector is the product of the unit magnitude vector and a scaling factor. On the other hand, we set the scaling factor based on whether the attack achieves the objective in the past rounds. In particular, our attack objective is that the total aggregated model updates have the random update direction 𝒔𝒔\bm{s}. We leverage statistical hypothesis testing to check whether our attack in the previous rounds achieves this objective. If not, it means the malicious updates were filtered out by the defense in previous rounds and thus we decrease the scaling factor to be more stealthy.

We evaluate our PoisonedFL on five benchmark datasets. Our results show that, under the least knowledge scenario – that is, no knowledge about the defense nor genuine local training data/models – PoisonedFL can break eight state-of-the-art FL defenses (Blanchard et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2021a, 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2019; Yin et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022). For instance, PoisonedFL can reduce the global models to random guessing in many cases. Moreover, in most cases, PoisonedFL achieves higher testing error rates than existing attacks (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021; Cao and Gong, 2022) when a defense is deployed even if we assume the attacker has access to the local models of all genuine clients. In the remaining cases, both PoisonedFL and some existing attacks reduce the global models to random guessing. Furthermore, PoisonedFL remains effective across different FL settings including the fraction of malicious clients, the degree of non-IID of the genuine clients’ local training data, the number of epochs used to train a local model, and the fraction of clients selected to participate in each training round.

We also explore new defenses tailored to PoisonedFL. For instance, PoisonedFL enforces the malicious model updates across training rounds to have the same sign vector, but model updates from genuine clients may not satisfy such constraints. Therefore, we design a method to detect fake clients based on the sign vectors of the model updates across multiple training rounds. However, we can still adapt PoisonedFL to counter such defense with a minor impact on attack effectiveness. In particular, instead of using the same sign vector across all training rounds, our adapted PoisonedFL randomly flips a small portion of the sign vector in each training round when crafting the malicious model updates. Our results highlight the need for new defense mechanisms.

To summarize, our key contributions are as follows:

  • •

    We uncover a fundamental limitation of existing model poisoning attacks, i.e., their inconsistent malicious model updates across multiple training rounds self-cancel the attack effect, leading to suboptimal attack effectiveness.

  • •

    We propose PoisonedFL that leverages consistent malicious model updates, does not require knowledge about genuine local training data or local models, and is agnostic to the defense deployed by the server.

  • •

    We perform extensive experiments to demonstrate that PoisonedFL breaks state-of-the-art FL defenses and outperforms state-of-the-art attacks.

  • •

    We explore new defenses tailored to PoisonedFL and show their insufficiency by adapting PoisonedFL to counter them.

2.2. Poisoning Attacks to FL

In model poisoning attacks (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020; Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021), malicious clients corrupt the global model via sending carefully crafted malicious model updates to the server. In a targeted attack (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020), a poisoned global model only misclassifies attacker-chosen inputs with certain properties (e.g., embedded with an attacker-chosen trigger). In contrast, in an untargeted attack (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021), a poisoned global model indiscriminately misclassifies a large number of clean inputs, leading to a large testing error rate and eventually denial-of-service attacks. Therefore, we focus on untargeted attacks in this work.

Requiring genuine clients’ information:  One category of model poisoning attacks (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021) require that an attacker has access to genuine clients’ model updates or local training data. To obtain such knowledge, these attacks often assume an attacker can compromise a large fraction of genuine clients. For instance, in a little is enough (LIE) attack (Baruch et al., 2019), the attacker computes the average of the genuine model updates of the compromised genuine clients and adds perturbation to them to craft the malicious model updates in each training round. Concurrent to LIE, Fang et al. (Fang et al., 2020) proposed the first optimization-based attack framework that can be tailored to any aggregation rule. Their key idea is that the attacker crafts the malicious model updates such that the aggregated model update after attack deviates substantially from the before-attack one in each training round. Building upon this attack framework (Fang et al., 2020), the authors in (Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021) assume the malicious model update to be the sum of the before-attack aggregated model update and a fixed perturbation vector scaled by a factor, where the scaling factor is optimized to maximize the deviation between the after-attack aggregated model update and the before-attack one.

Not requiring genuine clients’ information:  The other category of model poisoning attacks (Cao and Gong, 2022) do not rely on any genuine clients’ information. Therefore, they only assume that an attacker can inject fake clients into an FL system, which is more realistic than compromising genuine clients. Since genuine clients’ information is not available, these attacks craft malicious model updates based on heuristics. For instance, in MPAF (Cao and Gong, 2022), the attacker crafts malicious model updates such that the global model progressively moves towards a random model in each training round.

Limitations of existing attacks:  Existing attacks suffer from the following limitations. First, the first category of attacks require an attacker to compromise a large fraction of genuine clients, which is not affordable in production FL (Shejwalkar et al., 2022). Second, both categories of attacks craft malicious model updates independently in different training rounds, leading to self-cancellation of attack effect across training rounds. As a result, they achieve suboptimal attack effectiveness when defenses are deployed.

2.3. Defenses against Poisoning Attacks

Defenses against poisoning attacks to FL can be roughly categorized into Byzantine-robust aggregation rules, provably robust defenses, and malicious clients detection.

Byzantine-robust aggregation rules:  Instead of using the average of the clients’ model updates to update the global model, Byzantine-robust aggregation rules (Blanchard et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2021a; Mhamdi et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2019; Yin et al., 2018; Fang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024) typically employ statistical analysis to filter out or clip outlier model updates before aggregating them. For instance, Multi-Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017) iteratively selects the model update that is the closest to its nearest neighbor model updates, and aggregates them. Dimension-wise Median (or Trimmed Mean) (Yin et al., 2018) uses the median (or trimmed-mean) of the clients’ local models for each dimension. Norm Bound (Sun et al., 2019) aims to minimize the impact of potential malicious model updates by clipping the magnitudes of all model updates. FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021a) assumes the server uses a clean and small dataset to bootstrap trust. Specifically, in FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021a), the server trains a server model and assigns trust scores to clients by measuring the similarity between the server model update and clients’ model updates. Under some assumptions about the learning problem and clients’ local training data distribution, these Byzantine-robust aggregation rules often can theoretically guarantee that the learnt global model does not change much when the number of malicious clients is bounded. However, they are still vulnerable to our attack because these assumptions often do not hold and a bounded change in the parameters of the global model does not guarantee a small change of error rate.

Provably robust defenses:  Provably robust defenses (Cao et al., 2021b, 2022; Xie et al., 2021) can provide stronger theoretical guarantees than Byzantine-robust aggregation rules. In particular, given a number of malicious clients, a provably robust defense can guarantee a lower bound of testing accuracy no matter what poisoning attacks the malicious clients use. For instance, FLCert (Cao et al., 2022) trains multiple global models, each of which is trained using a subset of clients. Given a testing input, FLCert predicts its label by taking a majority vote among the multiple global models. A major limitation of these provably robust defenses is that they can only tolerate a small number of malicious clients, i.e., the lower bound of testing accuracy reduces to 0 when the number of malicious clients is moderate. As a result, they are still vulnerable to our attack since an attacker can easily inject a large number of fake clients.

Malicious clients detection:  These defenses (Li et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022) aim to detect malicious clients during or after the training process. After detecting malicious clients, the server can further efficiently recover/re-train an accurate global model using the remaining clients (Cao et al., 2023). For instance, FLDetector (Zhang et al., 2022) aims to detect malicious clients by utilizing the inconsistency of a client’s model updates across multiple training rounds. Their key idea is that the model updates of a benign client in multiple training rounds are all calculated based on the client’s local training data, while the model updates of a malicious client are crafted without following the learning procedure. As a result, the model updates of a benign client satisfy certain consistency constraints across training rounds, while those of a malicious client do not, making it possible to distinguish between benign and malicious clients. In our attack, we enforce the malicious model updates of a malicious client to be consistent across training rounds, making FLDetector ineffective.

3. Threat Model

Attacker’s goal:  Following previous works  (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021), we consider the goal of an attacker is to poison the training process of FL such that the final learnt global model makes incorrect predictions for a large number of clean inputs indiscriminately. In other words, the poisoned global model has a large testing error rate. Such a global model would eventually be abandoned, leading to denial-of-service attacks (Barreno et al., 2006).

Attacker’s capability:  We assume that the attacker is capable of injecting fake clients into an FL system as malicious clients. We note that this is a practical threat model. Multiple platforms (sim, [n. d.]; Nox, [n. d.]; blu, [n. d.]) can be used to automatically inject fake devices/clients in a distributed system. For instance, when the FL system learns a global model on Android smartphones (i.e., clients), e.g., Google’s next-word prediction on Android devices (gbo, [n. d.]), an attacker can use a laptop to simulate fake smartphones via Android emulators (Cao and Gong, 2022).

Attacker’s background knowledge:  We consider the minimal background knowledge scenario, which represents the most realistic threat model. Specifically, the attacker has access to the global models during the training process since the server sends them to the clients including the fake ones. However, the attacker does not have access to genuine local training data nor local models on any genuine client. Moreover, we assume the attacker does not know the defense (e.g., Byzantine-robust aggregation rule, provably robust defense, or detector) deployed by the server.

We note that our threat model is the same as previous fake client based model poisoning attacks (Cao and Gong, 2022), which do not require genuine clients’ information.

4. Motivation

We first introduce our key observation about why existing model poisoning attacks achieve suboptimal attack effectiveness, which motivates the design of our PoisonedFL in the next section. We show experimental results on the MNIST dataset with the default parameter settings described in Section 6.1. We assume the server uses Trimmed Mean (Yin et al., 2018) as the defense. Moreover, we assume the ratio of fake clients to genuine clients is 20%percent2020\%. We consider MPAF (Cao and Gong, 2022), an attack not requiring genuine clients’ information, and Fang (Fang et al., 2020), a representative attack that requires genuine clients’ information. To give advantages to Fang, we assume the attacker knows the local models of all participating genuine clients in each round when crafting malicious model updates on fake clients.

In each training round, these attacks craft a malicious model update for each fake client selected by the server to participate in training. 5. PoisonedFL

5.1. Overview

Our PoisonedFL aims to craft malicious model updates on the fake clients such that the final global model is substantially moved along a random update direction, leading to a large testing error rate. Towards this goal, our malicious model updates on the fake clients are a dimension-wise product between a sign vector (characterizing the update direction of each dimension) and a magnitude vector (characterizing the update size of each dimension). The sign vector is randomly picked at the beginning and keeps the same across training rounds to enforce consistency; while the magnitude vector dynamically changes to simultaneously have large magnitudes and avoid being filtered out by the unknown defense that may be deployed by the server. In particular, we set the magnitude vector in a training round by considering whether our attack is successful in the previous training rounds to avoid being filtered out by a defense. Thus, the aggregated model updates across training rounds are likely to have consistent sign vectors (i.e., update direction) and add up instead of cancelling out with each other. As a result, the final global model is substantially moved along the random update direction, leading to a large testing error rate.

6.2. Main Results

Table 1 shows the testing error rates of the learnt global models under different attacks and defenses for the five datasets. “No Attack” means no fake clients are injected. We have the following observations.

PoisonedFL breaks state-of-the-art FL defenses:  PoisonedFL substantially increases the testing error rates of the global models learnt by FedAvg and state-of-the-art defenses, compared to No Attack. Specifically, PoisonedFL increases the testing error rate by 15.84 to 90.67 across the five datasets and nine FL methods. Moreover, in 29 out of the 45 cases, PoisonedFL makes the global model nearly random guessing or worse. Our results show that existing FL defenses are not robust even if an attacker has no knowledge about the defense nor the genuine clients’ local data or models.

Why PoisonedFL breaks FL defenses:  State-of-the-art defenses leverage filtering and clipping to reduce the impact of malicious model updates. They can weaken the attack effect in individual training rounds. However, since PoisonedFL crafts malicious model updates that are consistent across training rounds and avoid being entirely filtered out by dynamically adjusting attack magnitudes, its cumulative attack effect still substantially moves the global model towards the designated random update direction 𝒔𝒔\bm{s}, leading to a large testing error rate. The detailed analysis for each defense is defered to Appendix A.1.

PoisonedFL outperforms existing attacks:  For the non-robust FedAvg, both PoisonedFL and some existing attacks can reduce the global models to random guessing or worse. However, for the five datasets and eight FL defenses, our PoisonedFL achieves a much larger testing error rate than existing attacks in 32 out of the 40 cases with the gap ranging from 2.11 to 84.33. In six of the remaining cases, both our PoisonedFL and some existing attacks make the learnt global models nearly random guessing or worse. Our PoisonedFL achieves a smaller testing error rate than some existing attacks only in two cases. In particular, Fang achieves a testing error rate of 70.02 for the case (Purchase, TrMean), while PoisonedFL achieves a testing error rate of 53.86 in the case; and Min-Max (or Min-Sum) achieves a testing error rate of 94.88 (or 94.97) for (FEMNIST, FLDetector), while PoisonedFL achieves a testing error rate of 91.25 in the case. However, we note that PoisonedFL still achieves large testing error rates even in these rare cases.

Among the three attacks Random, MPAF, and PoisonedFL, which do not require knowledge about the local training data/models on the genuine clients nor the defense, Random is ineffective in most cases when a defense is deployed; while MPAF is effective to some extent at attacking some defenses, e.g., TrMean and Norm Bound, but ineffective at attacking other defenses, e.g., Multi-Krum, FLTrust, and FLDetector. As a result, the final learnt global model has a very large magnitude, leading to a large testing error rate. Fig. 6 shows the testing error rates under different defenses and attacks when the participation rate ranges from 0.01 to 1, where FashionMNIST dataset is used. Note that for these experiments, we use FashionMNIST instead of CIFAR-10 dataset, due to limited memory of our GPU server. Moreover, we do not have results for Multi-Krum in Fig. 6 when the participation rate is 1 due to the same reason on insufficient memory. For FLCert, the smallest participation rate is set to 0.03 instead of 0.01, because FLCert divides the clients into 10 groups, and if a participation rate of 0.01 is used, only one client is selected to participate in each training round. Consequently, attacks requiring genuine local models are not applicable. We observe that participation rate almost has no impact on the effectiveness of both existing attacks and our attack. Moreover, our attack consistently outperforms existing attacks across all considered participation rates.

6.4. Impact of Different PoisonedFL Variants

PoisonedFL crafts a malicious model update in each training round as a product of a given sign vector and a dynamically set magnitude vector, which is further decomposed into a product of an unit magnitude vector and a scaling factor. The unit magnitude vector and scaling factor play important roles in a malicious model update. Thus, we study different variants for designing them. We show results on CIFAR-10 dataset for simplicity. Due to limited space, we defer the discussion on impact of the sign vector in Appendix A.2.

Variants of the unit magnitude vector:  For the unit magnitude vector, we consider the following two variants.

  • •

    Same magnitude: In this variant, the attacker sets each dimension of the unit magnitude vector 𝒗tsuperscript𝒗𝑡\bm{v}^{t} to the same value. In other words, we set each dimension to be 1d1𝑑\frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}, where d𝑑d is the number of dimensions/parameters.

  • •

    Adaptive magnitude: In this variant, the dimensions of the unit magnitude vector have different magnitudes and are dynamically set in each training round based on how the global model changes and the malicious model updates in the previous training round. Equation 6 shows our adaptive unit magnitude vector adopted by PoisonedFL.

Fig. 7(a) shows the testing error rates under different defenses when PoisonedFL uses the two variants to set the unit magnitude vector, where the scaling factor is set using our adaptive method in Equation 7. Our results show that adaptive magnitude substantially outperforms same magnitude for some defenses (e.g., Multi-Krum and FLTrust) while the two variants achieve comparable effectiveness for other defenses. This is because adaptive magnitude can target specific dimensions with larger effectiveness.

Variants of the scaling factor:  We also consider two variants of setting the scaling factor as follows:

  • •

    Maximized scaling factor: This variant sets a very large scaling factor with a goal to maximize the magnitude of the aggregated model update. We use 100,000100000100,000 in our experiments.

  • •

    Adaptive scaling factor: This variant, adopted by PoisonedFL, leverages how the global model changes in the previous training round to set the scaling factor (i.e., Equation 7) to avoid that the malicious model updates are filtered out by the server.

Fig. 7(b) illustrates the testing error rates under different defenses when PoisonedFL uses the two variants of setting the scaling factor, where adaptive magnitude is used to set the unit magnitude vector. We observe that, under some defenses (e.g., Median, TrMean, and Norm Bound), the variant of maximized scaling factor achieves comparable testing error rates with adaptive scaling factor. This is because, for dimension-wise aggregation rules like Median and TrMean, although the malicious model updates with very large magnitudes are filtered out, they still deviate the aggregated model update; and although Norm Bound normalizes the norms of malicious model updates, the normalized malicious model updates still maintain consistency across training rounds. However, for other defenses like Multi-Krum and FLDetector that filter out entire model updates, adaptive scaling factor substantially outperforms maximized scaling factor. This is because these defenses filter out the entire malicious model updates with very large magnitudes. In contrast, adaptive scaling factor leverages how the global model changes in the past to achieve a balance between attack effectiveness and undetectability. Since PoisonedFL aims to be defense-agnostic, adaptive scaling factor is preferred.

7. Countermeasures and Adaptive Attacks

We explore new defenses tailored to PoisonedFL. Specifically, we study a new defense that normalizes the total aggregated model update since PoisonedFL aims to increase its magnitude. We also explore new tailored defenses to detect fake clients based on how PoisonedFL crafts the malicious model updates. We show that the normalization-based defense has limited effectiveness at mitigating PoisonedFL and we can still adapt PoisonedFL to break the detection based defenses.

In our tailored detection based defenses, the server extracts a feature for each client in each training round and then divides the clients into two clusters via fitting a two-component Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) (Permuter et al., 2006) for the features. We detect a particular cluster (e.g., with the smaller mean value) as fake clients. After detecting fake clients, the server removes them and re-trains a global model using the remaining clients. Note that if the distance between the mean values of the two clusters is smaller than the intra-cluster standard deviations of both clusters, no clients are detected as fake and the training continues since the difference between the two clusters may simply be caused by randomness.

We extract features for clients based on how PoisonedFL crafts malicious model updates. Depending on which information of the malicious model updates is used to extract features, we consider two variants of GMM, i.e., GMM-Sign and GMM-Magnitude, which extract features based on the magnitudes and signs of the model updates, respectively.

8. Discussion and Limitations

Using synthetic data:  In our previous experiments, when applying attacks that require genuine clients’ local models to craft malicious model updates, we assume the attacker has access to the local models on all genuine clients. This assumption is strong and not realistic. Therefore, we also explore that an attacker uses the global models to reconstruct synthetic data, train local models using the synthetic data, and then craft malicious model updates with them.

Reconstructing training data from a model is known as model inversion (Fredrikson et al., 2015). Previous work (Pi et al., 2023) shows that the global-model trajectory in multiple training rounds (especially early ones) of FL can be used to reconstruct synthetic data that is similar to the clients’ genuine local training data. We follow the setting and generation strategy in (Pi et al., 2023), which is the state-of-the-art reconstruction method in FL, to generate synthetic data for MNIST and FashionMNIST. We use MNIST and FashionMNIST because they are simpler datasets and easier to reconstruct, giving advantages to these attacks. Specifically, we assume no attacks in the first 404040 rounds and use the corresponding clean global models to reconstruct synthetic data. Table 2 shows the testing error rates of the classifiers trained on the synthetic data and evaluated on the genuine testing datasets of MNIST and FashionMNIST. The results show that the classifiers have decent testing error rates and thus the synthetic data mimic the genuine data well, which is consistent with prior work (Pi et al., 2023).

We then use the synthetic data on the fake clients to perform model poisoning attacks in the remaining training rounds. Since each genuine client has 50 local training examples, we sample 50 synthetic training examples for each fake client. In each training round, the fake clients selected to participate in training train local models using their synthetic local training examples; and then we craft malicious model updates based on the local models for existing attacks. Table 3 shows the testing error rates of the final global models under different attacks and defenses. We observe that an existing attack achieves higher testing error rates when having access to local models on the genuine clients in most cases, compared to using local models trained on synthetic data. PoisonedFL does not require genuine local models nor synthetic data but substantially outperforms these attacks.

Cross-silo FL:  PoisonedFL primarily targets cross-device FL (Kairouz et al., 2021), in which the clients are end-user devices such as smartphones. An attacker can inject fake clients into a cross-device FL system due to the open nature of the system. In particular, any client including fake ones can participate in the FL system. We acknowledge that it is harder to apply both PoisonedFL and existing attacks to cross-silo FL. Specifically, the clients are often a small number of verified institutions such as banks and hospitals in cross-silo FL. These institutions often go through certain verification processes before jointly training a model using FL. As a result, it is harder for an attacker to compromise genuine clients/institutions or inject fake clients/institutions in such a cross-silo FL system. We believe it is an interesting future work to identify a realistic threat model for cross-silo FL that considers the unique challenges and constraints.

9. Conclusion

In this work, we uncover the fundamental self-cancellation issue of existing model poisoning attacks to FL. Based on our discovery, we propose PoisonedFL, a new model poisoning attack to FL. We show that, by maintaining multi-round consistency, an attacker can substantially increase the testing error rate of the learnt global model, even if the attacker does not have access to the genuine local training data, genuine local models, or deployed defense. PoisonedFL highlights the severe vulnerability of FL and the urgency for developing new defense mechanisms.

Appendix A Additional Experimental Results

A.1. Analyzing the Effectiveness of PoisonedFL

Why PoisonedFL breaks FL defenses:  In this section, we provide a detailed analysis of why PoisonedFL breaks existing defenses as follows:

  • •

    Median and TrMean:  Even though the malicious model updates are not necessarily selected, their consistent nature over multiple training rounds biases the aggregation of each dimension towards the designated random update direction.

  • •

    Norm Bound:  Even if our attack effect is weakened in each training round, its cumulative effect over multiple training rounds remains pronounced with a significant magnitude for the total aggregated model update.

  • •

    Multi-Krum and FLAME:  For filtering-involved defenses based on Euclidean distance or cosine similarity, such as Multi-Krum and FLAME, PoisonedFL maintains effectiveness because the malicious model updates are not entirely filtered out in many training rounds due to dynamic magnitude adjustment. For instance, Table 6 shows that a large fraction of all malicious model updates are not filtered out by FLAME and Multi-Krum with non-zero false negative rates.

  • •

    FLTrust:  Table 7 shows the normalized trust score of a malicious/benign model update of FLTrust across rounds. The normalized trust score is calculated as T​Si∑T​S𝑇subscript𝑆𝑖𝑇𝑆\frac{TS_{i}}{\sum{TS}}, where T​Si𝑇subscript𝑆𝑖TS_{i} is the trust score for client i𝑖i, ∑T​S𝑇𝑆\sum{TS} is the summation of trust scores for all sampled clients in a round. The result shows that malicious model updates are not filtered out in multiple rounds (with a large normalized trust score), thereby the attack accumulates to corrupt the model.

  • •

    FLDetector:  The detection mechanism FLDetector relies on inconsistent model updates of malicious clients. Since the malicious model updates are consistent in PoisonedFL, FLDetector falsely classifies all malicious clients as benign across all datasets.

  • •

    FLCert:  FLCert is vulnerable to PoisonedFL since the global models for ensembling are learnt by existing aggregation rules, which are vulnerable to PoisonedFL.

A.2. Impact of the Sign Vector

We further analyze the impact of the random sign vectors. We experiment with PoisonedFL on CIFAR-10 with 666 random seeds (111-666) for various random sign vectors and defenses including TrMean, FLTrust, and FLAME, as shown in Table 8. The result aligns with our intuition that these random sign vectors do not affect the attack performance and PoisonedFL can successfully break the defenses with these 666 different random sign vectors. The reason is that in a continuous attack with the same random direction, the model inevitably updates substantially in a random direction in an uncontrolled way, and therefore leads to significantly degraded accuracy.

B.3. Defenses

We evaluate eight state-of-the-art defenses, including six Byzantine-robust aggregation rules (Multi-Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017), Median (Yin et al., 2018), Trimmed Mean (Yin et al., 2018), Norm Bound (Sun et al., 2019), FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021a), and FLAME (Nguyen et al., 2022)), one provably robust defense (FLCert (Cao et al., 2022)) and one malicious clients detection method (FLDetector (Zhang et al., 2022)). We also consider the non-robust FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) as a baseline.

Multi-Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017):  Multi-Krum uses an iterative method to select a subset of clients’ model updates. In each step, it selects the model update that has the smallest sum of Euclidean distance to its n−2𝑛2n-2 neighbors, where n𝑛n is the number of genuine clients. This selection process continues until n𝑛n clients are chosen. Finally, the server computes the average of the n𝑛n selected model updates as the aggregated model update.

Median (Yin et al., 2018):  In Median aggregation rule, the server computes the coordinate-wise median of the clients’ model updates as the aggregated model update.

Trimmed Mean (TrMean) (Yin et al., 2018):  For each dimension, the server first removes the largest m𝑚m values and smallest m𝑚m values, and then computes the average of the remaining n−m𝑛𝑚n-m values, where m𝑚m is the number of fake clients.

Norm Bound (Sun et al., 2019):  In Norm Bound aggregation rule, the server first clips each client’s model update to have a predetermined norm and then computes the average of the clipped model updates as the aggregated model update. We follow prior work (Shejwalkar et al., 2022) to set the predetermined norm as the average norm of the genuine model updates in each training round.

FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021a):  FLTrust assumes that the server has a small, clean dataset to bootstrap trust. We assume the server’s dataset includes training examples selected uniformly at random. The size of the server’s dataset is 100 for MNIST, FashionMNIST, and CIFAR-10, and 200 for Purchase and FEMNIST.

FLAME (Nguyen et al., 2022):  In each training round, FLAME considers the cosine similarity between clients’ local models (i.e., a client’s model update + current global model) to divide clients into clusters and filter out the clusters containing potentially malicious model updates.

FLCert (Cao et al., 2022):  FLCert divides clients into multiple groups and trains a global model for each group using any existing FL algorithm (we use Median (Yin et al., 2018)). Given a testing input, FLCert predicts its label based on the majority vote among these global models. We divide the clients into 10 disjoint groups uniformly at random in our experiments.

FLDetector (Zhang et al., 2022):  FLDetector aims to detect malicious clients during training. We first apply FLDetector with the full participation of clients for detection for 300 communication rounds. Following (Zhang et al., 2022), if some clients are detected as malicious, we remove them from the system. Then we re-train a global model from scratch based on the remaining clients using Median (Yin et al., 2018) aggregation rule with the default setting.

B.4. Compared Attacks

We compare our PoisonedFL with seven attacks, including five attacks (Fang (Fang et al., 2020), Opt. Fang (Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021), LIE (Baruch et al., 2019), Min-Max (Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021), and Min-Sum (Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021)) that require genuine clients’ information and two attacks (Random (Cao and Gong, 2022) and MPAF (Cao and Gong, 2022)) that do not require such information. Note that when applying the first category of attacks to craft the malicious model updates on fake clients, we assume that the attacker has access to the model updates of all genuine clients and the aggregation rule used by the server. In Section 8, we also study the scenarios where an attacker uses the global models to reconstruct synthetic data on the fake clients and uses the local models trained on them to perform attacks. We do not make these assumptions for Random, MPAF, and our PoisonedFL. Therefore, we give advantages to the first category of attacks.

Fang (Fang et al., 2020):  In this attack, the attacker crafts malicious model updates for fake clients such that the aggregated model update after attack deviates substantially from the before-attack one. Fang has different versions for different aggregation rules. We apply their Krum attack for Multi-Krum defense and TrMean attack for others.

Opt. Fang (Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021):  Following Fang, this attack also aims to maximize the deviation between the after-attack aggregated model update and before-attack one, but it uses a different way to solve the malicious model update. Specifically, a malicious model update is a variant of the average of all genuine clients’ model updates.
