Abstract.

Model poisoning attacks are critical security threats to Federated Learning (FL). Existing model poisoning attacks suffer from two key limitations: 1) they achieve suboptimal effectiveness when defenses are deployed, and/or 2) they require knowledge of the model updates or local training data on genuine clients. In this work, we make a key observation that their suboptimal effectiveness arises from only leveraging model-update consistency among malicious clients within individual training rounds, making the attack effect self-cancel across training rounds. In light of this observation, we propose PoisonedFL, which enforces multi-round consistency among the malicious clientsâ€™ model updates while not requiring any knowledge about the genuine clients. Our empirical evaluation on five benchmark datasets shows that PoisonedFL breaks eight state-of-the-art defenses and outperforms seven existing model poisoning attacks. Moreover, we also explore new defenses that are tailored to PoisonedFL, but our results show that we can still adapt PoisonedFL to break them. Our study shows that FL systems are considerably less robust than previously thought, underlining the urgency for the development of new defense mechanisms.

1. Introduction

Federated Learning (FL) enables clientsâ€“such as smartphones, self-driving cars, and IoT devicesâ€“to collaboratively train a model without sharing their raw local training data with a central serverÂ (KoneÄná»³ etÂ al., 2016; McMahan etÂ al., 2017). Typically, FL involves multiple training rounds, each of which consists of three key steps: 1) the server sends the current global model to the clients or a subset of them; 2) a client trains a local model based on the current global model and its local training data, and sends the model update (i.e., the difference between local model and global model) to the server; and 3) the server aggregates the clientsâ€™ model updates according to an aggregation rule and adds the aggregated model update to the global model. FL has wide applications across diverse domains, including healthcareÂ (mel, [n.â€‰d.]; Kairouz etÂ al., 2021; Rieke etÂ al., 2020) and financeÂ (web, [n.â€‰d.]; Yang etÂ al., 2019).

However, due to the distributed nature, FL is fundamentally vulnerable to model poisoning attacksÂ (Baruch etÂ al., 2019; Cao and Gong, 2022; Fang etÂ al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021). In particular, an attacker can control some malicious clients and send carefully crafted malicious model updates to the server to poison the global model. The malicious clients could be compromised genuine clients or injected fake clients. The poisoned global model misclassifies many inputs indiscriminately, i.e., it has a large testing error rate (also known as untargeted model poisoning attacks).

Existing model poisoning attacks suffer from two key limitations. First, theyÂ (Baruch etÂ al., 2019; Fang etÂ al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021; Cao and Gong, 2022) only leverage in-round consistency, i.e., they send the same or similar malicious model updates in a single training round to amplify the attack effect, but neglect multi-round consistency. For instance, Fang attackÂ (Fang etÂ al., 2020) crafts the malicious model updates in a given training round to maximally deviate the global model in that round, while MPAFÂ (Cao and Gong, 2022) sets the malicious model updates in a training round as the magnified difference between the current global model and a random model. Such a strategy limits the attack effectiveness when defenses are deployed. This is because defenses weaken the attack effect in every training round by filtering and clipping the malicious model updates; and the weakened attack effects further self-cancel across training rounds due to the lack of multi-round consistency, as shown by our measurement results in SectionÂ 4. Second, most existing attacksÂ (Baruch etÂ al., 2019; Fang etÂ al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021) require knowledge of genuine clientsâ€™ model updates or local training data. They often assume compromising a significant number of genuine clients to obtain such knowledge, which is notably impractical. These attacks with suboptimal effectiveness and/or impractical assumptions give a false sense of FL security.

Our work:Â  In this work, we propose PoisonedFL, a novel model poisoning attack to FL, which overcomes the above limitations of existing attacks. Specifically, to enhance attack effectiveness, PoisonedFL leverages two key components: 1) multi-round consistency, and 2) dynamic attack magnitude adjustment. The first component ensures that even if the attack effect is weakened in individual training rounds by defenses, the cumulative attack effect over multiple training rounds remains undeterred. The second component aims to prevent the malicious model updates from being entirely filtered out in individual training rounds by defenses. Moreover, PoisonedFL does not require genuine clientsâ€™ information, and is agnostic to the defenses deployed by the server.

For the first component, our key observation is that a model, when updated substantially in a random direction, exhibits significantly degraded accuracy (see Fig.Â 10 in Appendix). Based on this observation, we formulate an all-round optimization problem that facilitates multi-round consistency. Specifically, the objective of our optimization problem is to maximize the magnitude of the total aggregated model updates in a random update direction, which is characterized by a random sign vector ğ’”ğ’”\bm{s}. Each dimension of the sign vector ğ’”ğ’”\bm{s} is either +1 or -1 uniformly at random, meaning that the total aggregated model updates increase or decrease the corresponding parameter of the global model. With such total aggregated model updates, the final global model is substantially moved along the random update direction, leading to a large testing error rate. Note that our objective stands substantially distinct from those of existing attacks, as it enforces multi-round consistency without self-cancellation. However, it is challenging to solve the all-round optimization problem in a given round because the model updates in future training rounds are not available. To address the challenge, we transform the optimization problem as a per-round one, which aims to find malicious model updates on the malicious clients to maximize the magnitude of the aggregated model update along the update direction ğ’”ğ’”\bm{s} in each round. In particular, our malicious model update is a dimension-wise product of the sign vector ğ’”ğ’”\bm{s} and a magnitude vector, a dimension of which indicates the magnitude of the corresponding dimension of the malicious model update.

For the second component, we propose to dynamically adjust the magnitude vector in each training round based on the attack results in the past rounds, making our attack effective against the unknown defense that the server may have deployed. Our key idea is to find a magnitude vector that has a large magnitude while avoiding the malicious model update being filtered out by the unknown defense. On one hand, based on the global models and malicious model updates in the previous training rounds, we estimate the model updates of genuine clients to determine an unit magnitude vector. The magnitude vector is the product of the unit magnitude vector and a scaling factor. On the other hand, we set the scaling factor based on whether the attack achieves the objective in the past rounds. In particular, our attack objective is that the total aggregated model updates have the random update direction ğ’”ğ’”\bm{s}. We leverage statistical hypothesis testing to check whether our attack in the previous rounds achieves this objective. If not, it means the malicious updates were filtered out by the defense in previous rounds and thus we decrease the scaling factor to be more stealthy.

We evaluate our PoisonedFL on five benchmark datasets. Our results show that, under the least knowledge scenario â€“ that is, no knowledge about the defense nor genuine local training data/models â€“ PoisonedFL can break eight state-of-the-art FL defensesÂ (Blanchard etÂ al., 2017; Cao etÂ al., 2021a, 2022; Nguyen etÂ al., 2022; Sun etÂ al., 2019; Yin etÂ al., 2018; Zhang etÂ al., 2022). For instance, PoisonedFL can reduce the global models to random guessing in many cases. Moreover, in most cases, PoisonedFL achieves higher testing error rates than existing attacksÂ (Baruch etÂ al., 2019; Fang etÂ al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021; Cao and Gong, 2022) when a defense is deployed even if we assume the attacker has access to the local models of all genuine clients. In the remaining cases, both PoisonedFL and some existing attacks reduce the global models to random guessing. Furthermore, PoisonedFL remains effective across different FL settings including the fraction of malicious clients, the degree of non-IID of the genuine clientsâ€™ local training data, the number of epochs used to train a local model, and the fraction of clients selected to participate in each training round.

We also explore new defenses tailored to PoisonedFL. For instance, PoisonedFL enforces the malicious model updates across training rounds to have the same sign vector, but model updates from genuine clients may not satisfy such constraints. Therefore, we design a method to detect fake clients based on the sign vectors of the model updates across multiple training rounds. However, we can still adapt PoisonedFL to counter such defense with a minor impact on attack effectiveness. In particular, instead of using the same sign vector across all training rounds, our adapted PoisonedFL randomly flips a small portion of the sign vector in each training round when crafting the malicious model updates. Our results highlight the need for new defense mechanisms.

2.2. Poisoning Attacks to FL

In model poisoning attacksÂ (Bagdasaryan etÂ al., 2020; Baruch etÂ al., 2019; Fang etÂ al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021), malicious clients corrupt the global model via sending carefully crafted malicious model updates to the server. In a targeted attackÂ (Bagdasaryan etÂ al., 2020), a poisoned global model only misclassifies attacker-chosen inputs with certain properties (e.g., embedded with an attacker-chosen trigger). In contrast, in an untargeted attackÂ (Baruch etÂ al., 2019; Fang etÂ al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021), a poisoned global model indiscriminately misclassifies a large number of clean inputs, leading to a large testing error rate and eventually denial-of-service attacks. Therefore, we focus on untargeted attacks in this work.

Requiring genuine clientsâ€™ information:Â  One category of model poisoning attacksÂ (Baruch etÂ al., 2019; Fang etÂ al., 2020; Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021) require that an attacker has access to genuine clientsâ€™ model updates or local training data. To obtain such knowledge, these attacks often assume an attacker can compromise a large fraction of genuine clients. For instance, in a little is enough (LIE) attackÂ (Baruch etÂ al., 2019), the attacker computes the average of the genuine model updates of the compromised genuine clients and adds perturbation to them to craft the malicious model updates in each training round. Concurrent to LIE, Fang et al.Â (Fang etÂ al., 2020) proposed the first optimization-based attack framework that can be tailored to any aggregation rule. Their key idea is that the attacker crafts the malicious model updates such that the aggregated model update after attack deviates substantially from the before-attack one in each training round. Building upon this attack frameworkÂ (Fang etÂ al., 2020), the authors inÂ (Shejwalkar and Houmansadr, 2021) assume the malicious model update to be the sum of the before-attack aggregated model update and a fixed perturbation vector scaled by a factor, where the scaling factor is optimized to maximize the deviation between the after-attack aggregated model update and the before-attack one.

Not requiring genuine clientsâ€™ information:Â  The other category of model poisoning attacksÂ (Cao and Gong, 2022) do not rely on any genuine clientsâ€™ information. Therefore, they only assume that an attacker can inject fake clients into an FL system, which is more realistic than compromising genuine clients. Since genuine clientsâ€™ information is not available, these attacks craft malicious model updates based on heuristics. For instance, in MPAFÂ (Cao and Gong, 2022), the attacker crafts malicious model updates such that the global model progressively moves towards a random model in each training round.

Limitations of existing attacks:Â  Existing attacks suffer from the following limitations. First, the first category of attacks require an attacker to compromise a large fraction of genuine clients, which is not affordable in production FLÂ (Shejwalkar etÂ al., 2022). Second, both categories of attacks craft malicious model updates independently in different training rounds, leading to self-cancellation of attack effect across training rounds. As a result, they achieve suboptimal attack effectiveness when defenses are deployed.

2.3. Defenses against Poisoning Attacks

Defenses against poisoning attacks to FL can be roughly categorized into Byzantine-robust aggregation rules, provably robust defenses, and malicious clients detection.

Byzantine-robust aggregation rules:Â  Instead of using the average of the clientsâ€™ model updates to update the global model, Byzantine-robust aggregation rulesÂ (Blanchard etÂ al., 2017; Cao etÂ al., 2021a; Mhamdi etÂ al., 2018; Nguyen etÂ al., 2022; Sun etÂ al., 2019; Yin etÂ al., 2018; Fang etÂ al., 2022; Xu etÂ al., 2024) typically employ statistical analysis to filter out or clip outlier model updates before aggregating them. For instance, Multi-KrumÂ (Blanchard etÂ al., 2017) iteratively selects the model update that is the closest to its nearest neighbor model updates, and aggregates them. Dimension-wise Median (or Trimmed Mean)Â (Yin etÂ al., 2018) uses the median (or trimmed-mean) of the clientsâ€™ local models for each dimension. Norm BoundÂ (Sun etÂ al., 2019) aims to minimize the impact of potential malicious model updates by clipping the magnitudes of all model updates. FLTrustÂ (Cao etÂ al., 2021a) assumes the server uses a clean and small dataset to bootstrap trust. Specifically, in FLTrustÂ (Cao etÂ al., 2021a), the server trains a server model and assigns trust scores to clients by measuring the similarity between the server model update and clientsâ€™ model updates. Under some assumptions about the learning problem and clientsâ€™ local training data distribution, these Byzantine-robust aggregation rules often can theoretically guarantee that the learnt global model does not change much when the number of malicious clients is bounded. However, they are still vulnerable to our attack because these assumptions often do not hold and a bounded change in the parameters of the global model does not guarantee a small change of error rate.

Provably robust defenses:Â  Provably robust defensesÂ (Cao etÂ al., 2021b, 2022; Xie etÂ al., 2021) can provide stronger theoretical guarantees than Byzantine-robust aggregation rules. In particular, given a number of malicious clients, a provably robust defense can guarantee a lower bound of testing accuracy no matter what poisoning attacks the malicious clients use. For instance, FLCertÂ (Cao etÂ al., 2022) trains multiple global models, each of which is trained using a subset of clients. Given a testing input, FLCert predicts its label by taking a majority vote among the multiple global models. A major limitation of these provably robust defenses is that they can only tolerate a small number of malicious clients, i.e., the lower bound of testing accuracy reduces to 0 when the number of malicious clients is moderate. As a result, they are still vulnerable to our attack since an attacker can easily inject a large number of fake clients.

Malicious clients detection:Â  These defensesÂ (Li etÂ al., 2020; Zhang etÂ al., 2022) aim to detect malicious clients during or after the training process. After detecting malicious clients, the server can further efficiently recover/re-train an accurate global model using the remaining clientsÂ (Cao etÂ al., 2023). For instance, FLDetectorÂ (Zhang etÂ al., 2022) aims to detect malicious clients by utilizing the inconsistency of a clientâ€™s model updates across multiple training rounds. Their key idea is that the model updates of a benign client in multiple training rounds are all calculated based on the clientâ€™s local training data, while the model updates of a malicious client are crafted without following the learning procedure. As a result, the model updates of a benign client satisfy certain consistency constraints across training rounds, while those of a malicious client do not, making it possible to distinguish between benign and malicious clients. In our attack, we enforce the malicious model updates of a malicious client to be consistent across training rounds, making FLDetector ineffective.

5. PoisonedFL

5.1. Overview

Our PoisonedFL aims to craft malicious model updates on the fake clients such that the final global model is substantially moved along a random update direction, leading to a large testing error rate. Towards this goal, our malicious model updates on the fake clients are a dimension-wise product between a sign vector (characterizing the update direction of each dimension) and a magnitude vector (characterizing the update size of each dimension). The sign vector is randomly picked at the beginning and keeps the same across training rounds to enforce consistency; while the magnitude vector dynamically changes to simultaneously have large magnitudes and avoid being filtered out by the unknown defense that may be deployed by the server. In particular, we set the magnitude vector in a training round by considering whether our attack is successful in the previous training rounds to avoid being filtered out by a defense. Thus, the aggregated model updates across training rounds are likely to have consistent sign vectors (i.e., update direction) and add up instead of cancelling out with each other. As a result, the final global model is substantially moved along the random update direction, leading to a large testing error rate.

We do not observe any particular pattern with respect to how the effectiveness of an existing attack varies as the number of local training epochs changes. For instance, MPAF achieves a larger testing error rate as the number of local training epochs increases under Multi-Krum, but its testing error rate decreases under defenses like TrMean and Norm Bound.

Impact of the Participation Rate:Â  In each training round, the server typically selects a fraction (called participation rate) of the clients to participate in training. We use participation rate of 0.1 by default in our experiments. Fig.Â 6 shows the testing error rates under different defenses and attacks when the participation rate ranges from 0.01 to 1, where FashionMNIST dataset is used. Note that for these experiments, we use FashionMNIST instead of CIFAR-10 dataset, due to limited memory of our GPU server. Moreover, we do not have results for Multi-Krum in Fig.Â 6 when the participation rate is 1 due to the same reason on insufficient memory. Consequently, attacks requiring genuine local models are not applicable. We observe that participation rate almost has no impact on the effectiveness of both existing attacks and our attack. Moreover, our attack consistently outperforms existing attacks across all considered participation rates.

6.4. Impact of Different PoisonedFL Variants

PoisonedFL crafts a malicious model update in each training round as a product of a given sign vector and a dynamically set magnitude vector, which is further decomposed into a product of an unit magnitude vector and a scaling factor. The unit magnitude vector and scaling factor play important roles in a malicious model update. Thus, we study different variants for designing them. We show results on CIFAR-10 dataset for simplicity. Due to limited space, we defer the discussion on impact of the sign vector in AppendixÂ A.2.

Variants of the unit magnitude vector:Â  For the unit magnitude vector, we consider the following two variants.

  • â€¢

    Same magnitude: In this variant, the attacker sets each dimension of the unit magnitude vector ğ’—tsuperscriptğ’—ğ‘¡\bm{v}^{t} to the same value. In other words, we set each dimension to be 1d1ğ‘‘\frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}, where dğ‘‘d is the number of dimensions/parameters.

  • â€¢

Our results show that adaptive magnitude substantially outperforms same magnitude for some defenses (e.g., Multi-Krum and FLTrust) while the two variants achieve comparable effectiveness for other defenses. This is because adaptive magnitude can target specific dimensions with larger effectiveness.

Variants of the scaling factor:Â  We also consider two variants of setting the scaling factor as follows:

  • â€¢

    Maximized scaling factor: This variant sets a very large scaling factor with a goal to maximize the magnitude of the aggregated model update. We use 100,000100000100,000 in our experiments.

  • â€¢

    Adaptive scaling factor: This variant, adopted by PoisonedFL, leverages how the global model changes in the previous training round to set the scaling factor (i.e., EquationÂ 7) to avoid that the malicious model updates are filtered out by the server.

Fig.Â 7(b) illustrates the testing error rates under different defenses when PoisonedFL uses the two variants of setting the scaling factor, where adaptive magnitude is used to set the unit magnitude vector. We observe that, under some defenses (e.g., Median, TrMean, and Norm Bound), the variant of maximized scaling factor achieves comparable testing error rates with adaptive scaling factor. This is because, for dimension-wise aggregation rules like Median and TrMean, although the malicious model updates with very large magnitudes are filtered out, they still deviate the aggregated model update; and although Norm Bound normalizes the norms of malicious model updates, the normalized malicious model updates still maintain consistency across training rounds. However, for other defenses like Multi-Krum and FLDetector that filter out entire model updates, adaptive scaling factor substantially outperforms maximized scaling factor. This is because these defenses filter out the entire malicious model updates with very large magnitudes. In contrast, adaptive scaling factor leverages how the global model changes in the past to achieve a balance between attack effectiveness and undetectability. Since PoisonedFL aims to be defense-agnostic, adaptive scaling factor is preferred.

7. Countermeasures and Adaptive Attacks

We explore new defenses tailored to PoisonedFL. Specifically, we study a new defense that normalizes the total aggregated model update since PoisonedFL aims to increase its magnitude. We also explore new tailored defenses to detect fake clients based on how PoisonedFL crafts the malicious model updates. We show that the normalization-based defense has limited effectiveness at mitigating PoisonedFL and we can still adapt PoisonedFL to break the detection based defenses.

In our tailored detection based defenses, the server extracts a feature for each client in each training round and then divides the clients into two clusters via fitting a two-component Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)Â (Permuter etÂ al., 2006) for the features. We detect a particular cluster (e.g., with the smaller mean value) as fake clients. After detecting fake clients, the server removes them and re-trains a global model using the remaining clients. Note that if the distance between the mean values of the two clusters is smaller than the intra-cluster standard deviations of both clusters, no clients are detected as fake and the training continues since the difference between the two clusters may simply be caused by randomness.

We extract features for clients based on how PoisonedFL crafts malicious model updates. Depending on which information of the malicious model updates is used to extract features, we consider two variants of GMM, i.e., GMM-Sign and GMM-Magnitude, which extract features based on the magnitudes and signs of the model updates, respectively.

8. Discussion and Limitations

Using synthetic data:Â  In our previous experiments, when applying attacks that require genuine clientsâ€™ local models to craft malicious model updates, we assume the attacker has access to the local models on all genuine clients. This assumption is strong and not realistic. Therefore, we also explore that an attacker uses the global models to reconstruct synthetic data, train local models using the synthetic data, and then craft malicious model updates with them.

Reconstructing training data from a model is known as model inversionÂ (Fredrikson etÂ al., 2015). Previous workÂ (Pi etÂ al., 2023) shows that the global-model trajectory in multiple training rounds (especially early ones) of FL can be used to reconstruct synthetic data that is similar to the clientsâ€™ genuine local training data. We follow the setting and generation strategy in (Pi etÂ al., 2023), which is the state-of-the-art reconstruction method in FL, to generate synthetic data for MNIST and FashionMNIST. We use MNIST and FashionMNIST because they are simpler datasets and easier to reconstruct, giving advantages to these attacks. Specifically, we assume no attacks in the first 404040 rounds and use the corresponding clean global models to reconstruct synthetic data. Table 2 shows the testing error rates of the classifiers trained on the synthetic data and evaluated on the genuine testing datasets of MNIST and FashionMNIST. The results show that the classifiers have decent testing error rates and thus the synthetic data mimic the genuine data well, which is consistent with prior workÂ (Pi etÂ al., 2023).

We then use the synthetic data on the fake clients to perform model poisoning attacks in the remaining training rounds. Since each genuine client has 50 local training examples, we sample 50 synthetic training examples for each fake client. In each training round, the fake clients selected to participate in training train local models using their synthetic local training examples; and then we craft malicious model updates based on the local models for existing attacks. TableÂ 3 shows the testing error rates of the final global models under different attacks and defenses. We observe that an existing attack achieves higher testing error rates when having access to local models on the genuine clients in most cases, compared to using local models trained on synthetic data. PoisonedFL does not require genuine local models nor synthetic data but substantially outperforms these attacks.

Cross-silo FL:Â  PoisonedFL primarily targets cross-device FLÂ (Kairouz etÂ al., 2021), in which the clients are end-user devices such as smartphones. An attacker can inject fake clients into a cross-device FL system due to the open nature of the system. In particular, any client including fake ones can participate in the FL system. We acknowledge that it is harder to apply both PoisonedFL and existing attacks to cross-silo FL. Specifically, the clients are often a small number of verified institutions such as banks and hospitals in cross-silo FL. These institutions often go through certain verification processes before jointly training a model using FL. As a result, it is harder for an attacker to compromise genuine clients/institutions or inject fake clients/institutions in such a cross-silo FL system. We believe it is an interesting future work to identify a realistic threat model for cross-silo FL that considers the unique challenges and constraints.

9. Conclusion

In this work, we uncover the fundamental self-cancellation issue of existing model poisoning attacks to FL. Based on our discovery, we propose PoisonedFL, a new model poisoning attack to FL. We show that, by maintaining multi-round consistency, an attacker can substantially increase the testing error rate of the learnt global model, even if the attacker does not have access to the genuine local training data, genuine local models, or deployed defense. PoisonedFL highlights the severe vulnerability of FL and the urgency for developing new defense mechanisms.
