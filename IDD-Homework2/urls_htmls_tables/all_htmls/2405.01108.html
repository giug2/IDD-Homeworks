Abstract

In the pursuit of refining precise perception models for fully autonomous driving, continual online model training becomes essential. Federated Learning (FL) within vehicular networks offers an efficient mechanism for model training while preserving raw sensory data integrity. Yet, FL struggles with non-identically distributed data (e.g., quantity skew), leading to suboptimal convergence rates during model training. In previous work, we introduced FedLA, an innovative Label-Aware aggregation method addressing data heterogeneity in FL for generic scenarios.

In this paper, we introduce FedProx+LA, a novel FL method building upon the state-of-the-art FedProx and FedLA to tackle data heterogeneity, which is specifically tailored for vehicular networks. We evaluate the efficacy of FedProx+LA in continuous online object detection model training. Through a comparative analysis against conventional and state-of-the-art methods, our findings reveal the superior convergence rate of FedProx+LA. Notably, if the label distribution is very heterogeneous, our FedProx+LA approach shows substantial improvements in detection performance compared to baseline methods, also outperforming our previous FedLA approach. Moreover, both FedLA and FedProx+LA increase convergence speed by 30% compared to baseline methods.

I Introduction

To enable fully autonomous driving, Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADASs) must exhibit solid reliability across a spectrum of scenariosÂ [1]. Conventionally, sensory data collected from the vehicleâ€™s sensors, including Light Detection and Rangings (LiDARs), Cameras, and Radars, is utilized by perception models, such as Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), to construct a thorough representation of the surroundings â€“ a local environment modelÂ [2]. The environmental modelâ€™s accuracy significantly influences vehicle control decisions. Yet, solely training models centrally with constrained data compromises reliabilityÂ [3]. To overcome these limitations and enhance adaptable perception models, the crucial transition to continuous online model training becomes apparentÂ [4]. This shift is pivotal for establishing a robust foundation for fully autonomous driving. FL has recently emerged as a technique for training models without requiring clients to exchange data with a central entity during the training processÂ [5, 6, 4].

Federated Learning (FL) enables training on distributed client data at each communication round. As illustrated in Figure 1, client updates are aggregated iteratively to converge towards the global optimum. Notably, these updates are much smaller than clientsâ€™ entire datasets, a departure from conventional training methods requiring extensive data exchange. FLâ€™s ability to support continuous online training and reduce data exchange makes it ideal for vehicular Machine Learning (ML) applications. Its adoption in vehicular networks has sparked interest, where vehicles serve as clients, generating perception data without overwhelming the communication network. Additionally, RSUs or cloud servers can coordinate training by initiating and aggregating model updates from vehicles during each communication roundÂ [7]. FL performs well with Independent and Identically Distributed (IID) data but struggles with Non-Independent and Identically Distributed (non-IID) data, where data heterogeneity among clients poses challenges to its performance [8, 9]. In our previous workÂ [10], we explored the impact of non-IID data on training processes, categorizing strategies into Data-based and Parameter-based approaches. FedLA, one of our previous contributions [10], addressed label distribution skew using a data-based approach. In that work, we evaluated FedLA using common FL datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in mitigating non-IIDness in non-vehicular scenarios.

In this paper, we focus on FL in vehicular scenarios, e.g., propose a novel FL method called FedProx+LA, and evaluate the applicability of our previous work in vehicular scenarios. Our FedProx+LA approach combines our previous work with the capabilities of FedProxÂ [11] to address the data heterogeneity issue explicitly in vehicular scenarios. FedProx+LA represents a hybrid approach that takes into account both data characteristics and model parameters. Furthermore, we evaluate the effectiveness of FedLA and FedProx+LA in the context of online model training for object detection. To assess their performance, we conduct a comparative analysis against Federated Averaging (FedAvg)Â [5] as a traditional aggregation method, our newly introduced method FedAvgL, and FedProxÂ [11] as a state-of-the-art alternative. We evaluate the approaches using the famous realistic vehicular perception dataset (Nuscenes). While our analysis demonstrates considerable improvements in detection performance and convergence rates already with FedLA compared to FedAvg, FedAvgL, and FedProx, the detection performance was further improved by our FedProx+LA approach tailored for perception in vehicular networks. This improvement is underscored by a substantial increase in the detection performance measured by mean Average Precision (mAP), with FedLA and FedProx+LA achieving enhancements of 5% and 6%, respectively. Additionally, FedLA and FedProx+LA exhibit remarkable improvements in convergence speed, achieving a significant 30% enhancement compared to the baseline methods. II Related Work

Heterogeneous data distribution among clients presents a significant challenge in FL, potentially degrading its performance [12, 13, 9, 8]. Initial solutions, like FedAvgÂ [5], addressed data size differences but did not consider other non-IID scenarios, neglecting scenarios with data heterogeneity in FL. Differences in local objectives and long local epochs may lead to the divergence of the local models from the global model, hindering convergence or causing divergence.

In Zhao et al.â€™s studyÂ [14], they observed a notable decrease in accuracy when models were trained on highly skewed non-IID data. To mitigate this, they proposed a strategy of creating a small subset of globally shared data among edge devices. Li et al.Â [15] explored the impact of non-IID settings on model performance in FedAvg, highlighting how data heterogeneity significantly slowed convergence. These findings underscore the necessity for tailored approaches to address non-IID data challenges in FL scenarios.

FedAvgM, proposed by Hsu et al.Â [16], aimed to enhance model aggregation in FL by incorporating momentum during the global model update on the server. This momentum-based approach aimed to improve aggregation efficiency and effectiveness. Conversely, FedNova, introduced by Wang et al.Â [17], normalized local updates before aggregation to stabilize the process, addressing issues related to varying scales and distributions of updates. FedProxÂ [11] addressed variable local updates in statistically heterogeneous settings by adding a proximal term to the local problem, expediting convergence in such scenarios. MOONÂ [18] introduced model-contrastive FL, aligning local and global model representations to enhance FL system performance.

In the realm of vehicular applications, the challenge of heterogeneity in FL has prompted innovative solutions. Liu et al.Â [19] addressed the direct impact of local parameter heterogeneity by introducing Federated Learning Mechanism in Asynchronous and Adaptive Modes (Fed2A). This approach aimed to mitigate the effects of heterogeneity to enhance the model performance by leveraging the temporal and informative attributes of local parameters. The proposed aggregation scheme incorporated considerations for the freshness of received local models through the application of temporal weights. Additionally, it emphasized consistency by penalizing stale models, contributing to a more robust and adaptive FL framework tailored to vehicular applications. Shang et al.Â [20] presented the Federated Bidirectional Knowledge Distillation (FedBiKD) framework as a targeted solution to address the issue of local deviation in vehicular scenarios. In FedBiKD, global knowledge played a pivotal role in guiding local training, while common knowledge derived from an ensemble of local models was employed to fine-tune the aggregated global model.

III Federated Label-Aware Averaging in Vehicular Networks

In this section, we present our FedProx+LA approach, which combines our previous FedLA approach for generic FL with the FedProxÂ [11] approach from related work, which was specifically tailored for vehicular scenarios. In the following, we first give a short introduction to FedLA and then describe our new FedProx+LA approach.

III-A FedLA

In addressing label distribution skew, our prior research, outlined inÂ [10], introduced FedLA. The core of FedLA, as depicted in Algorithm 1, illustrates the serverâ€™s computation to determine client weights based on their accumulated weights across all labels.

In our investigation of FedLA, we utilized two commonly used datasets in FL: Emnist-balancedÂ [21] and CIFAR-100Â [22]. Itâ€™s important to recognize that FedLAâ€™s effectiveness relies on the assumption that the central server has comprehensive insights into label distributions throughout every round of communication, encompassing all participating clients. However, itâ€™s worth acknowledging that this assumption may not always be practical in certain FL applications, e.g., due to privacy considerations. Nonetheless, in FL applications like training object detection models for vehicular contexts, where clients share lightweight anonymized label information, such as car presence or vegetation, along with their frequencies, the privacy risks may not be significant. This observation motivates our exploration of FedLAâ€™s performance in the context of online training for vehicular object detection models.

III-B FedProx+LA

FedProxÂ [11] emerges as a leading-edge FL technique, tailored to tackle data heterogeneity. It introduces a proximal term into local updates and leverages client sample sizes similar to FedAvg. In this study, we introduce FedProx+LA, a novel extension of FedProx. Illustrated in Figure 2, FedProx+LA not only integrates proximal terms into client-side operations but also leverages label distribution knowledge (similar to FedLA) during model aggregation on the central server. This integration promises enhanced performance and robust adaptability to data heterogeneity. We evaluate the FedProx+LA method in Section IV, where it demonstrates exceptional performance, underscoring its efficacy in practical non-IID settings.

IV-A1 Data Set and Data Distribution

The NuScenes DatasetÂ [23] is chosen for its widespread use in vehicular perception research. The study involves a total of N=10ğ‘10N=10 clients (vehicles), each assigned 10 scenes. To ensure IID conditions in the NuScenes dataset, we randomly distribute scenes as complete blocks, ensuring both Pedestrian (P) and Car (C) classes are available for each image (FigureÂ 3(a)). Conversely, for data heterogeneity or non-IID scenarios, we use the Dirichlet distributionÂ [24]. Unlike common uses of the Dirichlet distribution, realistic object detection tasks exhibit correlations between all labels. We assign a preference for one class to each client and distribute scenes accordingly, maintaining identical sample counts and diverse label counts (FigureÂ 3(b)), simulating real-world scenarios.

IV-B Results and Discussion

IV-B1 Object Detection Performance

The analysis of the object detection performance reveals several key insights. Figure 4 and Table I provide comprehensive comparisons across different methods. In the IID setting, all methods exhibit similar performance characteristics, showing comparable mAP values. Across all of the compared approaches, there is a notable decrease in mAP scores when switching from the IID scenario to the non-IID scenario. This is expected, given that, the data distributed among the clients in IID setting exhibit minimal variations in label distribution (Figure 3(a)). However, in the non-IID setting, our label-aware approaches (FedLA and FedProx+LA) demonstrate a clear advantage over other methods across all global training epochs. FedLA achieves up to a 5%percent55\% improvement in mAP compared to the baseline, while FedProx+LA achieves even higher enhancements, with improvements of up to 6%percent66\%. Interestingly, FedAvgL initially performs reasonably well, but its performance deteriorates notably after Eg=25subscriptğ¸ğ‘”25E_{g}=25, ultimately becoming the least effective method in the non-IID setting, even underperforming FedAvg. This arises from the emphasis solely on label counts, which may reduce the weight of client models with fewer total labels, even in cases where they hold marginal differences in label counts across different classes. This influences the aggregated model to prioritize one class in a given global epoch and subsequently transition to the other class in the following epoch posing challenges to the convergence. Similar to FedLA, FedProx+LA addresses this issue by taking into account the label count per class of selected clients, thereby balancing the weights and boosting the detection performance. However, FedProx+LA additionally incorporates proximal terms to prevent deviation from the global optimum, resulting in a slight improvement in detection performance compared to FedLA. Finally, both FedAvg and FedAvgL exhibit limitations in reaching higher target mAP values in the non-IID setting, indicating challenges in adapting to the non-IID data distribution.

IV-B2 Communication Efficiency and Convergence Rate

The analysis of communication efficiency and convergence rate provides insight into the behavior of different methods across various settings, as depicted in Figure 5 and Table I. In the IID setting, all methods demonstrate relatively similar convergence patterns, although FedProx exhibits slightly slower convergence compared to other methods. Across both IID and non-IID settings, fewer global training epochs are required to attain the target mAP in the IID scenario compared to the non-IID scenario, indicating the impact of data distribution on convergence efficiency. In the non-IID settings, FedAvgL exhibits a notable deceleration in the convergence speed that impedes the trained model from reaching higher target mAP values. This is associated with the factors contributing to the previously discussed poor detection performance of this method. On the other hand, FedLA demonstrates marginally greater efficiency compared to FedProx+LA in achieving the target mAP. However, the difference in efficiency between the two methods is relatively minor. Moreover, as the target mAP value increases in the non-IID setting, both FedLA and FedProx+LA demonstrate substantial improvements in convergence speed, achieving a remarkable 30%percent3030\% enhancement compared to the baseline methods. These findings highlight the subtle relationship between communication efficiency, convergence rate, and data distribution characteristics, highlighting the effectiveness of data heterogeneity handling mechanisms in enhancing convergence rate, particularly in non-IID scenarios.

V Conclusion

In our study, we highlighted the vital role of continual training for perception models in autonomous driving, exploring the efficacy of FL within vehicular networks. A key challenge we addressed was data heterogeneity, which can hinder the FL process due to data disparities. In this study, we proposed FedProx+LA, a hybrid method built on FedProx and FedLA, which considers both data characteristics and model parameters. Conducting a comparative analysis, we evaluated FedProx+LA against methods like FedAvg, FedAvgL, FedProx, and our previous work FedLA. Our results, particularly with the Nuscenes dataset, highlight the superior performance of FedProx+LA. We observed significant enhancements in detection performance (6% for FedProx+LA, 5% for FedLA) and a remarkable 30% increase in convergence speed over baseline methods.
