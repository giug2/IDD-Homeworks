FLASHE: Additively Symmetric Homomorphic Encryption for Cross-Silo Federated Learning

Abstract.

Homomorphic encryption (HE) is a promising privacy-preserving technique for cross-silo federated learning (FL), where organizations perform collaborative model training on decentralized data. Despite the strong privacy guarantee, general HE schemes result in significant computation and communication overhead. Prior works employ batch encryption to address this problem, but it is still suboptimal in mitigating communication overhead and is incompatible with sparsification techniques.

In this paper, we propose FLASHE, an HE scheme tailored for cross-silo FL. To capture the minimum requirements of security and functionality, FLASHE drops the asymmetric-key design and only involves modular addition operations with random numbers. Depending on whether to accommodate sparsification techniques, FLASHE is optimized in computation efficiency with different approaches. We have implemented FLASHE as a pluggable module atop FATE, an industrial platform for cross-silo FL. Compared to plaintext training, FLASHE slightly increases the training time by ‚â§6%absentpercent6\leq 6\%, with no communication overhead induced.

1. Introduction

Cross-silo Federated learning (FL)¬†(Kairouz et¬†al., 2021) has emerged as a promising paradigm that allows multiple organization clients (e.g., medical or financial)¬†(flm, 2020; Courtiol et¬†al., 2019; web, 2019) to collaboratively train a shared model with decentralized private datasets. In cross-silo FL, there is a central server maintaining a global model state (e.g., gradients or weights) by iteratively aggregating local updates collected from clients. To minimize information leakage, homomorphic encryption (HE) is used as the de facto privacy-preserving technique, allowing clients to encrypt their updates in a way such that the server can perform aggregation directly on ciphertexts without deriving anything meaningful about the plaintexts.

However, general HE schemes suffer from significant inefficiency in both computation and communication (¬ß¬†2.2). For example, prevalent schemes like the Paillier (Paillier, 1999), FV (Fan and Vercauteren, 2012) and CKKS111Also called HEAAN, though we will stick to CKKS in this paper. (Cheon et¬†al., 2017) all entail computationally intensive cryptographic operations such as modular multiplications and polynomial reductions, yielding a latency of around 24-81 minutes to encrypt a CNN with 1.2M parameters. In comparison, an iteration of the corresponding plaintext training only takes 13 seconds in our testbed experiment. To make matters worse, the size of the resulting ciphertexts is increased to around 0.6-483 GB, incurring prohibitive communication latency, especially in the geo-distributed settings.

To address these problems, existing solutions¬†(Zhang et¬†al., 2020) propose to batch multiple plaintexts into a single one so that en-/decryption and other operations can be performed in a data-parallel manner. This is typically achieved by concatenating plaintexts as with Paillier (Liu et¬†al., 2019; Zhang et¬†al., 2020), or applying more advanced encoding techniques as with FV and CKKS (Smart and Vercauteren, 2010, 2014). While batch encryption techniques significantly amortize the computation overhead, they still lead to high communication overhead due to their intrinsic nature, e.g., 2√ó\times-42√ó\times inflation of traffic in our characterization (¬ß¬†2.3).

Batch encryption is also incompatible with sparsification techniques (Dryden et¬†al., 2016; Aji and Heafield, 2017; Lin et¬†al., 2018; Wangni et¬†al., 2018; Sattler et¬†al., 2019) which allow clients to dramatically reduce communication overhead by sending only a sparse subset of local states to the server. For example, after computing its local gradient, a client may select its largest s% components in magnitude to communicate for some s. As selection results vary with clients, upon receiving the sparsified gradients, the server needs to align them by coordinating before aggregation. While the alignment can be done without decryption when the selected gradient components are encrypted separately, it becomes infeasible when they are packed into one ciphertext. As a result, the communication benefits offered by sparsification are heavily counteracted by the message inflation effect of general HEs. For example, using top 10% sparsification can shrink the network traffic by 10√ó\times in plaintext training. However, when Paillier is also in use, the encrypted sparsified traffic can end up with 20√ó\times larger than the plaintext dense one, as Paillier can inflate the message by around 200√ó\times in the non-batch mode.

To get rid of the heaviness of general HE schemes as well as the limitations of batch optimization, an intuitive idea is to customize a lightweight HE scheme for cross-silo FL so that there is no need to do batch optimization. Implementing this intuition raises a fundamental problem: what makes general HE schemes so heavy? Our answers are two-fold. First, most of them, like Paillier and FV, are asymmetric (Fun and Samsudin, 2016; Papisetty, 2017), meaning that their implementation relies on trapdoor functions (Yao, 1982) that entail expensive computation and prolong messages. Second, even for those schemes that are symmetric or have a symmetric version like CKKS, their complexity is still lifted by the requirement of being versatile, i.e., allowing multiple types of operations to be performed on ciphertexts.

Fortunately, we found that these two features are over-tailored for FL scenarios and can thus be dispensed. As for the asymmetric-key design, though it can facilitate outsourced computation (Benaloh, 1989; Kushilevitz and Ostrovsky, 1997) in a general sense, it does not boost up any security in FL scenarios where it is only the clients who perform encryption and decryption (Yang et¬†al., 2019; Kairouz et¬†al., 2021). In terms of the versatility, since weighted average is the only operation primitive over model updates demanded by prevailing aggregation algorithms (McMahan et¬†al., 2017; Li et¬†al., 2020; Li et¬†al., 2019; Huang et¬†al., 2021), the HE applied in FL can be free from supporting multiplications.

Based on these observations, in this paper we propose FLASHE, an optimized HE scheme that provably meets the minimum requirements in FL: semantic security (that leaks no information on plaintexts) (Shafi and Silvio, 1982) and additive homomorphism (that allows additions to be computed on ciphertexts). These properties are achieved by adding random numbers to plaintexts where the addition is defined in an additive group. On top of the vanilla scheme, we give two variants depending on whether to accommodate sparsification or not. (1) When sparsification is not needed, we envision a chance of further optimizing the computational overhead in performing double masking (¬ß¬†3). (2) When sparsification is employed, we adaptively navigate between double masking and single masking for the best performance (¬ß¬†4).

We have integrated FLASHE with FATE, an open-source platform for cross-silo FL (fat, 2021) (¬ß¬†5). To demonstrate its efficiency, we evaluate it in a practical environment with 11 AWS EC2 instances in five data centers across three continents (¬ß¬†6). These clients collaboratively train three models of different sizes: a ResNet-20 (He et¬†al., 2016) with CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et¬†al., 2009), a GRU (Bahdanau et¬†al., 2015) with Shakespeare dataset (Caldas et¬†al., 2019), and a 5-layer CNN (Chai et¬†al., 2020) with FEMNIST dataset (Cohen et¬†al., 2017; Caldas et¬†al., 2019). Compared to plaintext training, FLASHE only yields ‚â§6absent6\leq 6% overhead in training time, while adding no network traffic, which demonstrates its near-optimal efficiency. Compared with the optimized version of Paillier, FV, and CKKS, FLASHE reduces the monetary cost by up to 73%‚Äì94%. We demonstrate that FLASHE can efficiently secure sparsified model updates, achieving a speedup of 13-63√ó\times and network reduction of 48√ó\times, compared to Paillier, the most efficient scheme of general HEs. To our knowledge, FLASHE is the first HE scheme with highly optimized end-to-end performance for cross-silo FL.

2. Background and Motivation

We start with a quick primer on cross-silo FL and its privacy concerns (2.1), followed by the inefficiencies of homomorphic encryption, a de facto privacy-preserving technique in cross-silo FL (2.2). Next, we highlight the key shortcomings of the state-of-the-art optimization that motivate our work (2.3).

2.1. Cross-Silo Federated Learning

Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et¬†al., 2017) allows clients to lock private data in local storage while building a shared model. This is achieved by introducing a central server which iteratively collects local model updates from selected clients and broadcasts a global model update which is an aggregate of the local ones. Depending on the target domain, FL can be classified into two categories (Kairouz et¬†al., 2021): cross-device FL where participants are a mass of less capable mobile or IoT devices (Chen et¬†al., 2019; Bonawitz et¬†al., 2019), and cross-silo FL where participants are typically 2‚Äì100 organizational entities (flm, 2020; Courtiol et¬†al., 2019; web, 2019). We focus on cross-silo FL.

Cross-silo FL seeks strong guarantees on privacy as the data in use are commercially valuable or under the protection of governmental legislations (Shastri et¬†al., 2020; Shastri et¬†al., 2019). Intuitively, the information contained in model updates is narrowly scoped compared to the underlying training data. Nevertheless, it has been recently demonstrated that exploratory attacks including property inference (Melis et¬†al., 2019), membership inference (Song and Shmatikov, 2019; Shokri et¬†al., 2017) and data reconstruction (Wang et¬†al., 2019; Lam et¬†al., 2021) can be made possible even only with global model updates. As such, to respect privacy, both local model updates and global ones should be exclusively released to those participating organizations‚Äìno external party, including the server, should have access to them. In industrial practice like FATE (fat, 2021), homomorphic encryption is a de facto technique to secure cross-silo FL.

2.2. Homomorphic Encryption

Homomorphic encryption (HE) is one kind of encryption schemes that allows a third party (e.g., cloud platform or service provider) to compute on encrypted data, without requiring access to the secret key or the plaintexts (Rivest et¬†al., 1978). The Paillier scheme allows additions to be performed on encrypted data, while the FV and CKKS schemes permit both additions and multiplications on ciphertexts. The Paillier and FV schemes take integers as plaintexts, while the CKKS scheme can encrypt real or complex numbers but yields only approximate results. 2.3. Limitations of State-Of-the-Art Optimization

So far, batch encryption is the state-of-the-art optimization direction for accelerating general HE schemes. Basically, it allows both en-/decryption and homomorphic evaluation to be performed on multiple plaintexts in a data-parallel manner. As for Paillier, this can be achieved by concatenating multiple plaintexts into a large-yet-legitimate one (Liu et¬†al., 2019; Zhang et¬†al., 2020). In terms of FV and CKKS, more advanced methods such as CRT-based encoding technique can be applied (Smart and Vercauteren, 2010, 2014). To exemplify, for encrypting a model of medium size with 40M 16-bit integers, even a message expansion factor as small as 2.4√ó2.4\times (as in fully optimized Paillier) can translate into a substantial per-round communication overhead of 45s. We thereby conclude that the geo-distributed nature of cross-silo settings urges further mitigation in HEs‚Äô message inflation effects.

Incompatibility with sparsification¬†¬† For collaborative learning, sparsification (Dryden et¬†al., 2016; Aji and Heafield, 2017; Lin et¬†al., 2018; Wangni et¬†al., 2018; Sattler et¬†al., 2019) is commonly used in practice for communication reduction. It works by allowing each distributed client to transmit a sparse representation of its model update, which can be obtained by performing element-wise multiplication on the original update with some 0/1 mask of the same length. The state-of-the-art variation is top s% sparsification, where each client agrees on some value sùë†s and independently constructs its own mask by setting 1 in the coordinates that rank top s% in absolute value and 0 otherwise. We formally describe this method in Algorithm¬†1. It has been shown that top s% method can reduce the amount of communication per step by up to three orders of magnitude, while still preserving model quality (Lin et¬†al., 2018; Aji and Heafield, 2017).

Unfortunately, top sùë†s% sparsification is not compatible with batch encryption. As the coordinates that each client masks are inconsistent, their sparsified model updates cannot be directly summed up. Instead, the server needs to first perform coordinate alignment on them, which can still be achieved when their values are encrypted separately. When it comes to batch encryption, however, this becomes infeasible as values of different coordinates are packed and encrypted in a way that they are not distinguishable unless decrypted. Figure¬†2 further illustrates this issue.

As a result, we cannot simultaneously enjoy the performance merits of sparsification and batch encryption. For example, if a client employs top 10% sparsification in order to achieve a target compression ratio of 10, she actually ends up with 20√ó\times message inflation after her sparsified model update is encrypted by vanilla Paillier which expands a plaintext by 200√ó\times (¬ß¬†2.2). We consistently observe such a counteract effect during empirical evaluation as in Section¬†6.4. In short, as long as we need to protect the confidentiality of model updates and the underlying data with HE, the performance benefits of sparsification will be counteracted, while batch encryption is by no means a relief.

Summary¬†¬† To our best knowledge, we are the first to present a comprehensive characterization for the limitations of batch encryption in realistic settings. Given the need for improving communication efficiency and accommodating sparsification techniques, we are motivated to optimize the use of HE in cross-silo FL by proposing a new one.

3. Vanilla FLASHE

We aspire to devise a lightweight HE for cross-silo FL that dispenses the need for batch encryption. To that end, we first crystalize our used threat model (3.1). We then reason about what leads to the heaviness of general HE schemes and whether we can unload them in cross-silo FL (3.2). After that, we provide a vanilla version of FLASHE, which is optimized when sparsification is not expected (3.3), along with in-depth cost and security analysis (3.4, 3.5, and 3.6).

3.1. Threat Model

Client¬†¬† All the clients are honest-but-curious: they follow any agreed-upon protocol without interfering with the federation; however, they might pry on each other‚Äôs sensitive information for improving their competitiveness in the market by observing the messages they received. In this case, we can tolerate the situation that the clients act on their own or collude with each other to form an adversary of size at most n‚àí2ùëõ2n-2, where nùëõn is the total number of participating clients.

Some clients may eavesdrop on the communication between the server and other clients for recovering their plaintext model updates. Fortunately, the defense against eavesdropping can be delegated to secure protocols (e.g., SSL/TLS) in the network layer. Note that the transmitted HE ciphertexts, though encrypted with the same key, will undergo an additional encryption process with different keys across clients as long as each server-client pair establishes its SSL/TLS channel using different keys.

Server¬†¬† The server also follows the training procedure in an honest-but-curious manner but does not collude with any single client. Usually, as a celebrated FL service provider, it makes profits from selling its federation algorithms and platforms sustainably. It thus has no incentive to collude with clients and risk devastating its reputation.

Practicality¬†¬† This threat model is consistent with those used by other HE alternatives, i.e., they do not possess more security benefits than FLASHE does. To date, WeBank adopts the same model and uses Paillier as a primary means of privacy preservation in FATE (fat, 2021), a production cross-silo FL platform. There are also many academic FL proposals using HEs under the identical threat model (Aono et¬†al., 2017; Liu et¬†al., 2020; Cheng et¬†al., 2021; Zhang et¬†al., 2020).

3.2. Technical Intuition

Tracing the source of the inefficiency in general HEs, we identified two major points that can be improved upon.

Asymmetricity¬†¬† Research on HEs is dominated by asymmetric schemes (that use different keys for encryption and decryption) due to their wide support in general deployment (Fun and Samsudin, 2016; Papisetty, 2017). In cross-silo FL, however, asymmetricity is redundant as the server does not incorporate its data. What is worse, the implementation of asymmetric schemes relies on trapdoor functions (Yao, 1982) which are computationally expensive. We are thus motivated to use symmetric schemes which are way lighter as they only entail perturbance of bits.

Versatility¬†¬† Standardized HE schemes are homomorphic to both additions and multiplications (Albrecht et¬†al., 2019). Yet, such a tantalizing power comes at a huge cost of both time and space brought by advanced mathematic operations and enlarged ciphertext space. Fortunately, multiplications are only useful when we have to calculate the product of two protected values from different parties, which is not the case in prevailing model aggregation methods (e.g., FedAvg (McMahan et¬†al., 2017), FedProx (Li et¬†al., 2020), q-FedAvg (Li et¬†al., 2019), and FedYOGI (Reddi et¬†al., 2020)). Summary¬†¬† We hence conclude that the minimum requirements that an HE needs to meet for cross-silo FL are:

  1. (1)

    Semantic Security (Shafi and Silvio, 1982): Knowing only ciphertexts, it must be infeasible for a computationally-bounded adversary to derive significant information about the plaintexts. In cross-silo FL, it basically captures the requirement that nothing can be learned about the clients from their encrypted model updates.

  2. (2)

    Additive Homomorphism: There should exist an operator on ciphertexts that supports the evaluation of summation over the associated plaintexts, which we have formulated in Section¬†2.2. This enables the support for a wide variety of aggregation algorithms.

The adherence to semantic security implies that an encryption scheme must be probabilistic or stateful. Otherwise, an adversary can tell if the same message was sent twice, contradicting the security notion. On top of that, a natural way to incorporate additive homomorphism is to have an integer encrypted by adding to it a random mask atop an additive group.

4. Tuning with Sparsification

While vanilla FLASHE is fully optimized for sparsification-free scenarios, there is still room for it to improve efficiency (¬ß¬†4.1) and enhance privacy (¬ß¬†4.2) when sparsification is expected in the cross-silo FL practice. We thus fine-tune vanilla FLASHE for best supporting sparsification (Algorithm¬†1).

4.1. Optimization in Performance

Ciphertext Structure Redesign¬†¬† A sparsified local update will be compacted, i.e., with all zero terms discarded, before being encrypted and transmitted for truly reducing communication traffic. In this sense, the participation record in vanilla FLASHE‚Äôs ciphertext (the SùëÜS in (ùêú,i,S)ùêúùëñùëÜ(\mathbf{c},i,S), ¬ß¬†3.3) should be constructed at a coordinate level, e.g., being a list of multisets ùêíùêí\mathbf{S} where Sd={j}subscriptùëÜùëëùëóS_{d}=\{j\} for 1‚â§d‚â§D1ùëëùê∑1\leq d\leq D. However, this information is redundant as it can be inferred from the sparsification mask which will be uploaded anyway. We hence end up dispensing the participation record in the ciphertext.

Adaptive Masking¬†¬† When top s% sparsification is applied to local updates to uplink communication, the coordinates that each client masks are naturally different. In other words, even if all clients keep attending throughout the federation, from the perspective of a specific coordinate, we still expect some clients to ‚Äúdrop out‚Äù of the aggregation, to which degree (1) varies across both coordinates and rounds, and (2) is not predictable without knowledge on clients‚Äô data. In this case, double masking is not necessarily more efficient in computation than single masking.

Therefore, instead of fixing the use of either masking scheme in FLASHE throughout the entire training, we propose to switch between them adaptively. At each round, after determining the sparsification mask for its current update, each client sends the mask to the server (Line¬†1). The server then helps clients determine whether to use single masking or double masking by first calculating the number of masks to generate required by both schemes and then selecting the scheme with the smaller outcome (Line¬†1). Clearly, the decision made in such a manner is optimal and does not require any knowledge a priori. Also, the brought overhead is foreseeably negligible: the choice of the encryption scheme on is merely a bit, and the computation performed at the server is trivial.

4.2. A Note on Security

As above mentioned, the target plaintext of FLASHE is the compacted version of a sparsfied model. In other words, FLASHE should not be responsible for protecting the sparsification mask. However, to minimize information leakage, we here suggest clients agreeing on some secrets, based on which they can consistently permutate their model updates before conducting sparsification (Line¬†1). This can obfuscate the structural information contained in the sparsification masks to upload while having no impact on the correctness of model update aggregation that is performed at the server.

5. Implementation

5.1. FLASHE Overview

At its core, FLASHE is an end-to-end implementation for privacy-preserving aggregation in cross-silo FL training. It is located in both clients and the server and interacts with the driver of an FL framework. Given developer-specified security parameters, it performs cryptographic operations on model updates, whereas the driver is in charge of performing local training and conducting communication.

Figure¬†6 shows how FLASHE interacts with FL execution frameworks. \raisebox{-0.7pt}{1}‚Éù Secure Channel Establishment: without loss of generality, all network traffic is assumed to go through the server. To communicate secrets in the presence of eavesdroppers, each server-client pair need to first establish a secure channel using different keys. \raisebox{-0.7pt}{2}‚Éù Key Generation: the coordinator at the server-side randomly selects a client as the leader whose initializer takes charge of generating a key for FLASHE‚Äôs use (\raisebox{0.3pt}{\normalsize2a}‚Éù). The generated key is then dispatched to the other clients‚Äô intializer (\raisebox{0.3pt}{\normalsize2b}‚Éù). \raisebox{-0.7pt}{3}‚Éù Local Training: in an iteration, a client trains the local model with its private data. \raisebox{-0.7pt}{4}‚Éù Encryption: the model update is then fed to executor, wherein floating-point (FP) numbers is first encoded to integers (\raisebox{0.3pt}{\normalsize4a}‚Éù) before being encrypted (\raisebox{0.3pt}{\normalsize4b}‚Éù). \raisebox{-0.7pt}{5}‚Éù Aggregation: at the server end, the aggregator waits until receiving all of the encrypted local updates and then performs homomorphic additions. \raisebox{-0.7pt}{6}‚Éù Decryption: The global update is then dispatched back to the clients whose executors go through the inverse process of encrypting and encoding. \raisebox{-0.7pt}{7}‚Éù Update Application: the training backend finally advances the local model accordingly.

We have implemented FLASHE as a pluggable module in FATE (fat, 2021), an open-source platform for cross-silo FL, with 3.3k lines of Python code. While we base our implementation on FATE, our modular design can be extended easily to other frameworks such as TensorFlow Federated (tff, 2021) and PySyft (Ryffel et¬†al., 2018). Substituting the encryption primitives, we seamlessly port Paillier, FV, and CKKS with additional 1.3k LOC.

5.2. Executor Details

To preserve the theoretical security level of FLASHE, careful implementation of the executor is needed.

Encoding¬†¬† As FLASHE is defined atop integer domain, before encrypting FP model updates we need to first quantize them. To that end, we truncate an FP number with a clipping threshold Œ±ùõº\alpha, and then map the range [‚àíŒ±,Œ±]ùõºùõº[-\alpha,\alpha] into the range [0,2M‚àí1]0superscript2ùëÄ1[0,2^{M}-1] with MùëÄM as the quantization bit-width. The determination principle of Œ±ùõº\alpha is the same as that in dACIQ (Zhang et¬†al., 2020), an analytical clipping technique used in cross-silo FL, except that we fit the distribution of update values merely using information from global update history, instead of requiring private information from each client. This prevents the privacy guarantees of FLASHE to be counteracted.

Encryption¬†¬† PRFs can be implemented with secure block ciphers, and we use Advanced Encryption Standard (AES), a symmetric block cipher standardized by NIST (Standard, 2001). The block size is fixed to 128 and the key length is set to 256. We implement AES using PyCryptodome (pyc, 2021) for enjoying the widely adopted hardware acceleration offered by AES-NI (Gueron, 2009), which is supported across Intel processors.

5.3. Mask Precomputation

To further unleash the performance potential of FLASHE, we spot the opportunity in utilizing idle time on the client-side. As implied in Section¬†5.1, after uploading its local model, a client cannot proceed until it receives the aggregated model. Such a stall usually lasts for 555-777 seconds as shown in Section¬†6.2. Since the generation of random masks is off the critical path, at each iteration, we let clients spend their idle time precomputing the PRFs needed in the next iteration. Note that the comparison between FLASHE and general HEs is still fair, as they have no intermediate results to precompute. We evaluate the effectiveness of performing mask precomputation at Section¬†6.6.

We next narrow down the evaluation scope to specific design choices in FLASHE. We first justify to perform double masking in encryption (¬ß¬†3.3). As mentioned, we expect no or mild client dropout as the common case in cross-silo FL and thus employ double masking for enjoying high efficiency in decryption brought by mask neutralization during model aggregation. Figure¬†12 reports the HE-related (i.e., encryption and decryption) time per round at the client end under double masking (solid lines) and single masking scheme (dashed lines) when the dropout rate is swept from 0 to 0.9. In particular, for each dropout rate under the double masking scheme, we randomly select the corresponding number of clients over 50 times to empirically estimate the average case777Note that in double masking scheme, the number of counteracted masks varies even under the same dropout rate..

We see that cases with different numbers of clients (20, 40, 80) share the same trend under both the double masking and single masking scheme. We hereby focus on the case when there are 80 clients (purple lines). We observe that when the dropout rate is no more than 0.4, the HE-related time in double masking is consistently less than that in single masking, where the latency gap is the most salient when there is no dropout (0.7‚Äì3.1s for the three applications) and diminishes as the dropout rate increases. The crossover point occurs when the dropout rate is 0.4, after which single masking becomes a slightly better choice as the dropout becomes more severe. These observations comply with Theorem¬†2 (¬ß¬†3.6). In brief, double masking outperforms single masking in time when the dropout is infrequent and modest, which is the case in cross-silo FL.

6.6. Effectiveness of Mask Precomputation

We finally examine the effectiveness of mask precomputation (¬ß¬†5.3), which is made possible by the stateful nature of FLASHE. We evaluate the impact on iteration time as the network traffic is not affected. Figure¬†13 illustrates the difference between the presence and absence of mask precomputation. We observe that mask precomputation expedites each iteration by 1.21.21.2s, 1.21.21.2s, and 2.12.12.1s for ResNet-20, GRU, and CNN, respectively, indicating that more performance potential can be unlocked by mask precomputation as the size of the model grows larger. The time breakdown further confirms that such performance benefits stem from the elimination of en-/decryption time (making it no more than 0.1s). Note that the application of mask precomputation of FLASHE does not impair the fairness when FLASHE is compared against the baseline HEs, as they are by design stateless and thus not compatible with precomputation. In all, mask precomputation reinforces the performance advantage of FLASHE.

7. Related Work

Additively Symmetric Homomorphic Encryption¬†¬† Only few additively symmetric HEs are seen in the literature. One line of the attempts encrypts a batch of integers by multiplication with random invertible matrices (Chan, 2009; Kipnis and Hibshoosh, 2012). However, they are shown to be insecure (Viz√°r and Vaudenay, 2015; Tsaban and Lifshitz, 2015). The other line of work focuses on scalar encryption(Xiao et¬†al., 2012; Papadimitriou et¬†al., 2016; Van¬†Dijk et¬†al., 2010), among which the ASHE proposed in Seabed (Papadimitriou et¬†al., 2016) is the most lightweight and closely related to our scheme. Motivated from data analytic applications, ASHE also spots the opportunity for performance improvement in double masking. Still, FLASHE is distinct from it:

  1. (1)

    We are the first to extensively characterize the overhead of traditional HEs through micro-benchmarks (¬ß¬†2.2, ¬ß¬†2.3) and real deployment (¬ß¬†6.2, ¬ß¬†6.3), whereby we point out their unnecessity in FL aggregation (¬ß¬†3.2), which has been overlooked in the literature.

  2. (2)

    We identify that ASHE would perform suboptimally under sparsification scenarios and thus propose to alternate between the single and double masking scheme (¬ß¬†4) with some side-evaluation (¬ß¬†6.4 and ¬ß¬†6.5).

8. Conclusion

In this paper, we present FLASHE to secure the model aggregation process in cross-silo FL. Compared to optimized versions of general HEs, FLASHE significantly outperforms in runtime performance, supports model sparsification, while retaining the same level of security guarantee.
