On the Byzantine-Resilience of Distillation-Based Federated Learning

Christophe Roux    Max Zimmer    Sebastian Pokutta
Abstract

\glsxtrprotectlinksFederated Learning (FL) algorithms using \glsxtrprotectlinksKnowledge Distillation (KD) have received increasing attention due to their favorable properties with respect to privacy, non-i.i.d. data and communication cost. These methods depart from transmitting model parameters and, instead, communicate information about a learning task by sharing predictions on a public dataset. In this work, we study the performance of such approaches in the byzantine setting, where a subset of the clients act in an adversarial manner aiming to disrupt the learning process. We show that \glsxtrprotectlinksKD-based \glsxtrprotectlinksFL algorithms are remarkably resilient and analyze how byzantine clients can influence the learning process compared to \glsxtrprotectlinksFederated Averaging. Based on these insights, we introduce two new byzantine attacks and demonstrate that they are effective against prior byzantine-resilient methods. Additionally, we propose FilterExp, a novel method designed to enhance the byzantine resilience of \glsxtrprotectlinksKD-based \glsxtrprotectlinksFL algorithms and demonstrate its efficacy. Finally, we provide a general method to make attacks harder to detect, improving their effectiveness.

1 Introduction

\glsxtrprotectlinksFederated Learning (FL) has emerged as a popular paradigm to address privacy and communication challenges in Deep Learning. Often, data collection occurs across multiple clients in a decentralized fashion, making centralized collection of the data infeasible due to privacy constraints. \glsxtrprotectlinksFederated Averaging (FedAVG) (mcmahan_communication-efficient_2017), a standard \glsxtrprotectlinksFL algorithm, avoids transmitting private data from clients to a central server. Instead, the clients train locally on their private data and send their parameters to the server. The server aggregates these parameters and broadcasts the updated model back to the clients, which then proceed training. While \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG has found success in diverse applications (rieke2020future; ramaswamy2019federated; yang2018applied; sheller2019multiinstitutional; chen2019federated), it has notable limitations such as high communication costs due to repeatedly transmitting the model parameters, susceptibility to inversion attacks which reconstruct private data from the parameters (melis2018exploiting; nasr2019comprehensive), or performance degradation due to non \glsxtrprotectlinksidentically independently distributed (i.i.d.) local data among the clients (kairouz_advances_2019). Most importantly for this work, \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG lacks robustness to byzantine clients, i.e., clients which behave adversarially. This has fueled efforts to create provably byzantine-resilient \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG variants (allen-zhu_byzantine-resilient_2020; alistarh_byzantine_2018; mhamdi2018hidden; chen_distributed_2017; yin2021byzantine).

A recent line of work uses \glsxtrprotectlinksKnowledge Distillation (KD) (hinton_distilling_2015) in order address some of these challenges. \glsxtrprotectlinksKD aims at transferring knowledge by training a student model on the aggregated predictions of a group of teacher models. It can be used to enhance or entirely substitute the transmission of model parameters to the server with predictions on a public dataset. The server then uses \glsxtrprotectlinksKD to incorporate this information into its model. Such methods offer reduced communication overhead, enhanced privacy and robustness to non-\glsxtrprotectlinksi.i.d. data (sattler2020communicationefficient; sattler_fedaux_2021; lin_ensemble_2021; he_group_2020; cheng_fedgems_2021; papernot_semi-supervised_2017; chang_cronus_2019; gong2022preserving; fancollaborative2023), but they are not well explored in the byzantine setting.

In this work, we explore \glsxtrprotectlinksFederated Learning using Distillation (FedDistill), a \glsxtrprotectlinksKD-based \glsxtrprotectlinksFL algorithm, in the byzantine setting. While many different algorithms have been proposed in this space, we focus on \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill, which is conceptually simple and satisfies the crucial property that clients communicate with the server only via their predictions on an unlabeled public dataset. Specifically, we examine how this new communication modality–sharing predictions on a public datasets rather than model parameters with the server–affects the byzantine clients’ ability to hinder the training process. We demonstrate that \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill exhibits greater byzantine-resilience than \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG by design. This is due in part to the fact that the attack space of byzantine clients in \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill, i.e., the probability simplex, is more restricted than the attack space in \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG, i.e., the parameter space. While the parameters space is high-dimensional and unbounded, the probability simplex is a bounded set and relatively low-dimensional. Further, the byzantine clients can only influence the server model in an indirect way by influencing the distillation objective of the server instead of directly altering its parameters as in \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG. Guided by our new insights, we propose novel \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill-specific attacks capable of evading prior byzantine-resilient \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill variants, as well as new defences, which are more byzantine-resilient than previous methods.

To summarize, our contributions are as follows:

  1. 1.

    We analyze \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill in the byzantine setting, highlighting its distinctive features compared to \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG. Our analysis reveals that Byzantine have only a limited impact on \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill, rendering it more resilient than \glsxtrprotectlinksFedAVG.

  2. 2.

    We introduce two novel byzantine attacks, which are specifically designed for \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill. We demonstrate that our attacks have a stronger impact on the learning process than previous approaches

  3. 3.

    We propose a novel defense mechanism for \glsxtrprotectlinksFedDistill that incorporates information on client behavior in both current and past communication rounds. This approach significantly enhances byzantine-resilience when contrasted with prior methods, which rely on robust aggregation techniques applied independently to each sample

  4. 4.

    We provide a general method to obfuscate byzantine attacks, making them harder to detect. In the presence of defence mechanisms, this method improves the effectiveness of byzantine attacks.
