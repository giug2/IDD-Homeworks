Abstract

Accurate field boundary delineation is a critical challenge in digital agriculture, impacting everything from crop monitoring to resource management. Existing methods often struggle with noise and fail to generalize across varied landscapes, particularly when dealing with cloud cover in optical remote sensing. In response, this study presents a new approach that leverages time series data from Sentinel-2 (S2) and Sentinel-1 (S1) imagery to improve performance under diverse cloud conditions, without the need for manual cloud filtering. We introduce a 3D Vision Transformer architecture specifically designed for satellite image time series, incorporating a memory-efficient attention mechanism. Two models are proposed: PTAViT3D, which handles either S2 or S1 data independently, and PTAViT3D-CA, which fuses both datasets to enhance accuracy. Both models are evaluated under sparse and dense cloud coverage by exploiting spatio-temporal correlations. Our results demonstrate that the models can effectively delineate field boundaries, even with partial (S2 or S2&S1 data fusion) or dense cloud cover (S1), with the S1-based model providing performance comparable to S2 imagery in terms of spatial resolution. A key strength of this approach lies in its capacity to directly process cloud-contaminated imagery by leveraging spatio-temporal correlations in a memory-efficient manner. This methodology, used in the ePaddocks product to map Australiaâ€™s national field boundaries, offers a robust, scalable solution adaptable to varying agricultural environments, delivering precision and reliability where existing methods falter. Our code is available at https://github.com/feevos/tfcl.

1 Introduction

The rise of digital agriculture has revolutionized how we monitor, manage, and optimize crop production. At the heart of this transformation lies the precise delineation of field boundaries, a task crucial for maximizing efficiency in agricultural resource management. These boundaries play a pivotal role in applications ranging from crop yield estimation to food security assessments [deÂ Wit and Clevers, 2004, Blaes etÂ al., 2005, Matton etÂ al., 2015]. In the past, boundary mapping was a laborious, error-prone process, often leaving farmers and digital service providers grappling with inaccuracies. But today, frequent satellite observations from the Sentinel 1 and Sentinel 2, programs present an opportunity to continuously refine these boundaries as they evolve with the cropping cycles. This work harnesses the power of these sensors to push the boundariesâ€“literally and figurativelyâ€“of whatâ€™s possible in digital agriculture.

Older methods for extracting field boundaries, such as edge-based, region-based, and hybrid techniques, have had varying degrees of success [Mueller etÂ al., 2004, Turker and Kok, 2013, Yan and Roy, 2014, Graesser and Ramankutty, 2017]. However, these approaches often stumble when faced with noise, the need for extensive preprocessing, and challenges in adapting to different landscapes. Enter deep learning: a breakthrough in remote sensing that sidesteps these pitfalls by learning complex features directly from satellite imagery.

Vision-based deep learning models offer a dynamic solution, enabling robust, scalable boundary extraction that adapts to the ever-changing agricultural landscape [Persello etÂ al., 2019, Waldner and Diakogiannis, 2020, Aung etÂ al., 2020, Waldner etÂ al., 2021, Wang etÂ al., 2022, dâ€™Andrimont etÂ al., 2023, Tetteh etÂ al., 2023]. But while these advancements have propelled the field forward, one persistent obstacle remainsâ€“cloud cover [Li etÂ al., 2022].

Clouds obscure the Earthâ€™s surface, complicating the task of boundary delineation. Conventional methods have relied on labor-intensive cloud removal or the creation of cloud-free composites, both costly and time-consuming. Furthermore, approaches that depend on uncontaminated pixels are hampered by the subjective nature of defining â€œcloudsâ€ and setting pixel contamination thresholds, leading to inconsistencies and errors. While the S1 SAR sensor sidesteps cloud-related issues, its spatial resolution has traditionally limited its effectiveness for precise boundary work.

In previous work, we tackled cloud contamination by selecting S2 imagery with minimal cloud cover and using a consensus approach [Waldner and Diakogiannis, 2020, Waldner etÂ al., 2021]. This technique mitigated cloud effects through averaging but struggled under dense cloud cover, where each prediction was still cloud-affected. Moreover, this approach didnâ€™t fully leverage the temporal correlations between imagesâ€“an essential factor for accurate boundary delineation.

Here, we introduce a 3D vision transformer that processes time series of either S2 or S1 images. When applied to S2 time series, the model effectively navigates sparse cloud coverage by drawing on the spatio-temporal correlations embedded in the data. For S1 time series, immune to cloud interference, the model achieves a spatial resolution comparable to S2, with predictions that are both precise and consistent. In both scenarios, the modelâ€™s ability to harness spatio-temporal relationships within the data marks a significant leap forward in the accuracy and reliability of field boundary delineation.

In this work, we present several key contributions:

  1. 1.

    We introduce a 3D vision transformer architecture, adapted from our previous work on Semantic Segmentation [SSG2 Diakogiannis etÂ al., 2024], specifically designed to process time series of satellite images, whether optical or SAR.

  2. 2.

    We detail a 3D vision attention mechanism that is both memory-efficient and pragmatically effective for time series data.

  3. 3.

    We offer two implementations for field boundary delineation: PTAViT3D, which processes time series of S2 or S1 images, and PTAViT3D-CA, a model that supports data fusion of S2 and S1 time series through cross-attention.

  4. 4.

    We demonstrate that these models can train and perform inference on time series images with partial cloud coverage (S2 input, or S2 and S1 fusion), as well as full cloud coverage (S1 images), with competitive performance.

  5. 5.

    For models trained on S1 time series inputs, we show that predictions match the ground truth label resolution, i.e., S2 pixel resolution of 10m, achieving performance parity with previous S2-based models [Waldner etÂ al., 2021].

  6. 6.

    Finally, we apply the PTAViT3D model to delineate agricultural boundaries across a vast cropping area in Australia using S2 time series images.

2 Methods

2.1 Data

2.1.1 Sentinel 2 and derived Training and Validation Data

The development of our training dataset, based on Sentinel-2 imagery for field boundary detection, follows the methodology outlined in [Waldner etÂ al., 2021]. For completeness, we recap the process briefly. Training data were sourced from 2019 imagery, level L2A, and in particular bands blue (B2 490nm), green(B3 560nm), red (B4 665nm) and near-infrared (NIR 842nm). Additional imagery with 10% cloud tolerance was obtained for the same dates using the Open Data Cube [Accessed: 2023] Software. This new imagery was used after pretraining on the original data, serving as a fine-tuning procedure.

The creation of the annotated data was a model-assisted manual process. Initial field objects were derived through automated segmentation on six selected tiles (50JPL, 53HNC, 54HXE, 55HDA, 55HEE, 56JLQ) with clear-sky imagery. The output was refined by masking non-agricultural areas using CLUM [ABARES, 2016] and delineating fields via a meanshift algorithm [Michel etÂ al., 2015]. Parameter optimization was guided by Bayesian techniques [Snoek etÂ al., 2012] against a sample of fields. This was the starting point for further manual edits and adjustments that ensured accuracy, significantly reducing the initial polygon count. This process yielded over 60,000 fields for training, validation, and testing.

To evaluate the performance of the 2023 ePaddocks product, we collected field boundaries across 67 test tiles, each approximately 1 km2. These areas are a subset of the test set used in [Waldner etÂ al., 2021] for the 2019 season. The 2023 annotation was conducted by our CSIRO team.

2.1.2 Sentinel 1

The Sentinel-1 mission, part of the European Copernicus Program, provides polar-orbiting, all-weather, day-and-night C-band radar imaging for land and ocean services. Sentinel-1A was launched on 3 April 2014, followed by Sentinel-1B on 25 April 2016. The default imaging mode, Interferometric Wide Swath (IW), captures imagery with a 250 km swath at a spatial resolution of 5 m by 20 m. Equipped with dual polarization radars (VV+VH or HH+HV), Sentinel-1 delivers data that is essential for medium- to high-resolution applications, with routine coverage of Australia every 12 days since December 2016, enabling continental-scale land surface mapping and monitoring.

The availability of high-quality dual-pol Sentinel-1 IW data allows the use of dual-polarization Entropy/Alpha decomposition. These decomposition products provide more detailed information about scatterers compared to single-channel backscatter, improving the accuracy of landcover classification and segmentation [Cloude and Pottier, 1997, Zhou etÂ al., 2017].

For this study, we selected Sentinel-1 dual-pol data in both SLC (Single Look Complex) and GRD formats, acquired over the testing sites between May and November 2019. Fig. 2 shows the footprints of Sentinel-1 scenes covering the three testing sites. The site in South Australia required one Sentinel-1 scene, while the others required two consecutive scenes to cover the areas of interest. Details of Sentinel-1 observations are provided in Table 1.

The Sentinel-1 SLC data were pre-processed using ESAâ€™s Sentinel-1 Toolbox. The following steps were applied to each dual-pol VV+VH SLC product: applying the Precise Orbit File, removing thermal noise, radiometric calibration, S1 TOPS deburst and merge, polarimetric speckle filtering, polarimetric decomposition, multi-looking, and terrain geometric correction (geocoding to a pixel spacing of 0.0002 degrees). After processing, adjacent scenes were mosaiced, and three dates were co-registered, resulting in a data stack ready for further analysis, including VV intensity, VH intensity, and three polarimetric decomposition parametersâ€”alpha (Î±ğ›¼\alpha), anisotropy (A), and entropy (H)â€”using the Eigen-based dual-pol decomposition method [Cloude, 2007]. Detailed processing steps are described in [Zhou etÂ al., 2017, Mascolo etÂ al., 2022, Zhou etÂ al., 2023].

In cases where coverage involved two consecutive scenes, the adjacent scenes from the same orbit were mosaiced, followed by co-registration of those data stacks. This prepared the data stacks, including VV intensity, VH intensity, and the three polarimetric decomposition parameters (Î±ğ›¼\alpha, A, and H), for further analysis (see Appendix A for details).

For modelling single input (S1 or S2) time series of observations, we adopt a PTAViT 2D model to account for 3D structure, by using the PTAViT3D Stages. Crucially, the Time dimension is kept along all of the blocks of the module, i.e. there is no pooling or dimensionality reduction along the time dimension. All exchange of information through various time snapshots is achieved with the 3D version of the PTAttention3D. At the end of the 3D features created, we attach another PTAViT3D stage before compacting the time dimension to create the final filtered multitasking segmentation predictions. 2.4 Data pre-processing and Augmentation for Deep Learning pipeline

For the S1 data, the initial imperative step involves extracting areas corresponding to the S2 scenes and reprojecting them to match the S2 pixel resolution. This alignment is essential because the Ground Truth labels are tailored to the spatial resolution of the S2 scenes.

The preprocessing of S1 data is conducted using the function transform_s1 (B.1, Listing LABEL:s1transformation)). This function adjusts the first band, representing the incidence angle Î±ğ›¼\alpha, by converting its values from degrees to radians to standardize its range. Furthermore, it applies a symmetric logarithmic transformation to the VH and VV polarization bands, effectively expanding the dynamic range of these values, making them more suitable for neural network processing. This transformation is particularly beneficial for accommodating the stark contrasts between high magnitude values (VH and VV) and lower magnitude values (Î±ğ›¼\alpha, anisotropy, and entropy), thus enhancing stability during the learning phase. Subsequently, the data are standardized to achieve zero mean and unit variance.

The S2 dataset undergoes a similar standardization process to ensure zero mean and unit variance across all four bands. Given the constraints posed by GPU memory, training chips of size 128Ã—128128128128\times 128 pixels are extracted with a stride of 128 pixels. This method ensures that the dimensions of the training chips remain compatible with the memory capacity of the GPUs utilized.

To further enhance the modelâ€™s generalization ability, a comprehensive suite of data augmentation techniques is employed. These techniques include equal probability selections from a set of geometric transformations. The array of transformations comprises horizontal and vertical flips, elastic transformations for perspective adjustments, grid distortions up to a limit of 0.4, and shift-scale-rotate operations with a shift limit of 0.25, a scale range from 0.75 to 1.25, and a full rotational freedom up to 180 degrees.

This augmentation strategy enriches the training dataset, thereby bolstering the robustness of the deep learning model. These transformations are implemented using the Albumentations library [Buslaev etÂ al., 2020].

3.3 Tackling sparse clouds

In this section we are trying to address the question qualitatively. Given that the way we source data through the open datacube (ODC) API we can only select a cloud percentage, that relates to the whole scene coverage and not individual pixels, as well as the fact that deep learning models see a wide field of view (not just pixels) to make a decision, it is very hard to quantify precisely contaminated pixels in the field of view. Our best hope is to show examples where the requested input imagery (for the case of optical data) was selected with a cloud coverage tolerance. We note that different cloud cover tolerances give in general different number of time series of observations. This affects the overall performance, as the general rule is that the more time observations we use the better the performance [Diakogiannis etÂ al., 2024]. In our approach, we request through the ODC API up to a maximum number of S2 tiles, within a given cloud tolerance, and we keep the ones that have the least cloud contamination and nodata ratio greater than 70%. The key idea in going around the problem of sparse cloud coverage is that clouds move, and within an extended time series, all ground surface regions will be revealed irrespective of partial occlusion due to cloud contamination in individual time instances.

3.3.1 S2 Solution

The proposed algorithm, PTAViT3D that consumes time series of S2A imagery, is very practical in that it can tackle sparse cloud coverage. This removes the need for labour intensive manual cloud masking. In practice we do not observe variation of performance (during inference) with up to 20% tolerance in cloud coverage.

Here, we show two qualitative examples of inference, on a time series of 13 S2A tiles. These where source by requesting cloud coverage tolerance up to 10% for the whole scene. The model used is the ePaddocks final product PTAViT3D trained on S2 time series of inputs. In Fig. 7 we show inference on a zoomed in area that has partially clouded images. In Fig 8 we show the same 3D algorithm applied to clear time series of S2A. By visual inspection of the predictions in these two cases, we see that there is no visible effect of the partially clouded areas on the predictions masks. We note that this is in stark contrast with the work of [Aung etÂ al., 2020] where the Authors there used also time series of input images into their spatio-temporal modeling: they report performance degradation in areas obscured by cloud. This can potentially be attributed in their treatment of time series of S2 images as well as the small number of time instances (they used three instances). In their work they consume time series via concatenation of the input images along the channel dimension, i.e. there is no explicit 3D structure into the deep learning modeling approach.

In comparison with our previous work [Waldner and Diakogiannis, 2020, Waldner etÂ al., 2021], where we were aggregating (averaging) predictions across various dates to achieve consensus, the proposed work here, removes significant overhead and compute, by avoiding sourcing, storing and averaging the predicted results. 3.4.1 Post processing: from raster predictions to shapefiles

Let eğ‘’e and bğ‘b represent the predicted extent and boundaries layers, respectively. These layers capture essential information for instance segmentation, with various algorithms transitioning from semantic to instance segmentation (e.g., watershed segmentation [Waldner and Diakogiannis, 2020], hierarchical segmentation [Waldner etÂ al., 2021]). Here, we present a practical and easy-to-implement approach that utilizes the predicted extent and boundary layers.

The first step involves refining the extent layer using two distinct thresholds: one for the extent, tesubscriptğ‘¡ğ‘’t_{e}, and another for the boundaries, tbsubscriptğ‘¡ğ‘t_{b}. Initially, the predicted boundaries, bğ‘b, are thresholded with tbsubscriptğ‘¡ğ‘t_{b} and undergo morphological thinning to reduce their thickness, followed by a dilation to ensure a single-pixel width. This thinned boundary layer provides a fine segmentation that ensures clear separation between neighboring fields.

Next, the extent layer, eğ‘’e, is combined with the inverted thinned boundaries, 1âˆ’b1ğ‘1-b, to produce a refined mask. This combination is achieved by taking the fuzzy set intersection of eğ‘’e and 1âˆ’b1ğ‘1-b, realized by multiplying the extent with the complement of the boundaries, eâˆ©bÂ¯ğ‘’Â¯ğ‘e\cap\overline{b}, and then thresholding the result based on the extent threshold, tesubscriptğ‘¡ğ‘’t_{e}. This process yields a binary mask that highlights the extents of the fields without the influence of boundaries, ensuring accurate delineation.

We then utilize the rasterio library [Gillies etÂ al., 2013â€“] to convert this refined raster mask into polygon shapefiles. The refined extent mask is passed to the rasterio features.shapes function, which identifies contiguous regions in the binary mask and converts them into geometric shapes. These shapes are then converted into a GeoDataFrame, maintaining the correct spatial reference system (CRS) from the input metadata. The resulting GeoDataFrame can be saved as a shapefile for further analysis or visualization (Listing LABEL:lst:shapefiles_refined). This approach leverages the strengths of the predicted extent and boundary layers to accurately delineate field boundaries, providing a straightforward and efficient method for converting raster predictions into vector formats suitable for geospatial applications. Polygon Matching Algorithm

To identify the best matching polygons between the predicted and ground truth datasets, we implemented the following algorithm: for each predicted polygon, we calculated key metrics such as Intersection over Union (IoU), Hausdorff Distance, and Mean Surface Distance (MSD) against all ground truth polygons. The IoU measures the overlap between the predicted and actual polygons, while the Hausdorff Distance captures the greatest distance between points on the predicted and actual boundaries. The MSD evaluates the average distance between the surfaces of the polygons by analyzing the distances between their vertices.

To ensure meaningful matches, polygons with an IoU below a threshold of 0.001 were excluded from further analysis. The matching process involved iterating through each predicted polygon and comparing it against all ground truth polygons to compute these metrics. Matches were then sorted by IoU in descending order, and only the highest IoU match for each pair was retained, provided it exceeded the IoU threshold. This approach ensures that only the most accurate and relevant matches are considered, enhancing the reliability of the evaluation metrics. Finally summary statistics are evaluated by the individual scores of the best matching polygons.

4 Discussion

This study introduces a suite of models for field boundary detection using time series of Sentinel-2 (S2) and/or Sentinel-1 (S1) imagery, designed to operate effectively even in the presence of sparse (S2 or S2&S1) or dense (S1) cloud cover. Our results demonstrate that using S1 input imagery achieves predictions of comparable spatial resolution to those from S2 data. This finding is particularly significant for applications in tropical regions, where persistent cloud cover poses a challenge for field boundary detection and crop type classification. Our proposed algorithms have been rigorously evaluated against simpler 2D approaches and the robust FracTAL ResUNet baseline.

Key contributions of this work include the introduction of a new 3D Vision Transformer architecture tailored for processing time series of satellite images, a memory-efficient 3D vision attention mechanism, and two implementations for field boundary delineation. The integration of time series data represents a major advancement from our previous consensus approach [Waldner and Diakogiannis, 2020, Waldner etÂ al., 2021], where averaging was used to mitigate cloud coverage effects. By incorporating the time dimension, our model learns from the correlations between different time instances, enabling more reliable predictions and effectively handling both sparse and persistent cloud coverage in an end-to-end manner. This capability holds significant economic value for digital agricultural service providers, allowing them to bypass the labor-intensive process of manually cleaning incomplete masked imagery. Additionally, this approach can be utilized for the creation of training data, as users can supply imagery with partial cloud coverage.

We also outlined the process of creating the 2023 version of Australiaâ€™s National Product of field boundaries, ePaddocks. This involved pretraining on 2019 data, fine-tuning with new imagery, performing inference across multiple tiles, and validating against a manually annotated test set.

Traditional remote sensing approaches often remove cloud-contaminated pixels to maintain data quality, but this can introduce errors or crude approximations that impact downstream modeling tasks. Our work suggests that deep learning models, with their broader field of view, can effectively utilize information from images with partial clouds or haze situations where methods like random forests might fail. Instead of discarding cloud-affected pixels, deep learning models can process these images directly, preserving more of the original data and potentially improving the accuracy and robustness of applications like water quality forecasting.

While this study lays a solid foundation, several avenues for future work could further enhance the modelâ€™s performance and applicability:

  1. 1.

    Crop Type Classification: By altering the target labels to crop type labels, our methodology can be directly applied to crop type classification, even in the presence of sparse cloud coverage, eliminating the need for manual cleaning of input imagery to obtain a clean pixel signal.

  2. 2.

    End-to-End Polygon Inference: Currently, our approach to field boundary detection involves a two-step process: first, semantic segmentation, followed by instance segmentation. To date, our focus has been on the first task, leveraging deep learning architectures for semantic segmentation. The secondary step relies on simplistic thresholding approaches that lack the sophistication of our segmentation methods. Significant performance improvements could be achieved by inferring polygons directly from input imagery in an end-to-end deep learning manner.

  3. 3.

    Geographical Information Encoding: There is potential to boost performance by encoding geographical information (e.g., longitude/latitude) into the modeling process. For instance, incorporating methodologies like the recently proposed SatCLIP [Klemmer etÂ al., 2023] could enhance the modelâ€™s capabilities.
