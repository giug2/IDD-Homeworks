Abstract.

Federated learning (FL) has recently emerged as a privacy-preserving approach for machine learning in domains that rely on user interactions, particularly recommender systems (RS) and online learning to rank (OLTR). While there has been substantial research on the privacy of traditional FL, little attention has been paid to studying the privacy properties of these interaction-based FL (IFL) systems. In this work, we show that IFL can introduce unique challenges concerning user privacy, particularly when the central server has knowledge and control over the items that users interact with. Specifically, we demonstrate the threat of reconstructing user interactions by presenting RAIFLE, a general optimization-based reconstruction attack framework customized for IFL. RAIFLE employs Active Data Manipulation (ADM), a novel attack technique unique to IFL, where the server actively manipulates the training features of the items to induce adversarial behaviors in the local FL updates. We show that RAIFLE is more impactful than existing FL privacy attacks in the IFL context, and describe how it can undermine privacy defenses like secure aggregation and private information retrieval. Based on our findings, we propose and discuss countermeasure guidelines to mitigate our attack in the context of federated RS/OLTR specifically and IFL more broadly.

2.3. Interaction-based FL (IFL)

We broadly define interaction-based FL (IFL) as a variation of FL in which users interact with items provided by the server and perform the local training using their interaction information (Figure 1). The nature of the items depends on the specific application (e.g., news articles for news recommendations or search results for web search). Unlike traditional FL, IFL has two distinctive characteristics: (1) the server can influence how the items are (re)presented to the users, and (2) the primary data to be protected is the user interactions. We describe below two major instances of IFL: federated online learning to rank and federated recommendation systems.

2.3.1. Federated Online Learning to Rank

3.4.3. Bypassing Private Information Retrieval (PIR)

PIRÂ (Chor etÂ al., 1998; Kushilevitz and Ostrovsky, 1997) aims to allow a user to retrieve an item from a database without the server knowing which item is retrieved. 5. Countermeasures

In this section, we discuss several possible countermeasures for RAIFLE. Although LDP can effectively defend against RAIFLE at sufficiently small Îµğœ€\varepsilon (Section 4.2.3), as previous research has demonstrated, it can significantly impact the utility of the learned FL modelÂ (Wei etÂ al., 2020), and FL practitioners often need to use large LDP Îµğœ€\varepsilon in the tens or hundreds range to get meaningful model performanceÂ (Bhowmick etÂ al., 2019). While we believe that DP (in conjunction with SA) is one of the best privacy defenses for reconstruction attacks, we also think that additional countermeasures can be incorporated with DP to help reduce the burden of privacy protection on DP.

5.1. Data validation

To prevent the server from executing ADM, users can validate the authenticity of the items sent by the server using cryptography techniques. For example, users can employ digital signatures to ensure that their training items are the same as all of their peers (similar to the parameters validation defense inÂ (Pasquini etÂ al., 2022)). However, different users can have different items, which may make it difficult for users to compare their itemsâ€™ features with one another. Furthermore, detecting inconsistency among users would fail if the server manipulates the data in the same manner for all users. Therefore, it may also be necessary to validate the quality of the data, e.g. by enforcing certain rules for the feature values and ranges to check for â€œdeadâ€ features (i.e. all zeroes) or gradients. Designing a comprehensive set of data quality checks can also be challenging due to the vast possibilities of manipulation techniques.

5.2. Minimizing information

While current FL schemes often rely on the sharing of gradients or parameters, such detailed information can both passively and actively breach user privacy as weâ€™ve empirically shown in our paper. The FOLtR-ES algorithmÂ (Kharitonov, 2019) is a federated OLTR algorithm that attempts to minimize the amount of shared information by only sharing a single number for the local rankersâ€™ utility, which also happens to be non-smooth w.r.t.Â the interactions, thus rendering RAIFLE ineffective. The gradient pruning defenseÂ (Zhu etÂ al., 2019) can also be considered a form of information minimization, but because it is based on magnitude, it fails to hide the strongest gradient signals and thus does not defend well against RAIFLE. Minimization strategies, therefore, need to be carefully designed to prevent/reduce leakage as well as maintain good FL utility.

5.3. Personalization

Personalized FL (PFL) is an emerging FL approach that aims to learn a customized model for each user to address the issue of heterogeneous data as well as provide user-specific personalizationÂ (Tan etÂ al., 2022). In the context of federated RS, collaborative filtering-based methods such as FCF and FNCF can also be considered PFL algorithms since they learn a private user embedding for each user. From a privacy point of view, PFL offers both opportunities and challenges: introducing a privately-learned component can potentially help users rely less on the global model, thus reducing the amount of information needed to share with the server, but at the same time, the private component also needs to be properly safeguarded.

6. Conclusion

This work identifies and explores the threat of reconstruction attacks in interaction-based federated learning (IFL), particularly in the context of federated recommender systems (RS) and online learning to rank (OLTR). Our novel optimization-based RAIFLE method exploits the IFL serverâ€™s knowledge and control over the interaction items to execute Active Data Manipulation (ADM), a unique attack vector where the server actively manipulates the items. We demonstrated that RAIFLE can achieve strong reconstruction performances in a variety of settings, including LDP with reasonably strong privacy budget. Our results shed light on the dire risk of inferring user interactions in IFL by malicious servers. We also discussed a variety of countermeasures against RAIFLE and ADM, including data validation, personalization, and information minimization. In future work, we aim to explore additional IFL settings such as multi-modal informational retrieval/recommendation, which involves images and texts.
