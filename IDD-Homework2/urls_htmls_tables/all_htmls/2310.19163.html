RAIFLE: Reconstruction Attacks on Interaction-based Federated Learning with Active Data Manipulation

Dzung Pham dzungpham@cs.umass.edu University of Massachusetts AmherstAmherstMAUSA ,  Shreyas Kulkarni svkulkarni@cs.umass.edu University of Massachusetts AmherstAmherstMAUSA  and  Amir Houmansadr amir@cs.umass.edu University of Massachusetts AmherstAmherstMAUSA
Abstract.

Federated learning (FL) has recently emerged as a privacy-preserving approach for machine learning in domains that rely on user interactions, particularly recommender systems (RS) and online learning to rank (OLTR). While there has been substantial research on the privacy of traditional FL, little attention has been paid to studying the privacy properties of these interaction-based FL (IFL) systems. In this work, we show that IFL can introduce unique challenges concerning user privacy, particularly when the central server has knowledge and control over the items that users interact with. Specifically, we demonstrate the threat of reconstructing user interactions by presenting RAIFLE, a general optimization-based reconstruction attack framework customized for IFL. RAIFLE employs Active Data Manipulation (ADM), a novel attack technique unique to IFL, where the server actively manipulates the training features of the items to induce adversarial behaviors in the local FL updates. We show that RAIFLE is more impactful than existing FL privacy attacks in the IFL context, and describe how it can undermine privacy defenses like secure aggregation and private information retrieval. Based on our findings, we propose and discuss countermeasure guidelines to mitigate our attack in the context of federated RS/OLTR specifically and IFL more broadly.

1. Introduction

Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) is an emerging approach to building privacy-preserving recommender systems (RS) (Yang et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022) and online learning to rank (OLTR) solutions (Kharitonov, 2019; Wang et al., 2021). In such systems, users interact with server-prepared “items” (e.g., news articles, media, and products) via clicks, ratings, and other types of interactions, then train their FL model using their private interactions (Figure 1). We broadly define this variation on FL as interaction-based FL (IFL). Unlike traditional FL, users in IFL do not inherently own any data other than their interactions with the server’s items, which are typically known and controlled by the server. As the two most prominent instances of IFL, RS and OLTR have many far-reaching applications such as web search (e.g., Google), online advertising (e.g., Facebook ads), and e-commerce (e.g., Amazon), which often rely on vast amounts of private and sensitive user data. Consequently, the use of IFL for RS/OLTR has gained popularity in academia, with a wide variety of algorithms proposed such as federated collaborative filtering (Ammad-ud-din et al., 2019; Perifanis and Efraimidis, 2022), federated graph neural net for recommendation (Wu et al., 2022), reinforcement learning-based federated OLTR (Kharitonov, 2019), etc. Although there are no real-world deployments of federated RS/OLTR currently, their potential to leverage data from diverse sources while maintaining user privacy and data ownership makes them a compelling approach to enhancing recommendation/ranking accuracy and addressing data silo challenges.

Despite substantial research on the privacy of traditional FL (Kairouz et al., 2021b), limited attention has been given to studying IFL’s privacy properties. Previous work on federated RS/OLTR (Sun et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021) has often overlooked the server’s knowledge and control over the items presented to users. This unique aspect of IFL is also not considered in existing FL privacy attacks like gradient inversion (Huang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Ovi and Gangopadhyay, 2023), since in traditional FL, the server usually cannot exert a great deal of influence on users’ data or behaviors. IFL’s departure from standard FL thus poses a significant privacy risk that is currently not fully understood. Of particular consequence is the possibility of leaking users’ private interactions and preferences in IFL, which can result in serious ramifications such as compromising user anonymity (Narayanan and Shmatikov, 2008), exposing sensitive interests (Weinsberg et al., 2012), and enabling targeted advertising (Toubiana et al., 2010), ultimately leading to the loss of user trust. Addressing these privacy challenges is crucial to ensure the responsible and secure implementation of FL for RS and OLTR.

In this work, we aim to study the risk of reconstructing users’ interaction data in IFL. Specifically, we present RAIFLE,111Pronounced like “rifle” a general reconstruction attack for interaction-based federated learning systems. Similar to the gradient inversion attacks in traditional FL (Huang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Ovi and Gangopadhyay, 2023), RAIFLE aims to find the most likely interactions by minimizing the “distance” between the simulated local update created with candidate interactions and the actual received local update. Utilizing automatic differentiation (Baydin et al., 2017), RAIFLE can calculate the gradients of the local updates’ distance w.r.t. the interactions for any types of models as long as they are “smooth” w.r.t. the interactions. However, unlike gradient inversion, RAIFLE exploits the IFL server’s knowledge and control over the items to execute Active Data Manipulation (ADM), a novel attack vector where the server modifies the training data features associated with the items to produce adversarial behaviors in the local updates. More concretely, the server can “fingerprint” the data presented to a target client by choosing which features to keep or to “zero out” in order to affect the gradients’ values in a known and controlled manner. We show that this unique active attack can outperform vanilla gradient inversion as well as undermine existing privacy-enhancing mechanisms such as secure aggregation (Bonawitz et al., 2017) and private information retrieval (PIR) (Chor et al., 1998; Kushilevitz and Ostrovsky, 1997). To the best of our knowledge, RAIFLE is the first optimization-based active reconstruction attack applicable to not only federated RS/OLTR but also the more general IFL setting.

Under less-than-ideal circumstances (e.g., more items than parameters, multi-batch training), enabling ADM can significantly improve RAIFLE’s performance. For example, in our evaluation for OLTR (without differential privacy applied), the AUC of attacking neural net using ADM increases from 0.5 (basically random guessing) to around 0.7. Moreover, our customized RAIFLE for federated RS manages to steal the private user embeddings in the federated collaborative filtering algorithm (Ammad-ud-din et al., 2019) with minimal error.

In light of these findings, we discuss the pros and cons of various potential countermeasures to alleviate the risks of our privacy attacks, including data validation, personalization, and data minimization. These defenses can be used in conjunction with differential privacy, which is considered the the gold standard for privacy defense (Dwork and Roth, 2014). We hope that our research can inform future endeavors aimed at developing more secure and private federated RS/OLTR systems specifically and IFL in general. 2. Background and Related Work

2.1. Privacy Attacks on FL

Federated Learning (FL) is a decentralized approach to machine learning that allows each participant to collaboratively train a machine learning model without having to share their private data with a central server (McMahan et al., 2017; Kairouz et al., 2021b). Research has shown that FL can be vulnerable to a class of attack called gradient inversion (Huang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Ovi and Gangopadhyay, 2023), which allows an honest-but-curious server to invert users’ gradients to find an approximation of users’ local data by solving an optimization problem of the following form:

The majority of research in gradient inversion has so far been focused on deep neural networks for computer vision and natural language processing tasks (Ovi and Gangopadhyay, 2023), which differ from RS and OLTR in the following ways: (a) user interactions in RS and OLTR research are typically structured discrete data (e.g., clicks vs no clicks), unlike images and texts; (b) RS/OLTR models can have much fewer parameters and are also not restricted to neural nets. These differences can render existing gradient inversion attacks not immediately applicable to the RS/OLTR context.

To the best of our knowledge, only one paper has attempted to devise a privacy attack to steal user interactions in federated RS (Yuan et al., 2023). However, their proposed Interaction Membership Inference Attack (IMIA) is a heuristic-based search that neither makes use of the gradients with respect to the simulated interactions nor attempts to estimate the private user parameters, resulting in poor inference performance. Furthermore, the attack is limited to only federated RS and cannot be directly applied to other IFL scenarios.

2.2. Privacy Defenses for FL

2.2.1. Differential Privacy

Considered the state-of-the-art privacy model and defense in statistics and machine learning, differential privacy (DP) (Dwork and Roth, 2014; Abadi et al., 2016) has been applied in numerous FL applications (McMahan et al., 2018; Ramaswamy et al., 2020; Kairouz et al., 2021b). where ε≥0𝜀0\varepsilon\geq 0 represents the privacy budget and δ∈[0,1)𝛿01\delta\in[0,1) represents the probability of privacy leakage. In FL, the randomized mechanism ℳℳ\mathcal{M} is typically the training process that occurs on users’ devices and the aggregation process.

The definition above describes the central DP model, which in the context of FL means that users trust a third-party curator with the collection, aggregation, and privatization of their local updates before releasing them to the server. In this paper, we rely on the stricter but more realistic local differential privacy model (LDP), where users do not trust any entity with their data (Kasiviswanathan et al., 2008). Formally, LDP requires equation 2 to hold for any pair of x,x′𝑥superscript𝑥′x,x^{\prime}. While LDP can effectively defend against gradient inversion attacks (Zhang et al., 2022), it often incurs a high utility cost (Wei et al., 2020). Practical levels of ε𝜀\varepsilon for LDP therefore tend to be “in the hundreds” (Bhowmick et al., 2019; Hartman and Kairouz, 2023) in order to achieve a decent model utility. Achieving a good balance between utility and privacy thus remains a challenging problem (Cummings et al., 2023).

2.2.2. Secure Aggregation

Another recent line of privacy defense for FL is secure aggregation (SA) (Bonawitz et al., 2017; Bell et al., 2020), which relies on cryptography techniques to securely aggregate (e.g., summing) the local updates before sharing them with the server. With this mechanism, the server can only observe the aggregated result without knowing the individual contributions from any user. SA, therefore, aims to achieve two objectives: “privacy by aggregation” – to make it difficult for the server to infer useful information about individuals from the aggregated updates – and “privacy by shuffling” – to hide the link between individuals and updates so that even if any information can be inferred, it cannot be traced back to any particular user (Pasquini et al., 2022). Nevertheless, research has shown that vanilla SA for FL is susceptible to a class of privacy attacks that we broadly refer to as active model manipulation, where the server directly modifies the model’s architecture and/or parameters to induce specific behaviors in the learned local updates that would remain visible in the final aggregation (Pasquini et al., 2022; Fowl et al., 2021). To prevent such attacks with theoretical guarantees, SA needs to be combined with user-applied noise to achieve distributed differential privacy (Kairouz et al., 2021a).

2.3. Interaction-based FL (IFL)

We broadly define interaction-based FL (IFL) as a variation of FL in which users interact with items provided by the server and perform the local training using their interaction information (Figure 1). The nature of the items depends on the specific application (e.g., news articles for news recommendations or search results for web search). Unlike traditional FL, IFL has two distinctive characteristics: (1) the server can influence how the items are (re)presented to the users, and (2) the primary data to be protected is the user interactions. We describe below two major instances of IFL: federated online learning to rank and federated recommendation systems.

2.3.1. Federated Online Learning to Rank

Learning to rank (LTR) is a machine learning task whose aim is to learn ranking models for information retrieval systems and has been applied to a variety of applications such as recommendation and web search (Liu, 2009). There are three major LTR approaches: (a) the pointwise approach compares each item to a relevance label, (b) pairwise compares pairs of items with different preferences, and (c) listwise considers the entire ranked list as a unit to optimize the overall ranking. In this paper, we focus on (federated) Online Learning to Rank (OLTR), which primarily learns from user interactions (i.e., implicit feedback), unlike traditional LTR which relies on labeled (i.e., explicit) relevance judgement (Grotov and de Rijke, 2016; Kharitonov, 2019; Wang et al., 2021). Of particular interest is the Federated Pairwise Differentiable Gradient Descent (FPDGD) method, which proposes using the Pairwise Differentiable Gradient Descent (PDGD) algorithm (Wang et al., 2021; Oosterhuis and de Rijke, 2018) in the FL setting. The authors of FPDGD claim a higher ranking performance than the existing state-of-the-art FOLTR algorithm (Kharitonov, 2019), even DP applied. The evaluation with DP is not fully substantiated, however, as it contains two issues: (a) the Laplace mechanism used only satisfies central DP, whereas the mechanism used in (Kharitonov, 2019) satisfies LDP, and (b) the authors clipped the L2subscript𝐿2L_{2}-norm of the model weights instead of L1subscript𝐿1L_{1}-norm as required by the Laplace mechanism.222FPDGD’s source code: https://github.com/ielab/fpdgd-ictir2021

2.3.2. Federated Recommender Systems

Recommender systems (RS) is a technology that assists users with discovering relevant items like products, news, media, etc. (Ricci et al., 2022). RS typically relies on user-item interactions, which unfortunately have been shown to be capable of revealing sensitive user attributes (e.g., age, gender) (Weinsberg et al., 2012) and even uniquely identifying users (Narayanan and Shmatikov, 2008). To keep user interactions protected, recent research has looked to FL as the foundation to build more privacy-preserving recommendation services (Yang et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022).

One of the earliest and most influential RS algorithms is collaborative filtering (CF), which recommends items based on the behaviors and preferences of similar users (Goldberg et al., 1992). A common approach to CF is matrix factorization (Canny, 2002): essentially, the user-item interaction matrix is factored into two lower-dimensional latent factor matrices, one for the user embeddings and one for the item embeddings. Building upon CF, federated collaborative filtering (FCF) formulates the item matrix as public parameters (i.e., known by all participants) and the user matrix as private parameters, which are privately updated by each user without being shared with anyone else (Ammad-ud-din et al., 2019). This idea has been incorporated in various subsequent works such as federated neural collaborative filtering (FNCF) (Perifanis and Efraimidis, 2022) and federated graph neural net for recommendation (FedGNN) (Wu et al., 2022). Keeping user embeddings private by itself does not automatically guarantee privacy for users since their interactions can be inferred through multiple observations of unprotected updates (Chai et al., 2021) or through RAIFLE (Table 3).

3. Introducing the RAIFLE Attack

In this section, we describe the high-level approach of RAIFLE, our reconstruction attack on IFL. Descriptions of useful notations used throughout the subsequent sections are provided in Table 1. 3.1. Threat Model

Our adversary is a server that not only knows which items are presented to which users but can also modify the (re)presentations of the items served to users, particularly the training features used in the local FL training. This threat model is consistent with previous research that involves malicious FL servers (Bonawitz et al., 2017; Bell et al., 2020; Pasquini et al., 2022). The server’s knowledge of user-item impressions is realistic in most practical IFL systems like federated RS and OLTR. While it is technically possible to hide the user-item mapping via Private Information Retrieval (PIR) (Chor et al., 1998), the prohibitively expensive computational costs prevent such a technique from being widely adopted at scale. The ability to modify items is also justifiable for various business/operational reasons, such as the need to experiment with new/updated item features to improve service quality.

3.3. Active Data Manipulation: Fingerprinting

In this section, we introduce a novel attack vector referred to as Active Data Manipulation (ADM), in which the server actively manipulates the items’ features. This technique is unique to IFL and is not applicable to traditional FL since users in IFL interact with data prepared by the server. We propose one ADM method called data fingerprinting, where the server modifies the items’ features to influence the training process such that the user’s local update for any feature parameter can be deterministically zero or non-zero, thus acting as a form of signal or identifier for the server.

Consider any single model parameter θjsubscript𝜃𝑗\theta_{j} that is in direct contact with at least one feature of the training items. For example, if the model is linear or logistic regression, then any non-bias weight is one such parameter. Similarly, if the model is a neural network, then any non-bias weight in the first layer of the neural net will satisfy the criterion, while weights in other layers are only indirectly involved and thus do not fit the criterion. We refer to parameters that are in direct contact with the training features as feature parameters (see Figure 2).

3.4. Privacy Exploits with ADM

3.4.1. Reconstruction Attack

As we later demonstrate in Section 4.2, ADM can significantly enhance our attack performance when there are more items than the model’s parameters, which can hinder reconstruction. More specifically, we use the proposed data fingerprinting technique to “allocate” a subset of the features to each item or item batch that the server is interested in. For example, in the multi-query setting for federated OLTR (Section 4.2), for each query, the server can choose a different subset of the features to exclusively “attend” to the query’s items by zeroing out the values of all other features (Figure 3). This can disambiguate the combined effects of multiple queries since training on each batch will now only affect a distinct subset of features, thus making it easier for RAIFLE to optimize. If the server knows all items that a user possesses but does not know exactly which items were used in the user’s training (e.g. user randomly sampling from their items), the server can also fingerprint items of interest such that training on those items would leave behind an easily identifiable mark in the local update. This trick can be used to bypass PIR (Section 3.4.3).

Thus, when the local updates from all users are securely aggregated (e.g. via summation), the final result will leak uksubscript𝑢𝑘u_{k}’s local update since we are only adding 00’s from other users to uksubscript𝑢𝑘u_{k}’s update. Consequently, the server can now execute RAIFLE on user uksubscript𝑢𝑘u_{k} using the singled-out updates. Note that this exploit is not restricted to just a single target user. By allocating a distinct portion of the feature space to each target user and making sure that only each such user can have non-zero values for their allocated features, the server can learn their individual update from the final aggregated result by looking at the corresponding feature parameters.

3.4.3. Bypassing Private Information Retrieval (PIR)

PIR (Chor et al., 1998; Kushilevitz and Ostrovsky, 1997) aims to allow a user to retrieve an item from a database without the server knowing which item is retrieved. If a user in a PIR protocol also participates in interaction-based FL training (e.g. for OLTR), then the privacy of their retrieved items can potentially be broken via active manipulation. Consider a user u𝑢u and an item i𝑖i; the server is interested in whether u𝑢u has retrieved i𝑖i or not. If i𝑖i was included in the local FL training by u𝑢u, then the local update sent to the server may reveal this fact (assuming that the server knows the local update belongs to u𝑢u). To achieve this, the server can choose a feature for i𝑖i and zero out the values of that feature for all items other than i𝑖i. Then, if the local update from user u𝑢u has a non-zero update to the feature parameters corresponding to the chosen feature, then it must be the case that i𝑖i was included in u𝑢u’s training data.

4. Evaluation

Here, we describe our experiment setup and results. For more details about the experiments, see Appendix C.

4.1. Experiment Setup

4.1.1. Scenarios

We evaluated our attack on recent federated OLTR and federated RS algorithms in the following main scenarios:

No defense, without manipulation: In this scenario, each user’s local update is sent to the server without any protection, and the server does not engage in any form of manipulation. We attacked each user once using a different random set of model parameters, then averaged the attack performance across all users.

For OLTR, we tested both single-query training (i.e. user only trains once on a single query) and multi-query training (i.e. user trains on multiple batches of queries). The multi-query setting is thus similar to FedAvg algorithm (McMahan et al., 2017) and therefore more practical and more challenging for RAIFLE than single-query (Dimitrov et al., 2022). We tested with {4,8,12,16}481216\{4,8,12,16\} queries, and for each query, we presented the user with no more than 10 items sampled according to the current ranking model (similar to (Wang et al., 2021)).

For RS, we tested 4 different variations of RAIFLE based on our specialized algorithm for RS (Appendix B.2): whether or not model parameters are included in the optimization target, and whether or not the private user embedding is jointly optimized along with the interactions. We defined the loss function for the item embeddings as the average of the L2subscript𝐿2L_{2} distance between each pair of original and updated item embedding.

No defense, with active data manipulation: This scenario is similar to the one described above, except the server engages in active manipulation of the item’s training features and/or model parameters for the multi-query setting of OLTR. 5. Countermeasures

In this section, we discuss several possible countermeasures for RAIFLE. Although LDP can effectively defend against RAIFLE at sufficiently small ε𝜀\varepsilon (Section 4.2.3), as previous research has demonstrated, it can significantly impact the utility of the learned FL model (Wei et al., 2020), and FL practitioners often need to use large LDP ε𝜀\varepsilon in the tens or hundreds range to get meaningful model performance (Bhowmick et al., 2019). While we believe that DP (in conjunction with SA) is one of the best privacy defenses for reconstruction attacks, we also think that additional countermeasures can be incorporated with DP to help reduce the burden of privacy protection on DP.

5.1. Data validation

To prevent the server from executing ADM, users can validate the authenticity of the items sent by the server using cryptography techniques. For example, users can employ digital signatures to ensure that their training items are the same as all of their peers (similar to the parameters validation defense in (Pasquini et al., 2022)). However, different users can have different items, which may make it difficult for users to compare their items’ features with one another. Furthermore, detecting inconsistency among users would fail if the server manipulates the data in the same manner for all users. Therefore, it may also be necessary to validate the quality of the data, e.g. by enforcing certain rules for the feature values and ranges to check for “dead” features (i.e. all zeroes) or gradients. Designing a comprehensive set of data quality checks can also be challenging due to the vast possibilities of manipulation techniques.

5.2. Minimizing information

While current FL schemes often rely on the sharing of gradients or parameters, such detailed information can both passively and actively breach user privacy as we’ve empirically shown in our paper. The FOLtR-ES algorithm (Kharitonov, 2019) is a federated OLTR algorithm that attempts to minimize the amount of shared information by only sharing a single number for the local rankers’ utility, which also happens to be non-smooth w.r.t. the interactions, thus rendering RAIFLE ineffective. The gradient pruning defense (Zhu et al., 2019) can also be considered a form of information minimization, but because it is based on magnitude, it fails to hide the strongest gradient signals and thus does not defend well against RAIFLE. Minimization strategies, therefore, need to be carefully designed to prevent/reduce leakage as well as maintain good FL utility.

5.3. Personalization

Personalized FL (PFL) is an emerging FL approach that aims to learn a customized model for each user to address the issue of heterogeneous data as well as provide user-specific personalization (Tan et al., 2022). In the context of federated RS, collaborative filtering-based methods such as FCF and FNCF can also be considered PFL algorithms since they learn a private user embedding for each user. From a privacy point of view, PFL offers both opportunities and challenges: introducing a privately-learned component can potentially help users rely less on the global model, thus reducing the amount of information needed to share with the server, but at the same time, the private component also needs to be properly safeguarded.

6. Conclusion

This work identifies and explores the threat of reconstruction attacks in interaction-based federated learning (IFL), particularly in the context of federated recommender systems (RS) and online learning to rank (OLTR). Our novel optimization-based RAIFLE method exploits the IFL server’s knowledge and control over the interaction items to execute Active Data Manipulation (ADM), a unique attack vector where the server actively manipulates the items. We demonstrated that RAIFLE can achieve strong reconstruction performances in a variety of settings, including LDP with reasonably strong privacy budget. Our results shed light on the dire risk of inferring user interactions in IFL by malicious servers. We also discussed a variety of countermeasures against RAIFLE and ADM, including data validation, personalization, and information minimization. In future work, we aim to explore additional IFL settings such as multi-modal informational retrieval/recommendation, which involves images and texts.

Appendix B RAIFLE’s Implementation Details

In this section, we describe some important implementation details for RAIFLE, including how to enable automatic differentiation and choices of optimization algorithm and loss function. We leave other implementation variations of RAIFLE for future work.

B.1. Enabling Differentiation w.r.t. Interactions

Usually, user interactions in RS/OLTR are discrete-valued. Furthermore, certain RS/OLTR algorithms might not be immediately differentiable w.r.t. the interactions ℐ′superscriptℐ′\mathcal{I}^{\prime}. To enable RAIFLE in these cases, the server needs to make some modifications to the learning algorithm g𝑔g. The general strategy is to treat discrete interaction values as continuous “degrees” of interactions and modify any non-smooth usage of interactions to become smooth. Most often, any “un-differentiable” operations involving the interactions can be found in the loss function ℒF​Lsubscriptℒ𝐹𝐿\mathcal{L}_{FL} used by g𝑔g. We outline some general methods to modify representative RS/OLTR learning paradigms below (Liu, 2009):

Pointwise: If ℒF​Lsubscriptℒ𝐹𝐿\mathcal{L}_{FL} is not differentiable w.r.t. to ℐ′superscriptℐ′\mathcal{I^{\prime}} (e.g. 0-1 classification loss), change it to a differentiable one such as L2subscript𝐿2L_{2} loss. In fact, if ℒF​Lsubscriptℒ𝐹𝐿\mathcal{L}_{FL} is L2subscript𝐿2L_{2} loss, then we can show that ∇ℐ′2g=𝟎superscriptsubscript∇superscriptℐ′2𝑔0\nabla_{\mathcal{I^{\prime}}}^{2}g=\mathbf{0} (see Appendix A.2), which implies that the attack is convex (theorem 1).

Pairwise: Instead of only including item pairs in which one item is “preferred” over the other (e.g. pairs of click and no click), ℒF​Lsubscriptℒ𝐹𝐿\mathcal{L}_{FL} can assign a weight to each pair based on how large the difference in preference is, then calculate the weighted sum of the losses of all pairs (see example in Section 4.1.3). Note that while this can enable once-differentiation, the Hessian of g𝑔g will likely not be equal to 𝟎0\mathbf{0} since we are now considering how user interactions “interacts” with one another.

Listwise: There is a wide variety of listwise losses developed in the learning-to-rank literature (Liu, 2009), some of which are naturally differentiable w.r.t. user interactions, while for many others the relationship is not immediately clear without redefining the loss formulas. As in the case of pairwise losses, even though once-differentiation might be possible, the Hessian of g𝑔g might not be 𝟎0\mathbf{0}. Due to the vast assortments of listwise methods and the lack of federated listwise algorithms, we leave a comprehensive investigation of listwise attacks for future research.

B.2. Customization for Federated RS

As mentioned in Section 2.3.1, users in many federated RS schemes keep a private user embedding eusubscript𝑒𝑢e_{u} that is not known to the server. eusubscript𝑒𝑢e_{u} is combined with the public item embeddings 𝒳𝒳\mathcal{X} to produce a personalized ranking score for the items. In addition to the learned parameters θ^^𝜃\hat{\theta}, users also send back the updated item embeddings 𝒳^^𝒳\hat{\mathcal{X}} to the server. To improve RAIFLE’s performance in this scenario, we propose joint optimization of user interactions and private user embedding, with the objective function being the sum of the model parameters’ loss and the item embeddings’ loss. Our modified attack for federated RS is presented in Algorithm 2.

B.3. Optimization Algorithms and Losses

There are various options for the loss function ℒℒ\mathcal{L} and the optimization algorithm of RAIFLE. Previous research in gradient inversion attacks has often used the L2subscript𝐿2L_{2} loss combined with the L-BFGS optimizer (Zhu et al., 2019) or the cosine distance loss with the Adam optimizer (Geiping et al., 2020). In our experiment, we tested the combination of L2subscript𝐿2L_{2} and L-BFGS versus cosine distance and Adam and did not find any significant difference in attack performance, likely because our optimization parameters and model size are much smaller compared to the typical deep neural nets with images/texts settings. For ease of analysis and implementation, we thus work with L2subscript𝐿2L_{2} and L-BFGS.

Since user interactions can have boundary constraints (e.g. ratings between 1 and 5), to enable bounded optimization, we initialize an interaction degree vector via the Uniform distribution Unif​(−1,1)Unif11\text{Unif}(-1,1), restrict it to between 00 and 111 via the sigmoid function, then shift/scale to the correct range. Thus, the optimization updates the interaction degree vector, not the interactions directly. For other constrained optimization approaches, see (Nocedal and Wright, 2006).

When the convergence conditions for RAIFLE cannot be met (Section A), we find that it can be helpful to include the following termination criteria:

    Number of optimization steps: Stops if the number of steps exceeds the computational budget

B.4. Prior on Interactions

In our paper, we did not include a prior on the interactions for RAIFLE. The use of a prior can potentially be helpful depending on how well the server can estimate the distribution of user interactions, which can get complicated by privacy requirements. In the case of RS/OLTR, the server can utilize the items’ popularity or build a model of the user interactions (e.g. click models (Grotov et al., 2015)) for the prior. While privacy-preserving prior estimation is not within the scope of our paper, we hope to investigate this direction in the future. While we’ve experimented with some representative learning algorithms for federated RS/OLTR, there are many other RS/OLTR methods that we have yet to explore such as listwise approaches to OLTR (Liu, 2009) and hybrid approaches to RS (Çano and Morisio, 2017).

Appendix C More Evaluation Details

C.1. Metrics

We used the area under the curve of the receiver operating characteristic (AUC) (Bradley, 1997) as the primary metric to measure the performance of RAIFLE since we don’t want to rely on a fixed threshold for the classification and we equally care about positive and negative interactions (even if they are imbalanced). We performed the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) statistical test (Dodge, 2008) to compare RAIFLE’s AUC against that of random guessing (as well as variations of RAIFLE against one another).

C.2. Code

We used PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) to implement the federated OLTR/RS models as well as RAIFLE, relying extensively on both PyTorch’s autograd library and its recently introduced JAX-like library (Bradbury et al., 2018) to calculate gradients for the models and RAIFLE. We used IBM’s diffprivlib Python library for the DP noise (Holohan et al., 2019).

C.4. RAIFLE against gradient pruning

We also evaluated RAIFLE with gradient pruning (Zhu et al., 2019) – another possible defense against reconstruction attack. Our results demonstrate that in order for gradient pruning to be effective, we would need to prune more than 90%percent9090\% of the local updates before RAIFLE’s AUC are close to random guessing (figures 9 and 10). Interestingly, for both OLTR and RS, neural nets tend to be less affected by gradient pruning than other FL algorithms, which is mostly likely because of the high number of parameters in neural nets. Note that we enhanced RAIFLE with ADM for OLTR (except for when the number of queries is just 1).
