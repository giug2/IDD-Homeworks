HealthQ: Unveiling Questioning Capabilities of LLM Chains in Healthcare Conversations

Ziyu Wang111Ziyu Wang and Hao Li contributed equally to this work. ziyuw31@uci.edu Hao Li222Ziyu Wang and Hao Li contributed equally to this work. hl3776@columbia.edu Di Huang di.huang@wustl.edu and Amir M. Rahmani333Corresponding author. amirr1@hs.uci.edu
Abstract

In digital healthcare, large language models (LLMs) have primarily been utilized to enhance question-answering capabilities and improve patient interactions. However, effective patient care necessitates LLM chains that can actively gather information by posing relevant questions. This paper presents HealthQ, a novel framework designed to evaluate the questioning capabilities of LLM healthcare chains. We implemented several LLM chains, including Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), Chain of Thought (CoT), and reflective chains, and introduced an LLM judge to assess the relevance and informativeness of the generated questions. To validate HealthQ, we employed traditional Natural Language Processing (NLP) metrics such as Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation (ROUGE) and Named Entity Recognition (NER)-based set comparison, and constructed two custom datasets from public medical note datasets, ChatDoctor and MTS-Dialog. Our contributions are threefold: we provide the first comprehensive study on the questioning capabilities of LLMs in healthcare conversations, develop a novel dataset generation pipeline, and propose a detailed evaluation methodology.

1 Introduction

The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in healthcare has significantly transformed the landscape of personal healthcare, enabling advancements in diagnostic accuracy, treatment personalization, and patient management [1, 2, 3, 4]. Central to these advancements are Large Language Models (LLMs), which have shown outstanding abilities in comprehending and producing human-like text. These capabilities have been harnessed to facilitate various healthcare interventions, from virtual health assistants to automated diagnostic systems [5]. Current research in digital healthcare has extensively explored the use of LLMs to enhance question-answering (QA) capabilities. QA systems are crucial as they provide patients with accurate and timely responses to their medical inquiries, thereby improving patient outcomes, increasing accessibility to healthcare resources, and enhancing overall patient satisfaction and well-being. Numerous datasets and benchmarks, such as MedQA [4], emrQA [6], and MedMCQA [7], have been developed to train and evaluate LLMs in their ability to answer patient questions effectively.

Recent advancements demonstrate LLMs can be organized into chains to tackle more complex interactions [5], for instance, interacting with patients via natural language while utilizing components that enhance the capabilities of LLMs by integrating additional tools and knowledge bases, such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) [8] and ReAct [9]. This structure allows the LLM to not only generate answers to questions but also retrieve relevant information, apply reasoning, and perform specific tasks more effectively.

In real-world LLM healthcare applications, the capability to answer patient questions is just one facet of their functionality [10]. Effective interaction requires LLM healthcare chains to actively engage patients by asking relevant and context-aware questions to gather comprehensive information. This includes symptoms, medical history, lifestyle factors, and other pertinent details that are critical for accurate diagnosis and personalized treatment. By actively soliciting detailed patient information, LLM healthcare chains can enrich the context in which they operate, leveraging integrated tools and smart agents to enhance diagnostic accuracy, provide better interventions, and offer more tailored health advice. Despite the critical importance of this interactive capability, the evaluation of LLM healthcare chains’ questioning abilities—specifically, their effectiveness in asking pertinent questions to elicit essential information from patients—remains significantly under-explored [11, 12].

Addressing this gap, our work proposes a novel evaluation framework to assess and benchmark the questioning capabilities of LLM healthcare chains during conversational interactions with patients. HealthQ not only evaluates the quality of the questions posed by the LLM healthcare chains but also examines whether these questions help in obtaining better answers. We employ mutual information validation to demonstrate the relationship between the quality of the questions and the improvement in the answers received. This approach ensures that the LLM healthcare chains can effectively interact with patients, leading to more accurate diagnoses and better healthcare outcomes.

In summary, our contributions are threefold:

  1. 1.

    We conduct the first comprehensive study on the questioning capabilities of LLM healthcare chains, filling a significant gap in healthcare AI research.

  2. 2.

    We introduce a novel dataset generation pipeline, specifically designed to evaluate the question-asking performance of LLM healthcare chains in realistic patient interaction scenarios.

  3. 3.

    We develop and rigorously validate an evaluation framework that integrates multiple LLM chain types and traditional Natural Language Processing (NLP) metrics. Our framework is validated through two key research questions, ensuring its robustness and providing a solid benchmark for future studies on LLM question-asking in healthcare.

We aim for this framework to serve as a platform for researchers to develop general chain models capable of solving complex real-world questions through extensive logical reasoning in both retrieval and comprehension stages. This approach will enhance the effectiveness and reliability of LLM healthcare chains in diverse healthcare scenarios. To further contribute to this area, we will publish our code and datasets.

2 Related Work

2.1 Digital Healthcare

The advent of digital healthcare interventions has revolutionized patient care by leveraging technologies such as wearable devices [13, 14, 15], AI-driven analytics [5, 16], and advanced decision-making frameworks for healthcare resource allocation and optimization [17, 18, 19, 20]. These interventions enable remote monitoring, personalized treatment plans, and predictive analytics, improving patient outcomes and reducing healthcare costs [21, 22]. AI integration, including machine learning and natural language processing for diagnostic accuracy and patient management [23, 1], as well as parameter-efficient fine-tuning approaches [24], are further enhancing healthcare efficiency. Moreover, modeling techniques and representation learning have shown promise in optimizing healthcare systems [3], emphasizing the transformative potential of digital healthcare.

2.2 LLMs in Healthcare

LLMs such as GPT-4 have shown remarkable proficiency in generating human-like text and understanding complex language constructs [25]. These capabilities have been harnessed in various healthcare applications, including automated medical record summarization, virtual health assistants, and clinical decision support systems [26, 27]. Automated medical record summarization condenses patient records into concise summaries, saving time for healthcare providers. Virtual health assistants interact with patients using natural language, providing answers to medical queries and reminders for medication, thereby enhancing patient engagement. Clinical decision support systems leverage the extensive knowledge embedded in LLMs to assist in diagnosing diseases and suggesting treatment options. LLMs streamline clinical workflows and improve patient engagement by providing accurate and timely responses to medical queries, ultimately enhancing the overall quality of care.

2.3 Interactive Questioning with LLM Healthcare Chains

Interactive healthcare conversational systems are crucial in providing patients with accurate and relevant information. Datasets and benchmarks like MedQA [28] and MedMCQA [7] have played a significant role in advancing QA systems within the medical domain, focusing on optimizing the accuracy and relevance of answers generated by LLMs. These systems use large datasets to fine-tune model responses, making them contextually relevant and patient-centered. However, while answer generation has been prioritized, strategies for optimizing question formulation remain largely unexplored.

Several approaches have been introduced to refine the questioning capabilities of LLMs. Techniques such as Chain of Thought (CoT) [29] prompting, reflective chains [30], and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) [8] integrate reasoning processes that help generate more contextually relevant questions. CoT prompting allows LLMs to break down complex queries into intermediate steps, leading to more precise and targeted questioning. Reflective chains enable the model to iteratively improve the questions by reflecting on previous intermediate answers, thus enhancing the relevance of subsequent queries. RAG, on the other hand, combines retrieval and generation processes to ensure that questions are informed by the most relevant information retrieved from a knowledge base. Despite these advancements, most existing work has primarily focused on optimizing task completion and response accuracy, with less attention given to the development of sophisticated questioning strategies that can drive more informative patient interactions. Our work addresses this gap by specifically evaluating the questioning capabilities of LLM healthcare chains, contributing a novel framework that benchmarks their ability to engage in meaningful and effective patient dialogues.

3 Method

3.1 Overview of HealthQ

For benchmarking, we customized several state-of-the-art LLM healthcare chains to simulate a doctor’s role, using training data for search and retrieval without prior knowledge of test patients. Virtual patients were simulated using test data, and the LLM chains’ generated questions were assessed. The evaluation framework includes generating patient statements, scoring questions, and comparing results with traditional NLP metrics. HealthQ uses two primary evaluation metrics: LLM judge interrogation and summarization-based evaluation. LLM judge interrogation involves an additional LLM evaluating question quality based on relevance, specificity, and informativeness. Summarization-based evaluation assesses how well-generated answers capture the complete patient case by comparing them to detailed patient information from the test data.

Figure 1 illustrates our system framework. Original medical notes are split into train and test sets. The train set builds a vectorized knowledge base. The test set, parsed by an LLM parser, produces ground truth pairs. The LLM healthcare chain generates questions using additional tools. A virtual patient simulator provides partial patient statements and answers. The generated questions are evaluated by an LLM judge based on various metrics. Finally, answers are assessed using summary-based metrics like NER2NER and ROUGE.

We also analyze the relationship between question quality and answer comprehensiveness through correlation and mutual information studies, demonstrating how well-formulated questions enhance patient information gathering and diagnostic accuracy.

3.2 Data

HealthQ necessitates a general data processing mechanism due to the unstructured nature of medical notes and the often ambiguous doctor-patient conversations. Patients may provide simple ”yes” or ”no” answers, leaving the context or symptoms embedded in the doctor’s questions. To accurately evaluate the questioning capabilities of LLM healthcare chains, we require a customized data format that aligns with our evaluation framework. This involves clearly separating patient known knowledge—which includes the patient’s feelings, self-reported symptoms, and background history—from the doctor’s question statements. These pairs are essential for our virtual patient mechanism, enabling the assessment of how effectively doctors ask questions to gather useful information for diagnosis. To illustrate how the dataset format changed through our processing, Table 1 shows an example of the original and processed data formats.

3.2.1 Dataset

To evaluate the questioning capabilities of LLM healthcare chains, we utilized two public datasets: ChatDoctor [31] and MTS-Dialog [32]. The ChatDoctor dataset comprises 110,000 anonymized and grammatically corrected medical conversations, covering a wide range of symptoms, diagnoses, and treatment plans. This dataset, originally used to fine-tune a medical chat model based on LLaMA [33], provides a robust foundation for assessing the LLM chains’ ability to generate relevant and context-aware questions. The MTS-Dialog dataset includes 1,700 doctor-patient dialogues and corresponding clinical notes, capturing detailed exchanges across various medical specialties. These clinical notes summarize patient encounters, highlighting key medical facts, diagnoses, and treatment plans. The comprehensive nature of this dataset allows for a thorough assessment of the LLM chains’ questioning capabilities in diverse medical scenarios. By utilizing these two datasets, we ensure a diverse and representative evaluation, covering a broad spectrum of medical conditions and conversational contexts.

3.2.2 Data Processing

As depicted in Figure 1, we split the original medical notes into training and test sets. The training set is transformed and stored in a vector database (VDB), which the LLM Chain can use for searching and retrieval. However, the LLM Chain has no knowledge of any new patients in the test set. The test set is parsed by the LLM to simulate virtual patients, allowing us to evaluate how effectively these LLM healthcare chains, acting as doctors, can ask relevant questions and make judgments based on the simulated patient responses.

3.3 Baseline LLM Healthcare Chains

HealthQ includes several state-of-the-art chains, each with unique mechanisms for generating diagnostic questions. Below are the detailed descriptions of each chain implementation, followed by a combined pseudo-code representation.

Hardcoded Workflow: This is the lightest chain that does not require LLM inference but only uses LLM embedding and NER in a pre-defined workflow. Initially, it takes in a patient’s first statement and retrieves relevant cases from the vector database using a search function. NER is applied to extract symptoms and medications from the retrieved cases. It then confirms each extracted symptom and medication with the patient using predefined templates and updates the state accordingly.

RAG  [8]: This chain integrates retrieval and generation processes. It uses a retrieval mechanism to gather relevant cases from the vector database and then employs a language model to generate questions. The questions are designed to be concise and specific, leveraging the retrieved information to enhance their relevance and utility. We compare two approaches: one using an existing framework, LangChain RetrievalQA666https://docs.smith.langchain.com/old/cookbook/hub-examples/retrieval-qachain, and another customized version tailored specifically for generating questions rather than answers, aligning with the goals of our evaluation framework.

RAG with Reflection [30]: Extending the basic RAG framework, this chain includes a reflection mechanism. After generating an initial question, the chain reflects on the quality and relevance of the question by re-evaluating the context of the retrieved cases. This reflective process allows the chain to refine and improve the question, making it more targeted and contextually appropriate.

RAG with CoT [29]: This chain incorporates a CoT approach, involving iterative reasoning steps. It generates an initial question(or intermediate thinkings) and then performs several iterations of reasoning to refine the question. Each iteration considers additional contextual information from the retrieved cases, progressively enhancing the question’s specificity and relevance.

RAG with Reflection and CoT-Self-Consistency (CoT-SC) [34]: Building on the CoT approach, this chain introduces self-consistency checks. It branches several CoT from the same query, and gather the results of which to see of the different branches reaches similar results. This ensures that the final question is not only refined but also consistent and robust, reducing the likelihood of contradictions. Different from the original CoT-SC which uses a voting approach for the final result, our version uses LLM to compare the several branches of the final results of CoTs to cater for phrasing text output.

ReAct [9]: This chain leverages an LLM prompted with tasks and tools, where the tools are functions described for LLM to call. For example, searching among the training set can be a tool. In each step, the LLM decides whether it can reach an answer or needs to use a tool. If a tool is needed, the LLM specifies the tool name and argument for an executor to call. The executor runs the corresponding functions and feeds the output back into the LLM input for the next step. To highlight the importance of the agent’s ability to choose from more than one tool, we included our VDB search function as one of the tools and added the medical abbreviation lookup tool, medialpy777https://pypi.org/project/medialpy/, as another. We used LangChain’s default implementation to facilitate this process. The ReAct chain’s capability to use heterogeneous sources makes it unique among the chains compared in this study.

3.5 Validation

To ensure the validity of our evaluation, we devised a framework distinct from traditional interrogation or cross-validation methods. Our approach does not directly rely on ground truth from a test set to evaluate the system. Instead, it employs separate LLM workflows for the roles of judge and doctor. The judge LLM, which has access to the test ground truth data, is responsible for evaluating the questions generated by the LLM healthcare chains. In contrast, the LLM healthcare chains, acting as doctor LLM, only have access to the virtual patient initial statement S⁢t⁢m⁢t𝑆𝑡𝑚𝑡Stmtitalic_S italic_t italic_m italic_t. This separation ensures that the question generation process remains unbiased and prevents data leakage.

To maintain the integrity of the evaluation, we strictly prevent data leakage. The LLM healthcare chains never directly access the test patient known knowledge K𝐾Kitalic_K. Thus, the phrasing of the questions is based solely on a subset of K𝐾Kitalic_K and the search results from the chains’ own knowledge base. This design ensures that the evaluation of the generated questions is impartial and based on the information provided in the virtual patient statement.

HealthQ addresses two critical research questions (RQs) to validate its effectiveness:

  • 1.

    RQ1: How well does the induced answer to the question summarize all the relevant information? This question assesses the completeness and quality of the answers generated by the LLM virtual patient in response to their own questions.

  • 2.

    RQ2: How are the results of LLM judge interrogation for the proposed questions correlated with the quality of the induced answers? This question explores the relationship between the quality of the questions and the quality of the answers they elicit, using well-established summarization metrics.

For RQ1, we evaluate the effectiveness of the questions using a virtual patient and established summarization metrics, measuring how well the induced answers encapsulate patient information. For RQ2, we analyze the correlation and mutual information between the quality of the questions and the comprehensiveness of the answers. A strong correlation would suggest that well-formulated questions lead to more informative answers, thereby enhancing patient information gathering and improving healthcare outcomes.

4 Experiments

4.1 Experiment Setup

To ensure the feasibility of our approach for local implementations in clinics and hospitals, all components of the LLM health chains are based on open-source models. On branches of Chain of Thought Self-Consistency (CoT-SC) processes, a temperature of 0.3 is used, while a temperature of 0 is applied for all other models. The NER component utilizes an open-source biomedical NER model [35] to extract medical entities to make set comparisons. We employ FAISS [36] with its default settings as the VDB index package, and the MiniLM101010https://huggingface.co/sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model for text embeddings. Data preprocessing—including the extraction of patient known knowledge (K𝐾Kitalic_K), virtual patient simulation, and question evaluation by the LLM judge—is conducted using Claude-3-Opus111111https://claude.ai/. The experiments are conducted on a dataset comprising 128 test cases (64 from each of the two datasets). We benchmark seven different LLM health chains, generating a total of 796 patient status-doctor statement pairs. For comparative analysis, we group the data by the LLM chain type and summarize the mean scores in Table 4.

This setup rigorously evaluates the questioning capabilities of various LLM healthcare chains, ensuring comprehensive and reliable results.

4.2 Results and Analysis

This section presents the evaluation results of the LLM healthcare chains, with explicit connections to the research questions RQ1 and RQ2, which were established to validate the effectiveness of our proposed evaluation framework.

The summary-based metrics presented in Table 3(b) are particularly relevant to RQ1, which assesses how well the induced answers summarize all relevant information. The results show that state-of-the-art chains like RAG_reflection and RAG_Reflection_CoT_SC consistently outperform more primitive methods, such as the hardcoded chain, across nearly all evaluated metrics. This is especially evident in the ROUGE and NER2NER metrics, where these advanced chains demonstrate superior performance. For instance, the RAG_reflection_CoT_SC chain achieves the highest overall scores in several categories, reflecting its ability to generate contextually relevant and informative questions that lead to more comprehensive and accurate patient responses. The lower performance of the ReAct chain could be attributed to its design in LangChain, emphasizing answering rather than questioning, along with the limited number of tools provided to it. These findings suggest that well-formulated questions by the more advanced chains lead to better information extraction, thereby validating the effectiveness of our evaluation framework in summarizing all relevant information, as addressed in RQ1.

For RQ2, which examines the correlation between the quality of questions generated by LLM chains and the resulting answers, the LLM judge interrogation results in Table 4 show that most LLM healthcare chains substantially outperform the hardcoded workflow across all interrogation metrics. The basic RAG chain shows notable improvements in specificity, usefulness, relevance, and fluency compared to the hardcoded method, highlighting its more flexible and adaptive approach to generating questions. Chains that incorporate reflection and iterative reasoning techniques, such as those using reflection and CoT, exhibit further enhancements, particularly in relevance and coverage. The reflection mechanism enables the chains to reassess and refine the context of the questions, leading to more targeted and contextually appropriate queries. The addition of self-consistency checks further boosts performance, with the RAG_CoT_SC chain showing the highest scores in relevance and fluency. This improvement is attributed to the iterative reasoning and self-consistency processes, which help in formulating more coherent and relevant questions. The reaction-based chain, while showing some improvements over the hardcoded method, still lags behind the more advanced RAG variants, particularly in specificity and coverage. This analysis demonstrates that chains performing well in interrogation metrics tend to produce better quality answers, thus validating the connection explored in RQ2.

5 Discussion

5.1 Sources of Bias and Correction

Our evaluation framework considers potential biases in both the sampling process and the construction of S⁢t⁢m⁢t𝑆𝑡𝑚𝑡Stmtitalic_S italic_t italic_m italic_t and G⁢T𝐺𝑇GTitalic_G italic_T.

5.1.1 Pre-judgement Errors

Randomized train-test splits and sampling from both ChatDoctor and MTS-Dialog datasets can lead to distributional biases. Re-weighting patient types can help mitigate this issue. Errors may arise from the LLM’s data cleaning process due to hallucinations, or from patient case data that does not fully capture essential diagnostic information. We assume the patient data is comprehensive and accurately reflects real-world doctor-patient interactions. It may be possible to reduce this bias through conditioning on patient profiling and matching them with simulation promptings. Bias can also occur in transitioning from K𝐾Kitalic_K to S⁢t⁢m⁢t𝑆𝑡𝑚𝑡Stmtitalic_S italic_t italic_m italic_t, as patient statements may vary. Addressing this as a sample bias problem with fixed conversation starters can help correct this.

5.1.2 Judgement Errors

Interrogation biases stem from LLM prompting and judgement errors, while summarization metrics biases arise from LLM-simulated answers. Mitigating these biases involves diversifying error sources by using LLM capabilities differently: one for scoring directly and another for extracting Q𝑄Qitalic_Q from K𝐾Kitalic_K. Effective evaluation should align with good diagnosis and ultimately improve patient outcomes.

5.2 Multi-shot Conversation with Memory

Real clinical interactions involve multiple QA rounds, which our current study does not fully capture due to text length and computational limitations. Future work with longer context windows and richer patient data can address this. As conversation complexity increases, it may be modeled as a dynamical system to study more intricate behaviors.

6 Conclusion

This paper presents a novel framework for evaluating the questioning capabilities of LLM healthcare chains, addressing a significant gap in the current research landscape. By implementing various state-of-the-art LLM chains and utilizing traditional NLP metrics for validation, we provide a comprehensive assessment of their ability to generate relevant and informative questions during patient interactions. Our approach includes the development of custom datasets and an evaluation framework that separates patient known knowledge from doctor questions, enabling precise evaluation. The results indicate that well-formulated questions enhance patient information gathering, thereby improving diagnostic accuracy and healthcare outcomes. Future work will focus on expanding the framework to support multi-shot conversations and further reducing biases in evaluation metrics. This framework sets a benchmark for future studies and aims to advance the effectiveness of LLM healthcare chains in real-world applications.
