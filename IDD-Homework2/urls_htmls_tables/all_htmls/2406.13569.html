Abstract

Within the machine learning community, reconstruction attacks are a principal attack of concern and have been identified even in federated learning, which was designed with privacy preservation in mind. In federated learning, it has been shown that an adversary with knowledge of the machine learning architecture is able to infer the exact value of a training element given an observation of the weight updates performed during stochastic gradient descent. In response to these threats, the privacy community recommends the use of differential privacy in the stochastic gradient descent algorithm, termed DP-SGD. However, DP has not yet been formally established as an effective countermeasure against reconstruction attacks. In this paper, we formalise the reconstruction threat model using the information-theoretic framework of quantitative information flow. We show that the Bayesâ€™ capacity, related to the Sibson mutual information of order infinity, represents a tight upper bound on the leakage of the DP-SGD algorithm to an adversary interested in performing a reconstruction attack. We provide empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of this measure for comparing mechanisms against reconstruction threats.

Bayes capacity, federated learning, reconstruction attacks, DP-SGD, information flow

I Introduction

Reconstruction attacks - in which an attacker can reconstruct the training data used to build a machine learning model - are a principal attack of concern in machine learning, since training data are often considered to be sensitive. These attacks are of particular concern in Federated Learning (FL), a distributed form of machine learning in which weaker machine learners collaborate to train a more powerful model without having to share their training data.

Numerous worksÂ [1, 2, 3] have shown that FL is susceptible to gradient-based reconstruction attacks. These describe an attacker who can observe the gradients produced in the stochastic gradient descent algorithm, and by incorporating their knowledge of the architecture, can reconstruct the sensitive input data. Significant works in this regard are deep leakage from gradients by Zhu et al.Â [1], see through gradients by Yin et al.Â [2] and inverting gradients by Geiping et al.Â [3]. Such attacks are feasible in distributed learning architectures such as FL because the gradients computed by each client are shared with a central server who, in the threat model we consider, is an honest-but-curious adversary.

We observe that theoretical work in this areaÂ [4, 5] differs from practical work in understanding reconstruction, particularly when the sensitive input data are images. Theoretically, a reconstruction attack occurs when an input data value is inferred exactly; practically, a reconstruction attack occurs when a sensitive image can be reconstructed approximately. This has implications for the information-theoretic measures used to describe leakage, which we discuss later in the paper.

A second important caveat is that the reconstruction attacks described in the literature assume that no privacy protections have been put in place. In this paper, we study reconstruction attacks with differential privacy applied to the gradients as in Alg. 1, also known as DP-SGD.

While it is standard practice in the DP literature to compare mechanisms by â€œcomparing their epsilonsâ€, we argue that a formal information-theoretic analysis of reconstruction attacks reveals that the Bayesâ€™ capacity better aligns with the attackerâ€™s strategy. The Bayesâ€™ capacity is known in the quantitative information flow literature as a robust upper bound on the leakage of a system against any Bayesian attackerÂ [6]; its logarithm is known in the information-theory literature as the Sibson mutual information of order Î±=âˆğ›¼\alpha=\inftyÂ [7]. We make the following contributions:

  1. 1.

    We formalise reconstruction attacks and show that Bayesâ€™ capacity acts as a measure of attack success.

  2. 2.

    We introduce the continuous version of the Bayesâ€™ capacity and derive a formulation for the Bayesâ€™ capacity of the DP-SGD algorithm.

  3. 3.

    We propose an alternative mechanism for DP-SGD based on the Von Mises-Fisher distribution and compute its Bayesâ€™ capacity in the DP-SGD algorithm.

  4. 4.

    We experimentally compare the Bayesâ€™ capacity and the Ïµitalic-Ïµ\epsilon values for the above mechanisms against proxies for reconstruction attacks, showing that the attacks correlate well with Bayesâ€™ capacity compared with Ïµitalic-Ïµ\epsilon.

V Discussion and Conclusion

We have shown that Bayesâ€™ capacity measures the leakage of an FL system incorporating DP-SGD to a Bayesian attacker whose goal is to perform a perfect reconstruction attack. We have provided a continuous version of Bayesâ€™ capacity and derived its formulation for 2 â€œincomparableâ€ differential privacy mechanisms. Experimentally we have demonstrated that the Bayesâ€™ capacity provides a better measure for reconstruction attack success than does the Ïµitalic-Ïµ\epsilon of differential privacy, even when â€œapproximateâ€ measures for a successful attack are used.
