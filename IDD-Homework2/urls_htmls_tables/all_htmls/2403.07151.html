Abstract

Federated Learning (FL) is a collaborative machine learning (ML) approach, where multiple clients participate in training an ML model without exposing the private data. Fair and accurate assessment of client contributions is an important problem in FL to facilitate incentive allocation and encouraging diverse clients to participate in a unified model training. Existing methods for assessing client contribution adopts co-operative game-theoretic concepts, such as Shapley values, but under simplified assumptions. In this paper, we propose a history-aware game-theoretic framework, called 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib}, to assess client contributions when a subset of (potentially non-i.i.d.) clients participate in each epoch of FL training. By exploiting the FL training process and linearity of Shapley value, we develop 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib} that yields a historical timeline of client contributions as FL training progresses over epochs. Additionally, to assess client contribution under limited computational budget, we propose a scheduling procedure that considers a two-sided fairness criteria to perform expensive Shapley value computation only in a subset of training epochs. In experiments, we demonstrate a controlled trade-off between the correctness and efficiency of client contributions assessed via 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib}. To demonstrate the benefits of history-aware client contributions, we apply 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib} to detect dishonest clients conducting data poisoning in FL training.

I Introduction

Over the last decade, Federated Learning (FL) [1, 2, 3] has emerged as the de facto standard for collaborative Machine Learning (ML) without exposing the private data. FL facilitates the involvement of multiple data owners or local clients in training a global ML model without the need to share any raw data [4, 5, 6], and thus, aligning with the standard data protection policies like GDPR [7]. As the success of FL depends on the participation of a diverse pool of data contributors (aka clients), a fair and accurate assessment of the client contributions is important to facilitate incentive allocation [8, 9], resolve free rider issues [10, 11], and encourage diverse clients to participate in an effective model training through FL [12, 13]. Thus, assessing client contributions in FL has become a question of growing interest [14, 15, 16].

Client Contribution in FL. The existing client contribution assessment techniques in FL can be categorized in two ways: individual and coalition assessment. In individual assessment, a local client is evaluated based on its similarity with the global model, the local relative accuracy, or the reputation of the client [12, 17]. In coalition assessment, the marginal effect of a local client is accounted for when it joins a set of other clients in training process [18, 14, 19]. Coalition assessment is known to be more effective than individual assessment in the heterogeneous data setting, where a client only has a partial coverage of the feature space but the global model needs to cover the whole feature space [16]. But a coalition-based assessment can depend on the order in which the clients join in the training and are being evaluated.

Shapley Values as Client Contributions. To address these issues of coalition assessment, tools from cooperative game theory, such as Shapley values [20], are proposed for assessing client contributions [18, 14, 21, 16, 19]. Computing an exact Shapley value has an exponential runtime complexity due to the exhaustive enumeration of all permutations of clients. Several approximations are proposed based on Monte Carlo sampling [22] and its variants [23, 21, 14], and by evaluating complementary contribution [24]. While applying Shapley value in FL for client contribution, majority of the methods consider simplified assumptions or rely on heuristic definitions of client contribution. For example, [19, 25, 15, 16] considered all clients participate in each epoch. [25] heuristically focused on the Shapley value in recent epochs to quantify the contribution index of clients. [26] computed client contributions of non-participating clients with restricted assumptions, such as Lipschitz continuous model loss and all clients participating in at least one epoch. Finally, [16] heuristically defined client contribution of non-participating clients without providing any theoretical justification. Therefore, we study the following research question:

Can we develop a framework for assessing client contributions in a centralized server federated learning, where a subset of clients participate in each training epoch?

As a response, we propose 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib}, a Shapley value based framework to assess the client contributions of both participating and non-participating clients in FL with respect to an utility function evaluated on a validation dataset. Specifically, as shown in Figure 1, FL yields a Markovian training process where in each training epoch, a central server sends the previous global model to participating clients. The clients update the local models with local data and send the local models back to the server. The server performs a federated aggregation of local models to derive a global model. To assess client contribution via Shapley value, the server computes the marginal impact of including a client to a coalition of clients using a utility function. A utility function corresponds to either loss or accuracy of the coalition model on a validation dataset. As a history-aware assessment of client contributions, 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib} aggregates Shapley values in all the past epochs.

II Related Work

Researchers have proposed several approaches to apply Shapley value to assess client contributions in FL. Notably, [25] utilized the gradients of local clients and proposed an one epoch evaluation (OR) and a multi-epoch evaluation (MR) for client contribution. [15] extended MR and proposed a truncated multi-epoch (TMR) evaluation to eliminate an entire epoch based on a pre-defined threshold. [16] improved Shapley value computation via random permutation sampling and group testing. [19] further improved computation by eliminating unnecessary epochs based on the incremental utility and proposed an improved permutation ordering for an effective client contribution assessment. In all methods, all clients participate in each training epoch – we aim relax the assumption in this paper.

To our best knowledge, two methods considered the non-participating clients setting like ours: [16] and [26]. For non-participating clients,  [16] heuristically defined a value for contribution without providing any theoretical justification – which we address in our work. In contrast,  [26] aimed to guess missing utility values of client combinations where a subset of clients do not participate in training and proposed a low rank matrix factorization to assess contributions. The said method has several limitations: low rank matrix factorization requires a suitable characteristic of the utility function and a restricted assumption of client participation. Furthermore, matrix factorization is a one-shot approach – assessing contribution in a new epoch requires re-computation of the whole matrix, which is infeasible in practice. As such,  [26] not only requires high computational time on increasing epochs but also incurs high estimation error. In our study, we aim to design a client contribution assessment framework that achieves not only efficiency in computation but also accuracy in estimation while considering the practical scenario of dynamic participation and non-participation of clients over epochs.

IV-C A Schedule for a Faster Contribution Assessment

We discuss a scheduling procedure to assess client contribution efficiently. The key idea is to consider a computational budget, such as the maximum number of epochs to compute Shapley value, and derive an optimal subset of epochs based on the solution of a constrained optimization problem.

In formalizing the optimization problem, we focus on the following aspects. (i) Server-sided fairness. The server optimizes the utility coverage by computing Shapley value in an optimal subset of epochs where the sum of incremental utilities is higher. In addition, the server prioritizes epochs where a higher number of clients participate in training. (ii) Client-sided fairness. Since a client does not necessarily participate in all epochs, the clients aim to minimize the absolute pair-wise difference of exposure probabilities. A client is said to be exposed in an epoch if it participates in training in that epoch and Shapley value is computed in the same epoch. Now, we discuss two mixed integer linear programs (MILP) to achieve (i) only server-sided fairness and (ii) two-sided fairness [31], where both server-sided and client-sided fairnesses are considered.

VI Applications of 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib}

We demonstrate the application of 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib} in analyzing client intention based on client contributions over epochs. Contrary to earlier methods, we aim to leverage the historic client contributions rather than a single contribution value after training [26, 16, 19, 25]. We consider a controlled experimental setup: each client possesses a uniform data distribution except a subset of dishonest clients who intentionally poison their data within a window of epochs. For simplicity, a dishonest client chooses to flip the data label with a certain flipping probability inside the window.

Our objectives are two-fold. (i) Can we detect the window in which a dishonest client poisons its data? (ii) Can we separate honest clients from dishonest ones? In both cases, we rely on the Shapley values of clients computed over epochs and answer affirmatively to the two questions.

Detecting Poisonous Window. In Figure 4a, 111 out of 444 clients is dishonest – the cumulative Shapley value on model loss of the dishonest client diverges from honest clients within the poisonous window (gray area). In Figure 4b, according to a Bayesian inference based change point detection algorithm [35], the probability of change point of Shapley values has higher mass within the window – the average probability mass is 0.950.950.95. In addition, increasing the flipping probability from 0.50.50.5 in Figure 4a to 0.70.70.7 in Figure 4b, the probability mass increases from 0.950.950.95 to 0.970.970.97. Finally, increasing both total clients to 888 and dishonest clients to 222 in Figure 4e decreases the average probability mass to 0.820.820.82, which is still evident of poisonous intention. Therefore, Shapley values have the potential of being an effective witness of client intention in FL.

Separating Honest Clients. We apply a K-means time-series clustering algorithm [36] on the cumulative Shapley values over epochs of all clients and aim to separate honest clients from dishonest ones. We hypothesize that majority of the honest clients may be in one cluster due to their uniform data distribution. In particular, we measure the Jaccard similarity index [37] between the set of honest clients and the set of all clients that are in a cluster containing at least one honest client. In Figure 5 (left), as we increase the flipping probability, the similarity index increases and eventually reaches to 111. The similarity index is higher for a higher number of clients as we keep the number of dishonest clients fixed to 111 in Figure 5 (left). In addition, as we increase the number dishonest clients in Figure 5 (right), the similarity index decreases. Therefore, Shapley value based client contribution assessment demonstrates the potential in separating out honest clients.

VII Conclusion

We study the assessment of client contribution in centralized single-server federated learning using Shapley values. We propose 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib} to exactly assess client contribution when a subset of clients participate in each training epoch. We discuss a scheduling procedure satisfying two-sided fairness between server and clients for a faster assessment of contribution. Empirically, 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib} demonstrates a well balance between efficiency of computation vs. estimation accuracy. In addition, we apply historic client contributions over epochs to analyze dishonest clients. In future, we extend 𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻𝖥𝖫𝖢𝗈𝗇𝗍𝗋𝗂𝖻\mathsf{FLContrib} to assess client contribution in a discentralized federated setting. Our framework opens up further research on client selection and federated aggregation based on historic client contributions.

VIII-C Interpretation of Shapley Value in FL

  • •

    According to Shapley value, a non-selected client is considered a null client in all training epochs except the first epoch.

  • •

    All non-selected clients are symmetric in all training epochs.

  • •

    In a specific training epoch, a selected client is a null client when its Shapley value is zero. This occurs when the gradient of the client is zero.

  • •

    In a specific training epoch, two selected clients are symmetric in Shapley value if their gradients are identical.
