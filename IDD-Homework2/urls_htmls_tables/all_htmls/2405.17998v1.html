Abstract.

Recently, researchers have uncovered that neural retrieval models prefer AI-generated content (AIGC), called source biasÂ (Dai etÂ al., 2024c; Xu etÂ al., 2024a). Compared to active search behavior, recommendation represents another important means of information acquisition, where users are more prone to source bias. Furthermore, delving into the recommendation scenario, as AIGC becomes integrated within the feedback loop involving users, data, and the recommender system, it progressively contaminates the candidate items, the user interaction history, and ultimately, the data used to train the recommendation models. How and to what extent the source bias affects the neural recommendation models within feedback loop remains unknown. In this study, we extend the investigation of source bias into the realm of recommender systems, specifically examining its impact across different phases of the feedback loop. We conceptualize the progression of AIGC integration into the recommendation content ecosystem in three distinct phases-HGC dominate, HGC-AIGC coexist, and AIGC dominance-each representing past, present, and future states, respectively. Through extensive experiments across three datasets from diverse domains, we demonstrate the prevalence of source bias and reveal a potential digital echo chamber with source bias amplification throughout the feedback loop. This trend risks creating a recommender ecosystem with limited information source, such as AIGC, being disproportionately recommended. To counteract this bias and prevent its escalation in the feedback loop, we introduce a black-box debiasing method that maintains model impartiality towards both HGC and AIGC. Our experimental results validate the effectiveness of the proposed debiasing method, confirming its potential to disrupt the feedback loop.

1. Introduction

The development of Large Language Models (LLMs) is advancing rapidlyÂ (Zhao etÂ al., 2023b), demonstrating strong capabilities and performing excellently in many text generation tasks, such as machine translationÂ (Lai etÂ al., 2023), summarizationÂ (Zhang and Liu, 2023), and complex question answeringÂ (Tan etÂ al., 2023; Xu etÂ al., 2024b). Due to the cost-effectiveness, high quality, and speed of generating Artificial Intelligence Generated Content (AIGC) compared to Human Generated Content (HGC), an increasing volume of online content is being produced by these models. This shift is altering the existing content creation paradigm and resulting in a notable prevalence of AIGC on the internetÂ (Dai etÂ al., 2024b). In retrieval systems, these abundant AIGCs are more likely to be ranked higher in the candidate items set by the neural retrievals, a phenomenon known as source biasÂ (Dai etÂ al., 2024c; Xu etÂ al., 2024a; Dai etÂ al., 2024a), which significantly impacts the current content ecosystem by reducing human creativity.

Alongside search engines, which serve as platforms for people to actively seek information, recommender systems also play a significant role as another important information system for information acquisition by users. They impact the daily life of humans through functions like newsÂ (Liu etÂ al., 2010), productÂ (Xiao and Benbasat, 2007), and videoÂ (Davidson etÂ al., 2010) recommendations. Therefore, an important research question emerges: RQ1: Whether source bias exist in recommender systems, as it has been identified in retrieval systems? On the other hand, unlike the fixed query-document pairs in retrieval systems, in recommender systems, feedback data from the user interaction is reused to update the recommender system, forming a feedback loop involving users, data, and the recommender system as described in FigureÂ 1. With the continuous increase in AIGC, they will gradually influence various stages of the feedback loop, raising another research question: RQ2: In the LLMs era, whether the feedback loop process amplifies the phenomenon of source bias?

To explore the aforementioned research questions, we first discuss the influx of AIGC into the content ecosystem of recommender systems across three phases: HGC dominate phase, HGC-AIGC coexist phase, and AIGC dominate phase as shown in FigureÂ 2. These phases respectively correspond to the past, present, and future, representing the influence of different stages of AIGC flooding on recommender systems. Initially, in the HGC dominate phase, the influx of AIGC begins to pollutes the candidate item set and influences the Top-Kğ¾Kitalic_K serving within the feedback loop, potentially introducing source bias. Subsequently, during the HGC-AIGC coexist phase, AIGC further taints usersâ€™ historical sequences via their interactions, which could amplify the existing source bias. Ultimately, in the AIGC dominate phase, the data tainted by AIGC predominates and is used to train the recommendation models, likely exacerbating source bias to significant levels.

To answer RQ1, we first construct evaluation datasets from three different domains by instructing LLMs to rewrite the provided text and obtain the AIGC copy. Then we evaluate several popular recommendation models using mixed candidate sets of HGC and AIGC items during HGC dominate phase. Our findings reveal that these models tend to rank AIGC at a higher position, uncovering that source bias is also prevalent in recommender systems.

For RQ2, we further conduct experiments by incorporating AIGC into usersâ€™ interaction history during the HGC-AIGC coexist phase and into the training data of recommendation models during the AIGC dominate phase in a feedback loop scenario. The results demonstrate that both usersâ€™ interaction behavior and the modelâ€™s updating process on polluted data will amplify the source bias. Finally, we can conclude that the source bias will continue to grow as the feedback loop progresses.

Furthermore, in the feedback loop scenario, our experimental results employing previously proposed debiasing methodsÂ (Dai etÂ al., 2024c; Xu etÂ al., 2024a; Dai etÂ al., 2024a) have shown that source bias not only persists but is also amplified through the loop. Therefore, it is necessary to disrupt the propagation of source bias in the feedback loop mechanism, which raises a new research question: RQ3: How can bias be maintained within a stable and controllable range in the feedback loop? In response to RQ3, we introduce a novel debiasing method aimed at preventing the escalation the amplification of source bias during the influx of AIGC in the feedback loop. Specifically, our method first instructs LLMs to uniformly rewrite all training data, without the need to distinguish between HGC and AIGC. We then apply an L1 loss to constrain the outputs of the item encoder and the history encoder, ensuring that both HGC and AIGC, when sharing similar semantics, are mapped to corresponding embedding representations. Experimental results demonstrate that our method can effectively stabilize bias within acceptable limits and maintain the neutrality of model prediction across varying AIGC proportions.

HGC Dominate Phase: With the widespread use of LLMs and the popularization of AIGC on the internet, it is easy for HGC to have corresponding AIGC copies or even be directly generated by LLMs. Thus, the items selected for the recommendation modelâ€™s Top-Kğ¾Kitalic_K ranking are a combination of HGC and AIGC. In the HGC-AIGC coexist phase, the research question is whether source bias will be amplified when AIGC interacted with users is added to usersâ€™ interaction sequence.

AIGC Dominate Phase: In the future, with the decreasing cost and increasing accessibility of LLMs, AIGC will dominate the ecosystem of recommender systems. 5. Debias During the Feedback Loop

In this section, we propose a debiasing method to eliminate source bias during the feedback loop and conduct several experiments to validate its effectiveness.

5.1. Motivation

In previous sections, we validate the presence of source bias in recommender systems. Furthermore, with the proliferation of AIGC on the internet, source bias amplifies throughout the feedback loop, thus accelerating the expansion of AIGC. Therefore, it is crucial to propose an effective debiasing method and maintain its effectiveness in dynamically changing feedback loop scenarios. In previous work, some effective methodsÂ (Dai etÂ al., 2024c; Xu etÂ al., 2024a) to mitigate source bias have been suggested in document ranking and text-image retrieval tasks. However, these methods can not be directly applied to the dynamic feedback loop scenarios of recommender systems. First, these methods focus on debiasing document representations while overlooking context representations that are intertwined with AIGC and expand with the progression of the feedback loop. This expansion contributes to the amplification of source bias. Second, previous methods are white-box and therefore require knowledge of whether the text comes from HGC or AIGC, which is challenging to achieve in the context of a feedback loop, even with text detection techniques. Third, these methods utilize hinge loss, which may cause the model to gradually favor HGC during the feedback loop process, leading to the development of new biases. Moreover, we employ the debiasing methods from previous studies to train the model in a feedback loop, and the results in TableÂ 4 demonstrate the necessity of new methods in dynamic scenarios.

Considering the aforementioned problems, we propose our debiasing methods from three perspectives. First, we propose a debiasing constraint on both item representation and history representation. Second, a black-box strategy is used to enable the method to focus directly on the differences between the rewritten text before and after, without requiring knowledge of the source of the training text. Third, the hinge loss is replaced with the L1 loss to ensure the model does not favor either HGC or AIGC.

6. Conclusion

In this paper, we delve into exploring the source bias in recommender systems. To mitigate source bias and prevent its further amplification in the feedback loop, we propose a black-box debiasing solution that ensures the impartiality of the model prediction towards both HGC and AIGC in the feedback loop.
