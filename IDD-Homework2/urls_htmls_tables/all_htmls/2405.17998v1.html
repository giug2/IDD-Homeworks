Abstract.

Recently, researchers have uncovered that neural retrieval models prefer AI-generated content (AIGC), called source bias (Dai et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024a). Compared to active search behavior, recommendation represents another important means of information acquisition, where users are more prone to source bias. Furthermore, delving into the recommendation scenario, as AIGC becomes integrated within the feedback loop involving users, data, and the recommender system, it progressively contaminates the candidate items, the user interaction history, and ultimately, the data used to train the recommendation models. How and to what extent the source bias affects the neural recommendation models within feedback loop remains unknown. In this study, we extend the investigation of source bias into the realm of recommender systems, specifically examining its impact across different phases of the feedback loop. We conceptualize the progression of AIGC integration into the recommendation content ecosystem in three distinct phases-HGC dominate, HGC-AIGC coexist, and AIGC dominance-each representing past, present, and future states, respectively. Through extensive experiments across three datasets from diverse domains, we demonstrate the prevalence of source bias and reveal a potential digital echo chamber with source bias amplification throughout the feedback loop. This trend risks creating a recommender ecosystem with limited information source, such as AIGC, being disproportionately recommended. To counteract this bias and prevent its escalation in the feedback loop, we introduce a black-box debiasing method that maintains model impartiality towards both HGC and AIGC. Our experimental results validate the effectiveness of the proposed debiasing method, confirming its potential to disrupt the feedback loop.

1. Introduction

The development of Large Language Models (LLMs) is advancing rapidly (Zhao et al., 2023b), demonstrating strong capabilities and performing excellently in many text generation tasks, such as machine translation (Lai et al., 2023), summarization (Zhang and Liu, 2023), and complex question answering (Tan et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024b). Due to the cost-effectiveness, high quality, and speed of generating Artificial Intelligence Generated Content (AIGC) compared to Human Generated Content (HGC), an increasing volume of online content is being produced by these models. This shift is altering the existing content creation paradigm and resulting in a notable prevalence of AIGC on the internet (Dai et al., 2024b). In retrieval systems, these abundant AIGCs are more likely to be ranked higher in the candidate items set by the neural retrievals, a phenomenon known as source bias (Dai et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2024a), which significantly impacts the current content ecosystem by reducing human creativity.

Alongside search engines, which serve as platforms for people to actively seek information, recommender systems also play a significant role as another important information system for information acquisition by users. They impact the daily life of humans through functions like news (Liu et al., 2010), product (Xiao and Benbasat, 2007), and video (Davidson et al., 2010) recommendations. Therefore, an important research question emerges: RQ1: Whether source bias exist in recommender systems, as it has been identified in retrieval systems? On the other hand, unlike the fixed query-document pairs in retrieval systems, in recommender systems, feedback data from the user interaction is reused to update the recommender system, forming a feedback loop involving users, data, and the recommender system as described in Figure 1. With the continuous increase in AIGC, they will gradually influence various stages of the feedback loop, raising another research question: RQ2: In the LLMs era, whether the feedback loop process amplifies the phenomenon of source bias?

To explore the aforementioned research questions, we first discuss the influx of AIGC into the content ecosystem of recommender systems across three phases: HGC dominate phase, HGC-AIGC coexist phase, and AIGC dominate phase as shown in Figure 2. These phases respectively correspond to the past, present, and future, representing the influence of different stages of AIGC flooding on recommender systems. Initially, in the HGC dominate phase, the influx of AIGC begins to pollutes the candidate item set and influences the Top-K𝐾Kitalic_K serving within the feedback loop, potentially introducing source bias. Subsequently, during the HGC-AIGC coexist phase, AIGC further taints users’ historical sequences via their interactions, which could amplify the existing source bias. Ultimately, in the AIGC dominate phase, the data tainted by AIGC predominates and is used to train the recommendation models, likely exacerbating source bias to significant levels.

To answer RQ1, we first construct evaluation datasets from three different domains by instructing LLMs to rewrite the provided text and obtain the AIGC copy. Then we evaluate several popular recommendation models using mixed candidate sets of HGC and AIGC items during HGC dominate phase. Our findings reveal that these models tend to rank AIGC at a higher position, uncovering that source bias is also prevalent in recommender systems.

For RQ2, we further conduct experiments by incorporating AIGC into users’ interaction history during the HGC-AIGC coexist phase and into the training data of recommendation models during the AIGC dominate phase in a feedback loop scenario. The results demonstrate that both users’ interaction behavior and the model’s updating process on polluted data will amplify the source bias. Finally, we can conclude that the source bias will continue to grow as the feedback loop progresses.

Furthermore, in the feedback loop scenario, our experimental results employing previously proposed debiasing methods (Dai et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2024a) have shown that source bias not only persists but is also amplified through the loop. Therefore, it is necessary to disrupt the propagation of source bias in the feedback loop mechanism, which raises a new research question: RQ3: How can bias be maintained within a stable and controllable range in the feedback loop? In response to RQ3, we introduce a novel debiasing method aimed at preventing the escalation the amplification of source bias during the influx of AIGC in the feedback loop. Specifically, our method first instructs LLMs to uniformly rewrite all training data, without the need to distinguish between HGC and AIGC. We then apply an L1 loss to constrain the outputs of the item encoder and the history encoder, ensuring that both HGC and AIGC, when sharing similar semantics, are mapped to corresponding embedding representations. Experimental results demonstrate that our method can effectively stabilize bias within acceptable limits and maintain the neutrality of model prediction across varying AIGC proportions.

The major contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

(1111) We first validate the phenomenon of source bias in the recommender system, which is another important information system besides search engines.

(2222) We uncover that source bias amplifies as the user feedback progresses and the model’s training in a feedback loop scenario, further emphasizing the urgency of addressing source bias.

(3333) We propose a debiasing method that can effectively alleviate source bias during the feedback loop without the need to know the data source, thereby balancing the content ecosystem.

2. Related Work

Large Language Models for Recommender Systems. Recent advancements in LLMs have attracted considerable interest among researchers to leverage these models (Wu et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) to develop an enhanced recommender system. Some works utilize LLMs to generate knowledge-rich texts or use LLM-derived embeddings to enhance recommender systems, known as LLM-enhanced recommender systems (Xi et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024). Another line of work leverages LLMs that act as the ranking model to approach recommendation tasks, known as LLM-as-recommenders (Hou et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2023). In addition to exploring how recommender systems can benefit from LLMs, we also need to consider the potential challenges that the development of LLMs may pose to recommender systems (Dai et al., 2024b). This paper primarily investigates the impact of AIGC on recommender systems, specifically focusing on the changes and influences of source bias in the feedback loop of recommender systems. luences of source bias in the feedback loop of recommender systems.

Effects of Artificial Intelligence Generated Content. The development of LLMs has propelled the prosperity of AIGC on the internet and bring about comprehensive impacts on society and technology (Cao et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2023a). Such vast amounts of AIGC can lead to a series of issues, such as the potential presence of misinformation (Chen and Shu, 2023; Xu et al., 2023) or the generation of damaging content (Huang et al., 2023). In addition to the impact on the content ecosystem, some recent studies have found that using AIGC to train AI models could lead to the collapse of next-generation models (Shumailov et al., 2023; Alemohammad et al., 2023; Briesch et al., 2023). These issues in AIGC have attracted widespread attention and research efforts. Some recent studies indicate that neural retrieval models favor AIGC and will rank them higher in text and image retrieval systems. This phenomenon is referred to as source bias (Dai et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2024a). Our study examines the influence of source bias in another crucial information system, where AIGC influences model recommendations, users’ interaction, and model training in a typical feedback loop.

3.2. Three Phases of Recommendation Content

After AIGC integrates into the content ecosystem of recommender systems, it will gradually impact the three processes of the feedback loop over time: Top-K𝐾Kitalic_K serving, users’ interaction, and model training. We divide the impact of AIGC on recommender systems by feedback loop over time into three phases: HGC Dominate, HGC-AIGC Coexist, and AIGC Dominate. Each phase corresponds to a real-world scenario representing the past, present, and future.

HGC Dominate Phase: With the widespread use of LLMs and the popularization of AIGC on the internet, it is easy for HGC to have corresponding AIGC copies or even be directly generated by LLMs. Thus, the items selected for the recommendation model’s Top-K𝐾Kitalic_K ranking are a combination of HGC and AIGC. In the HGC dominate phase, the research question aims to validate whether the recommendation models will rank AIGC at a higher position, a phenomenon known as source bias (Dai et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2024a).

HGC-AIGC Coexist Phase: With the increasing proliferation of LLMs and AIGC on the Internet, the presence of AIGC in users’ recommendation candidate lists will rapidly grow. These contents will be interacted with users and added to their interaction sequences, which will be used as input for recommendation models later. In the HGC-AIGC coexist phase, the research question is whether source bias will be amplified when AIGC interacted with users is added to users’ interaction sequence.

AIGC Dominate Phase: In the future, with the decreasing cost and increasing accessibility of LLMs, AIGC will dominate the ecosystem of recommender systems. Furthermore, AIGC will influence any stage of the feedback loop, namely Top-K𝐾Kitalic_K serving, users’ interaction, and model training in Figure 1. In other words, AIGC will pollute candidate list ℐℐ\mathcal{I}caligraphic_I, users’ interaction history sequence s𝑠sitalic_s, and the model’s training data 𝒮𝒮\mathcal{S}caligraphic_S. Furthermore, within the iterative feedback loop, recommendation models undergo training on data 𝒮𝒮\mathcal{S}caligraphic_S that has been contaminated by AIGC. In the AIGC dominate phase, the research question is whether source bias will be amplified when recommendation models are trained on data polluted by AIGC.

In conclusion, the integration of AIGC into the recommender system will impact various aspects, such as the candidate item set, users’ interactions, and data used for model training in the feedback loop. Based on the affected aspects, the evolution of the recommender system will exhibit three phases: HGC Dominate, HGC-AIGC Coexist, and AIGC Dominate. We will explore the changes in source bias across these three phases to answer RQ1 and RQ2.

4.1. Experimental Settings

4.1.1. Datasets

Our training and evaluation are conducted on a series of real-world datasets (Amazon (McAuley et al., 2015)), comprising large corpora of product reviews and descriptions obtained from Amazon.com. Top-level product categories are treated as separate datasets, and we focus on three categories: “Health”, “Beauty”, and “Sports”. We use the textual descriptions of products that users have commented on as input to predict which product the user might review next. Due to the low quality of short text rewriting111LLM frequently expands short texts during the rewriting process., we exclude items with descriptions containing fewer than 20 words from the training set to maintain training stability.

We sort the data based on the review time of the target item and split it into training and testing sets in a 7:3 ratio. In the training dataset, we exclude users and items with fewer than five interactions and randomly select 4 negative items from the entire set for each product reviewed by users.

4.1.2. Recommendation Models

For our experiments, we select four representative models: BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019), SASRec (Kang and McAuley, 2018), GRU4Rec (Hidasi et al., 2015), and LRURec (Yue et al., 2024). BERT4Rec: It is a sequential recommendation model based on a bidirectional transformer encoder architecture, which is learned via predicting a masked item. SASRec: It is the first unidirectional transformer-based sequential recommender that leverages unidirectional self-attention to capture user-item transition patterns. GRU4Rec: It is a GRU-based encoder architecture for sequential recommendation that models user-item interactions. LRURec: It is a novel sequential recommendation model that efficiently captures user transition patterns through linear recurrence with matrix diagonalization. These models are enhanced by various pre-trained language models, including BERT (Kenton and Toutanova, 2019) and RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019). We use the bert-base-uncased checkpoint for BERT and the roberta-base checkpoint for RoBERTa.

5. Debias During the Feedback Loop

In this section, we propose a debiasing method to eliminate source bias during the feedback loop and conduct several experiments to validate its effectiveness.

5.1. Motivation

In previous sections, we validate the presence of source bias in recommender systems. Furthermore, with the proliferation of AIGC on the internet, source bias amplifies throughout the feedback loop, thus accelerating the expansion of AIGC. Therefore, it is crucial to propose an effective debiasing method and maintain its effectiveness in dynamically changing feedback loop scenarios. In previous work, some effective methods (Dai et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024a) to mitigate source bias have been suggested in document ranking and text-image retrieval tasks. However, these methods can not be directly applied to the dynamic feedback loop scenarios of recommender systems. First, these methods focus on debiasing document representations while overlooking context representations that are intertwined with AIGC and expand with the progression of the feedback loop. This expansion contributes to the amplification of source bias. Second, previous methods are white-box and therefore require knowledge of whether the text comes from HGC or AIGC, which is challenging to achieve in the context of a feedback loop, even with text detection techniques. Third, these methods utilize hinge loss, which may cause the model to gradually favor HGC during the feedback loop process, leading to the development of new biases. Moreover, we employ the debiasing methods from previous studies to train the model in a feedback loop, and the results in Table 4 demonstrate the necessity of new methods in dynamic scenarios.

Considering the aforementioned problems, we propose our debiasing methods from three perspectives. First, we propose a debiasing constraint on both item representation and history representation. Second, a black-box strategy is used to enable the method to focus directly on the differences between the rewritten text before and after, without requiring knowledge of the source of the training text. Third, the hinge loss is replaced with the L1 loss to ensure the model does not favor either HGC or AIGC.

6. Conclusion

In this paper, we delve into exploring the source bias in recommender systems. Through extensive experiments with several representative recommendation models across three datasets from different domains, we uncover the prevalence of source bias in recommender systems. Furthermore, we validate that source bias is gradually amplified in the feedback loop, where AIGC will be incorporated into users’ interaction histories and the training data as time progresses. To mitigate source bias and prevent its further amplification in the feedback loop, we propose a black-box debiasing solution that ensures the impartiality of the model prediction towards both HGC and AIGC in the feedback loop.
