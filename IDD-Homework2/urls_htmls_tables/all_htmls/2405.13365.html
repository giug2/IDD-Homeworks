Abstract

This paper introduces an approach to employ clipped uniform quantization in federated learning settings, aiming to enhance model efficiency by reducing communication overhead without compromising accuracy. By employing optimal clipping thresholds and adaptive quantization schemes, our method significantly curtails the bit requirements for model weight transmissions between clients and the server. We explore the implications of symmetric clipping and uniform quantization on model performance, highlighting the utility of stochastic quantization to mitigate quantization artifacts and improve model robustness. Through extensive simulations on the MNIST dataset, our results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves near full-precision performance while ensuring substantial communication savings. Specifically, our approach facilitates efficient weight averaging based on quantization errors, effectively balancing the trade-off between communication efficiency and model accuracy. The comparative analysis with conventional quantization methods further confirms the superiority of our technique.

I Introduction

There has been extensive research on federated learning (FL) in which multiple decentralized devices or servers collaborate to develop a shared predictive model without transferring the training data. Different compression methods, including quantization, play a significant role in distributed learning by reducing the communication overhead, which is crucial for efficiency in federated learning environments [1]. This process involves converting models from floating point to lower precision formats, which not only decreases memory usage but also facilitates faster computation and less energy consumption on client devices.

Clipping is widely used in quantization where the values greater than a clipping threshold are treated at the threshold. Although it may not have the least quantization errors, clipped quantizes are often easier to implement. Clipped uniform quantization are also suitable for tasks where integer operations are preferred. Because of drawbacks of other methods like Kullback-Leibler Divergence measure (KLD), we adopted an extension of optimal clipping to the federated learning settings. Some papers have considered clipped quantization for machine learning, focusing on the memory saving and speed acceleration [3]. However, the clipping factors in federated learning are not fully examined. We consider it in this paper. Indeed, our proposed setting provides both computation and communication efficiency.

We specifically focused on the quantization of weights in the uplink (from clients to server) for two main reasons. First, the cost of communicating during the download phase is relatively low since distributing global parameters to all clients can be done efficiently through broadcasting. Secondly, quantizing weights on the server and distributing them to clients means that, in the next training round, all clients start from the same point. This uniformity eliminates the benefits of stochastic quantization, where having different weights‚Äô realizations can lead to a more robust model. Thus, we examined the implications of this approach on model performance and communication efficiency.

We conduct image classification on MNIST dataset, where 50000 samples have been used for training and 10000 samples for test of the model. For the MNIST dataset, to make the assumption of i.i.d, each client receives an equal number of images from every category, ensuring a uniform distribution across all classes. As we are trying to compare different methods, we have not fine-tuned to get the best possible accuracy. Indeed, we compared these configurations with the same hyper-parameters.

We found that by summing the quantization errors in each layer, hence, sending 4 values of square errors alongside the weights to the server, and weighting the weights of each neuron in the server based on average square error of each neuron divided by the sum of the average square error in the corresponding neurons in the other clients, the performance of this weighting have the similar performance as averaging based on the number of clients.

Numbers show the bidwidths, for instance, 4-2-2-4 shows that we used 4 bits for the weights in the first and last layers, and 2 bits for the weights in the middle layers.
