Abstract.

Mining information from graph databases is becoming overly important. To approach this problem, current methods focus on identifying subgraphs with specific topologies; as of today, no work has been focused on expressing jointly the syntax and semantics of mining operations over rich property graphs.

We define MINE GRAPH RULE, a new operator for mining association rules from graph databases, by extending classical approaches used in relational databases and exploited by recommending systems. We describe the syntax and semantics of the operator, which is based on measuring the support and confidence of each rule, and then we provide several examples of increasing complexity on top of a realistic example; our operator embeds Cypher for expressing the mining conditions.

MINE GRAPH RULE is implemented on top of Neo4j, the most successful graph database system; it takes advantage of built-in optimizations of the Neo4j engine, as well as optimizations that are defined in the context of relational association rules. Our implementation is available as a portable Neo4j plugin. At the end of our paper, we show the execution performance in a variety of settings, by varying the operators, the size of the graph, the ratio between node types, the method for creating relationships, and maximum support and confidence.

2. Related Work

The main challenge when extending the broad work on relational association rule mining to graph-based association rule mining is to move from a simple, tabular data model, where associations are discovered within simple groups, to a complex graph-based data model, where associations can be semantically richer, and at the same time adapt the notions of support and confidence so as to preserve the antimonotone property, guaranteeing good convergence of the discovery methods.

To the best of our knowledge, an operator syntax and semantics have not yet been introduced that define association rules in the context of graph databases. Generally, in the works in which graph association rules are studied, each specific example is evaluated in itself, without proposing a structured framework that collectively defines graph association rules for every possible instance.

A framework for Graph Pattern Association Rule Mining is defined inÂ (Fan etÂ al., 2015), where the isomorphism of frequent subgraphs is mathematically exploited to extract topological patterns that can be translated into rules. Authors look for a frequent structure formed by a set of labeled nodes and edges in the graph; a rule is defined as follows: when the frequent structure is found, then also a specific edge is present, equipped with its values of support and confidence. In addition, in Â (Fan etÂ al., 2022) their approach for the extraction association rules is enhanced by machine learning models and graph sampling. Each example, however, is treated on its own and the rules are not defined with a shared syntax. The work has been expanded inÂ (Wang etÂ al., 2020), building up on the premise of searching rules guided by topological isomorphism of subgraphs. Here, the authors divide the problem into two different steps: 1) extraction of frequent patterns and 2) generation of the rules. Taking advantage of a similar methodology, (Zhang etÂ al., 2022) develops an algorithm that acts as a predictor of missing/incorrect links in Knowledge Graphs and (Priyadarshini and Mishra, 2010) compares a traditional approach with a custom algorithm (based on the adjacent matrix) to extract frequent subgraphs for graph mining.

A different approach consists of transforming graph-based data into relational data and then searching for association rules. InÂ (Raja and Balakrishnan, 2012), graph data is re-modeled into a transaction database, for which association rules (with their support and confidence) are extracted. Interesting case studies are reported inÂ (CiftÃ§i etÂ al., 2021) (evaluating groups of artists appreciated by the same users) and inÂ (Standl and Schlomske-Bodenstein, 2021) (studying combinations of teaching methods that are more effective than others). In both works, data is extracted from Neo4j and structured in a way that the association rules are calculated thanks to established Python packagesÂ (apy, 2019; Raschka, 2018). (Campi and Palese, 2021) implements a case study in Neo4j to find popular Twitter hashtags that are posted together; here, authors evaluate the effect of pre-processing techniques that enhance the performance of the rule mining algorithm. InÂ (Sen etÂ al., 2021), an approach for a recommendation system of products is fully developed in Neo4j. Lastly, (Tiwari etÂ al., 2010) exploit graphs as a means to more easily extract rules from a relational transaction dataset. Each transaction is encoded in a graph with specific nodes and edges, and the rules are extracted by exploiting graph query languages.

Note that all the mentioned works apply algorithms and the concepts of association rules to graph data, without formalizing what a rule is and without fully exploiting the richness given by the natural structure of graphs.

3. Operator Syntax and Semantics

The syntax of MINE GRAPH RULE is shown in Fig.Â 2; it is inspired by MINE RULE operatorÂ (Meo etÂ al., 1996) but it is significantly more complex in order to exploit the many ways of building associations by using property graph databases. It embeds some constructs of the Cypher query languageÂ (Francis etÂ al., 2018), so as to be easily translated to an APOC procedure for the Neo4j system, and also to be more readable for graph database programmers.

The operator associates a <name> to the set of extracted association rules that are built according to three expressions, respectively defining the GROUPING, the ruleâ€™s BODY, and the ruleâ€™s HEAD. The grouping expression defines, through the <anchorLabel>, the set of anchor nodes of the graph that need to satisfy, when present, the WHERE condition. They provide the context of evaluation of association rules; by analogy, the anchor acts as a pivot for the set of rules in the same way the purchase transaction groups the basket of items that are bought together. The BODY and HEAD expressions build respectively the left and right parts of an association rule; they are both defined by the <itemSet> production.

An itemSet is defined as a conjunction of expressions, each called <pattern>, that extract the sets of items forming the head and body; the cardinality of each <pattern> varies between 1 (the default case) and a value <n> (the cardinality must be defined only if <n> is greater than 1). A <pattern> generally consists of a linear path of relationships that starts from the anchor nodes (i.e., the nodes defined in the grouping expression) and can include an arbitrary number of relationship patterns (<relPattern>); recursion is introduced by the <patternTail> production which can either close the current recursion step with a final node or recursively include another <patternTail>. Relationship patterns include three options (respectively denoted as <singleRel>, <countRel>, and <anyRel>) whose explanation is postponed to three specific subsections. Note that -for simplicity- we assume that each relationship pattern is to be traversed from left to right.

The three expressions, GROUPING, BODY and HEAD, extract nodes that are referenced by variables; these can be used both to build any Cypher-legal predicate (<cypherPredicate>) for evaluating restricted parts of the graph database or for adding other grouping conditions defined by the optional IGNORE clause with a list of at least one <variable>. The operator syntax is closed by setting two independent conditions for the minimum support <minsupp> and minimum confidence <minconf> that must be satisfied by the extracted rules.

4. Implementation

The MINE GRAPH RULE operator has been implemented in Java as a user-defined Neo4j Procedure, mineGraphRule, and it is available as a portable Neo4j plugin, like the other add-ons of the Awesome Procedures On Cypher (APOC) library. We selected the Neo4j Graph database as a development platform since it provides an easily expandable architecture. Still, our proposed algorithm does not strictly depend on any specific Neo4j components and can be ported to other openCypher-compatible databases.

Once the plugin is installed, the procedure can be invoked using its name â€apoc.mineGraphRuleâ€ in the Neo4j Cypher shell; it supports nine mandatory parameters: the anchorâ€™s variable, the anchorâ€™s label, the conditions on the anchor, a map structure for the HEAD itemSet, an identical structure for the BODY itemSet, a list of predicates for the WHERE clause, a list of IGNOREd variables, the minimum support threshold, and the minimum confidence threshold. A detailed specification of the parametersâ€™ format can be found in the projectâ€™s repositoryÂ (Invernici etÂ al., 2024). Once executed, the procedure outputs a stream of records with a fixed format, consisting of four fields; each record represents an association rule. The fields are:

    body a map of key-value pairs for the BODY columns. The keys are the names of the columns and recall the structure of the pattern that they materialize, while the values are the identifiers -i.e., properties- of the nodes of those patterns;

  • â€¢

4.2. Algorithm for general operators

We now consider general operators, i.e., those that include conjunctions and multiple patterns in the BODY and/or in the HEAD. Inspired by the fast Apriori algorithm implementation(Bodon, 2003), where the mining starts by exploring the smaller and, thus, most common sets of frequent items and combines them into bigger sets (while the minimum support threshold is met), our algorithm starts by considering the operators with one pattern in the BODY and one in the HEAD and then considers operators with more patterns, thus, performing a body-head pair expansion. At each step, exactly one pattern is added either to the BODY or to the HEAD, up to the maximum number of patterns in the input operator. The body-head pairs are organized from the simplest to the most complete one in a directed acyclic graph (DAG), as exemplified in Fig.Â 6, Phase (2), for a particular example where the BODY has two patterns in conjunction, where the first one ranges from 1 to 3 and the second one ranges from 1 to 2.

Continuing our analogy with the Apriori algorithm, the objective of organizing a DAG of pairs is to enable the early exclusion of pairs when their support does not meet the minimum conditions set by the users. Thus, pairs are considered in an order created by a breadth-first traversal of the DAG and, as soon as one pair does not meet the threshold, the pair and all its descendants are removed from the DAG. Each generation of queries corresponding to a given pair is performed with the mechanism defined for simple input operators, as illustrated in Fig.Â 5, Phases (3) and (4).

Note that tables HEAD+BODY and BODY accumulate the instances for all the pairs. As discussed in Sec.Â 4.1, a final assembling step produces all the association rules with their HEAD, BODY, support, and confidence.

5.1. Synthetic databases generation

For our evaluation tests, we generated synthetic datasets that adhere to the same schema as the previously reported motivating example. We generated three types of synthetic datasets:

  • â€¢

    The first one with a uniform distribution of all the edges between random nodes of the graph, ensuring that relationships are evenly spread.

  • â€¢

    The second one, named scale-free, with a power-law distribution of the relationship BUY, in order to better represent the real-case scenario of an online shop; all the other relationships are generated with a uniform distribution among all nodes.

  • â€¢

    The third one, named large scale-free, with the same distribution of relationships as the scale-free graph, but with higher density of relationships BUY.

For each type of synthetic dataset, we generated multiple versions varying the total number of nodes of the graph to test the scalability performance according to the dimensions of the graph. In addition, for each graph instance, we also varied the ratio of anchor nodes (in our example, nodes with label Person), which directly impacts the absolute count of BUY relationships within a graph. In Uniform graphs, the total count of BUY is proportional to the product of the number of Person nodes with the number of Item nodes, which is maximum when the graph is evenly split between Persons and Items. Therefore, increasing the ratio over 0.5 would produce less BUY relationships. For Scale-free distribution instead, graphs have a fixed smaller portion of Items that are way more likely to be bought, and increasing the percentage of Persons results in a higher number of BUY relationships; therefore, in graphs with Scale-free distribution, the ratio of anchor nodes has a nonlinear relationship with the total count of BUY edges. Scale-free distributions mimic the classical distribution of itemsâ€™ purchases ((Mahanti etÂ al., 2013; Fenner etÂ al., 2010)).

6. Conclusion

We presented a new operator for mining association rules from a graph database. All the interactions with the database rely on a translation mechanism that transforms any operator expression into a set of pure Cypher queries, according to the topological, logical, and cardinality constraints expressed by the operator. We exploit the pattern-matching capabilities of Cypher for locating the relevant portions of the graph, taking advantage of the optimizations provided by the databaseâ€™s query-planning engine for any arbitrary existing graph, without resorting to schema-dependant tuning or heuristics, which are impossible to know at the toolâ€™s compile time. Our implementation embeds the Apriori approach at a set-oriented level, thereby taking full advantage of the optimization provided by all commonly used graph database engines.

Prospectively, we expect that the proposed operator will enable fast and fully explainable association rules mining over production scale knowledge graphs in several applied domains. Examples may include academic author-citation networks (e.g., to investigate areas of collaboration or common patterns in domains that are similar), actor-movie-director networks (e.g., to uncover promising artistic collaborations that increase chances of prizes), customer-order-product networks (e.g., to pinpoint complex customer behavior and targeted marketing/discount policies), or playlist-song graphs (e.g., to suggest favorable compilation combinations for users).

While the provided working implementation is specific for Neo4j, the syntax/semantics of the operator are as general as possible; the principles followed in the implementation can be applied to any graph database. Our approach can be exploited to pave the way for standardizing the association rules mining process in graph databases (making it as natural as it has become for relational databases) along the guiding lines for graph analytics indicated by the graph communityÂ (Sakr etÂ al., 2021).
