Fully Authentic Visual Question Answering Dataset from Online Communities

Chongyan Chen1, Mengchen Liu2, Noel Codella 2, Yunsheng Li2 , Lu Yuan 2 , Danna Gurari 1,3
11~{}^{1} University of Texas at Austin 22~{}^{2} Microsoft33~{}^{3} University of Colorado Boulder
Abstract

Visual Question Answering (VQA) entails answering questions about images. We introduce the first VQA dataset in which all contents originate from an authentic use case. Sourced from online question answering community forums, we call it VQAonline. We then characterize our dataset and how it relates to eight other VQA datasets. Observing that answers in our dataset tend to be much longer (e.g., with a mean of 173 words) and thus incompatible with standard VQA evaluation metrics, we next analyze which of the six popular metrics for longer text evaluation align best with human judgments. We then use the best-suited metrics to evaluate six state-of-the-art vision and language foundation models on VQAonline and reveal where they struggle most. To facilitate future extensions, we publicly share the dataset at: https://vqaonline.github.io/.

1 Introduction

We are witnessing a renewed excitement in visual question answering (VQA), the task of answering questions about images, with recent successes of large foundation models in zero-shot settings. Moreover, many papers are reporting that such models can achieve or even exceed human performanceÂ [54, 37, 44].

While modern VQA datasets enabled this exciting progress in VQA model development, they have two key limitations. First, most publicly-available VQA datasetsÂ [17, 49, 46] are artificially-constructed to probe model performance rather stemming from authentic use cases. Yet, repeatedly, prior workÂ [19, 57, 20] has revealed that models developed for artificially-constructed settings generalize poorly to authentic use cases due to significant domain shifts. The second key limitation of existing datasets lies in a limited diversity of content representing authentic use cases. The only VQA dataset reflecting an authentic use case is VizWiz-VQAÂ [18]. However, VizWiz-VQA is constrained to one population, specifically blind individuals seeking assistance in learning about their visual surroundings. A further limitation of the dataset is that the answers were collected years later to support model benchmarking, rather than having been validated as acceptable by those who asked the questions.

We address these limitations by introducing a new dataset called VQAonline. Sourced from online question answering communities, it is the the first VQA dataset with all contents originating from an authentic use case. We conduct analysis to highlight how this dataset differs from prior datasets; examples include that it contains: (1) authentic context that clarifies the question, (2) an answer the individual asking the question validated as acceptable from all community-provided answers, (3) answers that are considerably longer (e.g., a mean of 173 words versus typically 11 words or fewer in prior work), and (4) user-chosen topics for each visual question from 105 diverse topics revealing the datasetâ€™s inherent diversity. Such differences are exemplified in FigureÂ 1. Next, observing that previous VQA evaluation metrics are ill-suited for lengthy textual answers, we assess which related evaluation metrics for long-form text best align with human judgement for our dataset. Using these metrics, we then benchmark the performance of six modern foundation models in the zero-shot setting. Our findings reveal that modern models perform poorly overall and our fine-grained analysis reveals opportunities for how to improve such models.

We expect improved models designed for VQAonline can also benefit many other real-world applications including other community question answering platforms (e.g., Yahoo! Answers, Wiki Answers, Quora), learning assistance VQA tools for children [22], virtual assistants, visual dialog and query, as well as visual search and browsing. To encourage community-wide progress, we publicly-share the dataset at: https://vqaonline.github.io/.

2 Related Work

Visual Question Answering Datasets.

A commonality of all VQA datasets (e.g., Â [1, 17, 19, 49, 27, 25, 11]) is the inclusion of artificially-generated content. The closest dataset to reflect an authentic use case is VizWiz-VQAÂ [19], as the visual questions were all submitted by individuals seeking assistance to learn about their visual surroundings. However, the answers were crowdsourced and never shared with or validated by those asking the questions. To our knowledge, our work is the first to provide an authentic dataset end-to-end, with each example including a natural language question, image, context, and answer validated by the individual asking the question. We conduct extensive analysis to reveal unique aspects of our dataset (SectionÂ 3.2).

Our work also aligns with VQA datasets providing context that supplements each visual question: Context-VQAÂ [42] and ScienceQAÂ [46]. However, context in Context-VQA [42] is defined as the kind of website the image is sourced from rather than our definition of supplementary data that supports answering the visual question. More similar to our work is ScienceQAÂ [46], however the context is both artificially-generated and has distinct compositions and content, as discussed in SectionÂ 3.2.

Community Question Answering (CQA) Datasets.

Numerous datasets have been developed around CQA. However, all focus on text-based question answeringÂ [50] or natural language questions with multimedia answers [43]. To our knowledge, our work is the first to introduce a community multimodal question answering dataset specifically for visual question answering.

VQA Models.

Traditional VQA models generate short answersÂ [52] and do not consider extra context to help answer the questions. Yet, our dataset features answers that are lengthy, typically containing multiple sentences, and provides additional context that can help answer visual questions. Accordingly, we benchmark large Vision and Language Models (VLMs)Â [12, 32, 44, 55, 59, 35] which, by their generative design, are better-suited to generate long-form answers using their strong natural language understanding capabilities. Overall, we find these models perform poorly and provide fine-grained analysis to reveal opportunities for improving such models.

3 VQAonline Dataset

We now introduce our VQAonline dataset originating from an authentic use case where people used a web-based platform to post and answer visual questions.

3.1 Dataset Creation

Source.

We created our dataset by scraping data from the online question-answering platform Stack Exchange. We used the publicly-available data that spanned from August 2010 to November 2022, and adhered to all data license restrictions (cc-by-sa 4.0) set forth by Stack Exchange.

From the Stack Exchange archive, we collected all examples that contained each the following four components: a natural language question, a natural language context, an image, and at least one answer. Originally, this information was collected from Stack Exchange users via four user entry interactions. The natural language question was collected as the post "title", using a text entry field paired with instructions "be specific and imagine youâ€™re asking a question to another person" when writing the question. The image was collected as a URL link. The natural language context was provided via a "body" text entry field that supported inline images (shared via a URL link) with users instructed to supply "all the information someone would need to answer your question". These three pieces of information were then shared publicly, after which users on the site could publicly provide answers.111Stack Exchange users could either propose edits to existing answers, which had to then be approved by high-reputation users, or could propose new answers. Further community deliberation to arrive at high-quality answers could occur when users posted comments under the question or answers. The mean number of answers per visual question is 1.45. Finally, the individuals who posted the visual questions could mark which answer was the â€œaccepted answer"; we only included examples for which the individuals did this.

Of note, the Stack Exchange platform has two key mechanisms that contribute to high quality VQAs. First, the platform enforces a stringent reputation system with moderation policies, as documented at https://meta.stackexchange.com/help. Second, it features topic-specific sites for users to engage with a relevant community; e.g., music.stackexchange.com and gardening.stackexchange.com. We used the data from 105 topical sites that belong to five super-categories, with 46 about culture & recreation (e.g., travel, sports, Christianity, French language), 25 about life & arts (e.g., physical fitness, parenting, pets), 24 about science (e.g., physics, chemistry, biology), 7 about being professional, and 3 about business.222We excluded the 75 technology topics (e.g., Stack Overflow, LaTeX), because they typically are language problems disguised as VQA problems. In particular, the questions typically ask about code shown in screenshots. All topics are shown in FigureÂ 2.

Filtering.

We started with 331,000 candidate VQAs from the source and removed visual questions without accepted answers. Then, we conducted two filtering steps to obtain our final dataset with 64,696 visual questions. First, we removed low-quality VQAs based on Stack Exchangeâ€™s feature that enables users to up-vote or down-vote each post (either question or answer). Each up-vote added 1 to the score and each down-vote subtracted 1 from the score. We removed visual questions with scores 0 or less for either the question or answer. Second, we excluded visual questions with multiple images (e.g., GIF) or with visual answers.

Dataset Splits.

While our work is inspired by the zero-shot setting, we designed our dataset to support few-shot settings given the recent exciting developments around in-context few-shot learning with foundation models. To do so, we randomly selected from each of the 105 topics covered in our dataset 7 examples for training and 3 examples for validation for all topics with at least 20 examples. The result is 665 examples in the training set, 285 in the validation set, and 63,746 examples in the test set.

3.2 Dataset Analysis

3.2.1 Comparison to Existing Datasets

Setup.

We start by contextualizing how our dataset compares to the current focus of the research community. To do so, we compare VQAs in our dataset to those in eight existing popular VQA datasets. We selected VQAv2Â [17] because it is the most popular (e.g., highly cited) VQA dataset, Context-VQAÂ [42] and ScienceQAÂ [46]Â 333We focus on the subset of ScienceQA with both image and context. because they similarly claim to provide context, VizWiz-VQAÂ [19] because it similarly originates from an authentic use case, INFOSEEKÂ [11] because it similarly centers on information seeking rather than probing an AI model, OK-VQAÂ [38] because it similarly requires domain-specific knowledge, as well as DocVQAÂ [39] and Infographic VQAÂ [40] because they similarly contain VQAs with documents and infographics.

For each dataset, we report the mean number of words in each question, mean number of words in each answer, number of images, number of topics which were authentically used to label clusters of VQAs by theme, whether context is included, whether the visual questions are authentic, whether the context is authentic, and whether the answers are validated by those asking the questions. Results are reported in Table 1.

Our findings reveal commonalities of our VQAonline dataset with existing datasets. For example, we observe that the visual questions themselves are similar in terms of the typical question length and number of images. Specifically, questions have a mean of 9 words in our dataset compared to 6 to 11 words in other datasets and the nearly 65,000 images in our dataset are comparable to existing datasets that typically contain tens of thousands of examples.

Our analysis also demonstrates significant differences from existing datasets across two key dimensions. The first dimension is the dataset authenticity. Our VQAonline dataset is the only fully authentic dataset for the entire VQA pipeline, meaning all contents originate from an authentic use case. The second key difference of our dataset is the typical answer length. Answers in our VQAonline dataset are orders of magnitude longer than in existing datasets, with a mean length of 173 words in VQAonline versus 11 or fewer words in existing datasets. This larger length is not anomalous, as the median answer length in VQAonline is 120 words. This longer length in part reflects responses are provided in paragraph form, with most commonly 3 sentences per answer. This distinction renders existing evaluation metrics for VQA models largely ineffective, as existing metrics assume brief responsesÂ [1]. This issue will be addressed in SectionÂ 4.1.

3.2.2 Fine-Grained Analysis

We next provide a richer analysis of the questions, images, context, and answers in our VQAonline dataset.

Topic.

The authentic user-based posting of each VQA within a topic community reveals the diversity of content in our dataset. We report the number of VQAs in each of the 105 topics in Figure 2. As shown, there can be as few as 10 examples in a topic to over 20,000.

Questions.

Our VQAonline dataset is the first with authentic visual questions asked by users of an online question-answering platform. This results in a domain shift from the only other VQA dataset with authentically asked visual questions, VizWiz-VQAÂ [19], which were asked by blind users of a mobile phone application. For instance, blind people typically asked about their physical surroundings (e.g., "What is this?", "What does this say?") while Stack Exchange users often asked for more fine-grained or specialized information about the images (e.g., "Whatâ€™s a better term to describe these â€˜stripesâ€™ in blouse (Screen shot attached)", "What is the third character on this sign seen in Taipei?"). Another distinction stems from Stack Exchangeâ€™s feature for avoiding redundant questions by letting users remove duplicates of other already submitted questions. The consequence is that only 0.2% of natural language questions in our VQAonline dataset are duplicated compared to 37.9% of questions in VizWiz-VQA.

Context.

Our VQAonline dataset is the first to include authentic context. The only other VQA dataset containing context, as defined by supplementary data that supports answering the visual question, is ScienceQAÂ [46]. However, the context was contrived with heuristic rules for extracting data from elementary and high school science curricula. Consequently, context in ScienceQA is typically narrated in third-person, while context in our VQAonline often are narrated in first-person. Another difference is ScienceQA has a shorter average length of the context compared to that in our dataset; i.e., a mean of 41 words, median of 28 words, and most commonly 3 sentences versus a mean of 127 words, median of 94 words, and most commonly 4 sentences. A further difference is that the context in ScienceQA only relates to 26 science topics while the context in our dataset spans 105 topics that extend beyond science.

Image Contents.

We next quantify the tendency for images to include screenshots and infographics to reflect the tendency for visual questions to be related to the digital realm and data analysis. We identified an image as a "screenshot" when contents were copied digitally from a computing device and an "infographic" when the image shows a chart, table, graph, attention map, map, or diagram. We manually labeled 105 images sampled from each of the 105 topics as well as an independent random selection of 100 images from the entire dataset. We found for the topic-sampled set that 79% are screenshots and 17% show infographics and for the samples from the entire dataset that 84% are screenshots and 33% show infographics. The similarly high prevalence of screenshots across both samples underscores Stack Exchange usersâ€™ tendency to ask questions about the digital realm. We hypothesized the different prevalence of infographics across both samples was due to topical differences, as 52% of all VQAs (i.e., 33,384 out of 64,696) belong to only 23% of the topics (i.e., 24 science topics). Supporting this hypothesis, we found from inspecting 100 random VQAs from each subset of the science topics and non-science topics that infographics were in 58% and 6% of images respectively.

Importance of Images.

We next examine the value of images for answering the questions. To do so, we flagged each image as question-aligned if elements described in the question are found in the image and context-aligned if elements described in the context are found in the image. We additionally flagged whether an image is necessary, based on whether the answer can be obtained without the image. We manually labeled 105 examples from each of the 105 topics. We found that 91.4% of the images are question-aligned and 99% of the images are context-aligned. This finding contrasts that of the only other dataset with authentic visual questions, VizWiz-VQAÂ [19], where only 71% are deemed answerable because blind photographers struggled to capture contents of interest in their images. We also found that 65.7% images are necessary for answering the question in our dataset. Often, the purpose of unnecessary images was to motivate or show scenarios in which the asker encounters the question. Altogether, these findings underscore the value of VQA models analyzing the images to arrive at the answers.

User Intention.

We finally characterized intents behind the visual questions to inform how AI models can better meet usersâ€™ information wants. Our work complements two prior works that identified user intentions for three community question answering platforms (Answerbag, Metafilter, and Yahoo! Answer)Â [21] and the music StackExchange communityÂ [15]. Our work is the first to investigate user intentions for VQAs. Through a multi-round analysis of the data, described in the Supplementary Materials, we defined eight potential intents: instruction, evidence, verification, advice, identification, opinion, reason, and other. Examples for three of these categories are shown in Figure 3. We then recruited vetted crowdworkers from Amazon Mechanical Turk to label one primary intent per visual question when shown the question, image, context, and answer. They labeled 105 examples, with one randomly selected from each of the 105 topics. We used the majority vote label per VQA. The resulting distribution of assigned categories is 19% are instruction, 18% evidence, 17% verification, 16% advice, 10% identification, 10% opinion, 8% reason, and 1% other. Examples from the three most common categories are shown in Figure 3.

4 Experiments

4.1 Evaluation Methodology

As noted in SectionÂ 3.2, the uniquely lengthy answers in our VQAonline dataset render the mainstream VQA evaluation metric unsuitable since it assumes short answersÂ [1, 17]. Consequently, we first establish an appropriate evaluation methodology. To do so, we assess which widely-used metrics for longer text evaluation align best with human judgments of the quality of model-generated results.

Candidate Evaluation Metrics.

We identified six popular evaluation metrics for longer text from related fields, including long-form question answering and image captioning. We primarily focused on reference-based metrics, which assess the similarity between model-generated answers and human-generated answers, because of their widespread use. Specifically, we chose ROUGE-LÂ [33], METEORÂ [13], RefCLIP-S Â [23], BERTscoreÂ [58], and the GPT-4 metricÂ [56, 16] 444For GPT-4 metric, we prompted GPT-4 to â€compare the ground truth and prediction from AI models and give a correctness score for the prediction. For completeness, we also include the reference-free metric CLIP-SÂ [23], which assesses compatibility between the image and model-generated answer. All metrics are set to output values in the range of 0 to 1.

Human Judgement Collection.

We hired ten human annotators for an IRB-approved human evaluation study. Each individual was a domain expert in one of ten topics covered in our dataset (i.e., Physical Fitness, Botany, Music, Mechanics, Politics, Law, Chinese Language, Economics, Statistics, and Artificial Intelligence), with at least a Masterâ€™s degree in a field related to their assigned VQA topic. Each domain expert was assigned 20 VQAs on their topic, and was required to rate the performance of six model-generated answers per VQA. As a result, we collected for 200 VQAs a total of 1200 human judgments (i.e., 10âˆ—20âˆ—61020610*20*6).

For the annotation task, each domain expert was shown for each VQA the original visual question, context, and answer alongside the six model-generated answers in a home-grown web-based annotation tool. The task was to rate each model-generated answer as either incorrect (i.e., score of 0), partially correct (i.e, score of 0.5), or correct (i.e., score of 1). The definitions for these ratings are: Correct: At least one of these two items needs to be true for the answer to be correct: (1) Aligns with all the key points in the reference answer. (2) The answer also makes sense based on your best judgment, even if not included in the reference answer. Partially Correct: The answer matches part of the key points in the reference answer that answers the question but has errors in the content. Incorrect: The response doesnâ€™t include any key points from the reference answer and doesnâ€™t make sense.

Analysis With Respect to Input Types.

We next analyze the predictive power of each input by analyzing the performance of ablated model variants where one input type is removed from each: i.e., Q+C, Q+I, and C+I. Results for the two top-performing models are shown in TableÂ 4. Unsurprisingly, we find that the model receiving the maximum amount of information (i.e., Q+C+I) performs the best.

We observe that the worst-performing variant across both models and all evaluation metrics are the models lacking context as input. This highlights that current models find the context (which is typically language-based) to be the most valuable source for arriving at an answer.

When comparing the models that lack questions as input (i.e., Q+C) with models receiving all inputs (Q+C+I), the questionsâ€™ predictive power appears to be nearly negligible. We suspect this is because often the information provided by the question is included in the context. For example, a question asking "What test am I taking, anyways?" while the part of the context includes "can you tell me what field of math the test was on?", which overlaps with the question.

When comparing the models lacking images as input (i.e., Q+C) with models receiving all inputs (Q+C+I), we similarly observe that the predictive power of the image seems very small. We suspect this result does not imply the irrelevance of images for the task, as further validated through a small-scale study in SectionÂ 3.2. Instead, we believe this argues for improving models so they can better utilize images when predicting answers.

We next analyzed each modelâ€™s performance per topic. First, we visualize performance for the two top-performing models with respect to the top two human-aligned evaluation metrics, METEOR and GPT-4 metric, in Figure 4. One common outlier for the per-topic visualization (i.e., Figure 4) is consistently very low performance for the puzzling topic across both evaluation metrics. We attribute this to such topics requiring jointly strong vision understanding and reasoning skills. An example of the puzzling topic with GPT-4V is shown in Figure 5, Example 1.

Another trend we observe for the per-topic visualization (i.e., Figure 4) is different performance for topics related to different languages. The models work best on visual questions related to Germanic Languages; e.g., English as exemplified in Figure 5, Example 2. The next best languages are Romance Languages (e.g., Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese), then East Asian Languages (e.g., Chinese, Korean), and finally Russian. We suspect causes of such consistent performance discrepancies across languages are: (1) Data availability: languages which have fewer datasets and less diversity (e.g., one exemplified in Figure 5, Example 3) perform worse than those with greater diversity and abundance of datasets (e.g., English), (2) Language Complexity: Languages with simpler grammar (e.g., Esperanto) or familiar grammatical structures (e.g., Romance languages for an English-trained model) are easier for models to learn, and (3) Text recognition can be more challenging for languages with unique scripts, such as Chinese or Korean while these language-related VQAs often pertain to text recognition. We believe these findings shed light on future research directions for multilingual VQA and text VQA research.

With respect to the five super-categories (i.e., TableÂ 5), overall across all evaluation metrics, the models perform worst on Culture & Recreation. We suspect this is because such visual questions require a higher reliance on vision understanding, an area where prevailing VLMs fail.

5 Conclusions

We introduce the first fully authentic end-to-end VQA dataset. Our analysis reveals numerous distinct features of our new dataset, most importantly atypically long answers. As a result, we conducted human-based studies to establish a new VQA evaluation methodology for our dataset and found that well-suited metrics are GPT-4 metric, METEOR, and BERTscore. Subsequent benchmarking of six modern VQA models in zero-shot settings reveals they all perform poorly. Our fine-grained analysis across the 105 topics covered in the dataset reveals some topics are easier (e.g., English-based and Computer Science-based topics) while other topics are very challenging (e.g., puzzling). To facilitate extensions of this work, we publicly share our dataset at https://vqaonline.github.io/.

I Dataset Collection

I.1 Dataset Source and Filtration

The Stack Exchange data is hosted at https://archive.org/details/stackexchange in XML format. We started with 331,000 candidate visual questions. After removing visual questions without an accepted answer, it resulted in 165,766 visual questions. As mentioned in the main paper, we then conducted two filtering steps. After removing visual questions with scores of 0 or less for either the question or answer, we had 119,177 visual questions. Next, after removing visual questions with multiple images, we had 85,573 visual questions. Subsequently, removing visual questions with visual answers, left 65,849 visual questions. Examples of filtered visual questions with visual answers and multiple images are shown in Figures 6 and 7 respectively. Finally, after removing examples for which the image could not be downloaded from the provided link, we got to our final dataset of 64,696 visual questions. For data format consistency, we converted all images to png format.

I.2 User Intention Taxonomy and Definitions.

User Intention Taxonomy. We first brainstormed 11 free-form user intention categories. To do so, we solicited help from GPT-3.5 by having it indicate via a zero-shot prompt the user intentions when given a question and context. We identified an initial 11 free-form user intention categories, which are shown in the second column of Table 7. We then refined these categories based on the frequency of their occurrence in the literature on user intention (shown in the third to the last column in Table 7) for related fields such as question answering and search queriesÂ [4, 7, 26, 21, 51, 9, 28, 36] to the following 7 categories: advice, evidence, identification, instruction, opinion, reason, and verification. We then drafted the definitions for each taxonomy, mainly by adapting definitions from [7, 4, 21].

We then finalized the taxonomy with definitions via four rounds of annotator analysis on a total of 105 visual questions. Specifically, two annotators (authors) tagged the category for each visual question. Any disagreements that arose were discussed and resolved, and the definitions associated with each taxonomy category were adjusted accordingly. We observed slight agreement accuracy 777The agreement accuracy is calculated by dividing the number of agreements between two annotators by the total number of examples annotated. improvement and Cohenâ€™s kappa agreement improvement after each round. Specifically, from round 1 to round 4, agreement accuracy improved from 53.3% to 87.5% and Cohenâ€™s kappa agreement improved from 45.24% to 84.69%. This process culminated in our final taxonomy and their definition, which includes the following eight categories mentioned in the main paper: advice, evidence, identification, instruction, opinion, reason, verification, and other.

As shown in Table 7, our final taxonomy is different from Â [4, 7, 26, 21, 51, 9, 28, 36] as they focus solely on text-based question or search queries whereas our work centers on visual questions. [5] also explores user intentions for authentic visual questions for blind individuals. However, blind peopleâ€™s user intents of visual questions significantly differ from that of online usersâ€™ visual questions. In fact, only one of the user intents identified for blind individuals (i.e., identification) overlaps with the user intents we have identified for our dataset.

Final user intention taxonomy and definitions.

We show the final user intention taxonomy and definitions as follows:

  • â€¢

    Verification: These fact-checking questions aim to confirm or refute a hypothesis through affirmative answers (yes/no or multiple choice options). Since this question is based on fact, it cannot include subjective questions which should be classified as Opinion.

  • â€¢

    Identification: The expected answer for these questions is a named entity or object identification. The answer is objective.

  • â€¢

    Reason: These questions require answers that explain causes underlying a particular action or event. Most "why" questions fall into this category.

  • â€¢

    Evidence-based: This category primarily includes questions that ask for the definition, features, description, or process of a concept, idea, object, event, or one of its attributes. It also covers translation questions of seeking the meaning of a sentence or word. If a question can be classified as Verification, it should not be classified as Evidence-based.

  • â€¢

    Instruction: These questions typically involve "how to do" inquiries, seeking instructions, guidelines, or procedures for a specific action in real life.

  • â€¢

    Advice: This category includes questions where the user seeks personalized guidance on a specific topic. Advice questions differ from Instruction questions in that they expect subjective recommendations, while Instruction questions seek objective, step-by-step processes for performing a specific action. Advice questions may also involve finding resources or seeking better translations of sentences/words. Additionally, this category can include questions where users are looking for ideas or comments on how to improve an existing solution. If a question can be classified as Instruction, it should not be categorized as Advice.

  • â€¢

    Opinion: These questions aim to elicit subjective opinions on a topic of interest (e.g., "what do you think about" or "is X good/bad"). It might include religious questions. This category excludes Advice questions, where the focus is on the user asking the question.

  • â€¢

    Other: Other.

I.3 User Intention Annotations.

Hiring Crowdworkers.

We hired three crowdworkers from Amazon Mechanical Turk to perform our task. For quality control, we only accepted workers located in the United States who had completed more than 500 Human Intelligence Tasks (HITs) with over a 95% approval rating.

Annotation Task Design and Collection.

We provided instructions, taxonomy definitions, and two examples per user intention category. The annotation task interface showed for each visual question the question, context, and answer.

To facilitate collecting high-quality results, we provided each crowdworker with one-on-one Zoom training. We also provided a qualifying annotation test that all workers passed to verify they understood the instructions.

To enable the assessment of high-quality results, we collected two user intention annotations from two independent workers (worker A and B) per VQA instance (i.e. image-question-context-answer) for 105 randomly selected VQAs, one from each of 105 topics. We then detected if their annotations matched. We found that 25 out of 105 didnâ€™t match. Thus, we hired a third worker (worker C) to break the tie by instructing that individual to select one of the two provided user intentions. We paid $40 Amazon Gift Card to workers A and B and $5 Amazon Gift Card to worker C.

Instructions for User Intention Annotation.

The following are the instructions provided for workers A and B:

  • â€¢

    Read definitions and two examples for each category.

  • â€¢

    We will present to you 105 questions. Each question contains a question, context, and a reference answer. Please read them and categorize the primary user intention according to step 1.

  • â€¢

    If there are any non-English questions, or if the questions contain many proper nouns that are difficult to interpret, you can use a translation tool to translate them into your native language.

  • â€¢

    Fill in the user intent category for each question in the provided spreadsheet.

Data Collection Quality Control Mechanism.

We provided a one-on-one training session with each domain expert to introduce the task and instructions. We also inspected the time each expert spent on each visual question as a proxy for assessing whether the hired domain experts were finishing the task by quickly selecting random options. We compensated each domain expert with a $75 Amazon Gift Card, resulting in an average hourly wage of $26/hour.
