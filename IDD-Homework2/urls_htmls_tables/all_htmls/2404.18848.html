FeDeRA: Efficient Fine-tuning of Language Models in Federated Learning Leveraging Weight Decomposition

Yuxuan Yan, Shunpu Tang, Zhiguo Shi, Qianqian Yang
College of Information Science and Electronic Engineering, Zhejiang University
Abstract

Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) have shown excellent performance on various downstream tasks after fine-tuning. Nevertheless, the escalating concerns surrounding user privacy have posed significant challenges to centralized training reliant on extensive data collection. Federated learning, which only requires training on the clients and aggregates weights on the server without sharing data, has emerged as a solution. However, the substantial parameter size of PLMs places a significant burden on the computational resources of client devices, while also leading to costly communication expenses. Introducing Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning(PEFT) into federated learning can effectively address this problem. However, we observe that the non-IID data in federated learning leads to a gap in performance between the PEFT method and full parameter fine-tuning(FFT). To overcome this, we propose FeDeRA, an improvement over the Low-Rank Adaption(LoRA) method in federated learning. FeDeRA uses the same adapter module as LoRA. However, the difference lies in FeDeRAâ€™s initialization of the adapter module by performing Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the pre-trained matrix and selecting its principal components. We conducted extensive experiments, using RoBERTa-base and DeBERTaV3-base, on three tasks and six datasets, comparing the methods including FFT and the other three different PEFT methods. FeDeRA outperforms all other PEFT methods and is comparable to or even surpasses the performance of FFT method. We also deployed federated learning on Jetson AGX Orin and compared the time required by different methods to achieve the target accuracy on specific tasks. Compared to FFT, FeDeRA reduces the training time by 95.9%, 97.9%, 96.9% and 97.3%, 96.5%, 96.5% respectively on three tasks using RoBERTa-base and DeBERTaV3-base. The overall experiments indicate that FeDeRA achieves good performance while also maintaining efficiency.

1 Introduction

Pre-trained language models (PLMs) have achieved state-of-the-art(SOTA) performances across various NLP tasks, including natural language understandingkenton2019bert ; sanh2019distilbert ; liu2019roberta , text generationbrown2020language ; zeng2022glm ; jiang2023mistral , and question answering. However, training these models from scratch demands a significant amount of resourcesnarayanan2021efficient , making it unattainable for most. Consequently, fine-tuning models for specific tasks has emerged as the primary method of leveraging large language models. This process typically involves training a pre-trained model on a significantly smaller dataset rather than the original training set. While fine-tuning in a centralized manner is typically preferred, aggregating all data onto a single device raises concerns about data privacy, thus making centralized training increasingly challenging.

The emergence of federated learningkonevcny2016federated ; mcmahan2017communication has effectively provided a solution to this challenge. Federated learning introduces a new paradigm for machine learning implementation, enabling models to be trained across decentralized clients, each with its own local data samples, without sharing raw data explicitly. Instead, clients periodically compute and send model information, such as parameters or gradients, to a server responsible for aggregating them to produce a global model.

However, fine-tuning PLMs in a federated learning setting faces significant challenges. Firstly, federated learning requires frequent exchange of model parameters or gradients between the server and the clients. The massive number of parameters in PLMs, often in the tens or even hundreds of billions, leads to substantial communication overheads. Additionally, devices participating in federated learning often communicate over limited bandwidth networks, causing significant delays during data transmission and thereby reducing training efficiency. Moreover, fine-tuning of language models demands substantial memory and computational resources, which many edge devices may not adequately meet.

Recently, parameter-efficient fine-tuning(PEFT) approaches like BitFitzaken2022bitfit , adapter tuningnarayanan2021efficient ; pfeiffer2021adapterfusion , prefix tuningli2021prefix and LoRAhu2021lora , which update only a small portion of the pre-trained modelâ€™s parameters, have gained a lot of attention due to their advantages in memory and computational efficiency. Existing works have demonstrated that these methods match or even surpass the performance of traditional full-parameter fine-tuning(FFT) methods. Adopting PEFT in the federated learning setting shows promise in addressing the challenges identified above, as it effectively reduces the number of parameters to be transmitted between clients and the server, as well as the memory and computation cost of model training on each client.

In this work, we explore PEFT methods under a federated learning setting and find that the heterogeneity of clientsâ€™ data leads to degraded performance of PEFT in federated learning compared to FFT and a slower convergence rate. As shown in FigureÂ 1, this performance gap widens with increased data heterogeneity. To address this issue, we propose FeDeRA, which conducts Singular Value Decomposition(SVD) on the pre-trained weight matrices to extract the principal components to initialize adapter modules similar to LoRA, while retaining and freezing the remaining components in the original matrix. This simple yet effective solution tackles the significant performance decline of the LoRA method within federated settings, specifically when faced with substantial not Independently and Identically Distributed (non-IID)kairouz2021advances data. FeDeRA demonstrates the capacity to achieve performance comparable to or surpassing that of FFT in diverse tasks, while exceeding other PEFT methods. Notably, when matching the accuracy of FFT, FeDeRA slashes training time by more than 95%. It ensures computational and communication efficiency and circumvents performance deterioration when faced with highly non-IID data distributions.

2 Related Works

2.1 Federated Learning with Non-IID Data

Federated learning is a widely adopted approach for distributed learning, especially in tasks with significant privacy considerations. However, it suffers unneglectable performance degradation when data across different clients are non-independently and Identically Distributed (non-IID). Research efforts have surged to mitigate the effects of non-IID data on the performance of federated learningzhu2021federated ; ma2022state ; han2022survey . Some studies address this issue directly on the training data on each client through data sharing like sharing a small portion of local datazhao2018federated or sharing the outputs of the modelitahara2021distillation , data enhancement like enhancing lacking or sparse labels data by using external datasetsjeong2018communication or generating new data from the encoded and protected data of other clientsshin2020xor , and data selectioncai2020dynamic ; wang2020optimizing . Some other works carefully design the process of model training such as using adaptive learning rates on the clientsma2021fedsa or using a control variable to prevent excessive drift in local updateskarimireddy2020scaffold to tackle this paper. Other studies have combined other algorithms such as Meta-Learningjiang2019improving ; zhang2021fedpd , Lifelong Learningshoham2019overcoming ; kopparapu2020fedfmc , Knowledge Distillationitahara2021distillation ; jiang2020federated , etc. Existing research has indicated that using pre-trained models for training in federated learning, rather than training models from scratch, can effectively mitigate the impact of non-IID data on performance.weller2022pretrained ; nguyen2022begin ; chen2022importance . However, as highlighted in SectionÂ 1, the substantial computational demand and communication overhead that accompany PLMs are difficult to ignore. Therefore, the application of PLMs in federated learning urgently requires optimization in both computation and communication.

2.2 PEFT in Federated Learning

PEFT methods aim to freeze all or most of the weights of PLMs, only fine-tuning some weights or those additionally introduced on a small scale. Therefore, PEFT methods can reduce the demand for computational resources and significantly decrease communication overhead. Some studies attempt to incorporate various PEFT methods in federated learning and demonstrate their high efficiency in training, while also evaluating the comparative advantages and disadvantages of different PEFT approaches in terms of performance, resource demands, or privacy aspectssun2022exploring ; chen2022fedtune ; zhang2023fedpetuning . Additionally, certain studies concentrate on exploring the potential of PEFT techniques within federated learning. For instance, employing LoRA to facilitate enhanced heterogeneous personalized learning in federated learningyi2023fedlora ; lu2024hyperflora , as well as the incremental enhancement of adaptor configurations during the training process of federated learning to accelerate convergencecai2023efficient . However, research has increasingly recognized that the PEFT method experiences significant performance degradation when dealing with highly non-IID data distributions in federated learningzhang2023fedpetuning ; babakniya2023slora . Despite this emerging awareness, a significant portion of prior research has either neglected this critical issue or failed to consider evaluation within the realm of severe non-IID data.

4 Methods

4.1 FeDeRA

In centralized learning, LoRA demonstrates robust performance across various tasks, exhibiting equivalency with FFT in terms of performance. Nevertheless, when faced with highly non-IID data, the performance of LoRA may not reach a level commensurate with FFT or tends to exhibit a slower rate of convergence. We hypothesize that the reason behind this scenario is attributed to the initialization process of the LoRA module, which appears to be unfavorable for federated learning under non-IID data. This mode of initialization can lead to each successive local update to matrices A and B being disproportionately larger and diverging further from the original matrix.

5 Evaluation

We conduct a variety of experiments to validate the superiority of FeDeRA. We constructed our experiments based on the foundation of FedMLhe2020fedml and FedNLPlin2022fednlp . Firstly, we compared the performance that FeDeRA and different baselines can achieve under highly non-IID conditions in federated learning. We constructed experiments on six datasets across three categories of NLP tasks with highly non-IID data built according to the Dirichlet distribution. Subsequently, we validated the efficiency of FeDeRA. We set different task target accuracy and constructed federated learning experiments on actual devices. We compared the time required for our method and the baselines to reach the same accuracy.

5.1 Baselines

We will use FFT along with various PEFT methods in federated learning as baselines. In order to ensure a thorough comparison, the experimental settings are consistent with the original settings as presented in the prior work unless explicitly stated otherwise.

FedFT: The most common approach to fine-tuning involves updating all the modelâ€™s parameters during the training process.

FedBF: Applying BitFit in federated learning, where only the bias terms within the model are updated, while the remaining parameters are kept frozen.

FedAP: Incorporating Adapter-tuning into federated learning. Adapter-tuning inserts some trainable adapter layers into the model while keeping the parameters of the original model frozen. We adopt the method proposed by Houlsby et al.houlsby2019parameter , inserting adapters after the attention and feed-forward modules. We implement the adapter module based on the adapters librarypoth-etal-2023-adapters ; pfeiffer2020AdapterHub .

FedLR: Combining LoRA with federated learning. For the FedLR and FeDeRA in the experiment, we only add the LoRA module to the query and value modules and strive to keep the experimental conditions consistent. We facilitate the implementation of LoRA with the aid of the PEFT librarypeft .

6 Conclusion

In this paper, we introduced FeDeRA, a method that applied Singular Value Decomposition to the weight matrices of pre-trained models in order to initialize adapter modules, which were similar to LoRA, using the principal components. The goal of FeDeRA was to address the significant degradation in accuracy that arose from unique data heterogeneity scenarios in federated learning when combining LoRA and federated learning. Our empirical findings demonstrated that FeDeRA not only maintained the high-efficiency training and communication of the PEFT method, but also achieved performance that was comparable to, or even surpassed, that of full-parameter fine-tuning. Furthermore, FeDeRA outperformed all other PEFT methods integrated with federated learning.

A.2 Classes of Datasets

We defined a specific number of classes for each dataset to facilitate the partition of the datasets.The following are the detailed information.

20Newsgroup. The 20Newsgroups dataset comprises around 18000 newsgroups posts on 20 topics and the topics cover a range of subjects from politics to religion to sports to science. The 20 Newsgroups dataset is employed for text classification tasks; consequently, its original labels are utilized as its classes.

SemEval2010Task8. SemEval2010-Task8 is aimed at addressing the challenge of Multi-Way Classification of Semantic Relations Between Pairs of Nominals. The task involves identifying and categorizing the semantic relations between specific pairs of nominals within given sentences. It delineates nine distinct types of semantic relations, with each relation type being subject to inversion, thereby totaling 19 potential labels when including an additional â€™Otherâ€™ category for relations that do not conform to any of the predefined types. We also utilize the original labels as classes.

WNUT2017. The WNUT2017 dataset refers to the data collection used for the Workshop on Noisy User-generated Text (WNUT) in its 2017 edition. This workshop series focuses on processing and understanding noisy user-generated text, often encountered in social media, online forums, and other web sources. WNUT2017 specifically aimed to address several key tasks in noisy text processing. One of the primary tasks was Named Entity Recognition (NER), which involved identifying and classifying proper names within text into predefined categories such as persons, organizations, locations, etc. The token labels comprise seven parts of speech, further divided into begin and intermediate positions, along with an additional category that does not belong to any of the aforementioned parts of speech, totaling thirteen labels in all. We observe that each training sample may comprise a unique set of token labels, thereby contributing to the learning capability across diverse token labels. Consequently, we catalogue the total types of token labels encompassed within each training sample as its class, as detailed in TableÂ 7.

PLONER. PLONER, the abbreviation for Person, Location, Organization Named Entity Recognition, aims to assess the cross-domain generalization capability. It involves selecting samples from representative datasets that include at least one of the three entity types: person, location, and organization. The representative datasets encompass CoNLL2003, OntoNotes 5.O, and WNUT2016. The English data of CoNLL2003 is a collection of news wire articles from the Reuters Corpus. The data for WNUT2016 were primarily collected from Twitter. The OntoNotes5.0 corpus is categorized into several types, which include newswire (News), broadcast news (BN), broadcast conversation (BC), telephone conversation (Tele), and web data (Web). The corpora across various themes inherently exhibit heterogeneity. Hence, while adopting the methodology used for class construction in WNUT2017, we also take into account the source dataset of the samples for further distinction, details are shown in TableÂ 8.

SQuADv1.1. SQuADv1.1 is a question-answering dataset released by Stanford University. The dataset comprises an extensive collection of question-and-answer pairs, with the questions formulated based on paragraphs from Wikipedia articles, and the answers are extracted directly from these respective passages. SQuADv1.1 does not annotate any tags pertinent to the questions, hence, we employ k-means to generate classes for all training samples directly. The number of clusters chosen for this study is 30.

MRQA. The MRQA dataset emphasizes the generalizability of question-answering tasks. This dataset is an amalgamation of subsets from 18 existing QA datasets, carefully curated and transformed into the format as SQuAD. Of these 18 datasets, six are designated for training, another six for development, and the final six are reserved for testing purposes. In our approach, we simply employ the source datasets as the classes.
