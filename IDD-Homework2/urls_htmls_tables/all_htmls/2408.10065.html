1 Introduction

In practice, we often need ranges of heavy evaporation residues (ERs) that produce complete fusion reactions induced by heavy ions (HIs) to assess the effectiveness of commissioning setups designed for the separation and detection of these extremely heavy ions. A gas-filled solenoid GASSOL [1] is an example of such a setup, which is scheduled to go into operation in the near future. The Mylar foil of a defined thickness is a good choice as a catcher for ERs collection in the focal plane of the solenoid. Typical energies for Rn to Th ERs produced in test reactions with medium-heavy ions (from Ar to Ni) are below 0.5 MeV/nucleon. SRIM calculations [2] show very narrow range distributions for mono-energetic Rn to Th ions of respective energies in Mylar. This implies the use of a foil of a definite thickness accompanied by some pre-stopper for unambiguous identification of the collected reaction products, such as Î±ğ›¼\alpha-radioactive ERs in particular. Obviously, the real range straggling (width of the distribution) is larger due to the evaporation of light particles from the compound nucleus formed in the chosen reaction and ERs stopping inside a target used in an experiment [3].

2.7 Stopping powers for heavy ERs

As mentioned above, typical energies for Rn to Th ERs produced in test reactions with medium-heavy ions (from Ar to Ni) are below 0.5 MeV/nucleon. In Fig.Â 9, the Mylar stopping powers calculated in the frameworks of the approximations considered in SectionsÂ 2.1 â€“ 2.6 are shown for 215Th that can be produced in fusion reactions using respective targets.

One can see from the figure that 2p-, 4p-, and Zieglerâ€™s Sâ€‹Peğ‘†subscriptğ‘ƒğ‘’SP_{e} [9] approximations show close values at energies above 10 MeV, which differ significantly from SRIM calculations. Simultaneously, the approximations [10, 15] give us significantly smaller Sâ€‹Pğ‘†ğ‘ƒSP values. As may also result from the figure, a lack of data for very heavy ions at low energies has led to very small Sâ€‹Pğ‘†ğ‘ƒSP values at energies below 10 MeV, where the nuclear (collisional) component has appeared according to the approximations [2, 9]. Note that the nuclear component plays a key role at the end of a HI range since it determines the final values of a mean range and the respective range struggling, and thus cannot be neglected. TRIM simulations (Monte Carlo version of SRIM) that take into account evaporation of particles from a compound nucleus (CN) and ERs stopping inside a target [3] might be the starting point in solving the problem.

6 Summary

A lack of data on ranges of heavy ions (HIs) in Mylar, which could be used for practical purposes, prompted the use of available stopping power (Sâ€‹Pğ‘†ğ‘ƒSP) data for HIs [4], implying their subsequent approximation and integration over energy for range estimates. The comparison of these data with the available semi-empirical approaches [2, 5, 9, 10, 15], including the one proposed in the present work, showed unsatisfactory reproduction of some Sâ€‹Pğ‘†ğ‘ƒSP data at low HI energies. Applying the semi-empirical approximations to the Sâ€‹Pğ‘†ğ‘ƒSP estimates of very heavy evaporation residues (ERs) produced in complete fusion reactions with HIs, such as 215Th, it seems that the nuclear (collisional) Sâ€‹Pnğ‘†subscriptğ‘ƒğ‘›SP_{n} was not taken into account in the most recent ones [5, 10, 15]. One more observation, to which attention can be paid, is larger values of the electronic stopping power Sâ€‹Peğ‘†subscriptğ‘ƒğ‘’SP_{e} predicted by SRIM [2] in comparison with other approximations at energies below 10 MeV. Along with that, differences in the total Sâ€‹Pğ‘†ğ‘ƒSP values obtained in the considered approaches could also be explained by a lack of low-energy data for a proper approximation. Another explanation could be smaller values of nuclear stopping than it is used in the earlier approaches [2, 9].

The integration of the inverse values of the total Sâ€‹Pğ‘†ğ‘ƒSP calculated according to [2, 5, 9] and obtained in the present work for 215Th, as an example of an evaporation residue (ER) produced in fusion reactions, was performed. The initial energy corresponding to its average value derived from TRIM simulations was used. Energy losses in the target layer and hydrogen gas were taken into account. Four different range distributions were obtained as a result. The largest value of the average range determined with the 2-parameter approximation [5] differs from the smallest one according to SRIM by a factor of 1.7. Given these differences, there may be difficulties in choosing the proper thicknesses of the pre-stopper and catcher foils, implying a total collection of ERs and providing minimal energy losses of Î±ğ›¼\alpha-particles inside the catcher to identify ERs. TRIM simulations for the 215Th Î±ğ›¼\alpha-particles emitted from the catcher showed that the distance between the catcher and detector is a crucial value, ensuring the smallest width of Î±ğ›¼\alpha-lines in detected spectra if the catcher is a remote distance from the detector.

In conclusion, it may also be noted that using a thick catcher and varying the range distribution in simulations, one can determine true ranges of 215Th ERs by comparing measured Î±ğ›¼\alpha-spectra with the simulated ones, as was done in [21]. These ranges can also be derived by measuring an integral range distribution through the variation of the thickness of a pre-stopper (degrader) foil installed in front of the catcher position, with subsequent differentiation of this distribution. The comparison of both values is of particular interest.
