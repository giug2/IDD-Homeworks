Abstract

The International Standards on Auditing require auditors to collect reasonable assurance that financial statements are free of material misstatement. At the same time, a central objective of Continuous Assurance is the ‘real-time’ assessment of digital accounting journal entries. Recently, driven by the advances in artificial intelligence, Deep Learning techniques have emerged in financial auditing to examine vast quantities of accounting data. However, learning highly adaptive audit models in decentralised and dynamic settings remains challenging. It requires the study of data distribution shifts over multiple clients and time periods. In this work, we propose a Federated Continual Learning framework enabling auditors to learn audit models from decentral clients continuously. We evaluate the framework’s ability to detect accounting anomalies in common scenarios of organizational activity. Our empirical results, using real-world datasets and combined federated-continual learning strategies, demonstrate the learned model’s ability to detect anomalies in audit settings of data distribution shifts.

1 Introduction

The International Standards in Auditing (ISA) demand auditors to collect reasonable assurance that financial statements are free from material misstatements, whether caused by error or fraud [57, 27]. The term fraud refers to ‘the abuse of one’s occupation for personal enrichment through the deliberate misuse of an organisation’s resources or assets’ [79]. According to the Association of Certified Fraud Examiners (ACFE), organisations lose 5% of their annual revenues due to fraud [1].111The ACFE study encompasses an analysis of 2,110 cases of occupational fraud surveyed between January 2020 and September 2021 in 133 countries. The ACFE highlights that respondents experienced a median loss of USD 100K in the first 7-12 months after a fraud scheme begins. Detecting fraud and abuse quickly is critical since the longer a scheme remains undetected, the more severe its financial, reputational, and organisational impact [53].

In the last decade, the ongoing digital transformation has fundamentally changed the nature, recording, and volume of audit evidence [82]. Nowadays, organizations record vast quantities of digital accounting records, referred to as Journal Entries (JEs), in Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems [21]. For the audit profession, this unprecedented exposure to large volumes of accounting records offers new opportunities to obtain audit-relevant insights [2]. Lately, accounting firms also foster the development of Deep Learning (DL) capabilities [37] to learn advanced models to audit digital journal entry data. To augment a human auditor’s capabilities DL models are applied in various audit tasks, such as accounting anomaly detection [67, 86, 49] illustrated in Fig. 1, audit sampling [64, 61] or notes analysis [70, 54]. However, learning DL-enabled models in auditing is still in its infancy, exhibiting two main limitations. First, most of today’s audit models are trained from scratch on stationary client data of the in-scope audit period, e.g., a financial quarter or year [71]. Disregarding that organizations operate in environments in which activities rapidly and dynamically evolve [77, 76, 24]. New business processes, models, or departments are constantly introduced, while current ones are redesigned or discontinued. Second, audit models are often trained centrally on data of a single client, e.g., the organization ‘in-scope’ of the audit [24]. Although large audit firms audit multiple organisations operating in the same industry [26]. Such ‘peer audit clients’ [36] are affected by similar economic and societal factors, e.g., supply-chains, market cycles, or fiscal policy [10].

In 1995 Thrun et al. proposed a machine learning setting in which a model incrementally learns from a stream of experiences [74]. The underlying idea of Continual Learning (CL) refers to a progressive model adaption; once new information becomes available [51]. Furthermore, in 2017 McMahan proposed Federated Learning (FL), a learning setting enabling distributed clients to collaboratively train models under the orchestration of a central server [45]. A key idea of FL is creating learning synergies while preserving data privacy [17]. Ultimately, the Federated Continual Learning (FCL) of highly adaptive audit models can be viewed as a central objective of Continuous Assurance [77, 76]. However, learning such models in dynamic and decentralised audit settings remains challenging due to the stability-plasticity dilemma [22]. It requires to cope with two types of data distribution shifts [20, 9, 30], as described in the following:

    A temporal distribution shift between time step t0subscript𝑡0t_{0} and t1subscript𝑡1t_{1} denotes a change in distributions pt0​(X)subscript𝑝subscript𝑡0𝑋p_{t_{0}}(X) ≠\neq pt1​(X)subscript𝑝subscript𝑡1𝑋p_{t_{1}}(X) . In this work, we investigate the practical application of FCL in financial auditing to improve the assurance of financial statements. In summary, we present the following contributions:

  • •

    We propose a novel federated continual learning framework that enables auditors to incrementally learn industry-specific models from distributed data of multiple audit clients.

  • •

    We demonstrate that the framework enables audit models to retain previously learned knowledge that is still relevant and replace obsolete knowledge with novel information.

  • •

    We conduct an extensive evaluation of scenarios of common organizational activity patterns using real-world datasets to illustrate the setting’s benefit in detecting accounting anomalies.

Ultimately, we view FCL as a promising, under-explored learning setting in contemporary auditing. 2 Related Work

The application of ML in financial audits triggered a sizable body of research by academia [2, 71] and practitioners [72, 16]. This section presents our literature study focusing on the unsupervised, federated, and continual learning of accounting data representations.

Representation Learning denotes a machine learning technique that allows systems to discover relevant data features to solve a given task [6]. Nowadays, most ML methods used in financial audits depend on ‘human’ engineered data representations [13]. Such techniques encompass, Naive Bayes classification [5], network analysis [44], univariate and multivariate attribute analysis [3], cluster analysis [73], transaction log mining [33], or business process mining [29, 80]. With the advent of Deep Learning (DL), representation learning techniques have emerged in financial audits [71, 48, 54]. Nowadays, the application of DL in auditing JEs encompasses, autoencoder neural networks [67], adversarial autoencoders [65], or variational autoencoders [86]. Lately, self-supervised learning techniques have been proposed to learn representations for multiple audit tasks [61].

Continual Learning (CL) describes a setting where a model continuously trains on a sequence of experiences [74]. The challenge of forgetting in model learning has been studied extensively [19, 15, 75, 39]. Rehearsal techniques replay samples from past experiences [58, 28, 12] to conduct knowledge distillation [55], while generative rehearsal techniques replay synthesised samples [68]. Recent methods use gradients of previous tasks to mitigate gradient interference [43, 11]. Regularisation techniques consolidate previously acquired knowledge and restrict parameter updates in model optimization. LwF [41], uses knowledge distillation to regularize parameter updates. EWC [35] directly regularises the parameters based on their previous task importance. In SI [84] regularization is applied in separate parameter optimization step. Dynamic architecture techniques prevent forgetting by parameter reuse or increase [59, 47] or. Lately, CL has been deployed in a variety of application scenarios, such as, healthcare [38], machine translation [4], and financial auditing [24].

Federated Learning (FL) enables entities to collaboratively train ML models under the orchestration of a central trusted entity under differential privacy [31, 17]. FedAvg [45] aggregates the client’s model parameters by computing a weighted average based on the number of training samples. PATE [50] aggregates knowledge transferred from a teacher model that is trained on separated data of student models. FedAdagrad, FedYogi, and FedAdam [56] use adaptive optimizer variations to improve FL convergence. FedProx [40] trains the local with a proximal term that restricts the updates to the central model. FedCurv [69] aims to minimise the model dispersity across clients by adopting a modified version of EWC [35]. Recent works [83, 78] also introduce Bayesian non-parametric aggregation policies. Nowadays, FL learning is applied in sensitive application scenarios, such as, healthcare [14], financial risk modeling [85], and financial auditing [62].

Federated Continual Learning (FCL) addresses the challenge of distributed distribution shifts over time and has only been preliminary studied [9]. Scaffold [32] applies variance reduction in the local updates to correct for a client’s concept drifts. FedWeIT [81] decompose a model into global shared and sparse task-specific parameters to prevent inter-client interference. CDA-FedAvg [9] extends FedAVG by distribution based drift detection and a long-short term memory for rehearsal. VER [52] conducts an additional server-side rehearsal based training using a VAE’s [34] embedding statistics or actual embeddings. FedDrift [30] uses local drift detection and hierarchical clustering to learn multiple global concept models. To the best of our knowledge, this work presents the first step towards the federated continual learning of highly adaptive audit models in financial auditing.

6 Conclusion

In this work, we proposed financial auditing as a novel application area of Federated Continual Learning (FCL). We demonstrated the benefits of such a learning setting by detecting accounting anomalies under different data distribution shifts usually observable in organizational activities. We believe that FCL will enable the audit profession to enhance its assurance services and thereby contributes to the integrity of financial markets. In future work, we aim to investigate (i) novel learning strategies, (ii) potential attack scenarios, and (iii) the addition of differential privacy.
