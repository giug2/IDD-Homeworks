Abstract

Existing question answering (QA) datasets are no longer challenging to most powerful Large Language Models (LLMs). Traditional QA benchmarks like TriviaQA, NaturalQuestions, ELI5 and HotpotQA mainly study “known unknowns” with clear indications of both what information is missing, and how to find it to answer the question. Hence, good performance on these benchmarks provides a false sense of security. A yet unmet need of the NLP community is a bank of non-factoid, multi-perspective questions involving a great deal of unclear information needs, i.e. “unknown uknowns”. We claim we can find such questions in search engine logs, which is surprising because most question-intent queries are indeed factoid. We present Researchy Questions , a dataset of search engine queries tediously filtered to be non-factoid, “decompositional” and multi-perspective. We show that users spend a lot of “effort” on these questions in terms of signals like clicks and session length, and that they are also challenging for GPT-4. We also show that “slow thinking” answering techniques, like decomposition into sub-questions shows benefit over answering directly. We release 1 1 1 https://huggingface.co/datasets/corbyrosset/researchy_questions ∼ similar-to \sim 100k Researchy Questions, along with the Clueweb22 URLs that were clicked.

1 Introduction

The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has ushered in a new era for the field natural language processing, with short- and long- form question-answering (QA) at the forefront of recent achievements OpenAI et al. ( 2023 ) . Historically, QA benchmarks served as the crucible for evaluating a model’s grasp of natural language understanding. However, LLMs have nearly perfected many QA datasets, particularly those involving answering short, factual questions like “What is the capital of Brunei?" . The re-emergence of chatbots and “agentic” AI assistants, which represent complex systems based on LLMs as a building block, have led to new opportunities for users to ask deeper, more nuanced questions as shown in Figure 1 .

However, the capabilities of powerful AI-assisted QA tools has outpaced the metrics needed to evaluate them. A number of traditional QA benchmarks like Natural Questions Kwiatkowski et al. ( 2019 ) , TriviaQA Joshi et al. ( 2017 ) , WebQuestions Berant et al. ( 2013 ) , SearchQA Dunn et al. ( 2017 ) have been more or less solved by modern LLMs. These datasets consist primarily of factoid questions (from search engine logs, Trivia, Jeopardy!, etc), where answers are typically found within a single sentence or paragraph that almost certainly exist in standard pre-training web corpora Zhou et al. ( 2023 ) . For instance, MS Marco Campos et al. ( 2016 ) is up to 55% factoid questions (Bolotova et al., 2022 ) . The obvious drawback to these kinds of QA datasets is that the answers can be memorized by LLMs or solved with simple pattern-matching or keyword-based search.

Multi-hop reasoning tasks including HotpotQA Yang et al. ( 2018 ) , HybridQA Chen et al. ( 2020 ) , MuSiQue Trivedi et al. ( 2022 ) aim to challenge QA systems to logically bridge information across multiple documents or paragraphs. While these datasets have made strides in increasing question complexity, the answers are still ultimately factoid, and it’s clear what sub-questions ought to be asked to recall the missing information. Furthermore, the construction of these datasets (e.g. synthetically generated from paths of wikipedia links) leads to a distribution mismatch w.r.t questions humans ask.

There exist several sources of long-form, non-factoid QA datasets like ELI5 Fan et al. ( 2019 ) , Stack Exchange, Yahoo Answers Zhang et al. ( 2016 ) , and WikiHowQA Bolotova-Baranova et al. ( 2023 ) . While answers to these questions are more involved than factoid questions, ELI5 and WikiHowQA elicit more expository answers than analytical ones. The Oxford Allsouls dataset Liu et al. ( 2023b ) contains 1k college-level essay prompts which are multi-perspective, but are designed to evaluate persuasive writing skills and do not have associated documents to ground responses. AQuaMuSe Kulkarni et al. ( 2020 ) is an excellent attempt at filtering Natural Questions (NQ) for multi-faceted queries, but their method was constrained by relying on the relatively short paragraph-length answers already present in NQ.

The rise of “LLM Agents” e.g. Wu et al. ( 2023b ) have opened the door for even deeper collaboration between users, LLMs, and tools. In response, more recent datasets have focused on completing challenging tasks in open-ended environments with tools like a web browser, file system, database, etc.

In particular, Gaia Mialon et al. ( 2023 ) tests understanding of multi-modal inputs (images and text), as well as complex reasoning across levels of difficulty to solve. AgentBench Liu et al. ( 2023c ) provides closed environments for an LLM to interact with APIs in various scenarios, including coding (interacting with file systems or databases), games/puzzles, and web browsing/shopping. While these datasets advance the field of metrics for LLM agents, they are small, consisting of only 466 and 1,091 questions respectively that were hand-curated by the authors.

The need for more challenging QA datasets also stems from some alarming trends: while there are hundreds of public LLMs, they are pretrained on only a handful existing corpora Gao et al. ( 2020 ); Raffel et al. ( 2023 ) , or distilled from one of a few teacher LLMs Peng et al. ( 2023 ) . Furthermore, much more data scraped from the internet for training is itself going to be AI-generated content, leading to an echo-chamber effect Dohmatob et al. ( 2024 ); Wu et al. ( 2023a ) . Hence, the convergent evolution Stayton ( 2015 ) of LLMs increases the risk that they will not recognize that they don’t know something , e.g. see GPT-4 and Mixtral 8x7b in Table 10 . This is especially true when LLMs act in the capacity of LLM-as-a-judge Zheng et al. ( 2023a ); Yuan et al. ( 2024 ) , or when faced with very multi-faceted/multi-perspective questions, where the consequences could be that users “miss the whole picture” or worse, are misled Zheng et al. ( 2023b ); Liu et al. ( 2023b ) . While retrieval-augmentation Lewis et al. ( 2021 ); Borgeaud et al. ( 2022 ); Guu et al. ( 2020 ) can help complement LLM agents, the risk is merely shifted to whether a sub-system retrieves the right information and uses it correctly Liu et al. ( 2023a ) .

We believe the well-studied phenomenon of “ unknown unknowns ” United States Congress et al. ( 1981 ) applies to LLM Agents in scenarios addressing complex questions requiring “slow thinking” Kahneman ( 2011 ) . Simply put, one strategy is to iteratively re-frame or decompose the problem into a set of “ known unknowns ” (which characterize most of the aforementioned QA datasets). For these sub-problems, it should be clearer what information is missing, how to find it, and once found, how the “known known” contributes to the final answer. Several techniques such as chain-of-thought question decomposition Radhakrishnan et al. ( 2023 ) and tree-of-thought Yao et al. ( 2023a ) prompting take a similar approach to plan long-horizon solutions to complex problems. However, those studies still operate over traditional QA benchmarks like HotpotQA, or over simple games like crossword puzzles. Hence, the right benchmark of questions for these advanced decomposition techniques still does not exist for open-domain web scenarios Krishna et al. ( 2021 ) .

We present Researchy Questions to study the dynamics of how LLM agents handle unclear information needs associated with very complex questions. We define a Researchy Question as a non-factoid question that expects a long-form answer (longer than a paragraph!) entailing substantial research or effort to synthesize. A Researchy Question can be instantiated as a complex search task Aula and Russell ( 2008 ) with unclear information needs that requires analyzing multiple documents or pieces of evidence. A Researchy Question does not have a single correct answer, but rather multiple perspectives allowing a dense manifold of answers over which varying criteria can determine which is better. In practice, the act of answering a Researchy Question probably involves decomposition into sub-questions that aid the retrieval of comprehensive information, reducing the risk of missing unknown unknowns. Lastly, a Researchy Question represents a genuine information need that real people asked. Figure 1 qualitatively compares other canonical QA datasets.

Researchy Questions is primarily a QA dataset to evaluate question answering systems or LLM Agents with the ultimate goal of achieving ever higher-quality answers using any tools necessary. Notwithstanding, it is also a search/retrieval dataset in the sense that finding and correctly incorporating the right evidence is a critical sub-system to meet expectations of trustworthiness and groundedness Zheng et al. ( 2023b ); Liu et al. ( 2023b ) . While we believe that question decomposition is a critical piece of solving Researchy Questions , it is unclear how to define or measure the quality of sub-questions. To aid this endeavor, we reveal what URLs end users found useful, with the hope that good sub-questions will at least lead to the information found in those clicked documents.

We release about 96K Researchy Questions consisting of real users’ queries to a commercial search engine, and additionally:

In Section 4 we verify that web users expend more effort on Researchy Questions over other queries. In Section 5 we evaluate and compare decompositional answering techniques from Radhakrishnan et al. 2 Researchy Questions Construction

Researchy Questions are real user queries from search logs. While search logs have a rich diversity of query types and intents Bolotova et al. ( 2022 ); Bu et al. ( 2010 ) , they mostly contain factoid or navigational queries, which need to be filtered.

2.1 Stage 1: Mining Search Logs

We obtained a set of query-url click pairs from a commercial search engine that were logged between July 2021 and August 2022, which maximally overlaps with the creation of the Clueweb22 snapshot of web documents Overwijk et al. ( 2022 ) . This way, we can simply indicate which Researchy Questions clicked on which documents. We began with a large sample of english, non-adult queries that had least one click. We denote these as “ General Queries ”, which were further filtered.

An important filtering criteria is frequency: we kept queries if they were issued at least 50 times in the logs. This criteria is simple but powerful: it helps denoise the dataset (reduces spelling errors), and also allows us to focus on questions that are not “one-offs”. This helps us glean insights into repeated user behaviors as they interact with the search engine.

In order to select queries which are answer-seeking intent (i.e. actual “questions” as distinct from navigational queries like “ facebook login ”, shopping intent like “ fast running shoes ”, or local intent like “ best pizza near me ”), we used a suite of rules and existing production classifiers:

To explain some of the points above: an Answer Card is a high-precision feature in a search engine where a paragraph containing the answer is shown at the top of the results page, distinct from the “ten blue links”. Because search engines are constantly being updated with new features and triggering rules, all the above statistics are aggregated and normalized over the full year period. For instance, “a lot of Ads” is determined by summing the total number of ads shown for the query over the full year and dividing by the number of times the query was issued, and then picking a threshold above which queries seemed “shopping intent”. The Ads requirement also helped catch any shopping intent queries missed by the Retail intent classifier.

We wanted to remove Coding/Technical queries because such questions are often motivated by very specific problems that are often solved by one piece of documentation after lots of clicking around, which is not the behavior we wish to target in this dataset. Health and medical questions are avoided largely because they often overlap too much with those that ought to be addressed by a licensed medical professional. Many shopping / retail queries could be construed as “researchy” e.g. “what are the best headphones”, but we avoid them in this dataset because it is hard to distinguish whether a URL was clicked due to aggressive advertising or a real information need.

After this stage of filtering, we arrived at 15.7M “ QnA Queries ” which are largely recognizable as natural language questions that deal with open-domain knowledge. This size was manageable enough to efficiently run our own bert-large scale classifiers we use in the next stage of filtering.

2.2 Stage 2: Factoid Classifier

We needed a way to distinguish which QnA Queries were factoid vs. non-factoid; we trained a binary classifier on auto-labeled data for this purpose. The training data was a uniform sample of 200k questions from the 15.7M QnA Queries. Labels for the questions were collected from gpt3 ( text-davinci-003 ) prompted with few-shot examples as shown in Figure 4 . The labels were then used to train a bert-large non-factoid question classifier, which was then inferenced on the full set of 15.7M queries. By manual inspection, a threshold was chosen above which we were satisfied that the questions were meaningfully non-factoid. The resulting 1.0M met the non-factoid threshold of 0.75 as shown on the left hand side of Figure 3 , which we denote as ” Non-factoid QnA Queries ”.

2.3 Stage 3: Decompositional Classifier

Not all the resulting non-factoid QnA queries exhibited the “decompositional” information needs. Namely, they often looked expository or “how-to” with generally one correct answer which did not have many perspectives. We trained a second classifier to score the extent to which a question requires asking sub-questions. The specific definition of “requiring sub-questions” is described in a prompt in Figure 5 given to ChatGPT ( gpt-35-turbo ) to collect labels. We used ChatGPT because we figured this was a relatively intense cognitive task. We inferenced ChatGPT on about 40k outputs from the non-factoid classifier that met the non-factoid threshold of 0.75. We used the labels to train a separate bert-large “decompositional” classifier.

Again, we chose a threshold by manual inspection to indicate which of the 1.0M Non-factoid QnA Queries were also decompositional, which happened to be 0.6 as shown on the right-hand side of Figure 3 . Out of the 1.0M queries that met the non-factoid threshold, 146k also met the decompositional threshold. These 146k became the Researchy Questions candidates before deduplication. This procedure is not perfect; in Table 6 we show examples of some non-factoid questions that did not meet the decompositional threshold.

2.5 Stage 5: Final GPT-4 Filtering

As a final quality control step after deduplication, we had all 102k questions labeled by GPT-4 for intrinsic attributes of the question like how multi-faceted it is, how reasoning-intensive it is likely to be, etc. The full set of eight attributes is defined in Figure 7 , along with histograms of those scores in Figure 6 for both Researchy Questions and Natural Questions. All eight are scored on a scale of 1-10. About 3% of 102k questions were removed based on the attributes “ ambiguous ” and “ incomplete ” which indicate defective questions too difficult to answer; some examples are shown in Table 7 . Another 2% were removed for being too “ assumptive ”, meaning the question was phrased in a presumptuous way that was likely to bias the answer as shown in Table 8 . Another 2% was removed for safety reasons as shown in Table 9 , where we deem risk of harm too high by attempting to answer the question. Not all “assumptive” questions are harmful per se. Finally, the remaining 96k queries are what we release.

3 Characterizing Researchy Questions

Across the 96k Researchy Questions (split into 90k train, 6.4k test), there are in aggregate 350k unique documents clicked, 48% of which can be found in the english subset of Clueweb22 Set B Overwijk et al. ( 2022 ) ; the rest are in Sets A or L. For each question we release, there are on average 4.9 +/- 3.5 clicked documents (See Figure 2 Right), indicating a good diversity of information needs and much higher than the average query overall. Conversely, for each document there is only 1.4 +/- 2.3 associated Researchy Questions (See Figure 2 Left) indicating good query de-duplication.

To get a sense of how intrinsically difficult Researchy Questions are compared to other datasets, we asked GPT-4 how many sub-questions or search engine queries would be necessary to ask/issue in order to fully answer each question. An example decomposition in shown in Table 2 , and the aggregate results are shown in Table 1 . Clearly, GPT-4 thinks that most factoid QA datasets (top of the table) require the fewest sub-questions to answer, whereas Researchy Questions require the most even among longer-form QA datasets.

We also compared Researchy Questions to another search-log based QA dataset – Natural Questions Kwiatkowski et al. ( 2019 ) – along the 8 quality dimensions descried in Section 2.5 , such as how reasoning- and knowledge-intense they are. The comparative histograms are shown in Figure 6 , Clearly, GPT-4 thinks that Researchy Questions require much more knowledge, reasoning, and are inherently more multi-faceted.

Table 3 shows the distribution of first words across Researchy Questions (as well as intermediate datasets used in the filtering funnel described in the next section). For comparison, MS Marco queries (also from web search logs) are much more factoid – e.g. only 1.64% of them start with “why” Bajaj et al. ( 2018 ) .

Lastly, an emergent property of Researchy Questions we observed is that some information found in the clicked URLs is very surprising, e.g. for the question “ should the death penalty be legalized ”, the fact that in the US, “the cost of enforcing the death penalty cost millions more than life imprisonment” 2 2 2 supremecourt.gov/opinions would have a large impact on the economic arguments in that answer. We define a Pivotal Fact as a piece of information that is so surprising and consequential that, once known, drastically changes how an LLM Agent would answer the question (and the answer wouldn’t be as good without it); but it wouldn’t know about it unless it asked the right sub-question to retrieve it, e.g. “ does the death penalty cost more than life imprisonment ”. Hence, a pivotal fact is the information analogue of a black swan, a critical event that is hard to predict but highly impactful Taleb ( 2008 ) ; another example is shown in the bottom of Table 2 . Although we don’t have a good way to quantify the prevalence of Pivotal Facts , we believe that Researchy Questions is the best dataset to study the dynamics of how LLM Agents search for, and respond to, these uknown unknowns.

4 Agreement with User Search Behavior

More complicated questions ought to require more effort to answer Kelly et al. ( 2015 ) . We can approximate the amount of effort users expend in terms of behavior signals like clicks and session length.

In Table 4 (Left) we show aggregate click statistics for each subset of queries. The results show that Researchy Questions , which are both non-factoid and decompositional, lead to more in-depth consumption (clicks and sat-clicks) of more diverse information (unique urls), agreeing with prior work Hassan et al. ( 2014 ) .

In Table 4 (Right) we show behavioral signals exhibited by users at the session level rather than individual click level. For instance, if a QnA-type query appeared at any time in any session in the date range, the whole session is included in the “QnA Sessions” row. The results clearly show users were twice as engaged answering non-factoid questions than sessions devoted to factoid-y intents, and six times longer than the average session overall.

5 Evaluating Answer Techniques to Researchy Questions

Since there is no one “correct” answer to a Researchy Question, we contend that they ought be evaluated in a relative, side-by-side fashion a la Alpaca-Eval syle Li et al. ( 2023 ) , with e.g. the “closed-book” answer as the reference.

Since Researchy Questions are intended to be answered by decomposing them into sub-questions, we evaluate two decompositional question answering answer techniques – chain-of-thought decomposition, and factored decomposition – against the direct answering baseline. Factored decomposition makes a separate call to an LLM for each sub-question independently, and then a final “recomposition” call to synthesize the main answer (Radhakrishnan et al., 2023 ) .

Table 5 shows the results of a side-by-side auto-eval of the three answering techniques across a range of datasets. The LLM providing the answers was GPT-4, and the LLM-as-a-judge was also GPT-4, prompted separately using the template in Figure 8 . Since the gold correct answers exist for many of the datasets we consider, the judge was asked to ascertain “Accuracy” as a binary score of whether the candidate answer was consistent with the gold. The “Score” is on a scale of 1-100 indicating the overall quality. The top half of Table 5 corresponds to short-answer datasets (where Accuracy is more important), while the bottom half is longer answer questions where the overall Score is better suited to evaluate. Out of the long-form answer datasets, Researchy Questions benefits the most from decomposition techniques.

We draw several conclusions from Table 5 . Firstly, we largely confirm the results of Radhakrishnan et al. ( 2023 ) that decompositional techniques improve the accuracy on short-form multi-hop datasets over zero-shot direct answering. Secondly, factored decomposition leads to the highest scores for long-form questions, particularly questions involving reasoning about complex processes like Wikihow and Researchy Questions . On the other hand, chain-of-thought decomposition may be better suited to deduce correct answers to more logic-intensive questions. We believe the results for Researchy Questions using factored decomposition would be much higher if we incorporated retrieved information.

6 Related Work

6.1 Human Behavior in Search Sessions

There has been some fundamental research into understanding user behavior in search sessions, from user studies Kelly et al. ( 2015 ) to large scale click log evaluations Hassan et al. ( 2014 ) . The latter attempt to ascertain signals that could indicate whether a user is “struggling” or “exploring” in their search session; we use many of the same signals. Namely, they conclude that “exploring” sessions contain more clicks because users want to locate information for multiple facets of a topic. We concur with their results, e.g. non-factoid questions involve more clicks than factoid ones. Similarly, the definition of a “complex search task” is almost congruent to that of a Researchy Question in its information seeking behaviors Aula and Russell . Other studies try to identify complex search tasks and offer recommended sub-tasks Hassan Awadallah et al. ( 2014 ); Zhang et al. ( 2021 ) . Much of the intuition on how to filter Researchy Questions stems from these and similar studies on user search behavior.

6.2 Iterative Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Many papers discuss variants of chain-of-thought Wei et al. ( 2023 ) adapted to decomposing multi-faceted questions such as query refinement prompts Amplayo et al. ( 2022 ) , Decomposed Prompting Khot et al. ( 2023 ) and ReAct Yao et al. ( 2023b ) . A step further is to marry a generative LLM with a vector-based retrieval system, e.g. Ren et al. ( 2023 ); Xiong et al. ( 2020 ); Karpukhin et al. ( 2020 ); Izacard and Grave ( 2021 ) . Such methods are instantiated in many flavors: Self-Ask Press et al. ( 2023 ) was one of the first chain-of-thought-like decomposed prompting techniques to integrate a search engine, forcing an LLM to iteratively ask and subquestions. IRCoT interleaves retrieval with chain-of-thought such that what to retrieve next depends on what was previously retrieved Trivedi et al. ( 2023 ) Iter-RetGen iteratively retrieves and generates candidate answers to use as inputs to the next phase of retrieval Shao et al. ( 2023 ) , while Beam Retrieval maintains running hypotheses of the relevant passages at every step Zhang et al. ( 2023 ) .

6.3 Agentic Question Answering

There are several “agentic” frameworks to facilitate the dynamic interaction between tools e.g. retrieval systems and LLMs-as-agents which are adept at iterative retrieval-augmented QA tasks. One is Demonstrate-Search-Predict Khattab et al. ( 2023 ) , another is AutoGen Wu et al. ( 2023b ) . Some existing Web-based Agents include WebGPT Nakano et al. ( 2022 ) which imitate how users surf the web and ask queries, others like WebAgent Gur et al. ( 2023 ) interact with the web programmatically by understanding the raw HTML.

There are also several consumer-facing products for agentic search assistants such as Bing Chat 3 3 3 https://bing.com/chat , YouPro 4 4 4 https://you.com/search (Research Mode) and SciPhi 5 5 5 https://search.sciphi.ai/research . All of these systems clearly decompose queries into sub-questions and then retrieve/crawl the necessary pages to synthesize the final outputs. An example of each addressing a Researchy Question is shown in Figures 9 , 10 , and 11 , respectively.

7 Conclusion

For some time now, many QA datasets have been saturated, and many researchers have dismissed search logs as sources of complex questions. We present Researchy Questions , a large dataset to advance the field of complex question answering over multiple documents and perspectives, targeting LLM-assisted web search agents. We detail how these complex queries are mined from search logs, and confirm they involve significantly more effort than other types of search queries. We also provide some preliminary evidence that decompositional answering techniques yield stronger results on Researchy Questions than direct answering.

By design, these questions don’t have a gold answer, so regrettably, it difficult to quantify the “headroom” of existing models, but qualitatively (e.g. Table 10 ) there seems to be substantial room for improvement. There also remains much work surrounding how to measure the quality of sub-questions, and studying how LLM agents find and interact with Pivotal Facts. We hope this dataset can help aid the development of new metrics and unlock new experiences for web users.

Limitations

One of the primary limitations of this study is that while we argue question decomposition is key to solving complex Researchy Questions , we don’t propose a good way to measure the quality of a candidate set of sub-questions. Instead, we loosely argue that good sub-questions ought to at least lead to the same set of information found in the documents users clicked on for the question. We admit these two things are not the same, but clicks are a powerful signal. We try to make up for this gap by releasing the hierarchical question/query decomposition that GPT-4 gives closed-book, but we do not evaluate whether that decomposition does indeed lead to the same set of gold documents in a real retrieval system e.g. built from the entirety of ClueWeb22.

We also admit that it would potentially be better to produce the question decomposition by “working backwards” from the documents that were clicked on – i.e. identify key information in the clicked document for a researchy question and then identify what sub-question would lead to that information being retrieved. On the other hand, one could argue that the “forward” direction is what needs to be implemented in practice. While we do not address these points in this study, others in the academic community can explore these topics more with the dataset we release.

Another limitation of this study is that Pivotal Facts are purely an observational phenomenon that we have in no way quantified. Future researchers could create a LLM-prompt to count the number of such statements in the clicked documents.

We regret that this dataset is not multi-lingual. This is due to the large amount of uncertainty and trial-and-error in curating this dataset, which meant frequent manual inspection of data. We believe the same framework can be used to construct a multi-lingual version of Researchy Questions .

Ethics Statement

We obtained approval to release this dataset after thorough IRB proceedings to ensure compliance with privacy, safety, and legal guidelines.

We would like to make a few notes: while it may appear that we tried to remove queries that seemed “controversial”, it is not our intention to act as moral or political regulators to decide if user queries are well-meaning. At the scale of web search, one will notice that users ask a lot of questions for a lot of reasons, and it is beyond the scope of our job to speculate what the motives are behind a query. It is is our job to assess whether the act of attempting to answer a question will lead to a reasonable risk of harm. Also, it is not the case that “GPT-4 is our moral authority” on the topic of safety, it is merely one of many tools we employ in a stack to ensure our internal requirements are being met.

Appendix A GPT-4-based Filtering Details

We had GPT-4 label certain attributes of each Researchy Question using the prompt given in Figure 7 . Particularly, we focus on removing questions that were too incomplete or underspecified to meaningfully answer (shown in Table 7 ), or those that are too presumptuous (shown in Table 8 ). Incomplete queries could arise when users ask queries that e.g. refer to previous queries or subjects in their session.

A.1 Additional Safety Filtering

In addition to the official offensiveness/adult query classifiers used in our commericial search engine, we also had GPT-4 label on a scale of 1-10 “ to what extent the question could be interpreted as being harmful (physically or psychologically to oneself, others, or animals), offensive, overly biased, sexually explicit, or otherwise inappropriate for e.g. someone of the age of 12 to be exposed to. ”. We found roughly 2% of queries had an unsatisfactory harmfulness score and hence were removed from the released dataset.

Table 9 shows some examples of those removed. Real people asked these questions: we acknowledge that some of these may be motivated by genuine information-seeking intent, notwithstanding, we believe there is too high a risk that the act of answering these questions may lead to harm.
