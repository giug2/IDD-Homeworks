Abstract

Recently, Vision Language Models (VLMs) have gained significant attention, exhibiting notable advancements across various tasks by leveraging extensive image-text paired data. However, prevailing VLMs often treat Visual Question Answering (VQA) as perception tasks, employing black-box models that overlook explicit modeling of relationships between different questions within the same visual scene. Moreover, the existing VQA methods that rely on Knowledge Bases (KBs) might frequently encounter biases from limited data and face challenges in relevant information indexing. Attempt to overcome these limitations, this paper introduces an explainable multi-agent collaboration framework by tapping into knowledge embedded in Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on extensive corpora. Inspired by human cognition, our framework uncovers latent information within the given question by employing three agents, i.e., Seeker, Responder, and Integrator, to perform a top-down reasoning process. The Seeker agent generates relevant issues related to the original question. The Responder agent, based on VLM, handles simple VQA tasks and provides candidate answers. The Integrator agent combines information from the Seeker agent and the Responder agent to produce the final VQA answer. Through the above collaboration mechanism, our framework explicitly constructs a multi-view knowledge base for a specific image scene, reasoning answers in a top-down processing manner. We extensively evaluate our method on diverse VQA datasets and VLMs, demonstrating its broad applicability and interpretability with comprehensive experimental results.

1 Introduction

The field of deep learning has witnessed remarkable advancements in Vision-Language Models (VLMs). By leveraging the capabilities of large pretrained visual and textual models [ 1 ] , such as the large ViT-based model [ 8 ] and Flant5 [ 6 ] , significant strides have been made in the performance of VLMs on zero-shot Visual Question Answering (VQA) tasks. However, current VLMs often exhibit limited consistency and generalizability over multiple datasets due to their limited pretraining data. Consequently, a substantial performance gap persists when compared to supervised methods.

To improve the performance of VLMs by overcoming the limitation imposed by pretraining data, numerous prior approaches have sought to augment these models with prior knowledge from external Knowledge Bases (KBs) [ 12 , 27 , 26 ] . The objective is to equip the models with enhanced capabilities to tackle the complexities and wide range of real-world images. However, these methods that rely on dataset statistics or specific KBs often struggle to provide unbiased prior information suitable for a diverse set of visual tasks, as pointed out by [ 30 ] . Moreover, these methods place considerable demands on KB indexing. Erroneous indexing, indexing to irrelevant information, and other related challenges can introduce undesirable inferences in question answering and consequently limit the performance of such approaches on VQA.

The rapid advancements in large language models (LLMs) have shown their potential to effectively model the complexities of the real world [ 34 , 35 , 3 ] . This capacity enables the acquisition of valuable insights regarding the relevance and probabilities of various real-world hypotheses. These advantages are not attainable through traditional knowledge bases. Consequently, numerous studies have leveraged the potency of LLMs as external knowledge bases. For instance, PICa [ 41 ] , PromptCap [ 17 ] , and Prophet [ 30 ] employ LLM’s knowledge by incorporating image information in the form of captions and feeding them alongside the question to prompt. Some works [ 13 , 23 ] , in another way, employ a combination of LLM’s implicit knowledge and an external knowledge base to address VQA questions. However, these approaches often under-utilize the capabilities of VLMs, particularly their strong visual power. Moreover, these implicit methods of leveraging LLM knowledge lack interpretability and robustness and are different from the human reasoning process.

In recent studies [ 33 , 38 ] , the efficacy of multi-agent collaboration in bolstering performance on intricate tasks has been substantiated. Inspired by this technique, we attempt to employ the multi-agent collaboration to combine the LLMs and VLMs for VQA. However, this is quite challenging, due to the huge linguist domain gaps between the LLMs and VLMs.

To address this challenge, we propose to explicitly utilize the associations between individual hypotheses in the real world learned by LLMs within their extensive corpus.

In this paper, we present a novel multi-agent framework, including three agents, i.e., S eeker for I ssues, R esponder, and I ntegrator ( SIRI ), which leverages the VLMs and LLMs to enhance the performance of VQA tasks. Our framework comprises three distinct agents: Responder , Seeker , and Integrator . The primary objective of our approach is to establish a collaborative relationship among these agents, thereby emulating the top-down reasoning process inherent in human cognition. More specifically, the Responder agent generates answer candidates based on the input Question-Image pair, serving as an initial step in the reasoning pipeline. The Seeker agent capitalizes on both the original question and its answer candidates to generate a set of relevant issues that exhibit a relationship with the original question. Subsequently, the Seeker agent employs the Responder agent to obtain responses for the relevant issues. By combining the answer candidates derived from the original question with the responses from the relevant issues, the Seeker agent formulates hypotheses. Throughout this process, the Seeker agent constructs a Multi-view Knowledge Base within the given image scene. Finally, the Integrator agent balances the original candidates with the generated hypotheses to produce the final answer via Multi-view Knowledge Base given by the Seeker agent. Through the coordinated efforts of the Responder , Seeker , and Integrator agents, SIRI offers a comprehensive solution that enhances the performance of VQA tasks by emulating the collaborative reasoning process observed in human cognition.

In summary, the main contributions of our work are listed as follows:

  • •

    We propose a novel multi-agent collaboration framework, i.e., SIRI. This framework can effectively capitalize on the LLM’s capacity and provide valuable explicit external knowledge to enhance the performance of VLMs on VQA.

  • •

    We construct a tailored multi-view knowledge base for the given image scene to explicitly perform probabilistic computations, which enables our SIRI framework consistent with the top-down human reasoning process and provides sufficient interpretations.

  • •

    Extensive experimental evaluations conducted on VLMs and diverse datasets validate the effectiveness and generalizability of our proposed framework.

2 Related Work

Knowledge-Based VQA: Traditional VQA models encounter difficulties in incorporating a vast amount of knowledge, resulting in sub-optimal performance for VQA tasks that require real-world knowledge. To overcome this challenge, an increasing number of researchers have focused on Knowledge-Based VQA methods [ 12 , 27 , 26 ] . Knowledge-based VQA necessitates the VQA model’s ability to leverage external knowledge bases to access information beyond the scope of the image. Some approaches employ explicit knowledge bases, such as Wikipedia [ 36 ] , ConceptNet [ 12 ] , and ASER [ 44 ] . Nonetheless, due to the limited size of these knowledge bases and the challenges in indexing relevant information, such knowledge-based VQA methods may not consistently achieve satisfactory performance on VQA tasks.

Vision-Language Models (VLMs): VLMs have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in various multi-modal tasks [ 1 , 20 , 21 , 16 ] . These models typically undergo a pretraining process on large-scale image-text datasets. Subsequently, to adapt to a specific downstream task, they are fine-tuned using datasets relevant to the task. Leveraging their extensive pretraining datasets, VLMs exhibit strong performance across different multi-modal tasks, with several achieving state-of-the-art results in zero-shot settings. Nevertheless, the increasing size of these models presents a significant challenge in terms of the computational cost of pretraining [ 7 , 2 ] . However, to effectively address complex real-world tasks, VLMs are required to possess robust common sense and knowledge. The high cost of pretraining poses a barrier to the development of more powerful VLMs. Consequently, these models do not often exhibit good performance when confronted with tasks that lie beyond the scope of their pretraining data.

Multi-Agent Frameworks: Numerous studies [ 37 ] have leveraged the diverse capabilities offered by LLMs, resulting in a significant body of work that centers around LLM-based agents. Within this context, certain approaches [ 9 , 39 ] have employed the technique of agent-based discussions to enhance problem-solving capabilities. Additionally, several studies [ 29 , 45 ] have drawn inspiration from sociological phenomena to construct multi-agent frameworks capable of emulating human behavior. These endeavors collectively underscore the potential of employing multi-agent frameworks in various domains.

LLMs for Visual Tasks: LLMs have achieved a profound impact on the field of artificial intelligence [ 34 , 35 , 3 ] . Several approaches have leveraged LLMs as controllers for visual models in visual programming. Examples include Visual Programming [ 14 ] , ViperGPT [ 31 ] , and AssistGPT [ 11 ] , which utilize LLMs in combination with Python code to address various visual tasks in a flexible method. Additionally, some works employ LLMs as knowledge bases to tackle the VQA task such as PICa [ 41 ] and PromptCap [ 17 ] etc. Other studies have focused on improving the alignment between LLMs and visual features, as seen in approaches like LLaVA [ 24 ] and BLIVA [ 16 ] etc. Recently, researchers have explored the integration of LLMs as auxiliary components for VLMs. For instance, Cola [ 4 ] utilizes LLMs to effectively coordinate multiple VLMs, capitalizing on their individual strengths and complementary capabilities. Similarly, IdealGPT [ 43 ] employs LLMs as questioners, generating sub-questions that facilitate reasoning about the main question. In an iterative fashion, LLMs serve as reasoners, assessing whether the answers to the sub-questions provide a suitable response to the main question. Woodpecker [ 42 ] , on the other hand, employs LLMs to evaluate the output of VLMs and rectify any inaccuracies in their responses. While these approaches effectively leverage the performance of VLMs through the inclusion of LLMs, they still exhibit limitations in terms of interpretability, primarily due to their black-box-like output methodology.

3 Methodology

Our SIRI framework capitalizes on the knowledge of LLMs to facilitate a human-like top-down reasoning process. The overview of our SIRI framework is presented in Fig. 2 . Specifically, the Responder agent assumes responsibility for generating responses to Question-Image pairs, presented in the form of answer candidates. The Seeker agent utilizes image information to actively seek out hypotheses that can effectively discriminate among the answer candidates. Finally, the Integrator agent combines the hypotheses provided by the Seeker agent with the answer candidates to arrive at a definitive answer for the given question.

3.1 The Responder Agent

The Responder agent is built upon a VLM to fulfill its role. It applies the VLM to handle various Question-Image pairs across different tasks. Moreover, the Responder agent is equipped to address the Captioning task by utilizing a specific prompt that is represented as a question. The generated caption can subsequently be utilized as image information for other agents within the framework.

By leveraging the capabilities of the VLM, the Responder agent generates answer candidates (referred to as A 𝐴 A in Algorithm 1 , in the form of text-probability pair). Given that the majority of answers in VQA tasks tend to be among the top-2 candidates, our framework specifically focuses on utilizing these top-2 candidates for VQA tasks. Detailed statistics regarding the utilization of the top-2 candidates can be found in supplementary material. The functionality of the Responder agent can be precisely formalized in Algorithm 1 .

3.2 The Seeker Agent

The Seeker agent plays a critical role in generating formatted hypotheses that are converted from the original question and the relevant issues( I r ​ e ​ l ​ e ​ v ​ a ​ n ​ t subscript 𝐼 𝑟 𝑒 𝑙 𝑒 𝑣 𝑎 𝑛 𝑡 I_{relevant} ) with their corresponding answer candidates. Upon receiving the caption and original candidates, the Seeker agent first generates a set of relevant issues that are conceptually related to the original question. Through the Responder, the Seeker agent obtains responses for these relevant issues. By combining the relevant issues, responses, the original question with its answer candidates, and the image’s information, the Seeker agent generates hypotheses that are likely to occur in real-world scenarios. The formalization of the Seeker agent’s operations is shown in Algorithm 2 .

Given the original candidates and the information of the image, represented as captions, relevant issues are generated by LLM, which can distinguish the original candidates. Through the utilization of the LLM, the responses from the Responder agent (in the format of answer candidates) for the relevant issues can be translated into real-world hypotheses combined with the original question and its answer candidates.

Multi-view Knowledge Base. Our SIRI framework introduces a module called the Multi-view Knowledge Base within the Seeker agent, exploiting the potential of LLMs. As illustrated in Fig. 3 , this Multi-view Knowledge Base module is constructed by organizing each hypothesis generated by the Seeker agent as a node under the scene overview. The scene overview represents the specific image’s information and serves as the root node. Since LLMs only accept text inputs, the Seeker agent utilizes the image’s caption to construct the scene overview. By incorporating this effective module that exhibits a strong relationship with both the question and the image, our SIRI framework achieves a top-down reasoning process and facilitates generating a more accurate VQA answer.

The Integrator agent reasons and determines the final answer for the given original question, leveraging the Multi-view Knowledge Base provided by the Seeker agent along with the original candidates from Responder . Subsequently, through the calculation, these probabilities are transformed into candidate probabilities (represented as C ​ P 𝐶 𝑃 CP in Algorithm 3 ), which means a new set of probabilities for original candidates. Image information for hypothesis probabilities estimating. It is important to note that the image information, such as the image context, has a profound influence on the estimation of probability. To illustrate this, consider the example where the original question is ”Is the dog a house dog?” and the hypothesis is ”A dog without a tag is a house dog.” Initially, this hypothesis may receive a low probability because it is commonly believed that a wild dog would not have a tag. However, if the image scene information reveals ”A dog sitting on a bed,” the probability estimation may be incorrect because a house dog inside a house may not necessarily wear a tag. On the other hand, a dog sitting on a bed is more likely to be a house dog. Therefore, without the image scene information, the LLMs cannot provide an accurate probability for the hypothesis.

Selection. The limited input type of the LLM restricts its access to the complete image information, resulting in certain hypotheses lacking a relationship to address the given question. Hence, it is crucial to identify hypotheses that establish a meaningful relationship with the image content. To address this challenge, we propose a selection rule aimed at discerning hypotheses that exhibit the strongest association with the original question. 4 Experiment

4.1 Datasets

To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we utilize three distinct VQA datasets: SNLI-VE [ 40 ] , ScienceQA [ 25 ] and GQA [ 19 ] . SNLI-VE is a dataset proposed for the Visual Entailment task. Each sample in SNLI-VE is accompanied by a corresponding statement, and the objective is to determine the relationship between the statement and the image. ScienceQA draws upon elementary and high school science curricula, encompassing a diverse range of multimodal multiple-choice questions on various scientific topics. Lastly, GQA is specifically tailored to address the challenges of reasoning and combinatorial complexities encountered in real-world scenarios. By integrating these diverse datasets, our objective is to conduct a comprehensive evaluation of the efficacy of our proposed method.

4.2 Implementation Details

In our implementation, we adapt our method to state-of-the-art VLMs including BLIP-2 (BLIP-2 ViT-g FlanT5XL) and LLaVA (LLaVA-v1.5-13B). As for the LLM, we invoke the official API provided by OpenAI for GPT3.5-turbo. Specifically, we set the parameter top-p to 1 and the temperature to 0.7. To better handle the response of LLM, we employ the in-context learning method to obtain responses formatted according to our design requirements.

Relevant Issues. To enhance the comprehension and generation of relevant issues related to the original question, we employ a well-designed prompt for LLM. we utilize the image captions as the contextual information for generating the relevant issues. We leverage image captions as contextual information to facilitate the generation of relevant issues. Our prompt consists of curated examples written by humans, each comprising four components: the original question, the original answer candidates, the corresponding image caption, and the relevant issues. By leveraging its commonsense reasoning capabilities, LLM comprehends the contextual information provided by the examples, enabling it to synthesize novel instances following the patterns exhibited in the human-written examples.

There are three fundamental components in the process of generating relevant issues for the original question: the original question itself, the original answer candidates, and the corresponding image caption. As an illustration, consider the following scenario: the original question is ”Can this person hear the sound of crashing waves?”, the original answer candidates are ”Yes” and ”No”, and the corresponding image caption reads ”Several people are rowing in small boats on a lake.” Subsequently, the LLM generates relevant issues that establish a relationship with the original question within this specific image context, such as ”Is there any indication of water splashing or waves in the image?” and ”Are the people wearing any ear protection or headphones?”

Hypothesis. The hypothesis is a declarative sentence that indicates the validity of an answer for the original question when the answer holds true for a relevant issue. In this step, an example input for the LLM consists of four components: the original question (”Can you turn right or left in the tunnel?”), the answer candidates for the original question (”no, yes”), the relevant issue (”Is there any signage indicating no turning?”), and the response to the relevant issue (”yes, no”). The desired output for this scenario would be as follows: (”If there is signage indicating no turning, you cannot turn in the tunnel.”, ”If there is no signage indicating no turning, you cannot turn in the tunnel.”, ”If there is signage indicating no turning, you can turn in the tunnel.”, ”If there is no signage indicating no turning, you can turn in the tunnel.”).

Hypothesis Probability. Once the hypotheses have been transformed, the next step is to determine the occurrence probability of each hypothesis in the real world. It is crucial to obtain an unbiased probability estimate from LLM. In this step, the scene information, such as the image context, also plays a significant role in influencing the probability. To illustrate this, consider the example where the original question is ”Is the dog a house dog?” and the hypothesis is ”A dog without a tag is a house dog.” Initially, this hypothesis may receive a low probability because it is commonly believed that a wild dog would not have a tag. However, if the image scene information reveals ”A dog sitting on a bed,” the probability estimation may be incorrect because a house dog inside a house may not necessarily wear a tag. On the other hand, a dog sitting on a bed is more likely to be a house dog. Therefore, without the image scene information, the LLMs cannot provide an accurate probability for the hypotheses.

Evaluation. The process of ”Evaluation” involves recalculating the probabilities of the original question answer candidates using the chosen hypothesis. A higher score for the top-ranked answer candidate indicates a greater likelihood of correctness. Evaluation is unnecessary when there is a significant discrepancy between the top two candidates. By assigning weight factors to the answer candidates and responses from the original question and relevant issues, a new set of probabilities is derived. The answer candidate with the highest probability is then selected as the final answer.

5 Discussion

Perception and Reasoning. Perception and Reasoning play integral roles in visual tasks, where a comprehensive understanding of both is essential for addressing various questions. Nevertheless, it is important to recognize that the demands for perception and reasoning may vary across different types of questions. For instance, certain questions necessitate a robust perceptual ability, where the answers heavily rely on extracting relevant information from the visual content. Conversely, other questions prioritize reasoning skills, as the image content alone may not provide a direct answer. In such cases, the model must not only comprehend the visual content but also integrate external knowledge to infer the question effectively.

Multi-view Knowledge Base. In this work, we propose the construction of a novel Multi-view Knowledge Base through the utilization of LLMs. In SIRI, the Integrator agent utilizes only one hypothesis per original question. However, it is worthwhile to explore the deeper layers of the knowledge. For instance, LLMs can generate additional relevant issues based on the existing knowledge, which can establish a more contextual relationship with the original question. By leveraging the existing relevant issues and their corresponding response, these newly generated relevant issues can offer a more nuanced hypothesis on the original question.

Limitation. The experimental results presented in Sec. 4 demonstrate that SIRI achieves superior performance on SNLI-VE and ScienceQA compared to the GQA. This performance discrepancy can be attributed to the inherent limitations of LLMs. LLMs only process textual input and lack the ability to incorporate visual perception information. As a result, the GQA, which heavily relies on visual perception for answering questions, poses a challenge for SIRI. We address this limitation by utilizing image captions as a representation of the image content, enabling LLMs to generate more contextually relevant issues. However, it is important to acknowledge that captions may sometimes be inaccurate or lack a strong association with the specific question being addressed. Consequently, the lack of direct access to image content for LLMs poses a significant limitation for SIRI.

6 Conclusion

In this paper, we presented SIRI, a novel multi-agent collaboration framework for VQA. Our SIRI leverages the knowledge of LLMs to facilitate a top-down reasoning process, mirroring human cognition. Comprising three distinct agents: Responder , Seeker , and Integrator , SIRI effectively seeks new evidence and constructs the Multi-view Knowledge Base within a specific visual scene. Experimental results demonstrate the significant performance enhancement of diverse VLMs across various VQA datasets.

7 Statistical Analysis

In the Methodology section of the submitted paper, we outlined our proposed SIRI framework. Currently, our framework exclusively utilizes the top-2 original candidates. Meanwhile, we acknowledge that the responses for issues may also be not accurate. Consequently, we conducted exploratory experimental analyses to solve these two concerns.

Upper limit of Acc of Top-2 original candidates. We conducted a statistical analysis to assess the upper limit of acc achievable by the top-2 original candidates. Specifically, we examined the accuracy when the correct answer resides within the top-2 original candidates, rather than being limited to the top-1 candidate. This statistical evaluation serves to quantify the performance ceiling of the current SIRI strategy. The obtained results are presented in Tab. 5 . Based on the statistical findings, we can deduce that despite considering only the top-2 answer candidates within the current SIRI framework, there remains substantial room for improvement.

Furthermore, it remains worthwhile to explore the inclusion of additional answer candidates within the SIRI framework. Nonetheless, it is important to acknowledge that such an extension comes with an exponential increase in associated costs, encompassing considerations related to issues, responses, and other facets.

Original candidates Top-2 difference. The concept of original candidates’ top-2 difference refers to the disparity in scores between the top-1 candidate and the top-2 candidate generated by the VLMs. Statistical results obtained from GQA and ScienceQA datasets using BLIP-2 are presented in Tab. 4 . From these findings, the following conclusion can be drawn: When the VLMs provide the correct answer (i.e., the first candidate is the correct answer), the difference between the top-2 original candidates is larger compared to cases where the VLMs fail to produce the correct answer. This observation suggests that VLMs exhibit greater confidence when generating accurate responses. Conversely, VLMs tend to display less confidence when generating incorrect answers.

In accordance with this statistical analysis, we employ the discrepancy in scores of the top-k candidates as thresholds for filtering the questions. This process enables us to determine which questions require evaluation and which question’s answer candidates can be considered reliable and trustworthy.
