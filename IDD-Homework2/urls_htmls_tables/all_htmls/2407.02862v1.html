Abstract.

The proliferation of Knowledge Graphs (KGs) that support a wide variety of applications, like entity search, question answering and recommender systems, has led to the need for identifying overlapping information among different KGs. Entity Alignment (EA) is the problem of detecting such overlapping information among KGs that refer to the same real-world entities. Recent works have shown a great potential in exploiting KG embeddings for the task of EA, with most works focusing on the structural representation of entities (i.e., entity neighborhoods) in a KG and some works also exploiting the available factual information of entities (e.g., their names and associated literal values). However, real-word KGs exhibit high levels of structural and semantic heterogeneity, making EA a challenging task in which most existing methods struggle to achieve good results. In this work, we propose HybEA, an open-source EA method that focuses on both structure and facts, using two separate attention-based models. Our experimental results show that HybEA outperforms state-of-the-art methods by at least 5% and as much as 20+% (with an average difference of 11+%) Hits@1, in 5 widely used benchmark datasets.

1. Introduction

Knowledge graphs (KGs) are becoming ubiquitous in a vast variety of domains, supporting applications like question answeringÂ (Ahmetaj etÂ al., 2021), entity searchÂ (Dong etÂ al., 2014), and recommendation systemsÂ (Tarus etÂ al., 2018). Typically, KGs store descriptions of real-world entities (e.g., people, movies, books) that capture both structural and factual information, in the form of head-relation-tail (aka subject-predicate-object) triples. A crucial task when integrating knowledge from several KGs is to detect which common entities are described by different KGs. Entity alignment (EA)Â (Fanourakis etÂ al., 2023b; Sun etÂ al., 2020; Suchanek etÂ al., 2011), also known as entity matchingÂ (Li etÂ al., 2020) or entity resolutionÂ (Christophides etÂ al., 2015, 2021), aims to identify pairs of entities from different KGs that refer to the same real-world object, which we call matching pairs or simply, matches.

Several KG embedding methods have been proposed for EA Â (Fanourakis etÂ al., 2023b; Sun etÂ al., 2020; Zeng etÂ al., 2021; Choudhary etÂ al., 2021; Wang etÂ al., 2017; Zhang etÂ al., 2020; Zhao etÂ al., 2022; Jiang etÂ al., 2021; Wang etÂ al., 2021; Leone etÂ al., 2022; Zhang etÂ al., 2022; Chaurasiya etÂ al., 2022), as they can potentially mitigate the symbolic, linguistic and schematic heterogeneity of independently created KGs. The idea is to represent the nodes (entities) and edges (relations or attributes) of KGs in a low-dimensional vector space, in a way that similar entities in the original KG are placed close to each other in the embedding space and dissimilar entities are far from each other. In this respect, they leverage the structural (i.e., entity neighborhood) and the factual (i.e., entity names/identities, attributes that represent literals) information of entity descriptions.

One of the major challenges of the EA task is to cope with the heterogeneity of independently created KGs. Each KG may describe entities in a different way, leading to entity descriptions that may be redundant, incomplete and sometimes contradictoryÂ (Christophides etÂ al., 2015). As a result, an EA task has to face two main kinds of heterogeneity that can take multiple forms:

(i) Semantic heterogeneity: The factual and structural information of entities may semantically differ from one KG to another. First, different KGs may employ different attribute types, and even the same attribute may have diverse data structures and value granularities. Second, the names of two aligned entities may largely differ. This is particularly common in the case of multi-lingual datasets, but mono-lingual datasets may also suffer from this issue. Embedding methods are exceptionally good at handling such semantic heterogeneity, as they can successfully cope with naming variations, synonyms, typos etc.

(ii) Structural heterogeneity: Complementary KGs often have non-identical neighborhood structures for common entities, due to the potentially incomplete descriptions of entities in different KGs, or the different focus of each KG (e.g., a KG about the musical culture of countries and a KG about sports played in different countries are expected to have very different content describing the same countries); thus leading to finding matches between non-isomorphic subgraphs. Moreover, not all relations are equally important for deciding whether two entities are matching or notÂ (Suchanek etÂ al., 2011). Structural heterogeneity cannot be effectively tackled by attention mechanisms solely on the relational structure of entities, but one has to also consider their factual information. This is particularly the case with mono-lingual datasets, which are generally obtained from different KGs, but this might also appear in multi-lingual datasets, typically obtained from the same sources, but with a different amount/focus of information.

Most existing embedding models for EA focus only on one of the two heterogeneity kinds described above, with the underlying assumption that semantic heterogeneity would be inherent to multi-lingual datasets, while structural heterogeneity would mostly appear in mono-lingual datasets. In this work, we are interested in simultaneously dealing with both kinds of heterogeneity, as real-world datasets are semantically and structurally diverse eventually at different degrees. Thus, it would be beneficial to design an EA method that addresses them in a flexible way according to the dataset characteristics.

In this paper, we propose a hybrid approach, calledÂ HybEA, to tackle the heterogeneity challenges using two separate components. The first exploits factual information and handles semantic heterogeneity at the level of the attributes describing entities. The other exploits the neighborhood structure of entities and handles structural heterogeneity at the level of relations connecting entities. Both models rely on attention methods, and in particular Transformers. On one side, our factual component uses a novel attention-based model to weigh the contribution of each attribute in the prediction of matching entity pairs. On the other side, the structural component uses self-attention in order to capture and represent the interaction among tokens in the triples. Each component model finally predicts its own entity alignments. For this reason, we employ a semi-supervised architecture, where at every iteration, some high-confidence matching pairs detected from each model are added to the training set used for the subsequent training of both models.

We experimentally show that our hybrid approach outperforms state-of-the-art baseline methods, such as BERT-INTÂ (Tang etÂ al., 2020), COTSAEÂ (Yang etÂ al., 2020), RREAÂ (Mao etÂ al., 2020), PipEAÂ (Wang etÂ al., 2024) and KnowformerÂ (Li etÂ al., 2023), in very challenging datasets (e.g., DBpedia-Wikidata) by as far as 20% in Hits@1, while it consistently outperforms the baseline methods in all datasets and in all measures, achieving 90+% scores.

2.2. HybEAÂ Overview

FigureÂ 1 presents the high-level architecture of HybEA, involving a factual component and a structural component. The factual component, built on an attention mechanism, is responsible for gathering matching evidence from attribute triples. The structural component, based on transformers, is responsible for gathering matching evidence originating from relation triples.

Our framework is semi-supervised; it runs in cycles, with each cycle involving the (re-)training of the factual component once and the (re-)training of the structural component once. Each component identifies a set of new matching pairs which are added into the training set and used for training the next component. The execution stops when no new matching pairs are identified by at least one component at the end of a cycle.

The new matching pairs, regardless of the component from which they originate, are considered as high-confidence pairs, so they are also part of our frameworkâ€™s returned matches. 2.3. Entity Initialization

Most embedding-based EA method randomly initialize the entity embeddings and later update them at each iteration, based on facts and relations. In HybEA, we exploit the valuable information offered by some of the attributes that can be used as entity names/labels (e.g., rdfs:label, foaf:name), when available. Typically, there are only a handful of attributes per KG that play this role and it is quite easy to detect them based on statistics (e.g., selectivity and coverageÂ (Efthymiou etÂ al., 2019)) or even manually. For those entities that are accompanied with such attributes, we use the pre-trained Sentence-BERTÂ (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019) embeddings of those attribute values as the initial entity embedding. For the rest, we use random initialization.

All experiments were performed on a server with 16 AMD EPYC 7232P @ 3.1 GHz cores, 64 GB RAM, one RTX-4090 GPU (24 GB) and Ubuntu 18.04.5 LTS.

3.4. Evaluation measures

Traditionally, entity matching methods have been evaluated using classification metrics, like precision and recall, comparing the predicted matches to the ground-truth matches. On the contrary, KG embedding-based EA methods, adopted rank-based evaluation metrics (e.g., hits at kğ‘˜kitalic_k). This is probably the result of two strong underlying assumptions that almost never hold in real-world datasets: the isomorphism assumption that we mentioned in the introduction, as well as the one-to-one assumption, considering that every entity in one KG has an aligned entity in the other KG. Nonetheless, for evaluating our method on an equal basis with existing methods, and in order to re-use the available open-source code as much as possible, we adopt the latter evaluation metrics, but we acknowledge that the criticism ofÂ (Leone etÂ al., 2022) is valid.

The measures used for the effectiveness evaluation are the standard ones used in the literature, i.e., hits at kğ‘˜kitalic_k (H@kğ‘˜kitalic_k) and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR). Furthermore, we also evaluate the effectiveness of the reciprocity filter alone, which is used for enriching the training set. As this filter returns final matching decisions, and not rankings of pairs, we evaluate its effectiveness using the standard classification measures, i.e., precision, recall, and F1-score, cumulatively, across all cycles, per dataset and per component (structural and factual). For example, assuming two execution cycles and a test set of 100 matching pairs, if the structural component returns 10 matches in the first cycle and 5 matches in the second cycle, all of which are correct, its cumulative precision is 100% and its cumulative recall is 15%.

For evaluating the efficiency of our approach, apart from time per training epoch, we also use FLOPS to assess the computational complexity of HybEAÂ on the D-WÂ (S)Â dataset. FLOPS serves as a basic unit of computation, which could denote one addition, subtraction, multiplication or division of floating point numbers. The FLOPS count is a rough measure of how expensive a model can be, regardless of the hardware architecture on which is deployed (i.e., GPUs vs CPUs). We use the Flop Counter tool for PyTorch Models from the fvcore555https://github.com/facebookresearch/fvcore/blob/main/docs/flop_count.md library to compute the FLOPS of our models that are developed in PyTorch.

4. Related Work

Entity alignment is a long-standing problem in computer science, appearing over the decades with different names and forms, from entity resolution and record linkageÂ (Christen, 2012; Bhattacharya and Getoor, 2007; Fellegi and Sunter, 1969; HernÃ¡ndez and Stolfo, 1995; Dong etÂ al., 2005; Benjelloun etÂ al., 2009) in the traditional database setting, to ontology and instance matching, knowledge graph alignment, entity matching and entity alignmentÂ (Fanourakis etÂ al., 2023b; Sun etÂ al., 2020; Li etÂ al., 2020; Suchanek etÂ al., 2011), more recently.

Despite the many names and settings, most related works can be broadly classified with respect to their focus on the entity structuring (e.g., entity relationships through foreign keys, dependency graphs, or relations/edges in KGs) or on the factual information of entities (e.g., their literal values). In the sequel we will position HybEAÂ in the context of these two general families of EA systems. Our method falls under the second family of approaches.

Structure-based approaches. Structural information is important for the EA task. Intuitively, if we know (or if we learn during the EA process) that two candidate matches have many adjacent nodes (aka neighbors) that are matching, even better, connected through semantically similar edges, then this is strong evidence supporting that those candidates are also matches; especially if we know that those edges denote relations that are functional (or close to functionalÂ (Suchanek etÂ al., 2011)).

KnowformerÂ (Li etÂ al., 2023) exploits the expressive power of Transformers and position encoding of relation triples to tackle the structural heterogeneity of aligned KGs. However, Transformers are permutation invariant and thus do not consider the direction of a relation, which is essential in KGs embeddings. They are thus extended with a position-aware structural component called relational composition, that integrates translation models at the level of relation triples. Knowformer can be used to both link prediction and EA tasks, due to its entity masking approach. In essence, if a masked entity has a counterpart in the seed entity alignment, Knowformer will predict not only the masked entity, but also its counterpart from the other KG, modifying the input triples. For these reasons, Knowformer is a good candidate for implemeting the structural component of HybEA. However, unlike the original random initialization of entity embeddings, HybEArelies on pre-trained Sentence-BERTÂ (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019) for initializing entity embeddings, and cross-domain similarity local scaling (CSLS)Â (Lample etÂ al., 2018) for normalizing their similarity score based on the density of entity neighbors in the embedding space.

PipEAÂ (Wang etÂ al., 2024) introduce a weakly supervised setting with aggregated-based EA methods. In this case, some entities that are too far away from labeled data in the graph might never receive information from those labeled data due to the specific behavior of GNNs. To tackle this challenge, PipEA proposes an extension module that can be built on top of different GNN architectures. Firstly, the GNN propagation strategy is extended in order to allow the information to also pass from one graph to the other, according to a node similarity measure. By iterating this propagation step, PipEA then computes a similarity measure that represents the similarity between subgraphs of both KGs. The key idea of these extensions thus rely on the fact that aligned entities belong to isomorphic subgraphs, which is true in many cases (e.g., multi-lingual KGs). However, this assumption does not hold in KGs exhibiting significant structural heterogeneity (such as DBpedia and Wikidata) on which HybEAÂ is focusing.

On the other hand, RePSÂ (Surisetty etÂ al., 2022) tackles directly the structural heterogeneity of aligned KGs. A Relation-aware Position Aggregator considers position information of nodes as the shortest path to a set of anchors. With the intuition that rare relations are more informative than frequent ones, each relation is weighted by its frequency in the path computation. This information is then added to the node representation under the form of positional encoding. Similarly to PipEA, entity attributes are not exploited by RePS.

To overcome the poor performance of other KG embedding models, RREAÂ (Mao etÂ al., 2020) constraints the transformation matrix of those methods to be orthogonal and hence preserve shape similarity. As such constraint is more easily applicable to GNNs, RREA combines translation-based with GNN-based methods for EA. More precisely, it integrates GCNs and GATs with a Relational Reflection Transformation, to obtain relation-specific embeddings of KG entities with the orthogonal constraint. RREA proposes also a semi-supervised setting that, at each iteration, adds to the training set every entity pair (ei,ejsubscriptğ‘’ğ‘–subscriptğ‘’ğ‘—e_{i},e_{j}italic_e start_POSTSUBSCRIPT italic_i end_POSTSUBSCRIPT , italic_e start_POSTSUBSCRIPT italic_j end_POSTSUBSCRIPT) for which eisubscriptğ‘’ğ‘–e_{i}italic_e start_POSTSUBSCRIPT italic_i end_POSTSUBSCRIPT and ejsubscriptğ‘’ğ‘—e_{j}italic_e start_POSTSUBSCRIPT italic_j end_POSTSUBSCRIPT are mutually nearest neighbors. RDGCNÂ (Wu etÂ al., 2019) captures structural information in entity embeddings by the means of a dual GCNs. Given two KGs, RDGCN builds a primal graph, whose nodes represent entities in the input KGs, and its dual graph, whose nodes represent relations in the input KGs. The primal and the dual graph interact during the training through a graph attention mechanism. In contrast to most EA methods based on random initialization, RDGCN initializes node embeddings using name embeddings. Despite the expressive power of GCNs, our previous experimental evaluationÂ (Fanourakis etÂ al., 2023b) demonstrated the RDGCN limitations in aligning structurally heterogeneous KGs.

More generally, regardless the sophistication of entity embeddings generated by structure-based approaches, exploiting only the information of neighborhood entities is not sufficient to effectively align structurally and semantically heterogeneous KGs. For this reason HybEAÂ tackles those problems by exploiting both structural and factual information, when available.

Joint approaches. To overcome structural bias, joint methods that also consider factual information, in addition to structural information, appeared, with impressive results.

Recent empirical studiesÂ (Fanourakis etÂ al., 2023b) demonstrated the effectiveness of BERT-INTÂ (Tang etÂ al., 2020) in aligning semantically heterogeneous KGs. Its strength lies in the use of BERTÂ (Devlin etÂ al., 2019) to embed entities based on their factual information (e.g., descriptions, names, literals) along with an interaction model capturing their neighborhood structure. However, factual information is not always available, resulting in poor performance in such KGsÂ (Fanourakis etÂ al., 2023b).

MultiKEÂ (Zhang etÂ al., 2019) first constructs the embeddings of every literal value and then, like BERT-INT, it creates entity embeddings considering three different views: the name view, the relation view and the attribute view. MultiKE utilizes the attribute names across the two KGs, requiring a homogeneous naming scheme; it aligns attributes and relations based on their names and semantics. The three views are jointly learned using a common loss function that weighs all three of them equally. Although entity relations are taken into account, the graph structure of entities is not globally considered.

KDCoEÂ (Chen etÂ al., 2018) is a semi-supervised, joint approach that leverages a weakly aligned (multi-lingual) KG. It iteratively co-trains two embedding models, one on the structural part of entities (practically, MTransEÂ (Chen etÂ al., 2017)) and another on the (long) textual descriptions of the entities (a self-attention GRU), respectively, given a small seed alignment. During each iteration, each embedding model proposes a new set of aligned entity pairs alternately that enriches the seed alignment. A semi-supervised approach iteratively proposes new matching pairs, using a similarity threshold-based strategy. The process runs until one of the models has no entity pairs to propose. As KDCoE structural component relies on MTransE, only the entity relations are taken into account without a full knowledge of the neighborhood graph structure.

Similar to KDCoE, COTSAEÂ (Yang etÂ al., 2020) co-trains a structure model (TransEÂ (Bordes etÂ al., 2013)) and a factual model (character-level Bi-GRU). However, instead of considering the text description of each entity, which is not available on many datasets, they only consider the name entity. Again, at each iteration, the models alternately take turns of the train-and-propose process. The co-training stops when one of the models has no new pairs to propose. Candidate alignments are represented in the form of a bipartite graph, by adding an edge to each potential pair of entities, computing entity similarities and filtering them by a threshold. The best bipartite graph matching algorithm is used to find the global optimum result of each iteration. In contrast to COTSAE and KDCoE, HybEAÂ exploits a full knowledge of the entity graph (not just relation triples exploited by TransE), while factual information is considered at sentence- and not at character-level, offering a higher-level comparison of the semantics provided by literal values.

We claim that hybrid approaches considering both the factual and structural information of entities are essential in order to cope with semantically and structurally heterogeneous KGs. In this respect, the HybEAÂ high-level architecture is inspired by KDCoE and COTSAE. However, as the current design choices implemented in these methods are not proven to excel when aligning highly heterogeneous KGs, HybEAÂ considers alternative solutions for the structural and factual components interacting when aligning entities. More specifically, in our experiments, we choose Knowformer to implement the structural component for multiple reasons. First, it has the ability to incorporate most translation models, which make it suited for many different datasets. Second, side information such as entity names can easily be integrated to the model. Last but not least, we found it was easier to reproduce and use the code of Knowformer than most of the other structural methods. We show that our novel attention-based factual component and Knowformer can effectively complement each other in discovering matching pairs that no other baseline method can.

Some very recent works have taken an interest in foundation models in order to solve the Entity Alignment problemÂ (Chen etÂ al., 2024; Yang etÂ al., 2024; Narayan etÂ al., 2022; Jiang etÂ al., 2024). For example, LLM4EAÂ (Chen etÂ al., 2024) aims at reducing the quantity of labeled data required for training, by proposing an unsupervised model that uses LLM to label entity pairs. It relies on active selection to detect crucial entities in the source KG that will help the global decision. An LLM is finally used to propose a set of potentially aligned entities with the crucial entities selected. Among this set of potentially aligned entities, some are excluded with the help of a label refiner, by considering the structure of each proposed entity. Unlike HybEA, LLM4EA trains on the selected entities an off-the-self EA model. Enabling unsupervised, or self-supervised EA tasks is orthogonal to our semi-supervised model.

Last but not least, joint approaches have presented robustness to the structural bias problem, compared to structure-based approachesÂ (Fanourakis etÂ al., 2023a). Yet, they still suffer from poor performance in several datasets that are closer to real-world data, than synthetically generated data, exhibiting high heterogeneity in both structural and factual levels. Compared to those approaches, our hybrid attention-based approach shows better robustness to those heterogeneous datasets, by taking the best of both worlds.

5. Conclusion

In this paper, we have introduced HybEA, an open-source, hybrid EA method that relies on two attention-based models to cope with datasets of high heterogeneity, with one model capturing structural evidence of alignment and the other model capturing factual evidence. We have shown experimentally that HybEAÂ systematically outperforms state-of-the-art baseline methods, including BERT-INT, Knowformer and RREA, by as far as 20% Hits@1.

Our choice of two separate models for tackling semantic and structural heterogeneity of KGs can also be considered as an important step towards the need to explain matching decisionsÂ (Teofili etÂ al., 2022b, a; Pradhan etÂ al., 2022), for two reasons: (i) coarse-grained: it makes it easy to see the provenance of the returned matches (i.e., matches returned by the structural component vs matches returned by the factual component). (ii) fine-grained: the factual model could report the most significant attributes of aligned entities, while the structural model the most significant relations of aligned entities.

We are currently working on improvements on the structural component to further exploit the graph-structure of KGs rather than relational triples, as well as on experimenting with additional benchmark datasets exhibiting various degrees of semantic and structural heterogeneity. This could allow to reduce the energy consumption of our model, and thus to consider some kind of trade-off between explainability and energy consumption.
