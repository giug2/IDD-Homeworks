Abstract

We propose a novel contrastive learning framework to effectively address the challenges of data heterogeneity in federated learning. We first analyze the inconsistency of gradient updates across clients during local training and establish its dependence on the distribution of feature representations, leading to the derivation of the supervised contrastive learning (SCL) objective to mitigate local deviations. In addition, we show that a naïve adoption of SCL in federated learning leads to representation collapse, resulting in slow convergence and limited performance gains. To address this issue, we introduce a relaxed contrastive learning loss that imposes a divergence penalty on excessively similar sample pairs within each class. This strategy prevents collapsed representations and enhances feature transferability, facilitating collaborative training and leading to significant performance improvements. Our framework outperforms all existing federated learning approaches by huge margins on the standard benchmarks through extensive experimental results. We plan to release the source code of our work for better reproducibility.

1 Introduction

Federated learning (FL) trains a shared model through the collaboration of distributed clients while safeguarding the privacy of local data by restricting their sharing and transfer. The primary challenge in this learning framework arises from the data heterogeneity across clients and the class imbalance in local data. These problems eventually lead to severe misalignments of the local optima of the client models, hindering the search for better global optima of the aggregated model and slowing down the convergence.

In addressing these challenges, most existing approaches focus on minimizing the discrepancy between the global and local models by incorporating regularization techniques on either model parameters [21, 12, 2, 44, 1] or feature representations [19, 23, 18, 40, 15], aiming to balance between local training and regularization. However, aligning the local models with the global model entails a trade-off as the global model is not necessarily optimal. Recently, there have been several attempts to analyze the inconsistent local training in a principled way [42, 33]. For example, Zhang et al. [42] investigate the label distribution skewness from a statistical perspective, and introduce a deviation bound for analyzing the inconsistency of gradient updates in local training.

We reformulate the deviation bound proposed in [42] and establish its dependence on the distribution of feature representations. Subsequently, we derive that incorporating the supervised contrastive learning (SCL) objective enhances this bound, resulting in consistent local updates across heterogeneous clients. In other words, we show that employing SCL facilitates federated learning by alleviating the variations of local models.

Although SCL is helpful for the optimization in federated learning, the empirical results show that a naïve integration of SCL suffers from slow convergence and limited performance gains as shown in Figure 1. Due to the limited and imbalanced data in the local training set, the intra-class attraction force in SCL hampers feature diversity and consequently weakens the transferability of neural networks to diverse tasks. Considering FL’s nature of ongoing updates through the aggregation of heterogeneous local models, the lack of transferability impedes the collaborative training process.

To tackle this feature collapse issue and enhance the transferability of models, we present a novel contrastive learning technique for federated learning. Our approach imposes the penalty on the sample pairs within the same class that exhibit excessively high similarity. Such a simple adaptive repulsion strategy effectively prevents the intra- and inter-class collapse of representations, enhancing the transferability across heterogeneous clients and leading to the discovery of better global optima. Furthermore, we expand the proposed approach to cover all intermediate levels of representations, promoting consistent local updates even further. In spite of its simplicity, the proposed approach demonstrates remarkable performance improvements in all datasets and settings consistently, surpassing existing baselines by substantial margins. We show the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method by thorough empirical analysis. Our main contributions are summarized as follows.

  • ∙∙\bullet

    Based on our deviation bound, we theoretically analyze that supervised contrastive learning mitigates inconsistent local updates across heterogeneous clients.

  • ∙∙\bullet

    We discover the feature collapse phenomenon caused by SCL in federated learning, resulting in slow convergence and limited performance improvement.

  • ∙∙\bullet

    We propose a relaxed supervised contrastive loss, which adaptively imposes the divergence penalty on pairs of samples in the same class and prevents the learning of non-distinguishable representations of the samples.

  • ∙∙\bullet

    We demonstrate that our approach significantly outperforms existing federated learning algorithms on the standard benchmarks under various settings.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. We review the prior works in Section 2 and discuss the preliminaries in Section 3. Section 4 presents our approach for federated learning and Section 5 validates its effectiveness. We conclude our paper in Section 6.

2 Related Works

Federated learning

McMahan et al. [22] proposed FedAvg, a pioneer FL framework that adopts a simple averaging strategy among the locally trained models and uses local SGD steps to alleviate the communication bottleneck. However, it suffers from slow convergence and performance degradation due to the heterogeneous nature of client data in practical use-cases [45]. To address the issue of heterogeneity in FL, numerous approaches have been proposed with two distinct focuses, local training and global aggregation. The major approaches in local training are imposing regularization constraints on model parameters or feature representations. Specifically, they incorporate proximal terms [21], control variates [12, 20], or leverage primal-dual analysis [44, 1] to regularize the weight parameters, while knowledge distillation [18, 40, 15], metric learning [46, 19, 23], logit calibration [42], feature decorrelation [33], or data augmentation [41, 37] are employed for effective representation learning. Our framework also belongs to representation learning, but it particularly focuses on gradient deviations in local training and transferability of trained models across heterogeneous local clients.

Besides the local training methods, server-side optimization techniques have been explored to expedite convergence using momentum [9, 27, 14] or decrease the communication cost by quantization [28, 35, 6, 24]. These server-side works are orthogonal to our client-side approach and integrate well into our framework.

Contrastive learning in FL

Recent works have explored the integration of contrastive learning techniques [25, 3, 7] into federated learning to prevent local client drift and assist local training. FedEMA [46] adopts self-supervised contrastive learning to deal with unlabeled data collected from edge devices. MOON [19] introduces a model-contrastive loss, which aims to align the current local model with the global model while deviating it from the previous local model. FedProc [23] employs a contrastive loss to align local features with the global prototypes to reduce the representation gap, where the global class prototypes are distributed from the server. FedBR [5] conducts contrastive learning to align local and global feature spaces using local and globally shared pseudo-data to reduce local learning bias. In contrast to prior approaches, our framework does not require additional communication overhead for contrastive learning and not rely on global models or prototypes to mitigate the deviations in local training.

4.2 Representation collapse in FL with SCL

Based on our analysis in Section 4.1, we empirically validate the effectiveness of SCL in federated learning under data heterogeneity configurations with Dirichlet parameter α∈{0.05,0.3}𝛼0.050.3\alpha\in\{0.05,0.3\} in Figure 2. We train the ResNet-18 model using the loss function ℒ=ℒCE+ℒSCLℒsubscriptℒCEsubscriptℒSCL\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}+\mathcal{L}_{\text{SCL}} at each local client on the CIFAR-100 dataset, using 5% participation rate over 100 distributed clients, where ℒCEsubscriptℒCE\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} represents the cross-entropy loss. As depicted in the figure, while SCL ultimately results in performance improvement by reducing local deviations over the baseline methods, it is accompanied by a noticeable lag in the convergence speed during the early stage of training. We conjecture that, due to limited and skewed local training data, SCL leads to excessively compact representations of the examples in the same classes, hindering effective task transfer across clients in federated learning.

To delve into these phenomena, we first compute the within-class and between-class covariance matrices for the feature embeddings provided by a local model, denoted by 𝚺Wsubscript𝚺𝑊\mathbf{\Sigma}_{W} and 𝚺Bsubscript𝚺𝐵\mathbf{\Sigma}_{B}, respectively. Figure 2 and 2 plot the trace of the two matrices. SCL effectively reduces the within-class variance compared to the baseline model based only on the cross-entropy loss, due to its attraction term between samples from the same class. However, it is noteworthy that SCL also yields a lower between-class variance than the baseline, especially at the early stages of training, despite the repulsion term between the examples in different classes. Since the attraction and repulsion forces interact in contrastive learning, the excessive representation similarity between positive pairs weakens the repulsion force between negative pairs. In other words, the collapse of intra-class representations negatively affects the separation of inter-class examples, leading to an overall reduction in the diversity of the feature representations. To evaluate this feature collapse quantitatively, we observe the effective rank [30] of the covariance matrix of all feature embeddings given by a local model, which measures the effective dimensionality of the learned manifold of training data. 4.3 Relaxed contrastive learning for FL

4.4 Multi-level contrastive training

Existing contrastive learning approaches [3, 25, 13] concentrate on aligning the feature representations of the last layer, resulting in predominant model updates in deeper layers while having limited influence on lower-layer parameters. As the parameters are aggregated at all levels of model architectures from heterogeneous clients, this can impede effective model aggregation, particularly in the early layers.

4.5 Discussion

FedRCL shares some similarities with existing methods in its use of contrastive loss for local updates, but it has clear differences and advantages over them. While most existing works [19, 23, 5] employ contrastive learning to regulate local training towards the global model for consistent local updates, this constraint often leads to suboptimal solutions as the global model is not fully optimized. Our approach is free from this issue, because FedRCL mitigates local deviations by itself so it does not need to align with the global model explicitly. Moreover, some approaches require additional proxies for contrastive learning such as global prototypes [23] or globally shared data [5], necessitating extra communication overhead and full client participation. In contrast, FedRCL does not require any additional overhead and consistently demonstrates strong performance improvement with an extremely low participation rate. Note that transferring such prototypes or data is even vulnerable to privacy concerns as well.

5 Experiment

5.1 Experimental setup

Datasets and baselines

We employ three standard benchmarks: CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 [16], and Tiny-ImageNet [17], covering various levels of data heterogeneity and participation rates. We generate i.i.d. data splits by randomly assigning training examples to each client without replacement. For non-i.i.d. splits, we simulate data heterogeneity by sampling label ratios from a Dirichlet distribution with the symmetric parameter α∈𝛼absent\alpha\in {0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.6} following [9]. If not specified, we employ a 5% participation rate over 100 distributed clients. Consistent with existing literature, each client holds an equal number of examples. For evaluation, we use the complete test sets for each dataset and measure the accuracy achieved at 500thth{}^{\text{th}} and 1000thth{}^{\text{th}} rounds. We compare our method, dubbed as FedRCL, with plenty of state-of-the-art federated learning techniques, which include FedAvg [22], FedAvg + FitNet [29], FedProx [21], MOON [19], FedMLB [15], FedLC [42], FedNTD [18], FedProc [23], and FedDecorr [33].

Implementation details

Our experiments employ a ResNet-18 as the backbone network without pretraining, and replace the batch normalization with the group normalization technique [36] as in [8]. We use the SGD optimizer with a learning rate of 0.1, an exponential decay parameter of 0.998, a weight decay of 0.001, and no momentum, following prior works [1, 39, 15]. We set the number of local training epochs to 5 and adjust the batch size to ensure a total of 10 local update iterations at each local epoch across all experiments. For contrastive learning, we extract the outputs of conv1, conv2_x, conv3_x, conv4_x, and conv5_x layers. We set λ𝜆\lambda to 0.7, β𝛽\beta to 1, and τ𝜏\tau to 0.05 for all experiments if not specified. All our experiments are implemented using the PyTorch framework [26] and executed on the NVIDIA A5000 GPU. Please refer to the supplementary document for further implementation details.

5.2 Results

We compare the proposed method, FedRCL, with numerous client-side federated learning baselines [22, 29, 21, 19, 15, 18, 23, 33, 42] on the CIFAR and Tiny-ImageNet datasets. Table 1 demonstrates that our framework outperforms all other existing algorithms by huge margins on all standard benchmarks with various settings. Among the baselines, MOON shows meaningful performance improvement, but its gains are limited under severe data heterogeneity, e.g., α=0.05𝛼0.05\alpha=0.05. FedLC employs adaptive label margin to mitigate local deviations, but its effectiveness on performance is constrained. FedDecorr exhibits marginal improvement and, in some settings, even degradation. This suggests that unconditional alleviation of dimensional collapse does not necessarily benefit FL. Compared to these works, our method achieves significant performance improvement across all datasets, regardless of the level of data heterogeneity.

5.3 Analysis

Low participation rate and large-scale clients

We validate our framework in more challenging settings with lower client participation rates and the larger number of distributed clients. Table 2 presents the robust performance improvement of FedRCL on three benchmarks, where we set a participation rate of 0.02 and vary the number of clients by {100,500}100500\{100,500\}. All methods suffer from performance degradation compared to Table 1, due to the reduced client data, increased data disparity, and lower participation rate. Particularly, FedProc suffers from a significant performance drop, because it relies on the global class prototypes aggregated from participating clients at each round, which may not accurately represent every class in this extremely low participation setting. MOON exhibits performance degradation compared to FedAvg in some challenging configurations, partly because it utilizes outdated previous local models due to the sparse participation of local clients. Despite these challenges, FedRCL consistently demonstrates outstanding performance across all datasets.

Contrastive learning strategies

Table 3 compares the effectiveness of various contrastive learning strategies. FedSCL is an ablative model of our framework, which incorporates a naïve supervised contrastive loss into the FedAvg baseline. While FedSCL improves upon the baseline, its gains are moderate and even undergo degradation in the early stage of training. In contrast, our full framework consistently enhances performance throughout the entire learning process, as also observed in Figure 1. We also employ another variant, denoted as FedCL, which adopts a self-supervised contrastive loss [25], but its benefits are minimal. This is partly because the objective of [25] does not directly align with mitigating the local deviations.

Multi-level training

Table 4 presents the ablative results of our framework using only the last-layer feature outputs on the CIFAR-100 under various levels of data heterogeneity, which verifies that employing early-level representations contributes to achieve additional performance gain.

Sensitivity on the weight of divergence penalty

Integration into server-side optimization approaches

Our framework is orthogonal to server-side algorithms, which enables seamless integration between FedRCL and other existing server-side approaches. Other backbone models

We evaluate our method on different backbone architectures, including VGG-9 [34], MobileNet-V2 [31], ShuffleNet [43], and SqueezeNet [11] on the CIFAR-100 in Table 6, where we set β𝛽\beta to 2 for MobileNet and 1 for other cases. Our framework consistently outperforms other existing approaches across various backbone networks by large margins, proving its robustness irrespective of the model architecture.

6 Conclusion

We presented a novel federated learning approach to address the challenges of data heterogeneity effectively. We initiated our investigation by analyzing gradient deviations at each local model and showed that the SCL objective mitigates the local deviations, but it entails representation collapses and limited transferability. To tackle this issue, we proposed a federated relaxed contrastive learning framework that successfully prevents representation collapses, which is further enhanced by encompassing all levels of intermediate feature representations. We demonstrated the superiority and robustness of our framework through extensive experiments and analyses.
