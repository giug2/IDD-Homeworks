Convex space learning for tabular synthetic data generation

Manjunath Mahendra ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Chaithra Umesh ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Saptarshi Bej ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Kristian Schultz ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Olaf Wolkenhauer [ [ [ [
Abstract
\justify

Generating synthetic samples from the convex space of the minority class is a popular oversampling approach for imbalanced classification problems. Recently, deep-learning approaches have been successfully applied to modeling the convex space of minority samples. Beyond oversampling, learning the convex space of neighborhoods in training data has not been used to generate entire tabular datasets. In this paper, we introduce a deep learning architecture (NextConvGeN) with a generator and discriminator component that can generate synthetic samples by learning to model the convex space of tabular data. The generator takes data neighborhoods as input and creates synthetic samples within the convex space of that neighborhood. Thereafter, the discriminator tries to classify these synthetic samples against a randomly sampled batch of data from the rest of the data space. We compared our proposed model with five state-of-the-art tabular generative models across ten publicly available datasets from the biomedical domain. Our analysis reveals that synthetic samples generated by NextConvGeN can better preserve classification and clustering performance across real and synthetic data than other synthetic data generation models. Synthetic data generation by deep learning of the convex space produces high scores for popular utility measures. We further compared how diverse synthetic data generation strategies perform in the privacy-utility spectrum and produced critical arguments on the necessity of high utility models. Our research on deep learning of the convex space of tabular data opens up opportunities in clinical research, machine learning model development, decision support systems, and clinical data sharing.

1 Introduction

We previously introduced models that utilize convex space learning to overcome imbalanced classification problem [1, 2, 3]. In these models, depending on the data distribution, synthetic samples are generated from specific regions of the minority class‚Äôs convex space, customized using linear interpolation on original samples. A convex space is the convex hull of the data space. The data points within a convex hull of a small neighborhood in the data space are assumed to share similar statistical characteristics in the convex space learning philosophy [2]. The idea of convex space learning is to generate synthetic samples from appropriate regions of the convex space of the original data. The appropriate regions depend on the dataset and the task at hand, and such regions are ‚Äòlearned.‚Äô For learning, traditional oversampling approaches use predefined rules that specify which regions of the convex space are appropriate for synthetic sample generation. However, these models are currently limited to skewed or imbalanced datasets with a clear distinction between majority and minority classes.

Recently, a convex space learning-based deep neural network model developed to tackle the imbalance issue in tabular data is the ConvGeN model by Schultz et al. [3]published on Patter Recognition journal. This model merges the convex space learning technique with deep generative modeling to create synthetic samples that balance the target classes for tabular data. The oversampling results with ConvGeN have been significant, at par with the state-of-the-art oversampling methods for imbalanced classification. However, this model was not generalized for the complete table generation. Following upon this, we broadened the application of the ConvGeN model beyond just imbalanced learning. We have adapted the model in a more general setting, exploring its potential for generating entire tabular data with diverse feature types.

Note that oversampling for imbalanced datasets is nothing but synthetic data generation. In the context of synthetic data generation, data modalities such as images or text are more popular. Significant progress has been made in synthetic data generation for computer vision and Natural Language Processing (NLP), offering exciting possibilities for clinical research. Among the most commonly used models in computer vision are Variational Auto Encoders (VAEs) [4, 5], Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [6, 7], and Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) [8].

Recently, synthetic data generation models have also been developed for tabular data, a widely used format for organizing and analyzing information consisting of rows and columns. Each column in a table represents a specific feature or attribute of the samples in rows [9, 10]. With well-defined features and an interpretable structure, clinical tabular data leads to more interpretable models.

However, in certain domains, such as bio-medicine, tabular data are subject to privacy. Therefore, access to data with confidential patient information, which includes sensitive medical records and personal identifiers, is difficult [11, 12]. Such data must be protected to meet the requirements of privacy laws such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the European Union [13]. Moreover, even when access is granted, the scale of available clinical data may only sometimes meet the requirements of modern data-driven approaches.

Tabular synthetic data generation strategies are recently gaining popularity to counter challenges related to data privacy and data enrichment [4, 14, 15, 16]. The idea is to generate synthetic data that mimics the characteristics of real-world clinical data. If the synthetic data is shared instead of the real data, then ideally, there is no breach of privacy. However, there is an important point to note here. If the synthetic data is too similar to the real data (in terms of statistical and other empirical measures), then an ‚Äòattacker‚Äô (an entity interested in privacy breach) can track the original data back from the synthetic data. This defeats the purpose of synthetic data generation. On the other hand, if the synthetic data is too dissimilar to the original data, then any model built on the synthetic data might produce inconsistent results, reducing the utility of such data. A synthetic data generation model should preserve privacy and utility in an ideal scenario. There has been no objective study that determines how well tabular synthetic data generation models balance privacy and utility measures.

In this paper, we contribute to the ongoing research on synthetic tabular data generation at the following two points:

  1. 1.

    We generalize the existing ConvGeN algorithm such that it can be used for synthetic tabular data generation in general, thereby proposing the NextConvGeN algorithm. The model comprises a generator and discriminator network, which cooperate among themselves (instead of competing like in adversarial learning) to learn regions in the convex data space that are more apt for sampling synthetic data points. We demonstrate the model‚Äôs effectiveness in comparison with existing tabular synthetic data generation algorithms using publicly available benchmarking datasets from the clinical domain.

  2. 2.

    We provide an objective comparison of major tabular synthetic data generation models in terms of how they balance between privacy and utility based performance measures.

In addition to these, the article further informs the community about some more points of interest, such as:

  1. 1.

    Most of the synthetic tabular data generation models currently available focus on larger datasets [14, 15, 16]. However, in the clinical domain, one frequently encounters smaller datasets. Our research reveals the effectiveness of several tabular synthetic data generation strategies on such datasets.

  2. 2.

    One-hot encoding is used for existing generative models to handle categorical features with numerous categories. However, this makes the original dataset sparse, which might affect synthetic data generation. The NextConvGeN model we proposed in this article addresses this issue.

Our research paves the way for a novel tabular synthetic data generation modeling approach. Moreover, it provides the community with a guideline on which synthetic data generation to choose depending on the purpose (whether preserving utility is more important or preserving privacy is more important).

2 Related research

VAEs and GANs are two pioneering techniques in generative modeling that have significantly shaped research on synthetic data. Initially used for synthetic image generation, now they have found diverse applications across various domains. This section discusses the origins and advancements of VAEs and GANs, highlighting their contributions to synthetic data generation [17]. Then, we discuss specialized approaches designed to tackle challenges posed by tabular data.

2.1 Variational AutoEncoders (VAEs)

VAE uses deep learning and probabilistic modeling to generate synthetic data. They have an encoder and a decoder and introduce a probabilistic interpretation of the latent space to account for underlying variability. A reparameterization method is used during sampling, and the training process maximizes the lower bound of evidence [4, 17, 18]. VAEs have advantages for data augmentation and creating more comprehensive datasets but also have limitations, such as difficulty handling discrete data. They can be used for anomaly detection but may suffer from information loss, posterior collapse, and sensitivity to prior distribution, likelihood function, and regularization term [17].

2.2 Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

GANs revolutionized synthetic data generation by providing a robust framework for creating highly realistic and diverse samples. It was proposed by Goodfellow et al. in 2014 [19]. GANs have two primary components: a generator network that produces artificial samples from random noise inputs during training and a discriminator network that distinguishes between real and generated samples. Once trained, GANs can produce synthetic samples with complex patterns, textures, and structures, making them suitable for various applications like image synthesis, text generation, and data augmentation. GANs have several challenges that make them difficult to train. These include oscillatory loss, uninformative loss, mode collapse, hyperparameter tuning, and vanishing gradients. These problems are common to GANs regardless of domain or architecture [17, 6, 7]. Addressing these issues is crucial for successful GAN training and high-quality data generation. Since the introduction of GANs, the field has seen significant advancements, including Conditional GAN, Deep Convolutional GAN, Coupled GAN, Auxiliary Classifier GAN, Wasserstein GANs, and more [7].

2.3 Conditional Generative Adversarial Network (CTGAN)

In 2019, Xu et al. presented a new method for synthesizing data in tabular format using CTGAN. This method aims to model both continuous and categorical columns effectively. The technique involves a unique normalization approach for continuous features and a strategy to handle imbalances in discrete data. For continuous features, CTGAN employs a three-step process. Initially, it uses a variational Gaussian mixture model to model non-Gaussian and multimodal continuous distributions and estimate the number of modes. Each continuous value is then represented as a one-hot vector indicating the mode and a scalar indicating the value within that mode. Categorical columns, on the other hand, are represented through one-hot encoding. The final representation of a row in the tabular data is obtained by concatenating continuous and categorical columns [14].

To address the challenge of imbalanced discrete data, CTGAN introduces a conditional vector, a one-hot-encoded vector representing the selection of a specific value for a categorical column. During training, a column with discrete values is uniformly selected, and a specific value is sampled based on the probability mass function across all possible values. The generator is then fed with this conditional vector and a noise vector, and the loss function is modified to penalize the generator for failing to produce the selected column with the chosen value. The concept of "packing" is introduced to prevent mode collapse in the generator. Training CTGAN involves providing a list of discrete columns; others are treated as continuous data [14]. According to the results of the experiments, CTGAN outperformed other generative models, including CLBN, PrivBN, MedGAN, VeeGAN, and TableGAN, regarding machine learning efficacy on real datasets [14].

2.4 Conditional TABle GAN (CTAB-GAN)

Imbalanced data classes and skewed distributions are prevalent problems in datasets across different fields [2, 3, 15]. These challenges can hinder effective data analysis, research, and practical applications. To overcome these, researchers have devised an approach to tackle this issue through CTAB-GAN, the extension of CTGAN. This method combines conditional generation and sampling-based training techniques to enhance the representation of all classes, particularly those that are underrepresented, and preprocess variables with long-tailed distributions using a logarithmic transformation.

Introducing a "conditional vector" to the generator allows it to adapt its synthesis procedure based on specific conditions, thereby improving the representation of all classes. The logarithmic transformation aids in compressing and reducing the distance between tail and bulk data, facilitating the encoding of all values, including tail ones, by the variational Gaussian mixture. To ensure the quality of synthetic data, researchers have introduced two terms into the generator‚Äôs loss function- information loss and classification loss. These terms minimize statistical differences between generated and real data, encourage the generator to match the real dataset‚Äôs statistical characteristics, and predict labels for synthetic data instances, ensuring adherence to expected semantic criteria. CTAB-GAN addresses challenges with continuous variables by employing mode-specific normalization, resulting in more accurate synthesis [15].

Comparative evaluation against four other tabular data generators has demonstrated CTAB-GAN‚Äôs high utility, statistical similarity to real data, and reasonable safeguards for sensitive information. However, it is essential to note that GAN-based models, including CTAB-GAN, require a large sample size for training. Therefore, users should consider this aspect when assessing the practical applicability of CTAB-GAN, particularly in scenarios where resource constraints may pose challenges. CTAB-GAN is a promising technique that offers a potential solution to a class imbalance in datasets, enabling practical data analysis, research, and applications [15].

2.5 CTAB-GAN+

In addition to the research gaps addressed in CTGAN and CTAB-GAN, Zhao et al. states that existing GAN-based tabular data generators overlook variables like Mortgage in the Loan dataset, which can be numerical and categorical. This oversight generates inaccuracies such as approximating zero values instead of exact zeros and assigning meaning to negative values where there is no reality. They have utilized sampling and mode-specific normalization methods from CTGAN to deal with imbalanced data and multimodal distribution, respectively. Additionally, to enhance the stability of GAN training, CTAB-GAN+ has incorporated the Wasserstein plus gradient penalty for the loss function, which was developed to address the exploding and vanishing gradient concerns that can arise in the original WGAN [20]. Furthermore, to safeguard the privacy of contributors while enabling meaningful aggregate analysis, the encoder part of the model uses differential privacy, which introduces controlled noise into datasets, ensuring that individual data points remain indistinguishable within the statistical analysis. CTAB-GAN+ outperforms other generative models regarding machine utility and statistical similarity, making it an excellent choice for data generation [21].

2.6 Tabular Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (TabDDPM)

TabDDPM uses diffusion models, known for generating images, and adjusts them to generate tabular data. These models add noise to the input data through iterative diffusion and then reverse it to produce a synthetic sample close to the original distribution. Recently, Kotelnikov et al. introduced TabDDPM, a state-of-the-art method generative model for handling diverse features in tabular data [16]. They address differences in feature types by preprocessing continuous features with Gaussian quantile transformation and representing categorical features with one hot encoding. The model uses multinomial diffusion for categorical features and Gaussian diffusion for continuous features. Each categorical feature undergoes a separate diffusion process, where noise components are sampled independently for all features. A multilayer perceptron models the reverse diffusion step, which takes noisy input data and predicts Gaussian and multinomial diffusion outcomes. Training involves minimizing mean squared error for Gaussian diffusion and KL divergence for multinomial diffusion. A class conditional model is used for classification, and regression, the target is treated as an additional numerical feature. TabDDPM performs well on benchmark datasets, consistently generating high-quality synthetic samples compared to existing GAN or VAE-based models [16]. However, this model treats categorical features with one-hot encoding, which makes the original data sparse. This might affect the synthetic data generation, especially when the sample size is small. In addition, the TabDDPM model processes the feature types separately, possibly not capturing the relationship between different feature types.

3 Methodology

This section introduces the NextConvGeN model, designed to generate synthetic clinical tabular data using convex space learning. We will provide a brief overview of NextConvGeN and the modifications made to the ConvGeN model to create it. Additionally, we will delve into the mathematical foundation and operational mechanisms of NextConvGeN.

3.1 Overview of NextConvGeN model

NextConvGeN model is developed with its underlying principle based on the ConvGeN model. The learning approach of NextConvGeN is based on deep cooperative learning, which involves using two neural networks to learn convex coefficients during the training process. The generator network produces synthetic samples within the convex space of the input data neighborhood, while the discriminator network classifies these samples against the rest of the data space. The networks work together, learning from each other‚Äôs feedback and improving their capabilities over multiple iterations. NextConvGeN learns the optimal coefficients for convex combinations during training by adjusting the neural network‚Äôs parameters iteratively.

Unlike ConvGeN, which takes shuffled batches of minority class data neighborhoods as inputs, NextConvGeN‚Äôs generator is designed to receive shuffled neighborhood batches from the entire data space as its inputs. These neighborhoods are identified using a unique nonlinear dimensional reduction approach called Feature-type Distributed Clustering (FDC) introduced by Bej et al. [22, 23]. This approach provides a more effective framework for generating informative low-dimensional embedding from high-dimensional tabular data than conventional Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP), a commonly used nonlinear dimension reduction technique with a choice of similarity measures. FDC applies the UMAP technique independently to each feature type (continuous, ordinal, and nominal) using specific similarity measures [22]. The resulting lower dimension is more meaningful, considering the diverse feature types present in the data. Hence, we explore the neighborhoods based on this lower dimension.

A training input for the NextConvGeN model contains data points from

  1. (i)

    a specific data neighborhood randomly shuffled

  2. (ii)

    a shuffled batch of data points sampled from the original data, equal in size to the considered data neighborhood, and disjoint to the chosen data neighborhood.

where (i), as described above, is passed on to the generator, which generates synthetic samples from the neighborhood. Those synthetic samples are classified against (ii) by the discriminator. By cooperating with the discriminator, the generator learns the appropriate coefficients of convex combinations for synthetic data generation in a way that improves the discriminator‚Äôs classification performance.

Note that the ConvGeN model could only be used in an imbalanced classification where there are well-defined minority and majority classes. Our proposed model does not require any such labels and can be used for general synthetic tabular data generation. Anticipating the primary application of the proposed model in the clinical and biomedical domain, an important change that we incorporated in the novel model is the choice of neighborhoods by incorporating different distance measures for various feature types (continuous, ordinal, and nominal).

3.3 Architectural design and operational principles of NextConvGeN

The NextConvGeN model is depicted in Figure 1. Its architecture is similar to ConvGeN. The neural networks that form components of the NextConvGeN model have a generic shallow structure, keeping in mind that we are not optimizing the model for specific applicative purposes but rather performing benchmarking studies. In NextConvGeN, the input layer of the generator takes neb nodes. Each of them contains fùëìf features. Where neb is the size of the neighborhood, and fùëìf is the number of features. Gùê∫G receives a shuffled batch of neb original samples during each iteration. The unique aspect is that each input batch represents a neighborhood NùëÅN accessed through a dimension reduction technique, utilizing suitable similarity measures for the feature types. This neighborhood NùëÅN consists of the neb closest data points to a specific original data point. Consequently, the dimension of the input becomes neb√ófnebùëì\texttt{neb}\times f.

Subsequently, the output of the convolution layer is flattened and reshaped in a way to obtain a matrix denoted as KNsubscriptùêæùëÅ{K}_{N}, whose columns contain convex coefficients. In other words, each column in this matrix sums up to one, and the entries are non-negative. The matrix KNTsuperscriptsubscriptùêæùëÅùëá{K}_{N}^{T} represents the transpose of KNsubscriptùêæùëÅK_{N}, ensuring that each row of KNTsuperscriptsubscriptùêæùëÅùëá{K}_{N}^{T} sums up to one. This process ensures that none of the elements in each row equals one. 4 Datasets and evaluation measures

4.1 Datasets used and general protocol

In our benchmarking experiments, we used a diverse set of ten publicly available datasets. These datasets vary regarding the number of samples they contain and the characteristics they represent. A detailed overview of each dataset is available in Table 1, providing insights into various attributes that highlight the diversity of the datasets.

We performed the necessary preprocessing steps to ensure that the datasets were free of missing values. Additionally, we have confirmed that all variables are numerical rather than strings, as some generative models cannot handle non-numeric variables. Also, it is essential to specify the different feature types (continuous, ordinal and nominal) for the generative models. Next, we partitioned each dataset into two parts: 70%percent7070\% of the data was used to train the generative model; whereas the remaining 30%percent3030\% was taken as holdout data for evaluation, allowing us to test the model‚Äôs performance on previously unseen data. To determine the effectiveness of the proposed NextConvGeN model, we compared it against adopting four state-of-the-art approaches as benchmarks: TVAE, CTGAN, CTAB-GAN, CTAB-GAN+, and TabDDPM. We generate synthetic samples five times the size of real training samples from each generative model. Later, we down-sample (to the size of the training data) to maintain the cardinality of the target feature as in the real training data. This down-sampled synthetic data is then used for both utility and privacy evaluation. By downsampling to match the cardinality (number of unique values) of the target feature in the real data, we create a fair comparison where all the models are evaluated on the datasets with the same class distribution as real.

4.2 Evaluation measures

Evaluation measures are crucial in determining the effectiveness and reliability of synthetic data generated to represent real data. However, there are no standard measures for evaluating tabular synthetic data [24]; hence, we have chosen some commonly used measures from the available sources for benchmarking. To compare the quality of synthetic data generated by several models, we evaluate the synthetic data in terms of Utility and Privacy.

Utility measures for synthetic data are determined by the performance of synthetic data for any given task, such as classification or regression. Fidelity refers to the statistical similarity between real and its corresponding synthetic data. This encompasses aspects like the distribution of features across both datasets. Jordon et al. states that fidelity is often considered alongside utility [25]. In our benchmarking evaluation, we therefore considered Fidelity measures under Utility. In the evaluation context, Privacy is a measure that quantifies the amount of information about the original data revealed by its corresponding synthetic data. 4.4 Privacy measures

In benchmarking studies, it is often taken into account how well a synthetic data generation model can preserve privacy. However, only a few privacy measures are available. Moreover, there is no well-established standard measure yet to quantify how much synthetic data preserves privacy. In our benchmarking experiments, we used three privacy measures: Distance metrics, Membership Inference Attack (MIA), and Attribute Inference Attack (AIA) [26, 27, 34]. If the synthetic data generated by a generative model is highly similar to the real data, then the average distance between the pairwise data points is expected to be very small. The smaller the distance, the higher the potential for privacy risks. MIA occurs when an attacker tries to determine if a patient record was employed in training a generative model. In contrast, AIA happens when an attacker has access to some attributes or features of the real data and tries to find the values of an unknown patient attribute using synthetic data. In the following, we provide further information on these measures.

  • ‚Ä¢

    Distance metrics: Hernadez et al. suggested three similarity measures to evaluate how private synthetic data is compared to real data. The metrics employed include Euclidean distance, Hausdorff distance, and cosine similarity. The pairwise similarity value is calculated for each pair of records between real and synthetic, and the mean values of those pairwise similarity values are analyzed. Euclidean distance captures the difference between two data points by calculating the straight-line distance in a multidimensional space. A smaller Euclidean distance indicates a high risk of privacy breach. Hausdorff distance calculates the maximum pairwise shortest distance between real and synthetic data. A smaller Hausdorff distance is associated with a higher privacy risk. Conversely, cosine similarity measures the directional similarity between data points, reflecting how closely their angles align in the multidimensional space. The higher the value of cosine similarity, the higher the privacy risk.

  • ‚Ä¢

    MIA: This measure evaluates the re-identification risk in synthetic patient data through a simulated attack scenario. The attacker has access to the complete synthetic data and a random subset of real patient data (special case where only 50% used for generative model training) ranging in availability from 20% to 100% (It might contain samples that have been used for model training). Following research by Goncalves et al., we compare real data samples to the synthetic data samples using Hamming distance, a metric indicating attribute differences [27]. If the distance falls below a predefined threshold (0.40.40.4, 0.30.30.3, 0.20.20.2, or 0.10.10.1), the attacker suspects a match, suggesting a patient record from the real data was used to generate the corresponding synthetic records. Precision, the proportion of correctly identified matches, is calculated to assess the effectiveness of the attack. According to Hernadez et al. and Mendelevitch et al., lower precision (below 0.50.50.5) indicates good privacy preservation, while higher values suggest an increased risk of re-identification [34]. By plotting precision against the percentage of accessible real data and the chosen threshold, researchers can analyze the impact of attacker ability and similarity thresholds on the re-identification risk of the synthetic data, which we have used later in Figure 3.

  • ‚Ä¢

    AIA: This measure investigates how attackers can use synthetic data to predict missing sensitive attributes in real data. The attackers will have access to complete synthetic data and quasi-identifiers for some real records. Quasi-identifiers are attributes or features that are not unique identifiers, such as age or gender. A decision tree classifier or regressor is trained on quasi-identifiers of synthetic data to learn the sensitive feature or attribute depending upon the target type [26]. These trained models are used to predict the sensitive information of the real data with the quasi-identifier. The model is evaluated by calculating accuracy for categorical features and root-mean-squared error (RMSE) for numerical features. High accuracy or low RMSE indicates the attacker successfully predicted missing attributes, posing a high disclosure risk. Conversely, lower accuracy and higher RMSE suggest a low risk of attackers inferring sensitive information using this approach [26, 34].

6 Discussion

We will now discuss some interesting observations that arise from our results. Our result reflects the dilemma of designing a synthetic data generation strategy that preserves privacy and utility. High value for utility measures broadly indicate that the statistical distributions of the real and the synthetic data are similar. Moreover, it also indicates that the performance of machine learning tasks such as clustering or classification is similar across real and synthetic data. If the synthetic data is to be used as an alternative to real data, then high utility is required.

In Table 6, notice the absolute five-fold cross-validation performance difference between the gradient descent classifier trained on real and synthetic data. For the CTGAN and CTAB-GAN models, the difference is more than ten percent for more than fifty percent of the datasets. There is no objective measure in the literature that comments on the effect of such difference once these synthetic data generation strategies are applied to practical, real-life scenarios. More than a ten percent difference on more than half the datasets intuitively translates to poorly performing models. However, if we focus too much on making the synthetic data similar enough to the real data, we automatically compromise on privacy. Once the data distribution of the synthetic data is too similar to that of the real data, it is easier to track the real data back from the synthetic data (reidentification risk). This is reflected in our results. We observe that TabDDPM and NextConvGeN models perform worse than CTGAN and CTAB-GAN regarding privacy measures. Therefore, our results reflect the utility-privacy dilemma.

Note that GAN-based models fundamentally rely on adversarial learning. Such models fundamentally try to learn a mapping from a random distribution (noise) to the data distribution. The real data is no longer needed for the synthetic sample generation step in such models. A random noise picked from the chosen noise distribution can be converted to a real data point. On the other hand, both TabDDPM and NextConvGeN use the real data points directly for the synthetic data generation. In NextConvGeN, for example, a data neighborhood sampled from a real data neighborhood is passed on to the generator to generate synthetic samples from the convex hull of that data neighborhood. This can be a key driving factor behind the high-utility performance of the TabDDPM and the NextConvGeN models. The poor utility performance of CTGAN, CTAB-GAN, and CTAB-GAN+ can be even more pronounced when the training dataset is smaller. We can expect the GAN models to require more training data, as they are essentially learning to map a completely random distribution to a known data distribution. In real-life scenarios, however, clinical data, particularly generated at a single health facility, is often small in size.

A natural question we can ask here is that which among the privacy and utility measures are more important. Unfortunately, not much research has been conducted to answer such a question. However, common sense suggests that a synthetic data generation model that performs poorly in terms of utility in the first place is of little value. Even though it preserves privacy, the synthetic data does not represent the real data well enough. Therefore, it is unsuitable for any machine learning analysis that is performed to make decisions based on real data. On the other hand, if at least the utility is high, then there are measures for the safe sharing of data in general, which can be used for sharing the synthetic data [35]. In such a case, the high-utility synthetic data can provide an additional level of data security. We would argue that a synthetic data generation model can be judged in terms of privacy, given that it satisfies utility requirements to some degree. We point out the necessity of an objective determination of a proper utility threshold for a synthetic data generation model to be considered for privacy preservation.

In this study, we would like to highlight the mode collapse problem in TVAE and CTAB-GAN+. Mode collapse occurs when generative models converge on a limited set of outputs, failing to capture the full diversity of the data. Although CTAB-GAN+ is designed with a specific architectural feature to address mode collapse (training by sampling and packing), its performance on small clinical data has not been evaluated. The mode collapse in CTAB-GAN+ might be attributed to the limited training data. With a smaller dataset, the generators may struggle to learn the complex underlying distributions of minority samples, resulting in a preference for majority samples. In TVAE, information compression in the latent space during encoding could hinder the decoder‚Äôs ability to reconstruct all target classes. On the other hand, in NextConvGeN, the generators are trained in every minority sample neighborhood and generate samples within each neighborhood. Overall, the evaluation study suggests that TVAE and CTAB-GAN+ may not be optimal if the dataset is small and imbalanced.

There are many generative models that lack some practical aspects, such as the preservation of functional and logical dependencies in synthetic data. Typically, the features in tabular datasets can be logically or functionally dependent. Let us take a crude example to understand this. Say a tabular dataset has two features: BMI (real-valued) and Diabetes (binary-valued). Say the dataset is curated in a health facility that considers a BMI threshold of tùë°t for the diagnosis of Diabetes. That is, if patients have a BMI of more than tùë°t, they are more likely to be diabetic. There is, therefore, a logical dependency between the two features of BMI and Diabetes. In synthetic data, if there is a data point for this, the BMI is greater than the threshold, but the Diabetes value is negative, then a logical fallacy arises. Notably, such intricate complicates arise in synthetic data generation for the tabular data modality, demanding more research on this topic.

We summarize a few observations that we believe require attention by the community:

  • ‚Ä¢

    The objective assessment of generative model performance remains a key challenge in the field. For example, what degree of performance is good enough for a synthetic data generation model to be labelled as a high-utility model is not clear. Such clarity can be attained when more such models are used in real-life scenarios. An objective measure to quantify the utility-privacy balance must also be devised to compare the versatility of the synthetic data generation models.

  • ‚Ä¢

    From our study, we noticed that different synthetic data generation strategies are apt for different tasks. The purpose of synthetic data generation can be diverse. For example, synthetic data can be used to mimic a classification or clustering of real data. Other purposes can be circumventing privacy issues or maintaining logical dependencies. A single synthetic data generation model is unlikely to serve all these purposes. In such a case, the idea of task-specific synthetic data generation can be explored. The idea is to develop synthetic data generation models specialized for different tasks.

  • ‚Ä¢

    Several synthetic data generation philosophies, such as adversarial learning, diffusion models, and convex space learning, have been used to develop the existing synthetic data generation models. We noticed, for example, that the diffusion model and the convex space learning philosophy work well for utility purposes, whereas the adversarial philosophy works better for privacy preservation, although it significantly compromises utility. A hybrid approach considering different synthetic data generation philosophies can also be speculated to produce a more balanced utility-privacy performance.

7 Conclusion

We proposed the NextConvGeN model, a deep learning architecture for generating entire tabular data. The model improves on previous convex space learning (ConvGeN) model built for oversampling on imbalanced datasets. However, there are some important additions to our new model‚Äôs synthetic data generation process. Firstly, we used an improved approach to identify data neighborhoods. This approach involves separate dimension reduction for each feature type using appropriate distance measures, leading to a more informative lower-dimensional representation of the real data. This allows NextConvGeN to capture non-linear similarities when determining data neighborhoods. Secondly, we added the ‚ÄòAlpha clipping,‚Äô a technique to control the variance of the synthetic samples generated in a data neighborhood. It also prevents the synthetic data generated from being too similar to the real data. Thirdly, we adapt the training procedure of NextConvGeN outside the context of imbalanced classification. In contrast to ConvGeN, which receives a minority neighborhood as input to the generator, which is discriminated against a batch of majority class samples, NextConvGeN receives an FDC-assisted data neighborhood, which is discriminated against a random batch drawn from the rest of the data distribution (complement of the FDC-assisted data neighborhood).

In general, the performances of NextConvGeN are comparable to the TabDDPM model. However, NextConvGeN shows superior performance for utility measures. For classification tasks, we saw that synthetic samples generated by NextConvGeN preserve classification performance for synthetic data compared to real data even better compared to TabDDPM. After comparing various synthetic data generation approaches, we delve into the critical challenge of balancing utility and privacy, an aspect overlooked in previous research. Finally, in Section 6, we address the limitations of our approach and explore the broader context of this field. Our work, therefore, can be a valuable addition to the literature on synthetic tabular data generation. The proposed model has the potential for wider applications in utility-focused synthetic data generation and balanced privacy.
