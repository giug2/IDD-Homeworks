Enhanced Semantic Segmentation for Large-Scale and Imbalanced Point Clouds

Haoran Gong â€ƒâ€ƒ Haodong Wang â€ƒâ€ƒ Di Wang diwang@mail.xjtu.edu.cn [ [
Abstract

Semantic segmentation of large-scale point clouds is of significant importance in environment perception and scene understanding. However, point clouds collected from real-world environments are usually imbalanced and small-sized objects are prone to be under-sampled or misclassified due to their low occurrence frequency, thereby reducing the overall accuracy of semantic segmentation. In this study, we propose the Multilateral Cascading Network (MCNet) for large-scale and sample-imbalanced point cloud scenes. To increase the frequency of small-sized objects, we introduce the semantic-weighted sampling module, which incorporates a probability parameter into the collected data group. To facilitate feature learning, we propose a Multilateral Cascading Attention Enhancement (MCAE) module to learn complex local features through multilateral cascading operations and attention mechanisms. To promote feature fusion, we propose a Point Cross Stage Partial (P-CSP) module to combine global and local features, optimizing the integration of valuable feature information across multiple scales. Finally, we introduce the neighborhood voting module to integrate results at the output layer. Our proposed method demonstrates either competitive or superior performance relative to state-of-the-art approaches across three widely recognized benchmark datasets: S3DIS, Toronto3D, and SensatUrban with mIoU scores of 74.0%, 82.9% and 64.5%, respectively. Notably, our work yielded consistent optimal results on the under-sampled semantic categories, thereby demonstrating exceptional performance in the recognition of small-sized objects.

1 Introduction

As urban areas undergo a continuous process of transformation from digitization to intelligent systems and subsequently to smart systems, digital twins emerge as a pivotal technological avenue for the realization of smart cities. Point cloud data, due to its powerful spatial representation capabilities, plays a crucial role in this process. It offers a comprehensive representation of the geometry and structure of physical objects and environments. Semantic segmentation is a critical task of leveraging point cloud data, which involves classifying each point within the cloud into meaningful categories. This process enables a deeper understanding of spatial environments, facilitating improved object recognition (Qi etÂ al., 2021), scene interpretation (Moyano etÂ al., 2021), and navigation (El-Sheimy and Li, 2021).

In the early stages of point cloud semantic segmentation research, the majority of methods relied on rule-based ideas or machine learning with manually designed features. However, in recent years, deep learning methods have emerged as the dominant choice. The initial deep learning-based point cloud methods directly leverage the advancements in image processing and related fields, thus these approaches frequently rely on transforming the point cloud data into intermediate representations. The classification of these methods is based on the preprocessing techniques that are employed, and they can be divided into two categories: projection-based and voxel-based. Projection-based methods involve mapping 3D point cloud data onto 2D images from multiple viewpoints, followed by the application of 2D deep learning algorithms to extract features from each image, and pooling operations to aggregate global features. However, this approach can introduce quantization artifacts and result in information loss, particularly due to the selection of projection angles (Bello etÂ al., 2020). In contrast, voxel-based methods convert 3D point clouds into uniform voxel grids, utilizing 3D convolutions for feature extraction. Although this approach addresses certain challenges associated with projection-based methods, it incurs a cubic increase in computational costs as voxel resolution rises (Wu etÂ al., 2015), making it less feasible for large-scale point cloud datasets. Overcoming these challenges is crucial for developing more efficient and accurate point cloud semantic segmentation techniques.

The groundbreaking development of PointNet (Qi etÂ al., 2017a) enabled the direct processing of 3D point clouds. Since its introduction, various advanced neural networks based on points have been proposed (Qi etÂ al., 2017b; Wang etÂ al., 2019; Qian etÂ al., 2022). These methods have greatly facilitated the development of point cloud processing techniques in the context of deep learning, especially semantic segmentation. Early works mainly focused on small-scale datasets and scenes. As the field of research progresses, there is an increasing focus on large-scale semantic segmentation of point clouds within real-world contexts. A variety of methods have been developed and evaluated for this purpose. For example, SPG (Landrieu and Simonovsky, 2018) addresses the issue of managing large data sets by converting extensive point clouds into hypergraphs prior to training the model. Nevertheless, this hypergraph preprocessing introduces a considerable computational burden. To address this challenge, the RandLA-Net was introduced as a lightweight network that employs random downsampling and local feature aggregation blocks, thereby enhancing segmentation performance while significantly reducing training costs. This represents a significant advance in the field of deep learning networks, establishing a new benchmark for large-scale point cloud segmentation tasks. Similarly, KPConv (Thomas etÂ al., 2019) is a kernel-based convolutional network designed for point clouds, effectively extending convolutional operations to 3D data. The network demonstrates particular efficacy in learning specific features of 3D structural objects, as evidenced by its success in segmenting objects with broad applicability.

Nevertheless, while current large-scale deep learning point cloud segmentation networks have demonstrated notable success in identifying larger samples, such as ground, buildings, and trees in urban environments, their performance in recognizing smaller samples (e.g., bicycles and railways) has been less satisfactory. The issues can be summarized as follows: 1) In large-scale real-world scenes, a substantial imbalance exists in the number of instances among various object categories, which results in inadequate training for the underrepresented categories. 2) Point cloud data is distinguished by its disordered, unstructured, and uneven distribution. A limitation of solely focusing on global feature extraction is the loss of local details. 3) Currently, numerous networks employ sophisticated multi-layer architectures. As the number of layers increases, there is a risk of losing global details. In general, the diversity of objects in real-world scenes represents a significant challenge for models to efficiently learn inter-object correlations within their contexts. Enhancing the performance of large-scale point cloud semantic segmentation in real-world scenarios is of vital importance, particularly in addressing the long-tail problem caused by sample imbalance and improving segmentation effectiveness for semantic categories that comprise a small portion of the overall dataset.

Given the aforementioned challenges, we introduce the Multilateral Cascading Network (MCNet), a novel network architecture designed for the semantic segmentation of large-scale and sample-imbalanced point clouds. Our model has achieved notable results on real-world point cloud datasets. It has been rigorously tested on three benchmarks and outperformed existing methods. In summary, our main contributions are:

    We propose a semantic-based weighted sampling module, which assigns additional weights to under-sampled objects. The proposed module has the potential to address the issue of training sample imbalance.

  2. âˆ™âˆ™\bullet

To enhance local feature learning, we introduce a Multilateral Cascading Attention Enhancement (MCAE) module at the decoding layer, which employs a flexible cascading approach. To ensure maximum feature information retention at each layer, we design a Point Cross Stage Partial (P-CSP) module at the decoding layer, which employs a new feature aggregation strategy. Our method enhances the local features of each point and augments the overall performance of the model by better maintaining the original characteristics of each scale layer.

2 Related work

2.1 Projection-based Scheme

Many studies on point cloud semantic segmentation have leveraged advancements in image convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Chen etÂ al., 2017; Li etÂ al., 2016; Yang etÂ al., 2018). These approaches typically transform 3D point clouds into 2D images through projection or flattening methods, followed by feature learning for object recognition. However, such techniques face challenges related to visibility and occlusion, which often limits their ability to utilize the intrinsic geometric properties of point clouds fully.

To overcome these challenges, several methods have integrated multimodal features derived from 3D scanning. For example, Kundu etÂ al. (2020) effectively utilized strategically positioned cameras to create multi-view images from point clouds, facilitating segmentation tasks. In another approach, Hu etÂ al. (2021b) put forth a method employing two U-Net architectures (Ronneberger etÂ al., 2015) to independently process 3D point clouds and 2D images. These architectures were interconnected at each stage using a projection matrix, which enhanced boundary constraints for point cloud segmentation. EllipsoidNet (Lyu etÂ al., 2022) adopted a novel strategy by projecting point clouds onto an ellipsoidal surface, which minimized point overlap and produced high-density feature maps. Nevertheless, these methods often depend on supplementary feature information, such as camera attitude data, and may encounter information loss during the projection process.

2.2 Voxel-based Scheme

Voxel-based approaches for point cloud feature learning convert raw data into 3D voxel grids, enabling the application of 3D convolutional neural networks (3D-CNNs) for feature extraction. This method allows the learned semantic information to be reallocated to individual points (Graham etÂ al., 2018; Le and Duan, 2018; Choy etÂ al., 2019). However, the resource demands are significant, primarily due to the cubic memory requirements. To address these challenges, sparse convolution techniques have been introduced. For example, DRINet++ (Ye etÂ al., 2021) employs sparse feature encoders and geometric enhancements to derive multi-scale features. Similarly, MVP-Net (Li etÂ al., 2023) presents a multi-scale voxel point adaptive fusion network tailored for semantic segmentation of point clouds in urban settings, effectively integrating geometric attributes and semantic information across diverse receptive fields. Despite the potential reduction in computational costs owing to sparsity, such methods frequently compromise detail preservation. PCSCNet (Zhang etÂ al., 2024a) focuses on voxelizing point clouds using larger voxels and employs point convolutions to derive features from each voxel. It then disperses these features to neighboring regions through 3D sparse convolutions, effectively broadening the receptive field. Nonetheless, voxel-based techniques often encounter difficulties in capturing fine-grained details in 3D environments, particularly with low-resolution voxels, complicating the identification of small objects.

2.3 Point-based Scheme

Point-based methods extract features directly from raw point cloud data without prior transformation. PointNet (Qi etÂ al., 2017a) pioneered this approach by employing a shared point-wise multi-layer perceptron (MLP) for feature development. Following this, DGCNN (Wang etÂ al., 2019) leveraged k-nearest neighbor (KNN) to gather local features, while KPConv (Thomas etÂ al., 2019) introduced a kernel convolution technique that naturally extends MLP to 3D point clouds. Building on KPConvâ€™s concepts, MappingConvSeg (Yan etÂ al., 2021) proposed continuous convolutional networks to learn spatially relevant features. RandLA-Net (Hu etÂ al., 2020) combined local feature encoding with attention pooling to improve semantic segmentation for large-scale point clouds. Additionally, PointNeXt (Qian etÂ al., 2022) explored effective training strategies, significantly boosting segmentation performance for PointNet++. However, these approaches are generally trained on smaller datasets and may struggle with large datasets or practical applications.

2.4 Large-scale Point Cloud Segmentation

Recent advancements have seen the development of methods tailored for semantic segmentation in large-scale and real-world point cloud datasets. NeiEA-Net (Xu etÂ al., 2023) focuses on maximizing high-dimensional feature spaces and optimizing local neighborhoods in 3D Euclidean space, enabling better capture of local details. LEARD-Net (Zeng etÂ al., 2022) employs multi-hop connections and color coding to learn contextual information. LACV-Net (Zeng etÂ al., 2024) introduces a Local Adaptive Feature Augmentation (LAFA) module that adaptively learns similarity weights among local neighbors, enhancing local information and minimizing ambiguity. PointVector (Deng etÂ al., 2023) presents a vector-based point set abstraction for aggregating neighboring features through higher-dimensional vectors.

While these approaches improve neighborhood feature representation and enhance semantic segmentation accuracy on extensive datasets, they often face challenges due to imbalanced data distributions. Categories that are underrepresented or small in size may be overlooked or misclassified, which adversely impacts overall segmentation accuracy.

3.2 Network Framework

The objective of the model training phase is to enhance the modelâ€™s capacity for learning local features across neighborhood point clouds and facilitate feature aggregation at different levels. To this end, we have developed a novel network, designated the Multilaterally Cascading Network (MCNet). Fig.Â 1 depicts the architecture of the MCNet. The model employs an encoder-decoder structure and comprises two modules: the Multilateral Cascading Attention Enhancement (MCAE) module and the Point Cross Stage Partial (P-CSP) module. First, the point cloud information is transmitted to the MCAE module for feature learning at the encoding layer. Then, the P-CSP module is used at the decoding layer to aggregate sampled features with comparable features. Finally, the neighborhood voting module generates the final result by voting based on the neighborhood information.

3.2.2 P-CSP Module

The decoding phase of the network primarily entails fusing sampled features with features at the same scale, maximizing the integration of valuable feature information across all scales. Motivated by the CSPNet (Wang etÂ al., 2020) architecture, we introduce a P-CSP enhanced learning module designed to aggregate features across different scales, depicted in Fig.Â 3. After feature entry, the feature matrix is divided into two branches. One branch should utilize a CBL layer and propagate through residual backpropagation, while the other should pass through a CBL layer and learn directly via residual blocks. The dashed line in the middle indicates that multiple residual blocks can be added as needed. Notably, the number of feature channels for both branches is halved during the learning process. This module enhances gradient combinations while also preserving or enhancing the scale connectivity accuracy in the presence of residual backpropagation. Finally, KNN and reshape methods are utilized to incorporate local feature information into the features. The output results are obtained after passing through a CBL layer.

4 Experiments and Results

4.1 Experimental Datasets

We utilized three datasets in our evaluation. These three datasets correspond to different scenarios in the actual world.

The S3DIS dataset (Armeni etÂ al., 2017) offers a comprehensive collection of indoor 3D point cloud data, representing a diverse range of environments. It consists of six distinct areas and includes 13 semantic categories, covering 11 different scene types, such as offices, meeting rooms, corridors, auditoriums, and open spaces with a total of 695,878,620 points.

The Toronto3D dataset (Tan etÂ al., 2020) was collected via the Teledyne Optech Maverick2 MLS system. It comprises millions of points covering a one-kilometre section of Avenue Road in Toronto. The dataset is divided into four sections, designated as L001 to L004. Section L002 is utilized for the assessment of semantic segmentation accuracy in outdoor urban environments.

The SensatUrban dataset (Hu etÂ al., 2021a) features a 3-billion-point photogrammetric point cloud from 7.6 square kilometers of Birmingham, Cambridge, and York, captured by UAV. It includes 13 semantic labels, with over 70% of points in ground, building, and vegetation categories, while classes like bicycles and railways account for just 0.025%, posing challenges for urban semantic segmentation.

Before training, we adopted voxel sampling to downsample the raw dataset. In the model preparation phase, we employed semantic-based weighted random sampling to mitigate category imbalances. 4.5 Ablation Study

We conducted ablation experiments on the SensatUrban dataset to evaluate MCNet. By removing or replacing different modules, we validated the effectiveness of the proposed components. The results of these experiments are presented in TableÂ 7.

Replace semantic-based weighted point sampling by randon sampling. The module for sampling based on semantic weights is removed and replaced with unweighted random sampling. The consequence of this operation is a reduction in the learning rate of small samples during the learning process, which in turn results in a decline in the performance of the model.

Removing the MCAE module. Remove the proposed MCAE module and replace it with the local feature aggregation (LAF) of RandLA-Net (Hu etÂ al., 2020). In theory, MCAE is capable of more effectively utilizing initial features and possesses a stronger local feature learning ability. It is therefore anticipated that this modification will result in a reduction in overall accuracy. As illustrated in Table 7, the removal of the MCAE has the most pronounced impact, underscoring its unique capacity for point feature learning.

Replacing P-CSP by MLP. The proposed P-CSP module improves the ability to fuse network features, reducing computational cost without sacrificing network accuracy. The most common method for point cloud feature fusion is multi-layer perception (MLP). Therefore, we replaced the P-CSP with MLP to test the contribution of the cross-stage feature aggregation module. TableÂ 7 indicates that replacing the P-CSP with MLP led to a slight drop in performance.

Removing neighborhood voting module. Remove the neighborhood voting module and directly output the semantic results of each point, while replacing the original loss function with the cross entropy loss function. This operation reduces the structural constraints on each point to a certain extent and does not allow the information of adjacent points to be combined in a more complete manner. As a consequence, the mIoU decreased by approximately 2%percent\%. As Fig.Â 10 demonstrates, the proposed neighborhood voting module worked as a means of label regularization, significantly enhancing the model trainingâ€™s speed of convergence and the segmentation taskâ€™s accuracy.

Overall, the preprocessing step involving weighted sampling is essential for effectively training small sample categories and improving the modelâ€™s overall mIoU. Among the components, the MCAE module exerts the most significant impact, highlighting the importance of enhancing local feature learning to boost model performance. In contrast, the removal of the P-CSP module significantly weakens the networkâ€™s ability to aggregate features, resulting in a noticeable performance drop. Similarly, eliminating the neighborhood voting module affects model accuracy and hampers the ability to fully exploit the learned features. These ablation experiments clearly demonstrate that the local modules we designed are key to achieving the modelâ€™s optimal performance.

5 Discussion

5.1 Semantic segmentation of large-scale and sample-imbalanced point clouds

Large-scale point cloud scenes in real-world applications consist of millions of data points. Effectively processing this data to extract point clouds with richer semantic information is a critical challenge. Moreover, real-world scenes encompass a diverse array of objects, with varying proportions. For example, Fig.Â 6 illustrates that in the SensatUrban dataset, ground and vegetation occupy the largest portions, while objects like bikes and railways are sparsely represented. This highly uneven distribution of samples can lead to under-sampling of small objects, resulting in poor segmentation performance for these categories. The significance of object segmentation should not be evaluated based solely on the percentage of occupied points; even minor objects can have a profound impact, such as vehicles and pedestrians, which play a pivotal role in applications such as autonomous driving.

Our approach incorporates a semantic-based weighted sampling module to down-sample the point cloud data. This method considers the varying proportions of different object point clouds, increasing the sampling rate for small object categories, thereby mitigating the issue of sample imbalance (see Fig.Â 5). As demonstrated in TableÂ 6, our method effectively segments the bike category, achieving a mIoU of 24.8%, whereas previous methods failed to segment this category. Additionally, the encoder-decoder structure and the feature extraction and fusion techniques we employed have shown robust performance across three large-scale datasets, confirming their efficacy in large-scale point cloud segmentation.

5.2 Limitations and future works

Our method is critically dependent on the scale and quality of the input dataset. Insufficient training data can significantly impair the modelâ€™s generalization capabilities. Additionally, our model leverages not only the coordinate information of the point clouds but also their RGB attributes. However, not all LiDAR systems are equipped to capture RGB information, and relying exclusively on point cloud coordinates can compromise segmentation performance. In real-world scenarios, point cloud data may be partially occluded due to complex architectural structures and intervening objects such as trees, leading to reduced segmentation accuracy.

In real-world large-scale scenarios, point cloud data are challenging to obtain, and their labeling relies on manual annotation, which is time-consuming, hindering the training of practical networks. Future work could focus on leveraging self-supervised or semi-supervised algorithms to reduce dependence on extensive labeled data. These approaches, combined with traditional machine learning algorithms, can potentially mitigate data dependency. To address the challenge of missing point cloud data, pre-processing steps could incorporate point cloud completion networks or generative models to generate comprehensive data. Additionally, as dataset sizes continue to grow, the development of more lightweight networks is an imperative direction for further research.

6 Conclusion

This study aims to advance semantic segmentation for large-scale, real-world point clouds, specifically targeting the recognition of under-sampled and small-sized objects. The primary contributions of this research are: First, a semantic-weighted sampling technique is proposed to increase the attention frequency on small-sized objects, thereby enhancing their recognition accuracy. Following this, a multilateral cascading encoding module is developed to effectively capture the structural characteristics of neighboring regions and integrate semantic data, resulting in more accurate segmentation. Furthermore, the point cross stage partial module is proposed for feature fusion. Finally, the output is augmented through neighborhood voting. Our research demonstrated that through this approach, we achieved performance that was comparable to other methods on three large-scale point cloud datasets, particularly excelling in semantic categories with small sample sizes. To comprehensively examine the properties of each component and to demonstrate the efficacy of our model, we also conducted ablation experiments. Overall, this study presents an advancement in the process of semantically segmenting large-scale and sample-imbalanced point clouds.
