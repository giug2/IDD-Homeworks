Abstract

In this work, we propose Salient Sparse Federated Learning (SSFL), a streamlined approach for sparse federated learning with efficient communication. SSFL identifies a sparse subnetwork prior to training, leveraging parameter saliency scores computed separately on local client data in non-IID scenarios, and then aggregated, to determine a global mask. Only the sparse model weights are communicated each round between the clients and the server. We validate SSFLâ€™s effectiveness using standard non-IID benchmarks, noting marked improvements in the sparsityâ€“accuracy trade-offs. Finally, we deploy our method in a real-world federated learning framework and report improvement in communication time.

1 Introduction

The ever-increasing size of deep learning models mandates distributed and collaborative training approaches. This is especially true in domains like the Internet of Things (IoT) and healthcare, where sensitive data is often spread across geographically distant sites. Privacy preservation is paramount in these settings, demanding models that can learn from distributed data without compromising individual data points. Conventional approaches pool data from disparate organizations or devices onto centralized platforms for model training. However, this risks exposing sensitive information. Consider a collaborative effort among hospitals to develop a neuroimaging model â€“ sharing patient data directly raises significant ethical and privacy concerns.

Federated Learning (FL) offers an elegant solution to this challenge. FL is a distributed learning paradigm designed for decentralized data McMahan and Ramage (2017). In the FL paradigm, a central server coordinates the training process and each participating client site (or device) communicates only the model parameters/parameter updates rather than raw data. Despite its advantages, FL faces challenges stemming from data heterogeneity (non-IID) Zhu etÂ al. (2021), limited communication bandwidth, and computational constraints on client devices Kairouz etÂ al. (2021); Li etÂ al. (2020b). In this work, we focus on improving the communication efficiency and computational cost within the FL framework.

2 Preliminaries

Federated Learning

We draw a contrast between the optimization process inherent to federated learning and distributed learning similar to McMahan etÂ al. (2017). There are distinctive features that mark federated optimization, clearly differentiating it from the standard distributed optimization problems. These include, but are not limited to: (1) Non-IID nature of the training data, where the local data might not be representative of the population distribution. (2) Unbalanced data, where clients varying amount of data (3) Massively distributed, where often the number of clients might exceed the average number of samples per client and (4) Limited communication, where communication among the clients in the decentralized setting or between clients and the server in the centralized setting are infrequent, as the connections are slow and expensive.

One of our primary focus in this work is to reduce this costly communication between server and the clients in the centralized or between clients in the decentralized setting, when the participating clients have non-IID and unbalanced data. We achieve this by discovering a sub-network based on the data distributions at each local sites and transmitting the parameters of this sub-network only at each communication round rğ‘Ÿr. In each of these communication rounds a fixed set of K^^ğ¾\hat{K} clients are sampled from all clients Kğ¾K and federated training is continued on the selected sub-network of those clients. The general federated optimization problem encountered is detailed next.

3 Related Work

In this section, we briefly discuss the related work in sparse and efficient federated learning. We first discuss the related work in (1) neural network pruning, followed by (2) Sparsity, pruning and efficiency in FL (3) Pruning at initialization and (4) Pruning at initialization in FL.

Neural Network Pruning

Like most areas in deep learning, model pruning has a rich history and is mostly considered to have been explored first in the 90â€™s (Janowsky, 1989; LeCun etÂ al., 1990; Reed, 1993). The central aim of model pruning is to find subnetworks within larger architectures by removing connections. Model pruning is very attractive for a number of reasons, especially for real-time applications on resource-constraint edge devices which is often the case in FL and collaborative learning. Pruning large networks can significantly reduce the demands of inference Elsen etÂ al. (2020) or hardware designed to exploit sparsity Cerebras (2019); Pool etÂ al. (2021). More recently the lottery ticket hypothesis was proposed which predicts the existence of subnetworks of initializations within dense networks, which when trained in isolation from scratch can match in accuracy of a fully trained dense network Frankle and Carbin (2019). This rejuvenated the field of sparse deep learning Renda etÂ al. (2020); Chen etÂ al. (2020) and more recently the interest spilled over into sparse reinforcement learning (RL) as well Arnob etÂ al. (2021); Sokar etÂ al. (2021). Pruning in deep learning can broadly be classified into three categories: techniques that induce sparsity before training and at initialization Lee etÂ al. (2018); Wang etÂ al. (2020); Tanaka etÂ al. (2020), during training Zhu and Gupta (2018); Ma etÂ al. (2019); Yang etÂ al. (2019); Ohib etÂ al. (2022) and post-training Han etÂ al. (2015); Frankle etÂ al. (2021). In Federated Learning applications, pruning at initialization is especially appealing due to the potential for communication cost reduction as no further overheads are required during the costly FL training phase.

Sparsity, pruning and efficiency in FL

Federated Learning (FL) has evolved significantly since its inception, introducing a myriad of techniques to enhance efficiency, communication, and computation. Interest in efficiency has increased further with the proposal of the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) by Frankle and Carbin (2019). However, identifying these tickets has traditionally been challenging. In the original work, the strategy employed to extract these subnetworks was to iteratively train and prune the network until the target sparsity was attained. However, this process is unsustainable in the FL setting and would in contrast make the training even more expensive due to the significant increase in training duration. Variations of this idea have been employed in the FL setting, as demonstrated by Li etÂ al. (2020a); Jiang etÂ al. (2022); Seo etÂ al. (2021); Liu etÂ al. (2021) and Babakniya etÂ al. (2022). However, many of them either suffer from the same issue of iterative pruning and retraining which is extremely costly or decline in performance when this train, prune and retrain cycle is skipped.

Pruning and sparsity are highly advantageous in federated learning (FL), especially since many applications involve clients with limited resources. As sparse models can be substantially efficient during inference (Li etÂ al., 2016), training sparse local models in resource constrained local sites could be an effective approach. In FL setting, pruning has been explored with mixed success, often employing a range of heuristics. Works, such as by Munir etÂ al. (2021) focused on pruning resource-constrained clients, Yu etÂ al. (2021) on using gated dynamic sparsity, Liu etÂ al. (2021) performs a pre-training on the clients to get mask information, and Jiang etÂ al. (2022) relies on an initial mask selected at a particular client, followed by a FedAvg-like algorithm that performs mask readjustment every Î”â€‹RÎ”ğ‘…\Delta R rounds. However, they either do not leverage sparsity fully for communication efficiency, suffer from performance degradation or is not designed for the challenging, realistic non-IID setting.

Works by Dai etÂ al. (2022) and Bibikar etÂ al. (2022) utilize dynamic sparsity similar to Evci etÂ al. (2020) and extends it to the FL regime, however, they both start with random masks as an initialization. A recent focus has also been placed on random masking and optimizing the mask Setayesh etÂ al. (2022) or training the mask itself instead of training the weights at all Isik etÂ al. (2022). Furthermore, in light of the recent surge in the development of large language models (LLMs), the exploration of federated learning in this domain is taking place, although relatively recently Fan etÂ al. (2023); Yu etÂ al. (2023). Investigating sparsity and efficiency within this context presents a fascinating and promising avenue for research.

Pruning at Initialization

The work on the lottery ticket hypothesis (LTH), which showed that from early in training and often at initialization, there exist subnetworks that can be trained in isolation to full accuracy, opened up the prospect for pruning at initialization (PaI). Several methods have been proposed for pruning at initialization such as SNIP (Lee etÂ al., 2018), which aims to prune weights that are least important for the loss, GraSP (Wang etÂ al., 2020), which prunes weights aiming to preserve gradient flow and SynFlow (Tanaka etÂ al., 2020) aims to iteratively prune weights with the lowest â€œsynaptic strengthsâ€. These methods have been later studied in depth by Frankle etÂ al. (2021), where they provide interesting insights about the efficacy of these methods, including the observation that SNIP Lee etÂ al. (2018), which is a variation of the gradient based connection saliency criterion (Mozer and Smolensky, 1988), consistently performs well. Hence, we choose gradient based connection saliency as the basis for our method.

Frankle etÂ al. (2021) found that PaI methods tend to prune networks at the layer level rather than the connection level in single-node deep learning scenarios. In sectionÂ 5, we explore the applicability of this observation to non-IID federated learning settings

Pruning at initialization in FL

We did not discover any study that directly employs connection importance at initialization in the Federated Learning (FL) setting. Nevertheless, research such as Jiang etÂ al. (2022) incorporated gradient-based importance criterion, SNIP Lee etÂ al. (2018); DeÂ Jorge etÂ al. (2020), within their comparative benchmarks, but calculating such scores only on the local data of one particular client site in the FL setting, resulting in subpar performance. Similarly, Huang etÂ al. (2022) presents connection saliency in their benchmark but assumes that to calculate meaningful parameter importance scores, the server has access to a public dataset on which such saliency scores can be calculated. However, that is in general unlikely to happen and would lead to privacy concerns if such constraints were enforced.

In this work, we demonstrate that it is possible to find sparse masks or sub-networks through gradient based connection saliency measures at initialization, considering the distribution of training data at local client sites. Since, to the best of our knowledge we did not find any comparable pruning at initialization methods in the non-IID FL setting, we compare our method to approaches such as Dai etÂ al. 4 Proposed Approach

We now introduce our parameter-importance based sparse FL framework, Salient Sparse Federated Learning (SSFL). The principle steps in our sparse FL training mechanism are: (1) compute a parameter saliency score based on the decentralized client data distribution; (2) find a common sparse-network for the local client models based on the distributed parameter saliency scores; (3) train the common sub-network in the client models and communicate only a subset of the parameters in each communication round. In sectionÂ 4.1 we elaborate the procedure to find sub-networks before training and in sectionÂ 4.2 we detail our federated training method.

6 Conclusion

In this paper, we introduce SSFL, a new federated learning approach for training local sparse models via efficient communication with robust performance. SSFL reduces communication costs and bandwidth usage in FL training. We use a distributed gradient-based saliency method to assess parameter importance, ensuring our approach is tailored to the local data at client sites. This results in a global sparse mask that is aware of client data characteristics. SSFL demonstrates strong performance across various tasks. By identifying sparse masks prior to training, it minimizes the need to transmit dense parameters, saving time and bandwidth. Additionally, we apply SSFL in a real-world federated learning setting, achieving improved wall-time performance.

A.2 Generating non-IID data parition with Dirichlet Distribution

In this section, we provide the necessary background on generating non-identical data distribution in the client sites using the Dirichlet Distribution, specifically for the context of federated learning.

non-IID data in FL

Federated Learning (FL), as introduced by McMahan and Ramage [2017], is a framework designed for training models on decentralized data while preserving privacy. It utilizes the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm where each device, or client, receives a model from a central server, performs stochastic gradient descent (SGD) on its local data, and sends the models back for aggregation. Unlike data-center training where data batches are often IID (independent and identically distributed), FL typically deals with non-IID data distributions across different clients. Hence, to evaluate federated learning it is crucial to not make the IID assumption and instead generate non-IID data among clients for evaluation Hsu etÂ al. [2019].
