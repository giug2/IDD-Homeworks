Mitigating Group Bias in Federated Learning for Heterogeneous Devices

Khotso Selialia, Yasra Chandio, Fatima M. Anwar
University of Massachusetts Amherst
Abstract

Federated Learning is emerging as a privacy-preserving model training approach in distributed edge applications. As such, most edge deployments are heterogeneous in nature i.e., their sensing capabilities and environments vary across deployments. This edge heterogeneity violates the independence and identical distribution (IID) property of local data across clients and produces biased global models i.e. models that contribute to unfair decision-making and discrimination against a particular community or a group. Existing bias mitigation techniques only focus on bias generated from label heterogeneity in non-IID data without accounting for domain variations due to feature heterogeneity and do not address global group-fairness property.

Our work proposes a group-fair FL framework that minimizes group-bias while preserving privacy and without resource utilization overhead. Our main idea is to leverage average conditional probabilities to compute a cross-domain group importance weights derived from heterogeneous training data to optimize the performance of the worst-performing group using a modified multiplicative weights update method. Additionally, we propose regularization techniques to minimize the difference between the worst and best-performing groups while making sure through our thresholding mechanism to strike a balance between bias reduction and group performance degradation. Our evaluation of human emotion recognition and image classification benchmarks assesses the fair decision-making of our framework in real-world heterogeneous settings.

1 Introduction

Federated Learning (FL) is a privacy-preserving machine learning (ML) technique that trains local models on decentralized devices which get aggregated at the server into a global model. FL is increasingly being used in critical and privacy-sensitive computer vision applications such as medical diagnosisÂ [42, 18, 28], next-character predictionÂ [51], activity recognitionÂ [49, 41, 15], and human emotion recognitionÂ [13, 33, 55]. Yet, increasing concerns arise over biased FL-trained models that excel for specific groups while underperforming for others.

Such bias and discrimination can be detrimental to society and erode public trust in ML models and their applicationsÂ [8]. Research has shown that racial bias towards minority groups in electronic health records is prevalent within the field of medical analysis, leading to potential disparities in the treatment received by these groupsÂ [52]. A primary cause for biased model, as cited in literature, is label heterogeneity in non independent and identically distributed (non-IID) data across clients. This stems from varying label distributions across clients due to the distinct environments of data collection devicesÂ [23].

In this work, we identify feature heterogeneity (varied features within the same group across clients) as another significant source of bias in FL models. This heterogeneity in data arises from domain variations, causing local client data failing to share similar feature distributionsÂ [36]. A common real-world domain variation is device variation. For example, devices with low-quality sensors/cameras may capture images with distortions such as Gaussian noise, yielding images that have different feature distributions than images taken with similar but high-quality sensors. This discrepancy in features among clients leads to group-bias. Here, â€groupsâ€ are defined by shared sensitive attributes like gender, race, or labelÂ [39].

Prior work proposes several techniques to mitigate overall bias and address fairness and discriminationÂ [38, 14, 25, 56]. While these techniques guarantee fairness with respect to label heterogeneity, they do not guarantee group-fairness in settings with domain variationsÂ [23]. Our work collectively addresses group fairness across clients with feature heterogeneity in data, as well as strives for fair performance across all groups within a client. Our contributions are two-fold: firstly, to illustrate the impact of domain variation on group-bias in real-world scenarios, we present a preliminary study examining FL group-bias in context of human emotion recognitionÂ [2] using human-face image data. Our study demonstrates the problem of feature heterogeneity towards group-biasÂ [7, 50], and highlights the need for fairness not only across clients, but also at the group level to avoid discrimination against certain groups (Â§3).

In doing so, we propose a group-fair FL framework that is built upon the idea of minimizing the worst-group performance loss. Essentially, our high-level idea is to compute group importance weights that represent the likelihood of data points belonging to a certain group and scale the group losses with these weights using the multiplicative update method Â [4]. But importance weighting performance vanishes due to the problem of exploding weight norms (caused by scaling the loss function with importance weights)Â [6] in large models with many parameters risking over-fitting. We propose neural network regularization techniquesÂ [40] for Regularized Multiplicative Update (MWR) to overcome this failure and to mitigate group-bias. We also propose a method for determining the performance thresholds for the worst- and best-performing groups. These thresholds ensure that the worst-performance group achieves at least a certain level of performance while the performance of the well-performing group does not fall below a given threshold. We summarize our contributions below.

Enabling Privacy-preserving Group Fairness: We highlight the notion of group fairness across clients in FL settings and propose a Multiplicative Weight (MW) update method to mitigate bias due to feature-heterogeneity. Our approach requires an estimate of the global group importance weights, which we compute as a mixture of cross-domain likelihood estimates of heterogeneous local data across clients in a privacy-preserving manner.

Ensuring Optimality through Regularization: We extend our approach by incorporating L1 regularization technique to increase its effectiveness in mitigating group bias, which we call Multiplicative Weight update with Regularization (MWR). It combats diverging weight norms that fail to converge to a model that optimizes worst group performance.

Satisfying Worst- and Best-group Performance: We propose a group performance threshold mechanism that assesses the importance of each groupâ€™s performance. We then ensure that MWR optimizes the accuracy of the worst-performing group to a desirable level while maintaining the accuracy of the best-performing group.

Implementation and Evaluation (code will be open-sourced upon publication): We implement and evaluate the Mâ€‹Wâ€‹Rğ‘€ğ‘Šğ‘…MWR method against existing methods on real-world human emotion recognition (CAER-SÂ [31]) and state-of-the-art image classification benchmark datasets (CIFAR10Â [27], MNISTÂ [29], and FashionMNISTÂ [54]). Our findings show that Mâ€‹Wâ€‹Rğ‘€ğ‘Šğ‘…MWR outperforms baselines methods, boosting accuracy of the worst groupâ€™s accuracy from 333% to 252525% without substantially degrading the best groupâ€™s performance.

2 Related Work

In this section, we provide a brief overview of bias studies in ML in general as well as group bias in FL.

Bias in Machine Learning. The concept of bias in ML refers to a modelâ€™s tendency of favoring or predisposition towards a particular individual or group in decision-making. Consequently, biased ML models and their decision-making become unfair towards certain groups [37]. The most common sources of this bias in centralized learning settings include prejudice, underestimation, and negative legacyÂ [1]. Several techniques have been adopted to mitigate this bias in ML applications [21, 19, 25, 57, 43, 35, 26]. Despite the effectiveness of these techniques in mitigating bias in centralized learning, they can not be directly utilized in FL settings because they need to access features of all data across clients, making it difficult to apply bias elimination techniques without compromising data privacy.

Group Bias. A group is defined as a subset of data with samples categorized by a specific sensitive attribute such as race, gender, class or labelÂ [8]. In this work, the notion of group is based on partitioning data with respect to classes or labels. For example, in human emotion recognition, all images that are labeled â€happyâ€ are in the group â€happyâ€. Group bias in FL is the difference in classification performance among groups based on a shared global model.

Mitigating Bias. Techniques such as data preprocessing, algorithmic fairness, and fairness-aware model training [24, 25, 56, 5, 14] have been proposed to mitigate overall bias to address fairness and discrimination. Prior work on bias mitigation can be divided into three categories: (1) client-fairness aims to maintain similar performances for all clients participating, regardless of their domain differencesÂ [38]; (2) collaborative-fairness makes rewards proportional to each clientâ€™s relative contribution to the federated learning processÂ [16]; (3) group-fairness develops models and applications that maintain similar performances for individual groups across clients in heterogeneous scenarios[58, 10]. However, these techniques mostly focus on solving bias in label heterogeneity scenarios without accounting for cases where local data features across clients are heterogeneous and do not guarantee group-fairness.

6 Conclusion

In this work, we study the FL group-bias problem that arises in decentralized heterogeneous edge deployments. We propose a bias mitigation framework centered around Multiplicative Weight update with Regularization (MWR) using average conditional probabilities approach that enforces group-fairness in FL with heterogeneous local data across clients. Preliminary study demonstrates that heterogeneous features in local group data across clients leads to group biased federated learning models towards groups in local data of minority clients. We also demonstrate empirically that this induced group bias has severe consequences of degrading certain group performance on a subset of clients. However, our framework MWR can mitigate this group bias in federated learning by optimizing the performance of worst groups while not substantially compromising the best performing groups with reasonable system costs compared with other federated learning methods.
