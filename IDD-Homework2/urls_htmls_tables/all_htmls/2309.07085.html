Mitigating Group Bias in Federated Learning for Heterogeneous Devices

Khotso Selialia, Yasra Chandio, Fatima M. Anwar
University of Massachusetts Amherst
Abstract

Federated Learning is emerging as a privacy-preserving model training approach in distributed edge applications. As such, most edge deployments are heterogeneous in nature i.e., their sensing capabilities and environments vary across deployments. This edge heterogeneity violates the independence and identical distribution (IID) property of local data across clients and produces biased global models i.e. models that contribute to unfair decision-making and discrimination against a particular community or a group. Existing bias mitigation techniques only focus on bias generated from label heterogeneity in non-IID data without accounting for domain variations due to feature heterogeneity and do not address global group-fairness property.

Our work proposes a group-fair FL framework that minimizes group-bias while preserving privacy and without resource utilization overhead. Our main idea is to leverage average conditional probabilities to compute a cross-domain group importance weights derived from heterogeneous training data to optimize the performance of the worst-performing group using a modified multiplicative weights update method. Additionally, we propose regularization techniques to minimize the difference between the worst and best-performing groups while making sure through our thresholding mechanism to strike a balance between bias reduction and group performance degradation. Our evaluation of human emotion recognition and image classification benchmarks assesses the fair decision-making of our framework in real-world heterogeneous settings.

1 Introduction

Federated Learning (FL) is a privacy-preserving machine learning (ML) technique that trains local models on decentralized devices which get aggregated at the server into a global model. FL is increasingly being used in critical and privacy-sensitive computer vision applications such as medical diagnosis [42, 18, 28], next-character prediction [51], activity recognition [49, 41, 15], and human emotion recognition [13, 33, 55]. Yet, increasing concerns arise over biased FL-trained models that excel for specific groups while underperforming for others.

Such bias and discrimination can be detrimental to society and erode public trust in ML models and their applications [8]. Research has shown that racial bias towards minority groups in electronic health records is prevalent within the field of medical analysis, leading to potential disparities in the treatment received by these groups [52]. A primary cause for biased model, as cited in literature, is label heterogeneity in non independent and identically distributed (non-IID) data across clients. This stems from varying label distributions across clients due to the distinct environments of data collection devices [23].

In this work, we identify feature heterogeneity (varied features within the same group across clients) as another significant source of bias in FL models. This heterogeneity in data arises from domain variations, causing local client data failing to share similar feature distributions [36]. A common real-world domain variation is device variation. For example, devices with low-quality sensors/cameras may capture images with distortions such as Gaussian noise, yielding images that have different feature distributions than images taken with similar but high-quality sensors. This discrepancy in features among clients leads to group-bias. Here, ”groups” are defined by shared sensitive attributes like gender, race, or label [39].

Prior work proposes several techniques to mitigate overall bias and address fairness and discrimination [38, 14, 25, 56]. While these techniques guarantee fairness with respect to label heterogeneity, they do not guarantee group-fairness in settings with domain variations [23]. Our work collectively addresses group fairness across clients with feature heterogeneity in data, as well as strives for fair performance across all groups within a client. Our contributions are two-fold: firstly, to illustrate the impact of domain variation on group-bias in real-world scenarios, we present a preliminary study examining FL group-bias in context of human emotion recognition [2] using human-face image data. Our study demonstrates the problem of feature heterogeneity towards group-bias [7, 50], and highlights the need for fairness not only across clients, but also at the group level to avoid discrimination against certain groups (§3).

In doing so, we propose a group-fair FL framework that is built upon the idea of minimizing the worst-group performance loss. Essentially, our high-level idea is to compute group importance weights that represent the likelihood of data points belonging to a certain group and scale the group losses with these weights using the multiplicative update method  [4]. But importance weighting performance vanishes due to the problem of exploding weight norms (caused by scaling the loss function with importance weights) [6] in large models with many parameters risking over-fitting. We propose neural network regularization techniques [40] for Regularized Multiplicative Update (MWR) to overcome this failure and to mitigate group-bias. We also propose a method for determining the performance thresholds for the worst- and best-performing groups. These thresholds ensure that the worst-performance group achieves at least a certain level of performance while the performance of the well-performing group does not fall below a given threshold. We summarize our contributions below.

Enabling Privacy-preserving Group Fairness: We highlight the notion of group fairness across clients in FL settings and propose a Multiplicative Weight (MW) update method to mitigate bias due to feature-heterogeneity. Our approach requires an estimate of the global group importance weights, which we compute as a mixture of cross-domain likelihood estimates of heterogeneous local data across clients in a privacy-preserving manner.

Ensuring Optimality through Regularization: We extend our approach by incorporating L1 regularization technique to increase its effectiveness in mitigating group bias, which we call Multiplicative Weight update with Regularization (MWR). It combats diverging weight norms that fail to converge to a model that optimizes worst group performance.

Satisfying Worst- and Best-group Performance: We propose a group performance threshold mechanism that assesses the importance of each group’s performance. We then ensure that MWR optimizes the accuracy of the worst-performing group to a desirable level while maintaining the accuracy of the best-performing group.

Implementation and Evaluation (code will be open-sourced upon publication): We implement and evaluate the M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR method against existing methods on real-world human emotion recognition (CAER-S [31]) and state-of-the-art image classification benchmark datasets (CIFAR10 [27], MNIST [29], and FashionMNIST [54]). Our findings show that M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR outperforms baselines methods, boosting accuracy of the worst group’s accuracy from 333% to 252525% without substantially degrading the best group’s performance.

2 Related Work

In this section, we provide a brief overview of bias studies in ML in general as well as group bias in FL.

Bias in Machine Learning. The concept of bias in ML refers to a model’s tendency of favoring or predisposition towards a particular individual or group in decision-making. Consequently, biased ML models and their decision-making become unfair towards certain groups [37]. The most common sources of this bias in centralized learning settings include prejudice, underestimation, and negative legacy [1]. Several techniques have been adopted to mitigate this bias in ML applications [21, 19, 25, 57, 43, 35, 26]. Despite the effectiveness of these techniques in mitigating bias in centralized learning, they can not be directly utilized in FL settings because they need to access features of all data across clients, making it difficult to apply bias elimination techniques without compromising data privacy.

Group Bias. A group is defined as a subset of data with samples categorized by a specific sensitive attribute such as race, gender, class or label [8]. In this work, the notion of group is based on partitioning data with respect to classes or labels. For example, in human emotion recognition, all images that are labeled ”happy” are in the group ”happy”. Group bias in FL is the difference in classification performance among groups based on a shared global model.

Mitigating Bias. Techniques such as data preprocessing, algorithmic fairness, and fairness-aware model training [24, 25, 56, 5, 14] have been proposed to mitigate overall bias to address fairness and discrimination. Prior work on bias mitigation can be divided into three categories: (1) client-fairness aims to maintain similar performances for all clients participating, regardless of their domain differences [38]; (2) collaborative-fairness makes rewards proportional to each client’s relative contribution to the federated learning process [16]; (3) group-fairness develops models and applications that maintain similar performances for individual groups across clients in heterogeneous scenarios[58, 10]. However, these techniques mostly focus on solving bias in label heterogeneity scenarios without accounting for cases where local data features across clients are heterogeneous and do not guarantee group-fairness.

3.2 Key Findings

Non-IID Study. To show the FL model’s unfairness, we investigate how the biased global model treats local groups on each client differently. The resulting global model is evaluated on each client’s local test data (with a similar distortion level as the training data) by comparing the accuracy of the best group with the worst group. As the distortion level of samples on client D𝐷D and E𝐸E increases, the difference between best and worst group performances increases. This indicates that heterogeneous local features across clients lead to local group-bias, and the model becomes more biased towards certain groups at the cost of worsening performance for already biased groups.

Limitation of Federated Averaging. Next, we empirically study how heterogeneous local data distributions impact local model weights. Our non-IID study and problems in typical federated averaging schemes show that even on two different applications and datasets, the same unfair model behavior is produced by domain-varying heterogeneous scenarios. For this reason, there is a need for bias mitigation methods to reduce the existence of severe consequences such as the model’s performance degradation in critical applications such as human emotion recognition and failure to adapt to dynamic environments with heterogeneous settings.

4.2 Enabling Privacy-preserving Group Fairness

The core idea of our approach is to normalize the losses by the group importance weights, wgsubscript𝑤𝑔w_{g}, as outlined in Equation 1. The group importance weights can be easily computed if the information about the data across all the clients is known at a single location, such as in centralized learning [17]. In FL settings, such a global view is not present at the server or any of the clients. Therefore, we need to estimate wgsubscript𝑤𝑔w_{g} while preserving the privacy of the client data. To solve this problem, our solution estimates an approximate denominator of wgsubscript𝑤𝑔w_{g} using group likelihoods mixture across clients, which is performed in two steps described next.

First, each client performs feature extraction on its local data and generates corresponding feature maps for individual groups using a deep neural network consisting of convolution and fully-connected layers. Convolutional layers reduce group data dimension by generating feature maps via sliding convolution layer’s kernels across input images to extract their important features. The generated feature maps are transformed into a column vector of logits, which are used to compute group likelihood mixture at each client. To do so, we forward mini-batch features through the Softmax function and then compute the average likelihood for each group in a batch. We maintain these mini-batch average likelihoods and select the one that corresponds to the mini-batch that yields the minimum loss. Each client then shares its group average likelihood estimates with the server.

5 Evaluation

In this section, we evaluate our Multiplicative Weight (M​W𝑀𝑊MW) update and Multiplicative Weight update with Regularization (M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR) group-bias mitigating techniques on four datasets: human emotion recognition (CAER-S) and image classification (CIFAR10, MNIST, and FashionMNIST). We benchmark our approach against the standard bias mitigation techniques in FL.

5.1 Experiment Testbed

Our evaluation setup uses the same number of clients, data partitioning scheme, and other learning components (such as learning rate, train/test split, batch size, epochs, rounds) described in §3.1 unless stated otherwise.

5.2 Efficacy and Robustness Analysis

We next experimentally evaluate the efficacy and robustness of our vanilla approach MW, our advanced MWR group-bias mitigation technique, and the baselines.

5.2.1 Effect on Group Bias

We evaluate M​W𝑀𝑊MW and M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR’s group-bias mitigation effectiveness using: (i) best- and worst- group performance, (ii) group variance per client, and (iii) average group accuracy per client on three datasets on low-grade distortion mimicking common real-world heterogeneity [23].

Figure 4 presents the accuracy for the worst- and best-performing groups on our benchmark datasets with low-grade distortion suitable for the corresponding dataset. In Figure 4(a) we can see the difference between worst- and best-performing on the CAER-S dataset. Our vanilla MW approach improves the worst group accuracy over FedAVg by 13% and 30% for groups with 0 variance noise (A, B, and C) and 0.03 variance noise (D and E), respectively. We observe a similar performance over AFL and FedNTD for the first 3 groups and more than 15% improvement for the D and E groups. However, M​W𝑀𝑊MW reduces the best group accuracy by ∼similar-to\sim1% for groups with low Gaussian noise variance. Applying regularization in M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR solves this problem and improves the worst and best group accuracies. In Figure 4(b), we show group accuracies on the CIFAR10 dataset; the vanilla M​W𝑀𝑊MW approach performs similarly to the CAER-S dataset. However, the M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR approach significantly improves the worst group accuracy by 10% for groups with high noise variance. In Figure 4(c), we see that all the baselines improved the accuracy for all groups while our approach M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR struggled due to nuances of MNIST. The results suggest that the increased noise levels might influence the accuracy performance, and one might expect to see a decreasing trend in the average worst-off group accuracies when the noise levels are elevated. However, no consistent trend is observed when the noise level gradually increases. This means that even when increased, the noise level doesn’t necessarily degrade the performance linearly or in a predictable pattern for the worst-off groups. Instead, the model’s performance remains relatively resilient, and the proposed M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR method, particularly, shows robustness in mitigating group-bias even under higher feature heterogeneity or distortion levels. It should be noted that the goal is to maintain the same best group performance while improving the worst group performance. Any improvements are the added benefits of our approach.

Table 1 shows the variance in accuracy performance on low-grade noise across baselines, M​W𝑀𝑊MW, and M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR on CAER-S, CIFAR10 and MNIST datasets. Variance is a measure of data spread, and a lower variance implies a higher fairness capability for a group-bias mitigation method [12]. M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR approach mitigates group-bias by minimizing the individual group accuracy variance for each client on unprivileged groups with distorted data and groups without distortions. During model aggregation, FedAvg fails to improve the performance of unprivileged groups because it focuses more attention on majority groups [39]. This implies that the performance of the already struggling groups will be compromised due to the presence of distortions in the data points of these groups. Furthermore, our M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR approach mitigates group-bias without compromising the overall utility (average accuracy) for all groups compared to other approaches.

Takeaway: M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR successfully reduces group-bias, as evidenced by its performance in three key metrics: best-worst group accuracy, variance, and average group accuracy.

To evaluate the robustness of MWR against higher feature heterogeneity, we increase the noise level in the partitioned datasets and evaluate the performance of all the techniques using the same three metrics from the previous section. We add Gaussian noise with a 0.050.050.05, 0.110.110.11, and 1.01.01.0 variance on the CAER-S, CIFAR10, and MNIST datasets.

We assess the model performance for group-bias mitigation on high heterogeneity/distortion. Overall, the final results for the best- and worst-group accuracy with M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR technique are similar to the previous section, as shown in Figure 5. However, it is worth noting that, compared to AFL and FedNDT, worst-group accuracies drop in the vanilla M​W𝑀𝑊MW approach for the CAER-S dataset. This highlights the need for our proposed regularization, especially at high noise levels. For the CIFAR10, both M​W𝑀𝑊MW and M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR are robust against high feature heterogeneity/distortion. Whereas, for the MNIST, FedNDT outperforms our approach, which is unsurprising since the FedNDT is optimized for MNIST.

Lastly, in Table 1, we observe that M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR, even with high-grade noise, improves the accuracy of all groups compared to baselines on CAER-S and improves the accuracy of worst-performing groups in CIFAR10. While M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR did not improve much on MNSIT compared to FedNDT, it has reduced the variance overall. This shows that the group variance shifted in high-grade noise for baselines, while it reduced consistently for M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR. Therefore, all groups are optimized for a uniform performance in accuracy, even in high-grade noise. On the other hand, AFL and FedNDT optimize for individual clients without regard to optimization for individual groups. While AFL and FedNDT improve the performance of best-performing groups, we can see that they do not guarantee the accuracy of poor-performing groups.

Takeaway. MWR mitigates group-bias by improving for worst-performing groups without significantly impacting the best-performing group, even with high heterogeneity.

5.3 Privacy Analysis

This section evaluates the impact of incorporating differential privacy on local data probability distributions shared with the server to compute the importance weights in M​W​R𝑀𝑊𝑅MWR. Applying differential privacy is important because it enables privacy preservation for each client’s local meta-data (the probability of selecting each local group per client), which might inadvertently disclose sensitive details about local clients’ probable choices of groups.

We use the FashionMNIST dataset with LeNet [3] trained on five clients for this evaluation. 6 Conclusion

In this work, we study the FL group-bias problem that arises in decentralized heterogeneous edge deployments. We propose a bias mitigation framework centered around Multiplicative Weight update with Regularization (MWR) using average conditional probabilities approach that enforces group-fairness in FL with heterogeneous local data across clients. Preliminary study demonstrates that heterogeneous features in local group data across clients leads to group biased federated learning models towards groups in local data of minority clients. We also demonstrate empirically that this induced group bias has severe consequences of degrading certain group performance on a subset of clients. However, our framework MWR can mitigate this group bias in federated learning by optimizing the performance of worst groups while not substantially compromising the best performing groups with reasonable system costs compared with other federated learning methods.
