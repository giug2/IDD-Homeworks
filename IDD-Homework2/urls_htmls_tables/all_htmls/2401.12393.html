A Learning-based Declarative Privacy-Preserving Framework
for Federated Data Management

Hong Guan, Summer Gautier, Deepti Gupta†, Rajan Hari Ambrish, Yancheng Wang,
Harsha Lakamsani, Dhanush Giriyan, Saajan Maslanka, Chaowei Xiao‡, Yingzhen Yang, Jia Zou Arizona State University, Texas A&M University-Central Texas†, University of Wisconsin at Madison‡ United States
(2024)
Abstract.

It is challenging to balance the privacy and accuracy for federated query processing over multiple private data silos. In this work, we will demonstrate an end-to-end workflow for automating an emerging privacy-preserving technique that uses a deep learning model trained using the Differentially-Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) algorithm to replace portions of actual data to answer a query. Our proposed novel declarative privacy-preserving workflow allows users to specify ”what private information to protect” rather than ”how to protect”. Under the hood, the system automatically chooses query-model transformation plans as well as hyper-parameters. At the same time, the proposed workflow also allows human experts to review and tune the selected privacy-preserving mechanism for audit/compliance, and optimization purposes.

1. Introduction

Data silos, which do not share data, widely exist inside and across various organizations and businesses. For example, a recent study published in Science (Borgman and Brand, 2022) showed that in most universities, the learning management systems for teaching classrooms, registrar systems, library systems, university portfolios for faculty, etc., cannot access each other’s data. Such data blindness hinders the improvement of operational efficiency and the advancement of fields of study. Data silos also widely exist across organizations. For example, in women’s health, patients meet with their obstetrics/gynecology, who refers externally to a lactation consultant, who refers externally to a therapist, and the chain continues (Jain, 2022). However, existing data platforms do not support efficient data sharing among these practitioners (Jain, 2022). Privacy is one of the biggest concerns that prevent these data silos from being broken down.

To address the issue, in this work, we focus on an emerging query-model transformation approach (Zeighami et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023a), leveraging the new advancement in AI/ML. We propose a novel declarative privacy-preserving federated data management workflow, which automatically chooses deep learning models trained using differentially private stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD) (Abadi et al., 2016) to replace the data and answer queries that involve sensitive/private attributes. At runtime, The privacy-preserving model takes the input parameters of the query as features and predicts the query results. The model is trained over the training samples extracted from the private dataset to be protected. Because DP-SGD protects the training process, the approach is robust against model inversion attacks (Fredrikson et al., 2015) and membership inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017). Intuitively, it indicates that the attacks cannot use the model to regenerate any data that exists in the training dataset and cannot accurately infer whether a data point (if without prior membership knowledge about the data point) was part of the training dataset under certain guarantees.

Motivating Example 1. In a border security check scenario, the officer wants to query the surveillance camera images from all airports near the city of San Francisco, trying to find out the total number of occurrences of a white male passenger with black hair wearing a black jacket at a height of approximately 6 feet and a weight of around 180 pounds, who arrived within the last 24 hours. Due to the data protection policy of the Department of Homeland Security (DHS), each airport stores its camera image datasets locally. The person objects extracted from the virtual collection of all surveillance videos from all airports is called Person. The officer issues a query like ”SELECT count(*) FROM Person p WHERE p.wearing = ’black’ AND p.haircolor=’black’ AND p.height ¡6.1 AND p.height ¿ 5.9 AND p.weight ¡ 190 AND p.weight ¿ 170 AND p.date ¿ ’2024-01-01:07:00’ AND p.date ¡ ’2024-01-02:06:59’ AND p.location in [’OAK’, ’SFO’, ’SJC’]”. However, each airport is a separate data silo, and they do not directly share data because of data privacy concerns. To solve the privacy-preserving data-sharing problem, using the technology proposed in this paper, each airport will locally train a differentially private deep learning model on their own data  (Abadi et al., 2016) and share the model with the DHS officer for sensitive query processing. Given features such as clothes color, hair color, estimated height and weight, and time range, the model predicts how many times person-objects satisfying the pattern have been spotted in the underlying images. This way, each local data silo, which is used to train the model, is protected under the differential privacy guarantee by DP-SGD. It means no attacker can accurately infer whether a passenger exists in the video dataset that is used to train the model or recover any passenger’s information.

Motivating Example 2. Monitoring Alzheimer’s patients in real-world scenarios is crucial for effective disease management, emergency response, prompt medical intervention, and improving care quality. To facilitate decision-making during the monitoring process, it is important to have patients’ healthcare providers share sensitive information, such as their clinical records and diagnosis details with the caregiving organization. Consider a query like ”SELECT predict_treatment(*) FROM Clinical_Records crec NATURAL JOIN Diagnosis_Records drec NATURAL JOIN Monitoring_Records mrec WHERE mrec.Location = ’Elderly Care-1’ AND Patient_Name = ’Peter Derr’ AND Patient_DateBirth = ’1960-01-01’ AND DateTime ¿ ’2023-12-01’ AND DateTime ¡ ’2024-01-30’”. Instead of directly sharing the clinical records and diagnosis data with the caregiver, the healthcare provider can train two differentially private models to make prediction for the treatment while keeping sensitive information private. The overall system takes Patient_Name, Patient_Datebirth, and DateTime range as input and predicts the treatment. Specifically, the first model generates some intermediate features required by the second model based on the inputs. The second model takes in these features and outputs the treatment. DP-SGD guarantees that the caregiver cannot reconstruct any patient’s clinical records and diagnosis records information from the model. Moreover, except their patients who have released their associations with the healthcare provider, the care organization cannot infer any other patients’ membership with the specific healthcare provider from the model. By leveraging these query-model transformations, we move away from sharing raw data and instead share the intermediate features, significantly enhancing privacy measures.

We can thus leverage the advances in AI/ML and DP-SGD to balance the privacy, accuracy, and efficiency of query processing. For example, with increased accuracy of diversified AI/ML model architectures (e.g., foundation models) for image recognition tasks and natural language processing, we can fine-tune such models over private data to achieve high accuracy with a small privacy budget. Our approach can be applied to various types of queries over structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data, including images and texts. Our approach is more flexible than traditional SQL/federated queries with differential privacy (DP). In Sec.  3, we show that our approach outperforms data perturbation by adding Gaussian noise  (Dwork et al., 2014) to the raw data.

2. System Architecture

Significant challenges lie in the complexity of identifying the sensitive part of the query to be replaced, the model architecture for replacing the sensitive sub-query, and the hyper-parameters for training the model. It would require a lot of human resources to manually figure out the optimal scheme and configurations for each query. The lack of declarative and automated tooling poses significant challenges for federated data management. To address this problem, we advocate for a novel privacy-preserving workflow. The proposed declarative privacy-preserving framework is illustrated in Figure  1. We describe the core components as follows:

Query Taint Analysis We enhanced the data catalog to enable the tainting of private attributes based on users’ privacy requirements (Zhou et al., 2023b). Attributes to be tainted are directly specified by the data owners or extracted from the view-based access control policy specified by the data owners. Then once a data scientist issues a query, the query will be lowered to a graph-based Intermediate Representation (IR) based on the nested relational algebra, where each node represents a relational operator, and each edge represents a dataset, which could be a relation/view or a collection of objects, such as images, video, and text files. The tainted sources will propagate the taint through the IR graph, so that the sensitive sub-queries that access private information are identified.

Privacy-Preserving Transformation Theoretically, our proposed learning-based privacy-preserving mechanism based on the query-model transformation (Zhou et al., 2023a) is applicable to various types of queries, whose input parameters and output can be closely captured by machine learning or deep learning models. For example, SELECT count(*) FROM Arrival WHERE Timestamp < $var1 AND Timestamp > $var2, can be learned as a model that maps ($var1,(\$var1, $var2)\$var2) to the count. After applying these query-model transformations, we only need to share the model and do not need to share the data anymore. To integrate with the federated query optimizer, we will abstract the process of learning a model to replace a sensitive query as a special type of query transformation rules, described as follows:

The query optimizer pushes down projections and selections to reduce the cardinality and arity of the sensitive (sub)queries to search to ease the model training efforts for such transformation.

Parameter-Efficient Privacy-Preserving Training based on Public Foundation Models When searching for query-model transformation schemes, we consider reusing models pre-trained on public datasets to reduce the training time (Zhou et al., 2022). Particularly, in order to improve the trade-off between accuracy and the privacy budget, our main idea is to search for one or more lightweight adaptive layersto connect to some foundation model (Bommasani et al., 2021) that is trained on large-scale public datasets, e.g. DeepFace and FaceNet for facial recognition, GPT-3 , BERT for natural language processing, TabTrasformer for tabular data, ALIGN, CLIP and DALL-E-2 for multi-modal data. When training the model on the private dataset, the parameters of the foundation model are all freezed, and only the parameters of the adaptive layers will be trained using DP-SGD (Yu et al., 2021). The benefits include (1) we can leverage the foundation model to achieve good accuracy while using only a small private training dataset; (2) the fewer number of training epochs further leads to smaller privacy budget (Abadi et al., 2016).

Differentiable Neural Architecture Search To find the optimal architecture of the neural network model with minimal human effort, we adopt a Differentiable Neural Architecture Search (DNAS) algorithm (Wan et al., 2020). When a foundation model is found (Zhou et al., 2022), DNAS will be used to search for the size of the adaptive layers; otherwise, DNAS will search for the architecture of a new model based on the modality of the data. DNAS searches for the optimal network architecture through gradient descent by optimizing both the prediction loss and the computation cost of the neural networks with respect to the architecture parameters and the network weights. In this demo paper, we specifically search for the optimal number of hidden layers and hidden units. In the search process, the network weights and the architecture parameters are iteratively optimized by SGD on the private data. Once the search is complete, we define the architecture of the neural network based on the optimized architecture parameters. Next, we reset the weights of the searched neural network to the initial random state and train it on the private data with DP-SGD. Note that although DNAS is conducted on private data, it does not increase the privacy budget as the network weights of the supergraph will not be released.

3. Demonstration Proposal

We will demonstrate a declarative privacy-preserving workflow for federated data management. The demo audience can select a database, and then run the following steps:

Step 1. Data Owners Annotate Sensitive Information. The data owners will label the attributes and tuples that contain private information for different user roles. Such information can also be automatically extracted from the traditional RDBMS view-based access control policies that are defined by the data owners. As illustrated in Figure  2 (1), under the view of another user, such as ”DHS officer”, the sensitive and insensitive information are listed, and a preview of the insensitive data is shown. The data owner can also specify the privacy-budget requirements.

Step 2. Query Request and Analysis. As illustrated in Figure  2 (2), after the data scientist will issue queries over federated datasets, an IR of the query is visualized and the sensitive sub-queries that involve private attributes is highlighted.

Step 3. Parameter Specification. The system recommends training and test parameters, such as accuracy and latency. The data scientist can change these default settings and choose to interactively tune the parameters, as illustrated in Figure  2 (3).

Step 4. Privacy-Preserving Recommendation. As illustrated in Figure  2 (4), the system will visualize the top k𝑘k privacy-preserving schemes that are recommended by the system. These recommended schemes are sampled from the Pareto-optimal plans. For each privacy-preserving scheme, it will list the path to the pre-trained model, or the model architecture and hyper-parameters for training a new model, as well as the estimated accuracy, privacy budget, model size, and prediction latency, etc. The data scientist can modify some privacy parameters and some recommended model hyper-parameters according to the task requirement.

Step 5. Query Execution. Once the data scientist confirms the settings in the previous step, the system proceeds to apply DNAS to obtain adaptive layers or a full new model, and then train the adaptive layers or model using some training data. Then it runs the query with visualized query results and statistics such as training accuracy,privacy budget, model search time, training latency, and query latency, as shown in Figure  2 (5). This information will also be used to update the cost model for better cost estimation.

The above steps outline the process for developing privacy-preserving mechanisms for an analytics query.

Implementation Status We have implemented the end-to-end workflow for scenarios, such as airport video queries and social media sentiment analysis. For the example query illustrated in Figure  3, our approach can achieve a better privacy-accuracy trade-off than perturbing input data using tuned Gaussian noises for the airport video query shown in Fig. 1. The privacy budget for data perturbation is proportional to the data dimension, so with the same level of privacy budget, higher dimensional input achieved lower accuracy. Similar observations are obtained for the IMDB sentiment analysis scenario. In our experiment, the range of hidden units is from 100100100 to 1000 with a step size of 10. Compared to a grid search over the whole search space that took 263 seconds, DNAS reduced the search time to 24 seconds. We plan to implement an identity document analysis scenario and a healthcare monitoring scenario for the demonstration.
