Abstract

In the evolving field of machine learning, ensuring fairness has become a critical concern, prompting the development of algorithms designed to mitigate discriminatory outcomes in decision-making processes. However, achieving fairness in the presence of group-specific concept drift remains an unexplored frontier, and our research represents pioneering efforts in this regard. Group-specific concept drift refers to situations where one group experiences concept drift over time while another does not, leading to a decrease in fairness even if accuracy remains fairly stable. Within the framework of federated learning, where clients collaboratively train models, its distributed nature further amplifies these challenges since each client can experience group-specific concept drift independently while still sharing the same underlying concept, creating a complex and dynamic environment for maintaining fairness. One of the significant contributions of our research is the formalization and introduction of the problem of group-specific concept drift and its distributed counterpart, shedding light on its critical importance in the realm of fairness. In addition, leveraging insights from prior research, we adapt an existing distributed concept drift adaptation algorithm to tackle group-specific distributed concept drift which utilizes a multi-model approach, a local group-specific drift detection mechanism, and continuous clustering of models over time. The findings from our experiments highlight the importance of addressing group-specific concept drift and its distributed counterpart to advance fairness in machine learning.

I Introduction

Fairness is a fundamental concern in machine learning, aiming to prevent discrimination in algorithmic decision-making processes with respect to sensitive attributes, such as race or gender [5, 18]. However, achieving fairness becomes even more challenging in the presence of concept drift, which refers to the phenomenon where the underlying data distribution changes over time. In this paper, we introduce the concept of group-specific concept drift, which specifically focuses on scenarios where one group, characterized by a sensitive attribute, experiences changes in the data distribution while another group remains unaffected.

To further illustrate the importance of the problem, let us consider a real-world example of group-specific concept drift in the context of loan lending. Suppose a financial institution operates in a region where historically marginalized communities, such as indigenous or ethnic minority groups, have limited access to financial services. Over time, societal changes, government initiatives, and increased awareness promote financial inclusion, leading to a significant increase in loan applications from these previously underserved communities. In this evolving environment, group-specific concept drift occurs as the data distribution for loan applicants from marginalized communities changes. Traditional lending models may not adequately capture the creditworthiness and financial profiles of these communities due to the historical underrepresentation and lack of relevant data. Consequently, there is a risk of perpetuating bias and discrimination in loan approval decisions. Addressing group-specific concept drift is crucial to ensure fair access to loans for individuals from historically marginalized communities.

Federated learning, a decentralized approach to machine learning, provides a unique setting for studying fairness under group-specific distributed concept drift. This framework allows multiple clients to collaboratively train models using their local data, while a central server aggregates their updates to create a global model [16]. However, the distributed nature of federated learning, combined with the presence of group-specific concept drift, poses new challenges that have not been addressed in the context of fairness. In this context, group-specific concept drift could manifest in specific clients at different times which can lead to challenges in maintaining fairness and accuracy. As such, in this work, we also introduce and formalize the concept of group-specific distributed concept drift in distributed environments.

Going back to the previous example of loan lending, in a federated learning setting, consider a scenario where clients representing different regions or demographic groups experience varying rates of change in the data distribution of loan applicants. For instance, a financial institution operating in a diverse geographic landscape may have multiple branches, each serving a unique community with its distinct characteristics. In this federated learning framework, if one branch undergoes changes in the data distribution for loan applicants from marginalized communities, while another branch remains unaffected, the result is a manifestation of group-specific distributed concept drift. The decentralized nature of federated learning implies that these shifts in data distribution occur independently across different clients at various times, introducing a layer of complexity in maintaining fairness and accuracy. As the financial landscape evolves and societal changes unfold, addressing group-specific distributed concept drift becomes imperative to uphold fairness and prevent perpetuation of biases in loan approval decisions for diverse and historically underserved communities.

II Related Work

II-A Fairness

Fairness-aware machine learning has gained significant attention, and algorithms designed to promote fairness can be categorized into three groups based on the stage at which they are implemented: pre-processing, in-processing, and post-processing [5, 18, 20]. The algorithm proposed in this study belongs to the in-processing category. Fairness metrics can be broadly classified into two main groups: group fairness and individual fairness. Group fairness emphasizes that both privileged and unprivileged groups defined by sensitive attributes should have an equal likelihood of favorable outcomes. On the other hand, individual fairness aims to ensure similar predictions for individuals who share similar characteristics [5, 20]. This research specifically focuses on optimizing group fairness.

II-B Fair Federated Learning

The exploration of fairness-aware federated learning has been gaining increasing attention, with multiple strategies being explored to promote fairness in decentralized model training [19, 9, 14, 17, 1]. These methods aim to mitigate bias while preserving privacy in a decentralized environment using techniques such as fair global model aggregation techniques [19, 9, 14, 17] and local debiasing methodologies at each client [1, 9].

These approaches, while promising for fairness in federated learning, are unsuitable for addressing fairness under concept drift due to their lack of mechanisms to detect and adapt to changing data distributions, making them ill-equipped to respond to evolving fairness concerns.

II-C Federated Learning under Concept Drift

Concept drift has been extensively studied in the centralized setting, and readers can refer to the survey by Gama et al. [10] for further details. However, applying centralized algorithms to federated learning may not be well-suited for handling distributed concept drifts with heterogeneous data distributions across time and clients [13, 7, 3, 4]. In the context of federated learning, research on concept drift is still in its early stages and most works do not account for the distributed nature of concept drift in their algorithms [13].

In their recent work, Jothimurugesan et al. [13] introduce a novel approach to tackle the challenge of distributed concept drift in federated learning. They treat drift adaptation as a dynamic clustering problem, emphasizing the limitations of single global models when dealing with distributed drifts. To address this issue, the authors propose a multi-model approach that leverages local drift detection based on the overall loss, offering a more robust solution to adapt to evolving data distributions over time. However, it is important to note that this algorithm, referred to as FedDrift, focuses on the global loss and does not consider group-specific losses. In the context of our work, where we specifically address group-specific distributed concept drift, FedDrift’s reliance on global loss renders it ill-suited for our scenario. Group-specific concept drift may not be effectively detected by FedDrift, as it does not account for variations in local models that are crucial for capturing changes specific to certain groups. This limitation underscores the need for a more nuanced approach to address fairness concerns under group-specific distributed concept drift, which we elaborate on in the subsequent sections. Hence, we draw inspiration from this work to the fairness setting and present more details about this algorithmic approach in the next sections.

II-D Limitations and Research Context

While certain studies have delved into fairness under concept drift in centralized streaming environments [12, 11, 24, 23], it is important to recognize their limitations when applied to fairness under concept drift in federated learning. These fairness-aware algorithms may not be well-suited for federated learning due to several reasons. Firstly, they typically require direct access to sensitive attributes, which is restricted in federated learning where only model parameters are shared among clients and the data of each client is kept private. Secondly, these algorithms rely on decision trees which may pose a security risk in a federated learning setting when sharing decision tree models with the server, requiring enhanced privacy and security measures. In addition, these studies primarily operate in a streaming context, with one example arriving at a time, which differs from our setup that is characterised by the simultaneous processing of multiple examples, and contrasts with the typical use of neural networks in federated learning.

Finally and most importantly, these existing works primarily concentrate on the study of concept drift affecting both groups simultaneously, rather than delving into datasets characterized by group-specific concept drift or its distributed counterpart. This limitation constrains the assessment of their approaches under realistic conditions with evolving fairness dynamics, where different groups may undergo distinct patterns of concept drift. In this work, we endeavor to bridge this gap by pioneering the formalization and exploration of group-specific concept drift in the context of federated learning, unveiling its vital role in advancing fairness within machine learning.

III-B Concept Drift

We assume that the underlying distribution of each Dksubscript𝐷𝑘D_{k} is not constant over time. This means that the characteristics of the data can vary across time, resulting in concept drifts. Concept drifts refer to changes in the joint distribution, where the distribution Pt​(X,y)superscript𝑃𝑡𝑋𝑦P^{t}(X,y) is different from Pt−1​(X,y)superscript𝑃𝑡1𝑋𝑦P^{t-1}(X,y) at two different timesteps [10]. We are particularly interested in observing real concept drifts, which occur when the distribution Pt​(y|X)superscript𝑃𝑡conditional𝑦𝑋P^{t}(y|X) is different from Pt−1​(y|X)superscript𝑃𝑡1conditional𝑦𝑋P^{t-1}(y|X) and require updating the model.

In this work, we introduce and formalize two distinct types of concept drifts: group-specific concept drift, and group-specific distributed concept drift.

In other words, group-specific concept drift occurs when one group experiences a change in the conditional distribution of the target variable over time, while other groups’ conditional distribution remain constant. This difference in concept drift between groups can result in a decrease in fairness, even if accuracy remains fairly stable. This occurs due to changes in accuracy within group S=s𝑆𝑠S=s attributed to concept drift that may not be readily noticeable in the overall accuracy, particularly when there is an imbalance in the dataset with respect to the sensitive attribute (i.e. when group S=s𝑆𝑠S=s is underrepresented).

In these situations, conventional drift detection mechanisms that rely solely on overall loss metrics may struggle to capture these underlying dynamics. This underscores the need to incorporate group-specific loss monitoring over time, as fairness concerns can arise when shifts in concept drift affect opportunities for favorable outcomes across groups. It is important to note that, although studies have delved into fairness under concept drift in centralized environments, the concept of group-specific concept drift had not been formally addressed in the literature. In addition, empirical experiments to validate its impact are lacking as the literature focuses on general concept drift, overlooking the nuances introduced by group-specific variations.

IV-B2 Group-specific distributed concept drift detection

A drift is identified if there is no global model in the set of global models where for any group s∈S𝑠𝑆s\in S the difference in losses of two consecutive timestamps of that group is smaller than the threshold for that group, δssubscript𝛿𝑠\delta_{s} (line 4). In this case, a new global model is created.

IV-B3 Global model merging

Importantly, since new concepts might simultaneously emerge at different clients, a merge operation is employed to consolidate models that correspond to the same concept. This operation involves the creation of a distance matrix, Z𝑍Z, where each cell is calculated for each pair of global models, i𝑖i and j𝑗j (lines 11-14). The distance Zi,jsubscript𝑍𝑖𝑗Z_{i,j} corresponds to the maximum value of Li,j−Li,isubscript𝐿𝑖𝑗subscript𝐿𝑖𝑖L_{i,j}-L_{i,i} and Lj,i−Lj,jsubscript𝐿𝑗𝑖subscript𝐿𝑗𝑗L_{j,i}-L_{j,j}, where Li,jsubscript𝐿𝑖𝑗L_{i,j} represents the sum of group losses of model θisubscript𝜃𝑖\theta_{i} when tested on data associated with model j𝑗j, which may belong to different clients. Consequently, all clients that have trained model θjsubscript𝜃𝑗\theta_{j} test the model θisubscript𝜃𝑖\theta_{i} using their data, and the maximum loss is chosen. In addition, if the loss of any group s𝑠s exceeds the threshold for that group, δssubscript𝛿𝑠\delta_{s}, the value ∞\infty is assigned to Li,jsubscript𝐿𝑖𝑗L_{i,j}. The merging process combines the two models with the lowest distances in the matrix (as long as they do not reach ∞\infty) and unifies cluster identities by averaging their models, with the weighting based on the size of each model’s training dataset (lines 16-21).

IV-B4 Training and Model Averaging

The training and model averaging process involves multiple communication rounds, denoted by R𝑅R. In each round, every global model θmsubscript𝜃𝑚\theta_{m} in G​M𝐺𝑀GM is updated based on the contributions from clients associated with that model. The update is performed through iterative optimization, where each client k:wkt=m:𝑘superscriptsubscript𝑤𝑘𝑡𝑚k:w_{k}^{t}=m creates its local model θm,ksubscript𝜃𝑚𝑘\theta_{m,k}. These local models are then aggregated to update the global model θmsubscript𝜃𝑚\theta_{m} by considering the weighted sum of individual models, where the weights are proportional to the sizes of the respective client datasets |Dkt|superscriptsubscript𝐷𝑘𝑡|D_{k}^{t}|.

To sum up, the choice of calculating and using the loss of each group s∈S𝑠𝑆s\in S in FairFedDrift, in contrast to FedDrift [13], stems from its effectiveness in handling group-specific concept drift as it inherently better suited to capture the nuances of fairness in scenarios where specific groups may experience concept drift at different timesteps. This is attributed to the fact that, in instances of group-specific concept drift, a global loss metric may not manifest substantial variations. We elaborate on this particular aspect in our experiments section, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the dynamics involved.

V Methodology and Experimental Design

In our experimental setup, we operate within a federated learning framework that operates continuously over time involving K clients. At each timestep, every client receives a new batch of training data. After completing training for a given timestep, which can span multiple communication rounds, for all clients, we test the global model associated with that client over the batch of data arriving at that client at the following timestep (between lines 3 and 4, in Algorithm 1).

V-A Dataset Selection and Distributed Group-Specific Concept Drift Simulation

In our experimental setup, we leverage diverse datasets derived from well-established benchmarks in machine learning: MNIST [8], FashionMNIST [21], Adult [2] and a synthetic dataset. These datasets were chosen due to their extensive size, making them well-suited for this federated learning setup, where each client necessitates a substantial number of data instances across different timesteps. In addition, these datasets have also been employed in fairness studies [22, 15], which makes them suitable to our experiments.

To simulate this federated learning scenario, we partition each dataset into K𝐾K clients and T𝑇T timesteps. Furthermore, we introduce group-specific concept drift within the datasets at specific timesteps, enabling the evaluation of the efficacy of our proposed method under evolving data conditions.

MNIST-GDrift

The MNIST dataset consists of gray-scale handwritten digit images, commonly used for digit recognition tasks [8]. We introduce one sensitive attribute, S𝑆S, with two groups (S=1𝑆1S=1 and S=0𝑆0S=0), representing distinct image characteristics. For S=0𝑆0S=0 images, we invert the background and digit colors in comparison to standard MNIST images (S=1𝑆1S=1). Additionally, we apply a 90-degree rotation to further differentiate these groups. To simulate two distinctive group-specific concepts (’B’ and ’C’) from the original distribution (’A’), we label-swap images for S=0𝑆0S=0: for drift ’B’, we swap labels ’0’ and ’1’; and for drift ’C’, we swap labels ’1’ and ’2’.

FashionMNIST-GDrift

The FashionMNIST is a dataset featuring gray-scale images of clothing items, designed as an alternative to MNIST for benchmarking image classification algorithms [21]. It consists of images of 10 different classes: ’T-shirt/top’ (0), ’Trouser’ (1), ’Pullover’ (2), ’Dress’ (3), ’Coat’ (4), ’Sandal’ (5), ’Shirt’ (6), ’Sneaker’ (7), ’Bag’ (8) and ’Ankle boot’ (9). To create different groups and simulate group-specific concept drift, we use the same strategies as the MNIST-GDrift dataset.

The parameter α𝛼\alpha is a crucial element in our experimental design, determining the size of the unprivileged group relative to the privileged group within each dataset. VI-A Results Across Multiple Datasets and Scenarios

We delve into the comprehensive evaluation of the algorithms on the MNIST-GDrift, FashionMNIST-GDrift, Synthetic and Adult-GDrift datasets, considering different α𝛼\alpha values and the three distinct scenarios of group-specific distributed concept drift in Figure 2.

Notably, FairFedDrift’s B-ACC results closely approaches that of the idealized Oracle algorithm, underlining its efficacy in handling the intricate challenges posed by group-specific distributed concept drift.

A noteworthy observation emerges for small α𝛼\alpha values, indicating situations where the unprivileged group is underrepresented, where balanced accuracy (B-ACC) tends to be lower for FedAvg and FedDrift. However, as α𝛼\alpha assumes higher values, the global loss undergoes more significant changes, allowing FedDrift to achieve results that closely match those of FairFedDrift.

These results showcase the versatility of FairFedDrift across diverse datasets and scenarios, emphasizing its ability to navigate the intricate landscape of this problem.

VII Conclusions and Future Work

In this work, we introduced the concept of group-specific concept drift and group-specific distributed concept drift in the context of federated learning. We demonstrated the impact of group-specific distributed concept drift on fairness. Our contributions include the formalization of group-specific concept drift and its distributed counterpart, an experimental framework for studying it, and the application of model clustering and fairness monitoring techniques to mitigate the challenges posed by this issue.

Revising the research questions presented at the beginning of this work:

RQ1

In the absence of group-specific distributed concept drift handling, our experiments reveal that fairness tends to decrease over time. Without mechanisms to address the evolving data distributions, fairness is compromised, while accuracy can remain relatively stable.

RQ2

For scenarios with small α𝛼\alpha (indicating group imbalance), our findings indicate that overall loss and accuracy do not change significantly. However, there is a noticeable decrease in fairness due to the changing loss of the unprivileged group. In contrast, as α𝛼\alpha increases, indicating a higher relative representation of the unprivileged group, both overall loss and the loss of the unprivileged group exhibit more noticeable changes.

RQ3

Our proposed algorithm, FairFedDrift, emerged as a practical solution for achieving fairness in federated learning amid group-specific distributed concept drift. By continuously monitoring group loss and adapting to changes in data distributions, FairFedDrift demonstrated its effectiveness in mitigating potential unfairness. The algorithm’s ability to handle the challenges posed by this problem showcased its practical utility in real-world scenarios.

As we look to the future, several directions for further research emerge. Automating the hyperparameter selection process for FairFedDrift could streamline its application across diverse scenarios. Additionally, developing specialized fairness metrics tailored for evaluating concept drift in distributed learning settings would provide more nuanced insights into the evolving landscape of fairness. The intricate issues of fairness and group-specific concept drift in federated learning demand ongoing exploration and refinement. Our work lays the foundation for addressing these challenges as machine learning continues to evolve in sensitive domains.
