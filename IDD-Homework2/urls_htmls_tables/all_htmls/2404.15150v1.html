Lost in Magnitudes: Exploring the Design Space for Visualizing Data with Large Value Ranges

\authororcidKaterina Batziakoudi0009-0003-7069-9339    \authororcidFlorent Cabric0000-0002-9326-9441    \authororcidStéphanie Rey0000-0002-2826-2489    and \authororcidJean-Daniel Fekete0000-0003-3770-8726
Abstract

We explore the design space for the static visualization of datasets with quantitative attributes that vary over multiple orders of magnitude—we call these attributes Orders of Magnitude Values (OMVs\xspace)—, and provide design guidelines and recommendations on effective visual encodings for OMVs\xspace. Current charts rely on linear or logarithmic scales to visualize values, leading to limitations in performing simple tasks for OMVs\xspace. In particular, linear scales prevent the reading of smaller magnitudes and their comparisons, while logarithmic scales are challenging for the general public to understand. Our design space leverages the approach of dividing OMVs\xspaceinto two different parts: mantissa and exponent, in a way similar to the scientific notation. This separation allows for a visual encoding of both parts. For our exploration, we use four datasets, each with two attributes: an OMV, divided into mantissa and exponent, and a second attribute, that is nominal, ordinal, time, or quantitative. We start from the original design space described by the Grammar of Graphics and systematically generate all possible visualizations for these datasets, employing different marks and visual channels. We refine this design space by enforcing integrity constraints from visualization and graphical perception literature. Through a qualitative assessment of all viable combinations, we discuss the most effective visualizations for OMVs\xspace, focusing on channel and task effectiveness. The article’s main contributions are 1) the presentation of the design space of OMVs\xspace, 2) the generation of a large number of OMV\xspacevisualizations, among which some are novel and effective, 3) the refined definition of a scale that we call E+M\xspacefor OMVs\xspace, and 4) guidelines and recommendations for designing effective OMV\xspacevisualizations. We also present a visualization tool to interactively explore viable combinations of marks and visual channels for mantissa and exponent across different datasets, demonstrating the potential of various design combinations for effectively representing OMVs\xspace. These efforts aim to enrich visualization systems to better support data with OMVs\xspaceand guide future research. All of our data and code are available online: https://osf.io/msvbe/?view_only=061aa4a15028449b91304339df89d40c

Introduction

In this article, we explore the design space for the static visualization of datasets with attributes encompassing several orders of magnitude. Such data, hereafter referred to as Order of Magnitude Values (OMVs), include values that span a broad range of exponents when presented in scientific notation [25]. Our goal is to achieve effective static visualization of OMVs. An illustrative example is the budget allocations of the French government (Fig. 1), which range from tens of millions of Euros (107superscript10710^{7}10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT 7 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT€) to hundreds of billions of Euros (1011superscript101110^{11}10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT 11 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT€), thereby covering five orders of magnitude or five distinct exponents.

OMVs are integral to various domains of daily life, including but not limited to financial analysis, pandemic tracking, demographic studies, environmental monitoring, and social media metrics. For instance, the “WDC Web Table Corpus 2015” dataset [44], which contains 90M tables extracted from the Web in 2015, includes 25,175 tables with OMVs. The effective static visualization of OMVs is essential for providing a clear and informative overview accessible to a broad audience. This audience ranges from the general public, who may seek insights into their country’s taxation system, to scientists and professionals looking to analyze and understand complex datasets. We argue that OMVs should be treated as a specific kind of quantitative data type in visualization, and thus, visualization systems should support these values effectively.

Common visualization techniques, such as linear and logarithmic bar charts, prove ineffective, as illustrated in Fig. 1. In the example of the French budget, using a linear scale (left) makes it impossible to read and compare budget categories with smaller magnitudes, resulting in information being entirely lost. While a logarithmic scale (right) can partially mitigate this issue by ensuring that all budget values are visible, it is not well understood by the general public [11, 45, 37] and may even lead to misconceptions among scientists [31]. Beyond legibility issues, logarithmic scales also hinder efficient quantitative comparisons [23], such as the fundamental tasks of estimating differences or ratios [36], between values within the same or neighboring magnitudes.

In recent studies in visualization [4, 25, 23, 7, 6], authors split the OMVs into mantissa and exponent parts, inspired by scientific notation, and visualize them as the combination of two distinct parts. For example, 16,000 is represented as 1.6E4 where 1.6 is the mantissa and 4 the exponent of the value. Thus, one quantitative attribute is represented by two new quantitative attributes that are strongly dependent, thereby introducing the challenge of visually maintaining their numerical relationship in a cohesive way.

The existing literature [4, 25, 23, 7, 6] supports the idea that the mantissa/exponent separation could enhance the visualization of OMVs, facilitating reading and comparison tasks. According to their studies, performed by non-specialists, these visualizations are effective and understandable. Nevertheless, at the date of this article, the number of visualization designs exploring this approach remains limited to six. We aim to discover whether other visualizations are effective and elucidate possible rationales behind their effectiveness.

Following the Grammar of Graphics (GoG) rules [51], the number of combinations of marks and visual channels that could integrate both a mantissa and exponent for a specific dataset is very large. It is the Cartesian product of different data attributes (including the mantissa and exponent) to visual channels (e.g., position, length, color intensity), using different marks (i.e., points, lines, areas). Given the dimensions of our design space (Lost in Magnitudes: Exploring the Design Space for Visualizing Data with Large Value Ranges), this number reaches 6,240 combinations. However, not every combination is likely to be effective; only a few may prove visually effective for performing specific tasks.

To discern these effective combinations, we draw upon insights from the literature on visualization effectiveness and graphical perception. We apply these insights as integrity constraints, following a similar approach to the one used from visualization recommendation systems [39, 53]. Through the application of these constraints, we refined the design space to 336 viable combinations (168 factoring the symmetrical ones). Conducting controlled evaluations for all these combinations, however, would be prohibitively expensive. Instead, our qualitative study systematically explores these combinations to provide a more holistic understanding of the factors influencing the effectiveness of OMVs visualizations [9]. Building on this exploration and qualitative evaluation, we introduce design guidelines and recommendations for static visualizations of OMVs.

In summary, we make the following contributions in this paper:

  • •

    We present a design space, for the static visualization of OMVs by decomposing them into mantissa and exponent parts.

  • •

    From our design space, we generate several (25) effective visualizations for OMVs—some of them being novel—, and other combinations that could be effective as discussed in section 4.

  • •

    We refine the definition of a scale, already alluded to in previous work [23, 6], that leads to effective visualizations for OMVs; we call it E+M.

  • •

    We introduce guidelines and recommendations for designing effective OMV visualizations.

Additionally, we introduce our open-source tool, OMVis (Fig. 3), that enables systematic exploration and facilitates the future empirical evaluation of the generated visualizations by saving and exporting them in Observable Plot [41] (called Plot in the remaining).

1.2 Visualization of Large Value Ranges

In the context of data visualization, visually representing OMVs for the general public poses significant challenges. While linear scales are widely understood, they fall short of effectively depicting large value ranges, rendering only the larger values legibly. If the screen has a width and height in the 103superscript10310^{3}10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT 3 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT pixels, it cannot use position to distinguish values that are more than three orders of magnitude apart. This distinctiveness issue [43] hinders fundamental analytical tasks, including value estimation, and comparison (ratio and difference) between different ranges. Although dual scale charts [26] and innovative designs like the Du Bois Wrapped Bar Chart [28] offer improvements for analyzing two disparate ranges, their effectiveness diminishes with OMVs [4], as they require the accurate representations of multiple (i.e., more than four) data ranges in the same view.

Logarithmic scales, capable of showcasing diverse ranges, facilitate qualitative comparisons of OMVs, like finding extrema. Nonetheless, they pose difficulties for quantitative tasks, such as estimating differences or ratios between values of similar or close magnitudes [23]. Despite their prevalence in scientific contexts, they may lead to misconceptions [31] and are less understandable for the general public, challenging perception and interpretation [11, 46, 32]. Landy et al. [32], conducted a study focusing on OMVs in the range of 105superscript10510^{5}10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT 5 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT to 1013superscript101310^{13}10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT 13 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT and found that adults rarely follow a purely logarithmic interpretation of the scale when estimating magnitudes or performing quantitative tasks. About half of the study participants estimated numbers linearly across this spectrum, while nearly all others followed a segmented linear model. In this model, magnitudes are uniformly distributed as in logarithmic scales, but numbers within these magnitudes are linearly interpolated. This behavior reflects our verbal description of large numbers, using units as categories (e.g., millions, billions) for magnitude and digits from 1 to 10 for precision [32]. Normalized scientific notation also embodies this separation, with the exponent indicating the order of magnitude and the mantissa providing higher precision within the order of magnitude.

Over the last decade, researchers have introduced visualizations that separate OMVs into their exponent and mantissa components. The effectiveness of these visualizations was evaluated based on tasks such as value retrieval, sorting, ratio estimation, difference estimation, and trend identification. Hlawatsch et al. [23] introduced the Scale-Stack Bar Chart (SSB) that visualizes large value ranges by stacking values at multiple scales, beginning from zero and mapping numbers linearly. Following a segmented linear model approach, this visualization demonstrates its effectiveness over traditional linear and logarithmic bar charts for quantitative comparisons. Borgo et al. [4] proposed visualizing exponents and mantissa, using two separate marks, as overlapping bars on a linear scale within a classic bar chart, termed Order of Magnitude Markers (OMM). Their approach showcased high accuracy for value retrieval and comparison tasks. Höhn et al. [25] introduced the Width-Scale Bar Charts (WSB), which uses position Y for the mantissa and a redundant combination of color intensity and width for the exponent. They compared WSB with SSB in a controlled experiment, showing that WSB is more effective for value retrieval and SSB for sort, ratio, and trend tasks. Additionally, Braun et al. [7] illustrated the use of color, specifically their Order of Magnitude Color (OMC) scheme, to aid in perceiving changes in orders of magnitude by mapping exponents to hue and mantissa to a sequential color scale. Lastly, Braun et al. [6], focused on visualizing large-scale time-series data, introducing the Order of Magnitude Horizon Graph (OMH) and the Order of Magnitude Line chart (OML). OMH modifies the standard horizon graph [22] by using position Y for mantissa and color intensity for the exponents, while OML combines an approach similar to the segmented linear model that SSB uses for position and the color scheme from OMC. Unlike SSB, where values are shown per magnitude in stacked rows, OML combines both exponent and mantissa in the position Y, introducing a new visualization scale termed “linear scaling within orders of magnitude” [6]. We clarify their scale and call it E+M in section 2.3. We summarise the existing literature in Table 1.

The studies highlighted above have conclusively demonstrated the advantages of visualizations that separate OMVs into their mantissa and exponent components. This separation enhances analytical tasks’ accuracy, confidence, and efficiency by offering a more effective representation of large numerical ranges. However, these studies have explored only a subset of the potential combinations of marks and visual channels for encoding the mantissa and exponent (Table 1). Recognizing this gap, our objective is to conduct a systematic exploration of these combinations along with different attribute types: nominal, ordinal, time, and quantitative. By doing so, we aim to identify and discuss which visualizations are most effective for OMVs, proposing guidelines for their representation and enhancing the comprehension of vast numerical data.

1.3 Visualization Effectiveness

The concept of visual channel effectiveness in visualization has significantly evolved since Bertin first introduced it [2]. Building on Bertin’s foundation, Cleveland and McGill [12] conducted experiments to evaluate the ranking of visual channels for quantitative data, with a specific focus on the accuracy of sorting and comparison tasks. Mackinlay [34] expanded this ranking to include both nominal and ordered data, thus broadening our understanding of visual channel effectiveness. Several subsequent experiments [21, 29, 36], have empirically validated and refined the proposed effectiveness rankings of visual channels, revealing additional factors that could influence them.

Zeng and Battle [53], through a systematic review of the literature on channel effectiveness, pointed out that position encodings are the most accurate channels across all data types. The accuracy of other visual channels (e.g., area, length, or color intensity) varies, influenced by factors like discriminability — the ability to perceive distinct values for each attribute [40]—, and separability—the independence of channels from one another [40]. Discriminability is affected by several factors, including the number of objects [20], the type and size of a mark [33, 49], the background color [33], visual attention limitations [20], and the interference effects with other channels [49].

Interference effects with other channels lead to separability issues. Separability allows for focused attention on one visual channel without interference from another [17]. Separable visual channels, such as position and color, show minimal cross-channel effects, causing no effectiveness issues when assigned to different attributes of a visualization. In contrast, when the encoding of one channel affects the perception of another, the channels are called integral [17]. Integral visual channel pairs include color hue and color intensity [18, 8], height and width [30, 35, 16, 38], shape and size [48], shape and color intensity [48], size and color intensity [49], and size and position [29]. The redundant combination of integral visual channels for the same attribute facilitates decoding. However, encoding two unrelated attributes with two integral channels can lead to interference, making it difficult to identify the attributes and affecting value perception [1].

Studies also suggest that the effectiveness of visualizations should not only be evaluated in relation to the used channels alone but also in terms of the tasks [29, 36]. McColeman et al. [36] highlight that the effectiveness of visual channels is task-dependent, asserting that data characteristics might significantly impact task performance more than the choice of visual channel. Hollands and Spence [24] demonstrate that the original ranking may not sufficiently explain average performance in “discrimination” tasks, where participants identify the larger of two quantities. Kim et al. [29] observed that different combinations of visual channels perform differently for comparison and summary tasks depending on a dataset’s cardinality (number of items). Similarly, Yuan et al. [52] discovered that the ranking does not apply to tasks involving comparisons of multiple values, such as average across data points.

In our exploration of the design space, we consider a broad spectrum of factors that could influence effectiveness. This exploration begins with channel effectiveness in relation to the accuracy, discriminability, and separability of the channels used. Alongside these theoretical considerations, we also address task-related factors, such as the subjective effort and confidence required to perform various tasks with visualizations. Our goal is to engage in a nuanced discussion about the effectiveness of visualizations for OMVs and to propose guidelines informed by critical observations that affect it.

2 Design Space

In this section, we define the specific visualization design space addressed in our paper. This design space is informed and constrained by 1) the principles of the Semiology of graphics [2] and the implementations of the Grammar of Graphics [51, 50, 47, 41], particularly the Plot [41] library, which is used to generate the visualizations in our study, 2) studies on graphical perception [1, 42] and visualization effectiveness [53, 39], and 3) the literature about visualization of large value ranges [4, 6]. We start by establishing the boundaries of our design space, proceed to describe its dimensions—how each visualization within this space is described— then we define the E+M scale, and conclude by outlining the constraints implemented to refine the design space, with the goal of improving visualization effectiveness.

2.1 Boundaries

Our design space’s boundaries are shaped by the objectives of our research, insights gained from our review of literature on the visualization of OMVs, and the choice of the visualization library used (i.e., Plot) . Firstly, we concentrate on tabular datasets with a single OMV attribute with positive exponents, excluding types such as networks, fields, and geometries. Given that Plot primarily uses the Cartesian coordinate system, our study is focused on visualizations within this system, ensuring a uniform layout and excluding polar or parallel layouts. Aimed at enhancing the effectiveness of OMVs visualizations in overview, we limit our scope to static visualization designs and exclude interactive elements. Moreover, our design space does not entail data transformations; these are handled during dataset creation. Our analysis explicitly targets visualizations that employ a single mark , and consequently we are removing OMM [4] from our design space. Additionally, we omit logarithmic scales, a decision detailed in the background section. Contrary to other OMV studies, we do not include sort and trend tasks because McColeman et al. [36] argue that these tasks, like many others in visualization, rely on the most fundamental “two-value ratio judgment task”, already included in our tasks. Their quantitative evaluation may reveal significant differences; however, within the context of our design space, we evaluate the tasks qualitatively.

2.2 Dimensions

After setting the design space boundaries, we define the dimensions that specify each visualization. Plot [41] describes a single visualization as the combination of data types, marks, visual channels, facets, scales, transforms, and projections. Considering the boundaries established, we describe each observed visualization design by a combination of the following elements: data, marks, and visual channels, considering faceting as part of visual channels. Lost in Magnitudes: Exploring the Design Space for Visualizing Data with Large Value Ranges showcases the dimensions of our design space.

Data

In this context, “data” pertains to the type and range of attributes. We consider OMV as a specific type of data attribute that is separated into two quantitative attributes: mantissa and exponent, as defined in the background section. We explore visualizations that display the mantissa, the exponent, and an additional attribute type (we call attribute-2) among nominal, ordinal, time, and quantitative. The range of these additional data attributes, and therefore the cardinality of the datasets chosen for our exploration, was seven, small enough to remain discriminable with all the visual channels [19]. Lost in Magnitudes: Exploring the Design Space for Visualizing Data with Large Value Ranges displays the DATA (blue) part, as rows on the top left of the table.

Marks

Marks are defined based on their spatial dimension requirements: points represent zero-dimensional (0D) marks, lines correspond to one-dimensional (1D) marks, and areas are considered two-dimensional (2D) marks [2, 40]. Within the framework of the GoG, as exemplified by Plot, we represent points either with the Dot or with the Rule- mark—when the visual channel of Length is chosen—, for lines, we use the Bar mark for nominal and ordinal data, and the Rect mark for time, and last for areas we use the Area mark. Lost in Magnitudes: Exploring the Design Space for Visualizing Data with Large Value Ranges displays the MARKS (green) part, as rows on the left of the table.

Visual channels

Visual channels (VC) are ways to control the appearance of marks [40]. In our design space, we selected channels based on insights from the literature on visualization effectiveness [53, 39] and constraints from Plot specifications. We focus on the following nine visual channels: Position X (PosX), Position Y (PosY), Row, Column (Col), Length, Area, Color Intensity (Intensity), Color Hue (Hue), and Shape. Length refers to both width and height, depending on the orientation of the line and the choice of the mark. Row and Col are channels derived from the faceting operation, as done by Moritz et al. [39]. Scales

According to the GoG, a visualization scale is a function that transforms attribute values into visual channel values, such as position, length, or color intensity. In our design space, scales are handled by Plot and are determined automatically by the attribute type. Band scales are utilized for nominal attributes, ordinal scales for ordinal attributes, and linear scales for quantitative attributes. To simplify interpretation and eliminate the need for calculations, we implement a tick transformation representing the exponent value as 10esuperscript10𝑒10^{e}10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT italic_e end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT. For instance, if the exponent is 3, the tick label displays 1,000 (103superscript10310^{3}10 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT 3 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT). Given that scales are selected based on the data attribute type, they are not discussed within our design space, with the exception of E+M (see below).

2.3 E+M Scale

As mentioned in the background, studies in cognitive psychology [32] suggest that people perceive OMVs following a segmented linear model, with magnitudes distributed uniformly as in logarithmic scales and numbers within these magnitudes being linearly interpolated. We believe the scale used by Braun et al. [6], and inspired by the positional encoding of SSB [23], reflects this segmented linear model well and represents an effective way to visualize OMVs.

We call this scale E+M and refine its definition, aiming to facilitate its experimental evaluation and wider adoption in OMVs visualizations. According to the figures of Braun et al. [6], we infer that their scale is s⁢(v)=e⁢(v)+(m⁢(v)−1)/9𝑠𝑣𝑒𝑣𝑚𝑣19s(v)=e(v)+(m(v)-1)/9italic_s ( italic_v ) = italic_e ( italic_v ) + ( italic_m ( italic_v ) - 1 ) / 9, a piecewise linear scale. When used for position, the axis units represent the values of the exponents, and the subunits correspond to the mantissa values. Labeling/ticks for this scale uses the exponent for the major labels/ticks, and the mantissa’s values for the minor ticks. Braun et al. [6] do not show subunit labels, but we realized that showing minor ticks and labels, even just one at m=5𝑚5m=5italic_m = 5, expresses well the linearity of the scale within orders of magnitude. In our design space, this scale is expressed by assigning both exponent and mantissa to the same positional channel.

2.4 Constraints

To ensure the effectiveness and expressiveness of the visualizations in our design space, we apply constraints related to the boundaries we have set, the expressiveness of marks and visual channels, and the interference of different channels. A visualization is considered viable only if all constraints are satisfied. Our approach is inspired by the literature on recommendation systems for visualization [39], where integrity constraints are used to encode expressiveness and restrictions to the dimensions of a visualization specification.

Constraints based on layout

As we limit our design space to the Cartesian system, we include only combinations that contain at least one position channel (PosX or PosY).

Constraints for the expressiveness of channels

To ensure the expressiveness of each assignment [40], we use Hue and Shape only for nominal data, Intensity for ordinal and quantitative data, and Length and Area only for quantitative data. For the Row and Col channels, we assign only the exponent, as mantissa is a quantitative attribute with continuous range, and attribute-2 has a one-to-one relationship with the OMV, leading to visualizations similar to those using a positional channel. An exception to this constraint is our decision to retain visualizations that include both row and column for the exponent and attribute-2 as a categorical attribute, as they result in novel visualizations.

Constraints based on mark type

for each type of mark, we impose constraints in accordance with its defined dimensions. The channels Shape and Area can be used only for point marks, and the channel Length is not feasible for area marks. We also apply constraints in relation to the dataset. We use area marks only for time data, and we exclude lines for quantitative data.

Constraints based on interference with identity channels

channels like Hue and Shape serve identification purposes. Combining Hue with Intensity within the same mark generates a new color. Similarly, merging Shape with Length can alter the shape (e.g., transforming a circle into an ellipse). These combinations modify the identity of the primary channel, and therefore, we do not consider them valid.

3 Exploration

Our exploration has been carried out using our tool, OMVis. We explored four datasets, generated all the viable visualizations, and explored them visually on our wall-size display to identify key properties, related to the visual channels, that made them effective. The four coauthors then performed the three tasks defined in our design space and subjectively assessed the visualizations in terms of suitability, accuracy, confidence, and effort. Following this, we convened to reach a consensus on the coding, resulting in a set of effective visualizations, rationales, and design guidelines.

3.1 Order of Magnitudes Tool

To facilitate the exploration of the OMVs design space, we implemented a web-based open-source tool, OMVis (Figure 3), written in JavaScript. Our tool includes two main components: 1) a menu for the interactive exploration of all possible combinations and 2) the resulting visualization. Within the menu, users can upload or select a dataset from our database. They then choose an OMV to separate into its mantissa and exponent parts, followed by the selection of additional attributes (nominal, ordinal, time, or quantitative). Subsequently, users decide on a mark—point, line, or area, and assign visual channels to the mantissa, exponent, and selected attributes. We apply the constraints by disabling the corresponding choices; for example, quantitative visual channels such as Length are not applicable to nominal attributes. A visualization is generated based on these selections. We use Plot to create the visualizations. The user can export the produced visualization either as an image or as JavaScript code. More details are provided in the supplemental material.

3.2 Datasets

We generated four datasets for our exploration, one for each data type combined with the OMV attribute. We used the “Trending YouTube Video Statistics” dataset from Kaggle [27]. This dataset comprises daily metrics (Views, Likes, Comments) for the top trending YouTube videos across categories (e.g., Music, Entertainment, Gaming) in nine Countries. We selected this dataset as it contains OMVs (e.g., Views) and has diverse attributes, including nominal (e.g., video Categories), ordinal (e.g., 7 Likert scale Sentiments), time (e.g., Days), and quantitative (Likes, Comments). 3.4 Systematic Exploration of Visualizations

Discussing the effectiveness of visualization is challenging and multi-faceted. We chose to explore the design space with a qualitative approach to collect observations about possible rationales for effectiveness [9]. We explore our design space in two stages: 1) Exploration of channel effectiveness and 2) Exploration of task effectiveness, gathering insights for design guidelines during the two stages.

3.4.1 Channel Effectiveness

The first stage of our exploration consisted of two three-hour meetings between three co-authors in which each visualization was displayed on a wall-sized display to facilitate both discussion and comparison (Figure 4). During this phase, we coded the channel assigned to the mantissa, exponent, and attribute-2, of the 336 visualizations according to three factors: Accuracy, Discriminability, and Separability with other channels. We first present results for each channel and then discuss the relationship between the factors.

Accuracy

We identified 48 (14.3%) visualizations conveying high accuracy for all three of the exponent, mantissa, and attribute-2. We classified 168 visualizations as accurate for two attributes (48 for exponent and mantissa, 84 for exponent and attribute-2, and 36 for attribute-2 and mantissa), enabling accurate retrieval of just one attribute’s value (48 for exponent, 24 for mantissa and 48 for attribute-2).

Discriminability

We classified 48 visualizations that convey discriminability of exponent, mantissa, and attribute-2 (14.3%). We counted 168 visualizations allowing discriminability for only two attributes (48 for exponent and mantissa, 84 for exponent and attribute-2, and 36 for attribute-2 and mantissa), and 120 that discriminate efficiently only one attribute (48 for exponent, 24 for mantissa and 48 for attribute-2).

Separability

We classified 224 (66.7%) that enable separability among the three pairs of data attributes ({exponent and mantissa},{exponent and attribute-2 },{attribute-2 and mantissa}). The remaining 112 visualizations (33.3%) enable separability between two pairs of attributes (20 for {exponent and mantissa} and {mantissa and attribute-2 }, 40 for {exponent and mantissa} and {exponent and attribute-2 }, 52 for {attribute-2 and mantissa} and {exponent and attribute-2 }).

Cross-analysis

By analyzing the visualizations, we noticed a correlation among the three factors. Visualizations with separability issues between two attributes (48 visualizations for exponent and mantissa, 20 for exponent and attribute-2, and 40 for mantissa and attribute-2) were deemed non-discriminable for those respective attributes. This classification indicates that the interference effect between two channels led to compromised discriminability. Furthermore, all visualizations classified with low discriminability for an attribute exhibited low accuracy for the same attribute, resulting in identical counts for our accuracy and discriminability analysis. Consequently, in our discussion, we treat accuracy and discriminability as a single factor influenced by the assignment of channels and the interference effects between them.

We also observed that all symmetrical visualizations shared the same set of strengths/weaknesses. Therefore, we removed the duplicated symmetrical visualizations, reducing our set of visualizations by half, from 336 to 168. We considered symmetrical combinations of both position and faceting, preferring to retain PosY and Row, over PosX and Col [13, 29]. Coding the visualizations informed us about good practices in visualizing OMVs. However, a second phase regarding task effectiveness was deemed necessary to assess visualization effectiveness in relation to the required tasks [36].

3.4.2 Task Effectiveness

Visualization effectiveness based on tasks is primarily evaluated with controlled user studies. However, performing this evaluation with the 168 viable visualizations in our design space would be intractable. Instead, we decided to explore visualization effectiveness based on tasks with a qualitative-coding approach. Each visualization was attributed to two coders selected between the four coauthors of this paper. After the first coding phase, three coders entered a collaborating coding phase where all the encodings were discussed and reviewed. The coders had to answer a set of 11 questions regarding three tasks: value retrieval, difference estimation, and ratio estimation. We subjectively evaluated the three tasks that belong to our design space in terms of suitability, accuracy, confidence, and effort.

Value retrieval

With many OMVs representations, retrieving a value is a two-step process: retrieving the exponent first, then the mantissa, to compute or visually perceive the whole value. Retrieving the exponent is a critical task as it can lead to an “order-of-magnitude” error (i.e., the retrived value can be ten times higher or smaller than the actual value). Therefore, we binary-coded whether the exponent retrieval was feasible without any potential confusion. As the mantissa defines the significant digits of a value inside an order of magnitude, the effect of an error in its estimation is narrower. In addition, the mantissa can be a number with an infinite number of decimals, making its exact retrieval impossible, so we focused on estimating the mantissa between two ranges: ±0.5plus-or-minus0.5\pm 0.5± 0.5 and ±1plus-or-minus1\pm 1± 1.

Difference estimation

Estimating the difference between two OMVs is a contrasting task that depends on the magnitude level to which the two values belong. Following Braun et al. [6], we differentiate three kinds of difference estimation tasks when two values belong to: the same order of magnitude, neighboring orders of magnitude, or distant orders of magnitude.

Ratio estimation

Similarly to difference estimation, estimating the ratio depends on the order of magnitude of the values. However, estimating the ratio between neighboring or distant orders of magnitude results in the same task. Therefore, we only evaluated ratio estimation for values that belong to either the same order of magnitude or distant orders of magnitude.

3.5 Results

From the 168 visualizations that we qualitatively evaluated in relation to their effectiveness to represent OMVs, 32 were identified as suitable. Within this subset, 25 allowed task performance without effort, and 7 required some effort. Of the 136 visualizations not classified within the effective group, 55 were deemed to necessitate a medium effort, and 81 were found to either demand considerable effort for the tasks or were outright impractical. We classify the combinations into four groups:

Effortless and Effective (25/168):

Visualizations that exhibit pre-attentive task performance for both value estimation and quantitative tasks, demonstrating high effectiveness for the visualization of OMVs. Figure 5 shows three examples of these visualizations.

Suitable with Effort (7/168):

Visualizations that are generally effective for value estimation but demand additional effort for quantitative comparison tasks (6(a)).

Medium Effort and Low Confidence (55/168):

Visualizations that require a moderate amount of effort and result in low confidence in their outcome (6(b)).

High Effort or Impractical (81/168):

Visualizations that are either too challenging to use effectively for the intended tasks or are outright infeasible for visualizing OMVs (6(c)).

The 25 visualizations we classified as “Effortless and Effective” are described using eight combinations of visual channels across different marks and attribute types, as shown in Table 2. Through our analysis of these 25 visualizations and their comparison with those requiring more effort, we identified common patterns that are both necessary and sufficient for the effective visualization of OMVs. We present these patterns as design guidelines and recommendations, which are detailed in the following section. The 168 images used in our evaluation are available at https://www.omvs-designspace.com/ and in the supplemental material.

4 Discussion and Recommendations

In this section, we discuss our results about the channel and task effectiveness. We introduce guidelines for the visualization of OMVs, suggest recommendations, and present rationales about how the channel effectiveness affected the perceived effort and confidence for the tasks. We also assess the impact of mark choice and data type on visualization effectiveness, underscoring the challenges and considerations for each. Furthermore, we explore redundant encoding and the risk for interference between channels. With these guidelines and rationales in mind, we revisit existing literature on OMV visualizations. Finally, we outline our study’s limitations.

4.1 Design Guidelines

We identified four requirements that we present as design guidelines: Accuracy for Magnitude, Detail Inside Magnitudes, Continuity Between Magnitudes, and Parsimony in Channels.

4.1.1 AcM — Accuracy for Magnitude

The exponent should be encoded with a highly accurate and discriminable channel to prevent order-of-magnitude errors. Errors in the estimation of the exponent could lead to large mistakes in value estimation and comparisons. Therefore, we consider this guideline the most critical for the effective visualization of OMVs. This guideline applies to all visualizations we consider suitable, including in the “Effortless and Effective” (25/25) and “Suitable with Effort” (7/7) categories. It is the main differentiating factor between “Medium Effort and Low Confidence” (51/51) and “High Effort or Impractical” (27/81). Non-compliance with this guideline results in visualizations that require high effort to accomplish tasks, potentially leading to an increased error rate (Example in 6(c)).

Recommendations: To comply with this guideline, we recommend favoring the use of highly accurate channels for the exponent, such as PosX, PosY, Row, and Col, while avoiding low discriminability channels as exclusive channels for the exponent, such as Length, Area, and Intensity, especially in cases where the dataset contains a range of five or more exponents [19].

4.1.2 DeM — Detail Inside Magnitudes

The mantissa should be encoded with a highly accurate and discriminable channel to facilitate the estimation of difference and ratio between values of the same magnitude. This guideline applies to all visualizations classified as suitable for OMVs: “Effortless and Effective” (25/25) and “Suitable with Effort” (7/7). Visualizations violating this guideline fall into the categories “Medium Effort and Low Confidence” (7/55) and “High Effort or Impractical” (42/81). Non-compliance with this guideline decreases the accuracy and confidence in value retrieval. Furthermore, it leads to visualizations that require significant effort to perform quantitative comparisons for values of the same magnitude and values of neighboring magnitudes. Specifically, employing the Area channel or Length not aligned or orthogonal to the exponent for the mantissa necessitates frequent legend consultation to identify values. This complexity intensifies with the use of Intensity, severely complicating the estimation of ratios between two Intensity (Example in 6(b)).

Recommendations: To comply with this guideline, we recommend favoring the use of PosY or PosX for encoding the mantissa. When this is not possible due to the violation of other guidelines (e.g., CoM), we advise using Length for point marks, requiring the drawn lines to be aligned, as alignment facilitates comparison. However, we do not advocate for using Area and Intensity, as these channels are not discriminable within the defined range of the mantissa [19].

4.1.3 CoM — Continuity Between Magnitudes

The channel used for the mantissa should express a transition from one exponent to the next, reflecting the mathematical relationship between the two attributes. CoM ensures a coherent and understandable progression across the entire range of values and facilitates the quantitative comparison of values that belong to neighboring exponents. Compliance with this guideline is the primary differentiating factor between the visualizations in the “Effortless and Effective” (25/25) category and those in the “Suitable with Effort” (1/7) category. It is also notably low in the “Medium Effort and Low Confidence” (4/55) and “High Effort or Impractical” (5/81) categories. While CoM is not necessary to perform the tasks, the absence of it leads to higher efforts to estimate differences between OMVs in neighboring magnitudes (Example in 6(a)).

Recommendations: To comply with this guideline, we recommend encoding the mantissa with PosY when either PosY (E+M scale) or Row is used for the exponent, and, symmetrically, using PosX when either PosX or Col is employed for the exponent. CoM can also be achieved by placing the exponent in a positional channel and using Length or Area for points, provided the marks are drawn in the same direction as the exponents, start from the same position, and reach their maximum range at the position of the next exponent. Given the boundaries we have established for our design space, no other combination can achieve CoM.

4.1.4 PaC — Parsimony in Channels

Borrowing the term from philosophy, we define PaC as the principle of using the fewest visual channels required to effectively visualize the exponent and mantissa. This approach serves two primary purposes: firstly, it minimizes interference effects between channels; secondly, it conserves visual capacity, allowing for the efficient integration of additional data attributes into the visualization. In our design space, we consider visualizations that adhere to PaC as those that employ the same visual channel for both the exponent and mantissa, specifically PosY and PosX resulting in the E+M scale. Additionally, visualizations that use Row or Col for the exponent and PosY or PosX for the mantissa, respectively, are also considered compliant, as they create a similar visual effect to using a single channel for both OMV attributes.

This guideline is applicable to the majority of the “Effortless and Effective” category (21/25). It exhibits low compliance in the subsequent categories: “Suitable with Effort” (1/7), “Medium Effort and Low Confidence” (2/55), and “High Effort or Impractical” (7/81). While PaC is not essential for performing tasks, we believe that its absence could lead to ineffective visualizations due to unnecessary complexity (Example in Figure 6).

Recommendations: Similarly to CoM, to comply with this guideline, we recommend using the E+M scale, or encoding the mantissa with PosY when Row is used for the exponent, and symmetrically, using PosX when Col is employed for the exponent. Given the boundaries of our design space, no other combination can achieve PaC.

4.2 Effectiveness in Relation to Mark and Data

Effectiveness per mark

The choice of mark significantly influences the effectiveness of visualizing OMVs. Our findings indicate that different mark types, even with identical combinations of visual channels, exhibit varied levels of effectiveness. The point mark, creating the least interference, facilitates the encoding of multiple channels, yielding more expressive visualizations. We noted that using the E+M scale, while highly effective for points, demanded greater effort for ratio tasks when lines were employed (Figure 5). However, this difficulty was not observed when using Row for the exponent and line for the mark. A possible reason for this difference is that, while bars are interpreted in a linear manner, the E+M scale that results from both exponent and mantissa in PosY is not linear, causing confusion in the visual comparison of the two lines. Conversely, areas behave differently; faceting (exponent encoded with Row) led to decreased performance. Other studies, such as Braun et al. [6], address this by overlaying facets to create a horizon chart. Moreover, area marks demand more design considerations to circumvent common pitfalls. Employing redundant encoding and additional marks, like point marks to highlight values, could enhance their effectiveness.

Effectiveness in relation to data

Different types for attribute-2 introduce various constraints that limit the effective combinations for visualizing OMVs. Nominal data might interfere with the mantissa and exponent if encoded using Hue or Shape, while the OMV attributes are represented with Intensity or Area. Ordinal data could interfere with an OMV attribute when Intensity is utilized for its encoding, and Area is allocated to either the mantissa or exponent. Time data, limited in expressiveness, do not significantly interfere with OMVs. Lastly, quantitative data is the most challenging, as their integration with OMVs appears feasible only when a positional channel is employed. Constraints or considerations could also be introduced in relation to the OMV. For instance, when there are exponent values with gaps (sparse), the use of Row or Col obscures these gaps, increasing the effort required for comparing values and potentially leading to errors. Yet, when data cardinality is high, positional channels are recommended for all attribute types, underscoring the need for PaC for the OMVs.

4.3 Redundant Encoding

For our qualitative analysis, we focused on combinations where each attribute is encoded using a single visual channel. This approach was chosen because allowing for more flexibility in encoding would lead to an unmanageable large number of combinations. However, employing redundant encoding can enhance total discrimination and information transmission [1], potentially improving the accuracy of the mantissa and exponent and thereby enhancing the overall viability and effectiveness of a visualization design (AcM, DeM). To support this, our tool (Figure 3) enables users to interactively explore the entire spectrum of possible redundant encoding configurations available, considering the boundaries and the dimensions of our design space.

Achieving more effective visualizations through redundant encoding, however, demands a meticulous approach to mitigate any interference between the employed channels. This is particularly crucial because, for redundancy to benefit a visualization, the selected combination of visual channels must exhibit a correlation [1]. This principle applies, for example, when both width and height or Intensity and Area are assigned to the same attribute. Nevertheless, this strategy comes with its caveats. Performance can significantly suffer when there is interference between channels assigned to different attributes, leading to confusion and misinterpretation of the data. For example, when a line is encoded with both height (PosY) and width (Length) for two different attributes, people cannot separate the two dimensions when estimating the visualized magnitude [1]. Therefore, it becomes imperative to apply separability rules when considering an OMV attribute alongside an additional attribute.

When implementing redundant encoding, it is crucial to maintain the inherent numerical relationship between the mantissa and exponent. Treating the exponent and mantissa as two completely separable attributes can lead to continuity issues, complicating task execution and potentially fostering incorrect perceptions of the OMV derived from their combination. For instance, assigning Area to the mantissa and Intensity to the exponent could lead to confusion regarding which channel represents the magnitude, thereby increasing the likelihood of errors. Such a setup might obscure the integral link between the mantissa and exponent. Consequently, we recommend against assigning Length, Area, and Intensity to the mantissa or exponent if one of the mentioned channels is already used by the other OMV attribute. Nevertheless, we advocate for selecting redundant encoding strategies that favor the exponent to enhance its accuracy (AcM). This approach helps ensure a coherent and effective representation of the OMV, facilitating easier comprehension and minimizing the risk of misinterpretation.

4.4 Revisiting Existing OMV Visualizations

In this section, we revisit the existing literature and critically discuss the proposed OMVs visualizations in light of our design space dimensions, exploration results, and proposed guidelines.

SSB

falls within the “Effortless and Effective” category of our design space. Specifically, it uses a line mark, Row for the exponent, PosY for the mantissa, and PosX for a nominal attribute, aligning with the guidelines we recommend for efficiently visualizing OMVs. However, our visualization design for the same combination diverges in a key aspect. We adhere to the scientific normalized notation, thus encoding the mantissa in PosY within the range [1,10[[1,10[[ 1 , 10 [. In contrast, SSB’s approach initiates the range within each row from 0. This leads to the depiction of all previous magnitudes within the [0,1[[0,1[[ 0 , 1 [ range for each row. SSB’s authors contend that this approach facilitates the independent interpretation of each row, suggesting that visualizations might be misleading if the axis does not start at 0. Nonetheless, we argue that this viewpoint contradicts the continuity guideline (CoM), potentially explaining why subsequent studies utilizing SSB reported that participants found comparisons challenging [25] and identified that the majority of errors in SSB occurred with values in neighboring orders of magnitude [6]. This hypothesis deserves further research.

WSB

uses line for marks, redundant encoding for the exponent, combining Length (width) and Intensity, and PosY for the mantissa. This setup corresponds to two distinct combinations in our design space due to the redundant encoding for the exponent. Both combinations are categorized as “High Effort or Impractical” for two main reasons: 1) the channels used for the exponent, Length and Intensity, are low in accuracy (AcM); 2) as mentioned in the background, there is an interference effect between both the height of the line (defined by PosY for the mantissa) and width (defined by Length for the exponent), and between size (defined by PosY for the mantissa) and Intensity for the exponent. However, the fact that they have a very small range for exponents (4) and the choice to use redundant encoding diminish the accuracy limitations for estimating the exponent. Nevertheless, the visualization does not follow the guidelines of CoM, which could lead to difficulties in performing quantitative comparisons, a fact verified by their empirical study. Lastly, the use of many channels to achieve accuracy for the exponent limits the potential for effectively visualizing an additional attribute (PaC).

OMC

is a colormap for OMVs, combining Hue for the exponent and Intensity for the mantissa. It is applied to a point mark alongside two quantitative attributes in PosY and PosX, demonstrating the flexibility to be combined with additional attributes (PaC). In our design space, we exclude combinations of both Intensity and Hue within the same mark, in alignment with literature on the interference caused by integral channels [18, 8]. Nonetheless, we recognize that since the exponent and mantissa originate from the separation of a single value, encoding them with integral channels might be justifiable if such a combination accurately represents their mathematical relationship, with the mantissa indicating a transition from one exponent to the next (CoM). OMC, however, does not provide continuity between magnitudes, and as they state, their approach “is not feasible for comparing values that are close to the borders of the exponents” [7].

OML

integrates the positional encodings of SSB with the colormap of OMC. Thus, it employs an area mark, PosY, and Hue for the exponent, PosY and Intensity for the mantissa, and PosX for a time-series attribute. The positional encoding for the exponent and mantissa ensures high accuracy (AcM, DeM) and continuity (CoM). However, we contend that the decision to use OMC for redundant encoding, which contravenes CoM, diminishes the visualization’s perceived continuity. They align with this argument as they also indicate that the slower response times and increased cognitive load might result from the redundant encoding by position and color. Finally, they conclude that “the separation of mantissa and exponent needs to be discrete, as the continuous separation in the case of OML does not seem efficient.” Nevertheless, we argue that continuous separation could be efficient, provided it adheres to the principle of CoM. This hypothesis warrants further investigation.

OMH

modifies the standard horizon graph, using area mark, Intensity for exponent, PosY for mantissa and PosX for the time attribute. Employing Intensity for the exponent contradicts the AcM principle but could be effective when the exponent range is lower than five. The authors also note that “the choice of colors could be improved to facilitate easier distinction between the bands,” indicating potential issues with discriminability. Utilizing PosY for the mantissa aids in quantitative comparisons within the same magnitude (DeM), yet the lack of CoM complicates comparisons between neighboring exponents.

4.5 Limitations

The main limitations of our design space arise from the specific boundaries we established. We focused on static visualizations of OMVs for overviews, thereby not exploring the potential benefits of interactive techniques. Our study is confined to tabular data and visualizations within the Cartesian coordinate system, suggesting that other data types and layouts could offer additional insights. We also did not include negative mantissa and exponents in our analysis, although we speculate their behavior would be similar to that of positive ones. Furthermore, we explore only visualizations that include one mark for both mantissa and exponent, excluding the OMM from our design space. Moreover, our exploration centers on three tasks we deem fundamental for OMVs. However, further tasks, such as correlation, could be explored.

A general limitation of design spaces, including the one we explore in this paper, is that it is not possible to encompass all the factors that have an impact on design. Our focus was on perception and we did not consider additional factors that might influence the overall effectiveness of visualization design, such as attention [15], visualization literacy [5], visual aesthetics [10], and the background and preferences of the user [14].

5 Conclusions and Future work

In this article, we presented the design space of OMV visualization decomposing values into mantissa and exponent parts, presented a list of effective visualizations for OMVs, define the E+M scale, and introduced guidelines and recommendations for designing effective OMV visualizations. Our design space is descriptive, generative since it allows presenting new OMV designs, and evaluative since it provides criteria to eliminate ineffective combinations, select viable ones, and improve their designs. Yet, it cannot quantitatively compare the effectiveness of different viable combinations.

We also introduced OMVis, an open-source web tool that enables the systematic interactive exploration of OMVs. We hope our study will motivate visualization system designers to define operators to easily separate OMV attributes into mantissa and exponent parts, and define the E+M scale to support them with positional visual channels. Our study addressed several questions regarding effective OMV visualization but raised new ones, and was not able to answer all the questions. Controlled experiments are still needed to compare the effectiveness of several OMV visualizations, using our set of tasks, as well as more tasks for specific applications and attribute types. Our design space is a subset of the whole visualization design space, and OMVs might also be useful for networks, fields, maps, and more specialized visualizations and application domains.
