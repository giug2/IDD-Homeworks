LLMBind: A Unified Modality-Task Integration Framework

Bin Zhu Peng Jin Munan Ning Bin Lin Jinfa Huang Qi Song Junwu Zhang Zhenyu Tang Mingjun Pan Xing Zhou Li Yuan
Abstract

While recent progress in multimodal large language models tackles various modality tasks, they posses limited integration capabilities for complex multi-modality tasks, consequently constraining the development of the field. In this work, we take the initiative to explore and propose the LLMBind , a unified framework for modality task integration, which binds Large Language Models and corresponding pre-trained task models with task-specific tokens. Consequently, LLMBind can interpret inputs and produce outputs in versatile combinations of image, text, video, and audio. Specifically, we introduce a Mixture-of-Experts technique to enable effective learning for different multimodal tasks through collaboration among diverse experts. Furthermore, we create a multi-task dataset comprising 400k instruction data, which unlocks the ability for interactive visual generation and editing tasks. Extensive experiments show the effectiveness of our framework across various tasks, including image, video, audio generation, image segmentation, and image editing. More encouragingly, our framework can be easily extended to other modality tasks, showcasing the promising potential of creating a unified AI agent for modeling universal modalities.

1 Introduction

Recently, there has been significant progress in the field of AI, including the Large Language Models (LLMs) for natural language processing tasks and the diffusion models for high-resolution image generation. As shown in Figure 1 , existing Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) can be broadly categorized into three classes and we specifically use two metrics to evaluate the efficacy of a framework. The first indicator, called “updatability”, quantifies the ease of replacing the freezed task-specific model while the second, “versatility”, indicates the ability of the framework to extend to other tasks. The first class of methods, such as MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023b ) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c ) , typically utilize encode-side adapters to handle inputs beyond language and can only perform the visual question-answering task. The second class of methods (Wu et al., 2023 ; Ghosal et al., 2023 ; Koh et al., 2023 ) , as shown in Figure 1 (b), incorporates decode-side adapters for various generation models, enhancing the adaptation of LLMs to complex multimodal tasks like generation. However, these methods lack updatability as they require training a new adapter when introducing a new generation model. For easy replacement of different generation models without additional training, the third class of methods (Xia et al., 2023 ; Brooks et al., 2023 ) directly generates desired text prompts for specific modality tasks, including image generation, audio generation, or image editing, as depicted in Figure 1 (c). But these approaches can only perform tasks that only have text prompts as additional inputs, and cannot solve more complex visual tasks such as image segmentation. In brief, existing methods tend to solve limited tasks and do not explore the unification of more tasks within a shared LLM.

In this work, we introduce LLMBind , a unified model integration framework designed to integrate models of various modality tasks, thereby unlocking the multitasking capabilities of the MLLM. As depicted in Figure 1 (d), LLMBind is capable of processing two modalities: image and text. It processes these inputs through a visual encoder (Radford et al., 2021 ) and LLM (Chiang et al., 2023 ) , ultimately producing task-specific text prompts or token embeddings. For tasks such as video generation (Ding et al., 2021b ; Rombach et al., 2022a ) , image generation (Nichol et al., 2022 ; Tang et al., 2023 ) , audio generation (Liu et al., 2023b ; Yang et al., 2023a ) , and image editing (Brooks et al., 2023 ; Fu et al., 2023 ) , we utilize text prompts as input to existing pre-trained generation models, significantly reducing the training complexity of our framework and enhancing its updateability. For modality tasks like image segmentation (Lai et al., 2023 ; Liu et al., 2023a ) , classification (Yang et al., 2023c ; Naeem et al., 2023 ) , and detection (Ge et al., 2021 ; Pi et al., 2023 ) that cannot be solely addressed with text prompts, we extract relevant semantic visual information from images using learnable task-specific token embeddings. Subsequently, these embeddings are utilized as an auxiliary input to the corresponding modality-task models.

To guide the LLM to generate task-specific text prompts and token embeddings, we expand the vocabulary of LLM with learnable task-specific tokens, which can be divided into two categories: Task-Prompt Tokens and Semantic-Embedding Tokens . By training the LLM with specially designed instruction data pairs and task-specific tokens, we can instruct the model to discriminate the task type from user input and generate the corresponding text prompt or token embedding for a given multimodal task. Moreover, to better unify different modality tasks within the shared LLM, we introduce a highly effective technique called Mixture-of-Experts (MoE), which has been used successfully in recent studies (Shazeer et al., 2017 ; Lepikhin et al., 2020 ; Fedus et al., 2022 ; Riquelme et al., 2021 ; Ahmed et al., 2016 ; Fedus et al., 2022 ) . By incorporating this technique, our LLMBind can handle various modality tasks by utilizing different experts to collaboratively process multi-modal tasks, as shown in Figure 2 .

Another fundamental contribution of this work is the creation of a dataset containing 400k instruction data for interactive generation and editing tasks. Each sample is generated by ChatGPT and formatted as a multi-round dialogue. This dataset addresses the scarcity of generation and editing instruction data and empowers LLMBind with robust interaction capabilities for generating and editing tasks.

Overall, LLMBind demonstrates promising results in advancing the development of human-like MLLM and AI agents. Contributions of this work are as follows:

  • •

    We design a unified model integration framework that expands task-specific tokens for diverse modality tasks, thus easily integrating different tasks into a unified LLM, where we introduce the MoE technique in our framework to better handle diverse modality tasks.

  • •

    We propose a unified MLLM that is compatible with various modality tasks, including image segmentation, image generation, image editing, video generation, and audio generation.

  • •

    To facilitate the development of user-friendly interactive tasks, we construct a dataset of 400k interactive generation and editing multi-turn dialogues using ChatGPT. We plan to release this dataset as an open resource to foster collaborative advancements in this field.

2 Related Work

2.1 Cross-Modal Understanding and Segmentation

With the introduction of frameworks such as LanguageBind (Zhu et al., 2023a ) and ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023 ) , which combine multi-modal data in a shared semantic space, several works focusing on cross-modal understanding (Liu et al., 2023c ; Zhu et al., 2023b ; Yin et al., 2023 ; Jin et al., 2023 ) have emerged. Along with these advancements, there has been a growing interest in text-guided image referring segmentation, a new approach that enables users to interact with images using user instruction. Unlike traditional semantic segmentation, which assigns class labels to pixels, referring segmentation aims to segment target objects based on complex textual instructions. Representative works in this area include LISA (Lai et al., 2023 ) , GLaMM (Rasheed et al., 2023 ) , GRES (Liu et al., 2023a ) , and LAVT (Yang et al., 2022 ) .

2.2 Cross-Modal Generation and Editing

Multi-modal generation has remained a highly active research area in recent years. These models (Ramesh et al., 2022 ; Rombach et al., 2022b ; Hong et al., 2022b ; Kong et al., 2020 ; Yang et al., 2023b ) allow the generation of corresponding images, videos, and audio based on specified text prompts. However, this approach requires users to directly write text prompts for image generation, lacking interactive capabilities. Another approach to cross-modal generation (Wu et al., 2023 ; Zeqiang et al., 2023 ) , combines dialogue with generation, making it more user-friendly and aligned with current user needs. Given that generated images may not always meet user requirements, image editing has emerged as another active research area within the field of artificial intelligence-generated content. Representative works in this area include Text2live (Bar-Tal et al., 2022 ) , Imagic (Kawar et al., 2023 ) , Sdedit (Meng et al., 2021 ) .

2.3 Mixture of Experts

The concept of Mixture of Experts (MoE) in artificial neural networks has evolved over time. Early methods activated all experts, leading to high computational requirements (Eigen et al., 2013 ) . Subsequent research introduced sparse MoE models, where distinct experts are selectively activated using a learnable gating mechanism (Shazeer et al., 2017 ; Lepikhin et al., 2020 ; Fedus et al., 2022 ) . These approaches have shown success in computer vision (Eigen et al., 2013 ; Ahmed et al., 2016 ; Gross et al., 2017 ; Wang et al., 2020 ) and natural language processing (Shazeer et al., 2017 ; Lepikhin et al., 2020 ; Zhou et al., 2022 ) domains. Notably, the recent Mixtrial-8x7B model (mistralai, 2023 ) has garnered attention by showcasing the benefits of leveraging different experts for different tasks, resulting in improved performance while reducing computational costs.

2.4 Multimodal Large Language Models

Recently, the emergence of Large Language Models (LLM) has ushered AI into a new era. With the open-sourcing of LLM models like LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023 ) and Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023 ) , numerous research works have successfully explored methods to extend LLMs to other modalities, including video, audio, and images. This has led to the development of a variety of related MLLM models, as shown in Table 1 . For instance, Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022 ) employs cross-attention structures for visual-text interaction, while DetGPT (Pi et al., 2023 ) connects multimodal LLMs with detectors, enabling object detection based on user instructions. BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023b ) encodes input images into queries using the Q-Former structure. mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2023 ) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c ) encode images into image features using image encoders, which are then combined with text embeddings for LLM learning. LISA (Lai et al., 2023 ) achieves referring segmentation by training LLMs to output mask token embeddings in an end-to-end manner. NExTGPT (Wu et al., 2023 ) implements multimodal generation by aligning the output of the adapter, which is added after the LLM, with the output text features from the text encoder of various generation models (Liu et al., 2023b ; Rombach et al., 2022a ) .

3 Method

In this work, we present LLMBind, a unified framework for model integration, aiming to handle various modality tasks into a shared LLM. In Section 3.1 , we detail the components of LLMBind and the process of their integration. In Section 3.2 , we discuss our implementation of the LoRA Mixture-of-Experts technique. In Section 3.3 , we focus on the training objectives and the specific loss functions we use for model optimization. Finally, we describe the dataset crucial for the training of LLMBind in Section 3.4 .

3.1 LLMBind

Overview. The framework of LLMBind is illustrated in Figure 3 . It primarily consists of a vision transformer (Radford et al., 2021 ) as the image encoder, a large language model called Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023 ) , a SAM-like mask decoder as well as image, audio, and video diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022a ; Tang et al., 2023 ; Singer et al., 2022 ) for generation and editing tasks.

Visual Encoder and Tokenizer. We prioritize the processing of inputs from two primary modalities: images and text. For image encoding, we employ the ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021 ) model to extract image features. Task-Specific Tokens. Different multimodal tasks often require distinct network structures, making it difficult to consolidate them within a shared LLM. To overcome this challenge, we expand the vocabulary of the LLM with specially designed, learnable task-specific tokens. As shown in Figure 4 , these tokens can be classified into two categories: Task-Prompt Tokens and Semantic-Embedding Tokens . The first category includes tokens similar to standard text tokens, such as <gen> and </gen> for image generation, <vid_cap> and </vid_cap> for video generation, and <edit> and </edit> for image editing. These tokens appear in pairs, and the content between them represents the intermediate text prompt required for the corresponding modality-task. In contrast, the second category is imbued with dense semantic information, diverging from traditional text tokens. For example, <seg> contains rich visual information related to segmented objects in an image. After decoding by a mask decoder, this token can be converted back into masks. This category also encompasses tokens like <detect> and <cls> , designed for extracting spatial coordinates and classification data in image-based tasks. This dual-token framework not only enhances the adaptability of the LLM across various modalities but also ensures scalability and extensibility.

Modality Task Integration. We seamlessly integrate various modality-task models in our framework using task-specific text prompts and token embeddings generated by LLM. For understanding tasks, we directly output text. In the generation and editing scenarios, we feed text prompts into the corresponding task model. Specifically, we use Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022a ) for image generation, CoDi (Tang et al., 2023 ) for audio generation, ModelScopeT2V (Wang et al., 2023 ) for video generation, Instruct-pix2pix (Brooks et al., 2023 ) for image editing, thus ensuring high-quality, modality-specific output. For complex tasks such as referring segmentation, we use a single task-specific token embedding to learn the semantic information of the image features with respect to the segmentation instructions, which is then processed by a dedicated segmentation decoder. This Integration method is adaptable to various task requirements and has good extensibility.

3.4 Dataset

The training datasets consist of both publicly available datasets and our custom proprietary ones, as visualized in Figure 5 . These proprietary datasets are specifically constructed through interactive processes enabled by ChatGPT.

Interactive Image Generation and Editing Dataset. To empower the model in executing image generation and editing tasks, following Mini-DALLE3 (Zeqiang et al., 2023 ) , we design a prompt for ChatGPT to construct 400k human-like interactive instruction data, tackling the problem of image generation and editing through textual description. As shown in Figure 6 , we first clarify the role and capability of ChatGPT within the prompt. We provide it with instruction text formats, tags, and examples for both generating and editing tasks. However, we notice that the generated samples closely resemble the examples in terms of text format, tone, and content. To address this, we further clarify the diversity of the target data in terms of the above multiple aspects in the prompt. Following a manual review of a portion of our dataset, we have confirmed that this approach is highly effective in ensuring significant diversity.

Interactive Video and Audio Generation Dataset. To construct video and audio generation instruction data, we adapt the t2x (Wu et al., 2023 ) dataset and change its format to resemble that used for the image generation task. Some examples have been listed in Appendix B .

Segmentation Datasets. For segmentation task, we incorporate several semantic segmentation datasets, including ADE20K (Zhou et al., 2017 ) , COCO-Stuff (Caesar et al., 2018 ) , PACO-LVIS (Ramanathan et al., 2023 ) , PartImageNet (He et al., 2022 ) , and PASCAL-Part (Chen et al., 2014 ) . We also utilize referring segmentation datasets like refCLEF, refCOCO, refCOCO+ (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014 ) , and refCOCOg (Mao et al., 2016 ) .

Visual Question Answering Datasets. We incorporates the LLaVA-Instruct-665k dataset (Liu et al., 2023d ) for visual question-answering task. This dataset enables the training of models to comprehend, reason, and make decisions accurately to questions based on visual content.

4 Experiments

4.1 Experimental Configuration

Training Specifications. During training, we employ 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs (40G). The training scripts are based on the deepspeed (Rasley et al., 2020 ) engine. We use the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017 ) optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0003 and weight decay of 0. The WarmupDecayLR learning rate scheduler is adopted, with a warmup iteration count of 100. The auto-regressive loss and segmentation loss have weights of 1.0 each. The BCE loss and DICE loss have weights of 2.0 and 0.5 respectively. We set the batch size per device to 2, and accumulate gradients over 10 steps, with a total of 15k steps.

Data Details. During training, to sustain the stability of multi-task training, we configure the data ratios for Segmentation data, Visual Question Answering data, and Interactive Generation and Editing instruction data at a 1:1:1 ratio. Within the segmentation dataset, the proportions for semantic segmentation, referring segmentation, and reasoning segmentation are established at 9:3:1. In the interactive instructions, the distribution for tasks like image generation, image editing, audio generation, and video generation is balanced at a 1:1:1:1 ratio. We train jointly on all task datasets to ensure that our model avoids loss fluctuations and balances losses across tasks.

Evaluation Metrics. Our experiments utilize some key metrics for precise evaluation. Text-to-audio generation is assessed using Fréchet Distance (FD) for distribution similarity and Inception Score (IS) for quality and diversity. Text-to-video generation leverages Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) for content quality and CLIPSIM for textual alignment. In text-to-image generation, FID evaluates image distribution similarity. For segmentation, gIoU is defined by the average of all per-image Intersection-over-Unions (IoUs), while cIoU is defined by the cumulative intersection over the cumulative union.

4.2 Quantitative Results

Referring Segmentation. For referring segmentation task, as illustrated in Table 6 , LLMBind achieves superior results on various datasets. On the refCOCO dataset, for the val, testA, and testB splits, LLMBind achieves scores of 76.9, 78.5, and 73.2, respectively. Similarly, on the refCOCO dataset, the model records scores of 67.8, 71.9, and 60.8 for val, testA, and testB. On the refCOCOG dataset, LLMBind achieves 69.8 and 70.8.

Video & Audio & Image Generation. In the text-to-audio generation task, as shown in Table 5 , LLMBind adopts CoDi (Tang et al., 2023 ) as the baseline generation model and achieves an FD score of 22.90 and an IS score of 8.77 on the AudioCaps (Kim et al., 2019 ) dataset, outperforming models like NeXT-GPT and AudioLDM. In the text-to-image generation task, as shown in Table 5 , LLMBind employs Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022a ) as the baseline generation model and attains an FID score of 11.21 on the COCO-caption (Lin et al., 2014 ) dataset, surpassing models like NeXT-GPT and CoDi. In the text-to-video generation task, as depicted in Table 5 , LLMBind selects ModelScopeT2V (Wang et al., 2023 ) as the baseline generation model and obtains an FID of 11.09 and a CLIPSIM score of 0.2930 on the MSR-VTT (Xu et al., 2016 ) dataset, with a higher FID score and a slightly lower CLIPSIM score than NExT-GPT. In terms of generation tasks, since LLMBind does not perform lossy training on the generation models, it can be assumed that its generative capability largely depends on the chosen generation models. With sufficient computational resources, LLMBind can employ better-performing models to achieve superior generation results selectively.

Reasoning Segmentation. We evaluate the performance of LLMBind on the referring segmentation task. For a fair comparison, we use the LISA-7B model as our benchmark. As shown in Table 6 , LLMBind achieves GIoU and CIoU scores of 62.4 and 66.9 on the ReasonSeg dataset (Lai et al., 2023 ) , which exceeds LISA-7B with a large margin (8.8 and 12.9) and comparable with LISA-13B.

4.3 Ablation Analysis

We select the reasoning segmentation for ablation analysis, since it is conducive to quantitative analysis and comparison.

Rank of LoRA. As shown in Table 9 , we conduct detailed experiments with different ranks of LoRA and found that our framework exhibits slight performance variation across various settings. Therefore, we chose a final rank of 8, for a balance between training cost and model performance.

Training Steps. Regarding the number of training iterations, we design experiments with four distinct settings: 5k, 6k, 7k, and 8k training steps. These variations were chosen to systematically evaluate the impact of training duration on model performance. As detailed in Table 9 , our findings reveal a clear trend: longer iteration steps consistently yield improvements in both gIoU and cIoU metrics.

Number of MoE Layers. For the configuration of MoE layers, we conduct experiments with 16, 10, 8, and 0 layers, with the results presented in Table 9 . The model’s performance generally improves with an increasing number of MoE layers. Due to limited model memory, we do not perform experiments with 32 MoE layers, but we believe that more MoE layers could further enhance the performance.

Number of All Experts and Top-K Activated Experts. In our MoE framework, the number of all experts and the number of Top-K activated experts are both crucial. In Table 10 , we briefly explore how different numbers of experts and activated experts affect model performance. The findings align with our expectations: when the number of experts is fixed, more activated experts improve model performance; similarly, increasing the number of experts enhances performance when the number of activated experts is constant.

5 Conclusion and Future Direction

In this work, we introduce the LLMBind framework, designed to address the limitations of current Multimodal Large Language Models in handling a wide range of multi-modality tasks. LLMBind efficiently integrates tasks across various modalities such as image segmentation, video generation, and more, through its unique Task-Specific tokens. To enhance the adaptability and efficiency of our framework, we introduce a novel dataset of 400k interactive instructions and an innovative Mixture-of-Experts architecture. Extensive experimental validation demonstrates that LLMBind excels in a variety of tasks and holds immense potential for expansion to other modality tasks.

Our framework marks a significant step towards creating a unified AI agent capable of handling universal modalities. Looking ahead, we aim to further expand the LLMBind framework to include more modality tasks while concurrently focusing on investigating and refining training strategies for multi-task joint training.

Appendix for LLMBind

Appendix A Interactive Architecture

A.1 Multi-Turn and Interactive

LLMBind allows users to accomplish various modality-tasks as mentioned above through a dynamic and interactive conversational approach. Unlike the traditional fixed-format prompt inputs, users have more freedom to express their requirements and can improve the model’s outputs through interactive dialogue, such as modifying the content of generated images. Additionally, LLMBind can automatically preprocess the user’s input text intent and analyze the corresponding modality-task, greatly reducing the user’s learning curve and enhancing the overall user experience.

A.2 Interactive Multi-modal Generation

LLMBind can automatically determine the corresponding data type for generation based on the details provided in the input text. This includes image generation, video generation, and audio generation. Unlike traditional generation methods that require specific text descriptions, LLMBind leverages the capabilities of LLM to create image captions that align with user-provided objects, scenes, or concepts. This assists users in conceptualizing image content and achieving their desired image generation outcome. For image generation, LLMBind generates textual descriptions that match the user’s intent by incorporating <gen> DETAILED IMAGE CAPTION </gen> in the output response. Subsequently, LLMBind employs a generation model prompt style using “DETAILED IMAGE CAPTION” to generate the corresponding image. It is worth noting that during this process, users are not required to adhere to a fixed prompt format. For video and text generation, LLMBind follows a similar approach, replacing <gen>,</gen> with <vid_cap>,</vid_cap> and <aud_cap>,</aud_cap> respectively.

A.3 Interactive Image Editing and Refinement

LLMBind offers interactive image editing and refinement capabilities. Image editing typically involves modifying image content based on textual instructions. This can involve significant changes to objects and attributes within the image. The edited image may differ greatly from the original, but it closely aligns with the user’s textual instructions. On the other hand, image refinement entails making adjustments and optimizations to enhance the details of the image. The edited image should maintain a significant resemblance to the original but incorporates specific image adjustments based on the provided text instructions.

A.4 Interactive Text Conversation

Text-based conversation is a potential capability of LLMs, and LLMBind excels in maintaining effective communication while simultaneously supporting other modality tasks. This functionality enables seamless switching between different modality tasks and highlights the user-friendly nature of the model.

A.5 Joint Training Challenge

In our initial experiments, we observe that LLMBind rapidly excels in generating task-specific text prompts for various modality-tasks. This result aligns with our expectations due to LLMs’ established strengths in text comprehension and generation. However, a significant challenge arises when incorporating semantic-Embedding tokens in tasks like segmentation. LLMBind struggles to differentiate between image comprehension and segmentation tasks, often confusingly including the segmentation token <seg> in its responses, regardless of the simplicity of the text instructions. To address this, we increase the weight of the text generation loss, decrease the weight of the segmentation loss, and augment the proportion of VQA data in training and successfully tackle this issue. It can be seen that in the process of joint multi-task training, the loss of weight coefficients between multiple tasks and the proportion of training data for different tasks are issues that need to be explored in practice.
