Abstract

Fine-tuning large-scale pre-trained models via transfer learning is an emerging important paradigm for a wide range of downstream tasks, with performance heavily reliant on extensive data. Federated learning (FL), as a distributed framework, provides a secure solution to train models on local datasets while safeguarding raw sensitive data. However, FL networks encounter high communication costs due to the massive parameters of large-scale pre-trained models, necessitating parameter-efficient methods. Notably, parameter efficient fine tuning, such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), has shown remarkable success in fine-tuning pre-trained models. However, prior research indicates that the fixed parameter budget may be prone to the overfitting or slower convergence. To address this challenge, we propose a Simulated Annealing-based Federated Learning with LoRA tuning (SA-FedLoRA) approach by reducing trainable parameters. Specifically, SA-FedLoRA comprises two stages: initiating and annealing. (1) In the initiating stage, we implement a parameter regularization approach during the early rounds of aggregation, aiming to mitigate client drift and accelerate the convergence for the subsequent tuning. (2) In the annealing stage, we allocate higher parameter budget during the early ’heating’ phase and then gradually shrink the budget until the ’cooling’ phase. This strategy not only facilitates convergence to the global optimum but also reduces communication costs. Experimental results demonstrate that SA-FedLoRA is an efficient FL, achieving superior performance to FedAvg and significantly reducing communication parameters by up to 93.62%.

1 Introduction

The advent of the pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm in machine learning, such as BERT and GPT, has revolutionized the traditional model training and generalization technique. Pre-trained foundation models, such as Swin Transformer Liu et al. (2021b) and Vision Transformer Dosovitskiy et al. (2020), have achieved significant success in speeding up convergence and delivering superior performance across various downstream tasks Lin et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023b). Pre-training is a self-supervised learning method, training on large datasets to capture general features for initialization, facilitating efficient domain-specific fine-tuning of pre-trained models and thus saving time compared to the traditional method training from scratch Chen et al. (2022a); Pan et al. (2022). However, acquiring large-scale datasets for pre-training is particularly challenging in sensitive fields like finance and medicine due to privacy concerns and regulatory restrictions Kairouz et al. (2021). Federated learning (FL) offers a promising privacy-preserving solution by enabling multiple data owners to collaboratively train a global model without sharing sensitive data. Specifically, local clients download the global pre-trained model from the central server and update the model based on their datasets. The local model parameters will be uploaded to the server to aggregate a new global model for future training and inference Almanifi et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2023). This iterative process, however, introduces heavy communication burdens on the resource-limited FL systems due to the massive parameter size of pre-trained foundation models Sun et al. (2022).

Previous studies of communication-efficient FL, such as model compression Konečnỳ et al. (2016) and one/few-shot communication Zhou et al. (2020); Park et al. (2021) reach suboptimal performance on the pre-trained models due to the neglect of the model transfer ability. Recently, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have gained increasing popularity in the literature by updating only a small fraction of model parameters (e.g., bias terms) and freezing the substantial parameters Houlsby et al. (2019); Zaken et al. (2021); Jie and Deng (2023). Among these, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) stands out as a representative reparameterization-based method, that minimizes the number of trainable parameters without increasing inference latency, by inserting trainable rank decomposition matrices alongside the pre-trained model with original pre-trained weights fixed Chen et al. (2023a); Hu et al. (2021). FedLora, which incorporates LoRA into the fine-tuning process within a federated framework, shares low-rank decomposition matrices instead of full pre-trained parameters, thus alleviating communication overhead Yi et al. (2023); Babakniya et al. (2023). However, the number of trainable parameters heavily depends on the rank r𝑟r. Existing work has observed that less trainable parameters with fixed lower rank require more time to converge while more trainable parameters with fixed higher rank may lead to overfitting of the global model Cho et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b). Therefore, determining trainable parameters flexibly with the optimal rank in FL setting remains a challenge. Can we design an efficient algorithm in FL to dynamically allocate parameter budgets and adaptively adjust rank, thus accelerating convergence and reducing communication costs while maintaining accuracy?

In this study, we propose a Simulated Annealing-based Federated Learning with LoRA tuning (SA-FedLora) approach by dynamically allocating parameter budgets with adaptive Lora rank over the communication round. More specifically, our method consists of two stages: initiating and annealing. (1) In the initiating stage of FL, we train the full pre-trained parameters and introduce parameter regularization into the pre-trained model to address the issue of incompatible optimization direction of local models due to federated client drift, accelerating the convergence for the subsequent annealing stage; (2) In the annealing stage, we allocate a larger parameter budget with high-rank LoRA during the early heating phase. This allocation is strategically designed to encourage the global model to converge to its local optima quickly. To prevent the global model from getting trapped into overfitting, we gradually decrease trainable parameters via adaptively lowering the Lora rank until the start of cooling phase, where the model keeps training with fewer parameter budgets until convergence. Overall, SA-FedLora achieves comparable performance to FedAvg with significantly lower communication overhead by adaptively allocating the parameter budget during training.

In summary, our contributions are:

  • •

    Our paper proposes Simulated Annealing-based Federated Learning with LoRA tuning (SA-FedLora) to mitigate the communication burden of the pre-training FL systems by allocating the parameter budget with adaptive Lora rank. We divide the training into two stages: initiating and annealing.

  • •

    The initiating stage: each client trains the entire pre-trained parameters and adds a parameter regularization method to address the federated client drift and speed up the convergence; The annealing stage: more parameter budgets with high-rank LoRA are allocated to the early heating phase, and trainable parameters are dynamically reduced to the cooling phase by lowering the Lora rank, which avoids overfitting and reduces communication costs.

  • •

    Experiments have been conducted on the CIFAR-10 dataset and realistic medical Face dataset, demonstrating that SA-FedLora allows for significantly lower communication costs by up to 93.62% decline and even slightly superior performance than FedAvg.

2 Related Works

2.1 Pre-training in Federated Learning

Pre-trained foundation model originated from the natural language processing (NLP) area and has boosted state-of-the-art performance in computer vision and many other application domains due to its generality and adaptability to various specialized downstream tasks Han et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021b); Dosovitskiy et al. (2020); Radford et al. (2021). Federated Learning (FL) first emerged as a distributed privacy-preserving paradigm in 2016, called FedAvg, allowing local clients to train models on their datasets and a central server to collect local models to aggregate a global model McMahan et al. (2017). Research about the combination of pre-training and FL can be divided into two directions: pre-training for FL and FL for pre-training. Pre-training for FL refers to that pre-trained models improve the inferior effectiveness and robustness of FL due to large-scale model training from scratch Tan et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022b). For example, the FL accuracy of pretraining on ViT and Swin Transformer model outperforms training with random weight initialization Qu et al. (2022). FL for pre-training means that FL satisfies the huge amount of data requirements for pre-trained model training Tian et al. (2022); Babakniya et al. (2023). However, the scarcity of communication resources is a primary bottleneck in the FL system, which restricts the training and sharing of large-scale pre-trained models with massive parameters.

2.2 Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning in Federated Learning

Communication efficiency in FL has been extensively studied in the literature, such as model compression Konečnỳ et al. (2016) and one/few-shot communication Zhou et al. (2020); Park et al. (2021). Considering the strong transfer ability of pre-trained models, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) is a prevalent technique when facing today’s large pre-trained models, which have been categorized into three main groups, including reparameterization-based methods, additive methods and selective methods Lialin et al. (2023). Reparameterization-based methods, represented by LoRA, optimize the number of trainable parameters by leveraging low-rank decomposition matrices Hu et al. (2021); Yi et al. (2023). Additive methods, such as Adapters, add a few parameters between adjacent transformer layers and only tune these newly introduced modules Houlsby et al. (2019); Cai et al. (2022). Selective methods aim to update a small fraction of influential components within the model to achieve comparable performance Gheini et al. (2021); Arivazhagan et al. (2019). Among them, Lora with the advantage of introducing no inference latency, has widely integrated with FL to mitigate the communication burden. However, higher-rank Lora with more parameter budget may put the global model at risk of overfitting while lower-rank Lora with fewer parameters extends the convergence time Cho et al. (2023). Therefore, it is a challenge to reasonably allocate the parameter budget with the adaptive rank to boost the efficiency of the FL system with little degradation in accuracy.

3 The SA-FedLora Algorithm

3.1 Overview

An overview of the DCFL framework is shown in Figure 1. Under the constrained parameter budget, SA-FedLora cuts down the communication overhead and accelerates the convergence by allocating the parameter budget with adaptive Lora rank, thus divided into two stages. (1) Initiating stage: we first initialize the next annealing stage by incorporating a parameter regularization penalty term into the supervised loss function to tackle the federated client drift, prohibiting local models from drifting away from the global optimization objective. In each iteration of training, the whole pre-trained parameters are updated with more local epochs and uploaded to the server with fewer T1subscript𝑇1T_{1} communication rounds. (2) Annealing stage: after the initialization, we freeze pre-trained models and fine-tune Lora modules simultaneously in the local training phase. Specifically, inspired by the simulated annealing method, we allocate more parameters budgets with higher-rank Lora at the early heating phase to approach the optima of the global model swiftly, and adaptively decrease the allocated parameters with lower rank to avoid the overfitting as the communication round increases to the cooling phase. The shared light Lora module with few parameters significantly relieves the communication burden during the global aggregation phase.

4 Experiments

4.1 Experimental Setup

4.1.1 Datasets and Models

To validate the efficiency of SA-FedLora, we conduct experiments on two transformer models: Swin-T Liu et al. (2021b) model and Query2Label Liu et al. (2021a) model on CIFAR-10 dataset Krizhevsky et al. (2009) and a real-world private medical Face dataset, respectively. To further validate our FL method in more challenging settings, we construct a healthcare dataset of Face data with multi-label disease tasks, including 9 types of diseases (e.g., hyperlipidemia, malignancy lung). Each disease can be binarized the labels to positive and negative. The CIFAR-10 dataset and Face dataset are manually partitioned into 5 and 4 clients, respectively. For a fair comparison, all models used in this paper are pre-trained on ImageNet Deng et al. (2009). The datasets and corresponding models are summarized in Table 1.

4.1.2 Implementation Details

Experiments were conducted using Pytorch 2.1.0 with Python 3.11 on NVIDIA A40 GPUs. For the CIFAR-10 dataset, we choose an SGD optimizer with a learning rate of 0.03 for the Swin-T model. The maximal communication round of initiating stage T1subscript𝑇1T_{1} and annealing stage T2subscript𝑇2T_{2} is set to 3 and 80, respectively. The maximal local epoch of initiating stage E1subscript𝐸1E_{1} and annealing stage E2subscript𝐸2E_{2} is set to 30 and 2, respectively. For the Face dataset, we choose an Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0001 for the Query2Label model. The maximal communication round T1subscript𝑇1T_{1} and T2subscript𝑇2T_{2} is set to 3 and 50, respectively. The maximal local epoch E1subscript𝐸1E_{1} and E2subscript𝐸2E_{2} are set to 30 and 2, respectively. Their specific values will be dynamically tuned based to the experimental design. Some important implementations are also detailed in Table 1.

4.1.3 Evaluation Settings

We employ communication cost, accuracy (ACC) and Area-Under-Curve (AUC) metrics to measure the performance of the CIFAR-10 and the Face dataset. We compare SA-FedLoRA with 5 baseline methods:

  • •

    Local: clients train local models independently;

  • •

    FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017): clients train local models from scratch and all model parameters are updated and shared;

  • •

    FedPer Arivazhagan et al. (2019): clients update the whole model from scratch, but part of selected layers are shared and the rest are updated locally as personalized designs;

  • •

    FedFFT: clients train and update local pre-trained models with the full fine-tuning (FFT) method Babakniya et al. (2023), and all model parameters are shared;

  • •

    FedBit: clients train and update local pre-trained models with the BitFit method Zaken et al. (2021), and only share the bias vectors while freezing everything else.

4.2 Experimental Results

4.2.1 Performances on the CIFAR-10 Dataset

Our study conducts experiments on the natural CIFAR-10 dataset to verify the efficiency and effectiveness. As the main results in Table 2 show, the converged cost (CC) is the communication cost when the algorithm reaches convergence. The target cost (TC) is the communication cost when the algorithm reaches an accuracy of 0.85. We divide algorithms into two groups based on whether they belong to the PEFT methods. Among all methods, FedBit and SA-FedLora are included in the PEFT. Our proposed SA-FedLora is a more efficient FL framework with lower communication costs compared to all algorithms except FedFFT. For instance, SA-FedLora is greater than FedAvg with improvement of accuracy by 6.35% and decline of the CC by 92.91% and TC by 93.62%. We can observe from the Figure 3 that FedBit even can not achieve the target accuracy due to the few trainable bias terms, although it’s initialized by the pre-trained model and saves the communication costs. In addition, the Local method performs worst due to the scarcity of available data. FedPer reduces the communication overhead due to its preservation of personalized layers. FedFFT is the upper bound among all algorithms since it fine-tunes the full model parameters based on the pre-trained weights and biases.

4.2.2 Performances on the Face Dataset

To further demonstrate the efficiency of SA-FedLora in more complex scenarios, we compare the performance of different algorithms on a healthcare Face dataset with the multi-label disease task, where AUC is a more common indicator after the training convergence to diagnose diseases in the medical field. As the Table 3 illustrated, SA-FedLora reduces the CC by 91.27% and improves the AUC of FedAvg by 8.26%, achieving the comparable performance in ACC simultaneously. Results in Figure 4 reveal the optimal AUC and ACC of the FedFFT among all methods. FedPer outperforms the Fedavg resulting from the mitigation of heterogeneity in FL.

4.2.3 Ablation Study

In this subsection, we conduct an ablation study to discuss the impact of the initiating stage and annealing stage. The M1 mode is the standard FedLora with a fixed parameter budget, and the M4 mode is our SA-FedLora with additional parameter regularization and annealing parameter budget. As illustrated in Table 4, SA-FedLora outperforms the FedLora with the imoprovement of ACC by 4.62% for the CIFAR-10 dataset and that of AUC by 5.14% for the Face dataset. The initiating stage of M3 and M4 improves the performance due to the parameter regularization mitigating the client drift, but increases the communication cost simultaneously owing to the shared all model parameters. Note that the rising cost of the initiating stage is acceptable since it is negligible compared to the total cost of traditional FedAvg. The annealing stage of M2 and M4 also has a positive effect on the performance due to the dynamical search for global optima, but puts more communication overhead owing to more parameter budget in the early heating phase.

4.2.4 Scheduler Analysis

We analyze the performance of three schedulers on the CIFAR-10 dataset. Different schedulers own different rank durations, thus influencing the parameter budget allocation. For example, we can see from Figure 2 that the cubic scheduler between round Tasubscript𝑇𝑎T_{a} and Tbsubscript𝑇𝑏T_{b} is a convex function and the cosine scheduler is a concave function. Therefore, the cubic scheduler drops rapidly and takes more time on the lower rank. Results in Table 5 indicate that the cubic scheduler improves the ACC of the worst cosine scheduler by 1.05% and reduces the converged cost (CC) by 0.17GB, which demonstrates that decreasing to the low rank earlier is more beneficial for the parameter allocation of the SA-FedLora.

5 Conclusion

This paper proposed a more efficient FL approach, called Simulated Annealing-based Federated Learning with LoRA tuning (SA-FedLora) to reduce communication costs with comparable performance. The SA-FedLora consists of two stages: the initiating stage and annealing stage. (1) The initiating stage introduces parameter regularization into the supervised learning and updates all pre-trained model weights; (2) The annealing stage incorporates the Lora method with the FL framework and dynamically allocates the parameter budget by adaptively adjusting the rank of Lora. Experimental results have demonstrated that SA-FedLora achieved superior performance and mitigated the communication burden compared to baseline algorithms. Although this work has shown its efficiency and effectiveness on the classical CIFAR-10 dataset and a real medical dataset, more complicated scenarios and tasks have not yet been discussed thoroughly in this work (e.g., the heterogeneous FL). For future work, we expect to take advantage of the current scheme and design a more generic framework for the FL system.
