Enhancing EEG Signal-Based Emotion Recognition with Synthetic Data: Diffusion Model Approach

Abstract

Emotions are crucial in human life, influencing perceptions, relationships, behaviour, and choices. Emotion recognition using Electroencephalography (EEG) in the Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) domain presents significant challenges, particularly the need for extensive datasets. This study aims to generate synthetic EEG samples that are similar to real samples but are distinct by augmenting noise to a conditional denoising diffusion probabilistic model, thus addressing the prevalent issue of data scarcity in EEG research. The proposed method is tested on the DEAP dataset, showcasing a 1.94% improvement in classification performance when using synthetic data. This is higher compared to the traditional GAN-based and DDPM-based approaches. The proposed diffusion-based approach for EEG data generation appears promising in refining the accuracy of emotion recognition systems and marks a notable contribution to EEG-based emotion recognition. Our research further evaluates the effectiveness of state-of-the-art classifiers on EEG data, employing both real and synthetic data with varying noise levels.

1 Introduction

Emotions are essential in human life, influencing perceptions, relationships, behaviour, and choices. They impact physical and mental health, mood, energy levels, and overall well-being, aiding in understanding others and achieving success. Understanding emotions is crucial not only in interpersonal relations but also in achieving personal and professional success. In this context, Electroencephalography (EEG), a non-invasive, cost-effective, and user-friendly technology, is used in Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) for real-time response and sensitivity to affective state changesÂ (1, 2). It is useful in human-computer interaction, emotion recognitionÂ (3), and medical diagnosisÂ (4). Despite its high temporal resolution and ease of capture from the scalp or intracranial, EEG classification remains a complex task due to its inherent characteristics. Researchers have studied feature extraction and classification in various fieldsÂ (5), yet the practical implementation of EEG-BCI technologies faces challenges such as suboptimal data quality, difficulty in collecting long recordings, low spatial resolution, and the need for large amounts of data to train accurate deep learning (DL) modelsÂ (6).

DL is gaining popularity in EEG signal processing due to its ability to automatically extract features from minimally preprocessed dataÂ (7), unlike traditional algorithms that require manual feature engineering. However, DLâ€™s success in BCI depends on the quality and quantity of training data. Large datasets are essential for learning patterns, relationships, predictions, generalizability, and model robustness. However, finding enough publicly available data for a functional DL algorithm is challenging due to the time and resource-intensive process of recording EEG datasets. EEG signal acquisition is also susceptible to body movements and external noiseÂ (8), so finding alternative ways to obtain EEG data is beneficial. GANs and DDPMs are popular deep generative models for image synthesis, with GANs gaining attention for quality output and DDPMs for training stability and results. However, GANs face challenges such as vanishing gradients and mode collapse. In contrast, diffusion models, with their non-special losses and specialized training requirements, have demonstrated remarkable capabilities, rivalling GANs in image and audio processing tasksÂ (9, 10).

While EEG emotion recognition has improved over time, DL techniques require large amounts of data. Data availability, especially in medical fields, is limited, expensive, and inconsistent due to the inaccessibility of existing data and time-consuming collection processesÂ (11). Balanced and voluminous data is essential for effectively training DL models and achieving desired goals. Synthetic EEG data generation is a potential solution to overcome limitations in real-world EEG data collection. However, it faces challenges such as the need for meaningful evaluation metrics, considering inherent characteristics of EEG, and generating raw EEG data, despite various methods for generating extracted features or signals transformed into image domainÂ (12). Despite these challenges, neural-based generative models generate synthetic EEG dataÂ (13). Synthetic data, while being similar to the real data and sharing its properties, should not be the same as the real data. This creates a contradiction and presents a challenge in generating and validating synthetic data.

Diffusion models outperformed other EEG data augmentation techniques like noise addition, Fourier transform surrogates, and frequency shift for motor imagery classificationÂ (14). This work aims to generate synthetic EEG signals with the following contributions:

  • â€¢

    This work utilizes the Conditional Denoising Diffusion Model to generate raw synthetic EEG data, thereby serving as a viable substitute for real datasets.

  • â€¢

    The Diffusion model employed in this study is trained with noise augmentation sourced from a standard normal distribution. It results in synthetic samples that are not mere replicas but meaningful variations of real EEG data.

  • â€¢

    This work enhances the classification accuracy of real EEG data by integrating synthetic data during the training phase. This enhancement is proportional to the increased volume of synthetic samples used.

  • â€¢

    The efficacy of this model is thoroughly evaluated on the DEAP dataset, with both raw and synthetic EEG data, establishing its applicability and effectiveness in EEG data classification.

2 Related Work

This section reviews the advancements in EEG-based emotion recognition and synthetic data generation, focusing on the significant contributions of the machine and deep learning. It comprises two subsections: analysis of recent EEG studies for emotion recognition and improving them with synthetic data generation. This aims to summarize the fieldâ€™s current status and suggest innovative directions for future research.

2.1 Emotion Recognition

EEG-based emotion recognition presents significant challenges, including temporal asymmetry, signal instability, and inter-individual and regional brain variabilityÂ (15). Nonetheless, advancements in DL have notably enhanced recognition techniques. Integrating Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models has been pivotal in improving EEG signal analysisÂ (16, 17). LSTMs effectively capture temporal dynamics and extract key features relevant to emotion recognitionÂ (18). Notably, one method involves augmenting LSTM with a multi-view dynamic emotion graph, which considers electrode channel relationships and maintains temporal detailsÂ (19).

Emotion recognition from EEG also depends on the selection of discriminative features. For instance, a study highlighted the extraction of narrowband rhythmic components from multichannel EEG, followed by the computation of short-time entropy and energy features for each component. This process was complemented by spatial filtering, which were then employed in emotion recognition using Support Vector Machine (SVM)Â (20). Another research utilized Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction, followed by SVM classification, showcasing SVMâ€™s effectiveness in EEG-based emotion recognitionÂ (21).

In EEG analysis, attention-based methods enhance neural network performance by focusing on specific input segments, an approach widely used in psychology, neuroscience, and machine learningÂ (22, 23). One approach uses a multi-scale feature fusion network (AM-MSFFN) that considers high-level features at different scalesÂ (24). Another uses a pre-trained convolution capsule network with an attention mechanism to recognize emotionsÂ (25). The four-dimensional attention-based neural network (4D-aNN)Â (26) and the Attention-based Multiple Dimensions EEG Transformer (AMDET)Â (27) transform EEG data into multidimensional representations and leverage global attention for emotion recognition. These studies have shown the effectiveness of the attention mechanism in improving emotion recognition and generalizability in EEG.

Emotion recognition using EEG can benefit from combining DL algorithms such as CNN and RNN. One approach is the CNN-RNN model that utilizes multiple CNN features for dimensional emotion recognitionÂ (28). Another model uses a CNNâ€™s univariate and multivariate convolution layers to recognize multichannel EEG signalsÂ (29). The bi-hemispheric discrepancy model (BiHDM)Â (30) employs four RNNs to extract deep EEG signals, achieving domain-invariant feature representation. Further, Shen et al. developed a 4D convolutional recurrent neural network (CRNN), by transforming differential entropy features into 4D structures and combining CNN and LSTM units for enhancing emotion recognitionÂ (31). EEGNetÂ (32) and TSceptionÂ (33) are both DL frameworks proposed for EEG emotion recognition. TSception, a multi-scale CNN, captures temporal dynamics and spatial asymmetry in affective EEG, with dynamic temporal, asymmetric spatial, and high-level fusion layers for simultaneous learning in time and channel dimensions. Conversely, EEGNet utilizes temporal and spatial convolutional layers to learn similar discriminative representations. Both have shown superior classification accuracy compared to other methods.

EEG emotion recognition can also be improved by incorporating graph-based methodsÂ (34). Graph neural networks (GNNs) are particularly effective in capturing EEG signalsâ€™ spatial and temporal featuresÂ (35). For instance, Li et al. developed a fusion graph convolutional network (FGCN) that extracts diverse relationships between EEG channels for a more detailed representationÂ (36). GNNs have been observed to discern global and local EEG channel relationships, thereby improving classification accuracyÂ (37). The application of GCNNs, leveraging functional connectivity among EEG features, has demonstrated superior emotion recognition capabilities compared to traditional techniquesÂ (38). Moreover, integrating GCNs with other models, like 1D CNNs, enhances depth in feature classificationÂ (39). These advancements highlight the potential of graph-based approaches in EEG emotion recognition, providing a deeper understanding of brain dynamics and boosting classification efficacy.

2.2 Synthetic EEG Data

In EEG emotion recognition, where diverse algorithms are utilized, DLâ€™s need for extensive datasets is a significant challenge. Synthetic data generation addresses this issue and improves sample size. Notably, one study developed a framework for generating synthetic tabular data from cognitive psychology experiments using DLÂ (40), while another demonstrated the creation of realistic biosignals, preserving domain-relevant featuresÂ (41). Hernandez et al.Â (42) proposed using a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network and statistical stages to generate synthetic biomedical signals, including EEG. This process involves noise removal, segmentation, BiRNN application, and statistical analysis to produce similar, yet unique, signals. Another study demonstrates that multiple GPT-2 models can generate synthetic biological signals (EMG and EEG), enhancing real data classificationÂ (43).

A Deep Convolutional Generative Adversarial Network (DCGAN) was also proposed for synthetic EEG data generation in epileptic seizure predictionÂ (44). It uses real EEG data and a patient-specific DCGAN, validated using SVM and Convolutional Epileptic Seizure Predictor (CESP). The CESP effectively associates features and labels, outperforming chance-level predictions. Ari et al.Â (11) propose a hybrid approach using Wavelet CNNs weighted layers and Multi-SVM to recognize emotions and increase dataset. The Extreme Learning Machine Wavelet Auto Encoder (ELM-W-AE) is used for data augmentation and ResNet18 for classification.

Several studies have explored the utility of GANs for EEG data augmentation, demonstrating their effectiveness in improving model performance and addressing the challenge of limited EEG dataÂ (45). Therefore, this work uses the diffusion model, which has proven its superiority over GANs in image and audio-related tasks for generating synthetic EEG data.

3 Methodology

This section describes the methodology of this study, which involves training a diffusion model using real EEG data to generate synthetic data. Both real and synthetic EEG data are then used to train the classifier. The classifier is tested only on the test set of real data. The diffusion process is shown in fig.Â 1. The methodology is divided into two main parts: the diffusion model and classifiers. The first part comprises five subsections; the first two discuss the conventional diffusion process and optimization of the denoising model, and the third subsection details iterative refinement, following Saharia et al.â€™s approachÂ (46). The fourth subsection describes the adapted model architecture and noise scheduling. The last one introduces the augmentation module developed for this study.

4.1 Experimental Data

DEAPÂ (55) is a multi-modal dataset for analyzing human affective states, widely used in research for emotion recognition, affective computing, and brain-computer interface (BCI) applications. It features EEG and peripheral physiological signals from 32 participants, recorded while watching 40 one-minute music videos. These videos were rated on a one to nine scale, which were binarized using a threshold of five, dividing emotional responses into â€™highâ€™ and â€™lowâ€™ affective states. Originally collected at a 512 Hz sampling rate, the preprocessed dataset provides downsampled EEG signals at 128 Hz. The study utilizes 32 EEG channels, with EOG artefacts removed and a band-pass frequency filter applied. Data was referenced to a common average and reordered according to the Geneva convention. Segmentation into 60-second trials was done post the removal of a 3-second pre-trial baseline, followed by epoching into one-second intervals, resulting in data shaped as (1, channel count, EEG length), i.e., (1, 32, 128), yielding a total of 76800 samples. The dataset is split into 70:15:15 ratios for train, validation, and test sets.

5 Conclusion

This study addresses the scarcity of high-quality, accessible raw EEG data by proposing a conditional denoising diffusion model to create synthetic EEG samples by augmenting noise to real data to create similar but unique samples. Using synthetic data enhances classifier training when mixed with real data. Using SVM, EEGNet, and TSception for emotion classification, the study finds that EEGNet and TSception outperform SVM, with up to a 1.94% accuracy increase when incorporating synthetic data. Synthetic data, containing unique information, can be used to advance EEG applications and shared without privacy issues. This study confirms that augmenting training with more synthetic data improves classification performance while random noise reduces it. The potential of the proposed method in generating synthetic EEG data using a conditional denoising diffusion model is promising, indicating a future direction to refine these techniques for emotion recognition and synthetic data generation.
