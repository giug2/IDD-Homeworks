Enhancing EEG Signal-Based Emotion Recognition with Synthetic Data: Diffusion Model Approach

Gourav Siddhad Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Indian Institute of Technology, Roorkee, Uttarakhand, 247667, India 0000-0001-5883-3863 Masakazu Iwamura Department of Core Informatics, Graduate School of Informatics, Osaka Metropolitan University, Sakai, Osaka, 599-8531, Japan 0000-0003-2508-2869 Partha Pratim Roy Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Indian Institute of Technology, Roorkee, Uttarakhand, 247667, India 0000-0002-5735-5254
Abstract

Emotions are crucial in human life, influencing perceptions, relationships, behaviour, and choices. Emotion recognition using Electroencephalography (EEG) in the Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) domain presents significant challenges, particularly the need for extensive datasets. This study aims to generate synthetic EEG samples that are similar to real samples but are distinct by augmenting noise to a conditional denoising diffusion probabilistic model, thus addressing the prevalent issue of data scarcity in EEG research. The proposed method is tested on the DEAP dataset, showcasing a 1.94% improvement in classification performance when using synthetic data. This is higher compared to the traditional GAN-based and DDPM-based approaches. The proposed diffusion-based approach for EEG data generation appears promising in refining the accuracy of emotion recognition systems and marks a notable contribution to EEG-based emotion recognition. Our research further evaluates the effectiveness of state-of-the-art classifiers on EEG data, employing both real and synthetic data with varying noise levels.

1 Introduction

Emotions are essential in human life, influencing perceptions, relationships, behaviour, and choices. They impact physical and mental health, mood, energy levels, and overall well-being, aiding in understanding others and achieving success. Understanding emotions is crucial not only in interpersonal relations but also in achieving personal and professional success. In this context, Electroencephalography (EEG), a non-invasive, cost-effective, and user-friendly technology, is used in Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) for real-time response and sensitivity to affective state changes (1, 2). It is useful in human-computer interaction, emotion recognition (3), and medical diagnosis (4). Despite its high temporal resolution and ease of capture from the scalp or intracranial, EEG classification remains a complex task due to its inherent characteristics. Researchers have studied feature extraction and classification in various fields (5), yet the practical implementation of EEG-BCI technologies faces challenges such as suboptimal data quality, difficulty in collecting long recordings, low spatial resolution, and the need for large amounts of data to train accurate deep learning (DL) models (6).

DL is gaining popularity in EEG signal processing due to its ability to automatically extract features from minimally preprocessed data (7), unlike traditional algorithms that require manual feature engineering. However, DL’s success in BCI depends on the quality and quantity of training data. Large datasets are essential for learning patterns, relationships, predictions, generalizability, and model robustness. However, finding enough publicly available data for a functional DL algorithm is challenging due to the time and resource-intensive process of recording EEG datasets. EEG signal acquisition is also susceptible to body movements and external noise (8), so finding alternative ways to obtain EEG data is beneficial. GANs and DDPMs are popular deep generative models for image synthesis, with GANs gaining attention for quality output and DDPMs for training stability and results. However, GANs face challenges such as vanishing gradients and mode collapse. In contrast, diffusion models, with their non-special losses and specialized training requirements, have demonstrated remarkable capabilities, rivalling GANs in image and audio processing tasks (9, 10).

While EEG emotion recognition has improved over time, DL techniques require large amounts of data. Data availability, especially in medical fields, is limited, expensive, and inconsistent due to the inaccessibility of existing data and time-consuming collection processes (11). Balanced and voluminous data is essential for effectively training DL models and achieving desired goals. Synthetic EEG data generation is a potential solution to overcome limitations in real-world EEG data collection. However, it faces challenges such as the need for meaningful evaluation metrics, considering inherent characteristics of EEG, and generating raw EEG data, despite various methods for generating extracted features or signals transformed into image domain (12). Despite these challenges, neural-based generative models generate synthetic EEG data (13). Synthetic data, while being similar to the real data and sharing its properties, should not be the same as the real data. This creates a contradiction and presents a challenge in generating and validating synthetic data.

Diffusion models outperformed other EEG data augmentation techniques like noise addition, Fourier transform surrogates, and frequency shift for motor imagery classification (14). This work aims to generate synthetic EEG signals with the following contributions:

  • •

    This work utilizes the Conditional Denoising Diffusion Model to generate raw synthetic EEG data, thereby serving as a viable substitute for real datasets.

  • •

    The Diffusion model employed in this study is trained with noise augmentation sourced from a standard normal distribution. It results in synthetic samples that are not mere replicas but meaningful variations of real EEG data.

  • •

    This work enhances the classification accuracy of real EEG data by integrating synthetic data during the training phase. This enhancement is proportional to the increased volume of synthetic samples used.

  • •

    The efficacy of this model is thoroughly evaluated on the DEAP dataset, with both raw and synthetic EEG data, establishing its applicability and effectiveness in EEG data classification.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the latest articles that have classified emotions and generated synthetic EEG data. Section 3 describes the methodology used for the whole experiment, including the dataset, diffusion model, and classifiers. Section 4 reports the results, and the work is concluded in Section 5.

2 Related Work

This section reviews the advancements in EEG-based emotion recognition and synthetic data generation, focusing on the significant contributions of the machine and deep learning. It comprises two subsections: analysis of recent EEG studies for emotion recognition and improving them with synthetic data generation. This aims to summarize the field’s current status and suggest innovative directions for future research.

2.1 Emotion Recognition

EEG-based emotion recognition presents significant challenges, including temporal asymmetry, signal instability, and inter-individual and regional brain variability (15). Nonetheless, advancements in DL have notably enhanced recognition techniques. Integrating Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models has been pivotal in improving EEG signal analysis (16, 17). LSTMs effectively capture temporal dynamics and extract key features relevant to emotion recognition (18). Notably, one method involves augmenting LSTM with a multi-view dynamic emotion graph, which considers electrode channel relationships and maintains temporal details (19).

Emotion recognition from EEG also depends on the selection of discriminative features. For instance, a study highlighted the extraction of narrowband rhythmic components from multichannel EEG, followed by the computation of short-time entropy and energy features for each component. This process was complemented by spatial filtering, which were then employed in emotion recognition using Support Vector Machine (SVM) (20). Another research utilized Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction, followed by SVM classification, showcasing SVM’s effectiveness in EEG-based emotion recognition (21).

In EEG analysis, attention-based methods enhance neural network performance by focusing on specific input segments, an approach widely used in psychology, neuroscience, and machine learning (22, 23). One approach uses a multi-scale feature fusion network (AM-MSFFN) that considers high-level features at different scales (24). Another uses a pre-trained convolution capsule network with an attention mechanism to recognize emotions (25). The four-dimensional attention-based neural network (4D-aNN) (26) and the Attention-based Multiple Dimensions EEG Transformer (AMDET) (27) transform EEG data into multidimensional representations and leverage global attention for emotion recognition. These studies have shown the effectiveness of the attention mechanism in improving emotion recognition and generalizability in EEG.

Emotion recognition using EEG can benefit from combining DL algorithms such as CNN and RNN. One approach is the CNN-RNN model that utilizes multiple CNN features for dimensional emotion recognition (28). Another model uses a CNN’s univariate and multivariate convolution layers to recognize multichannel EEG signals (29). The bi-hemispheric discrepancy model (BiHDM) (30) employs four RNNs to extract deep EEG signals, achieving domain-invariant feature representation. Further, Shen et al. developed a 4D convolutional recurrent neural network (CRNN), by transforming differential entropy features into 4D structures and combining CNN and LSTM units for enhancing emotion recognition (31). EEGNet (32) and TSception (33) are both DL frameworks proposed for EEG emotion recognition. TSception, a multi-scale CNN, captures temporal dynamics and spatial asymmetry in affective EEG, with dynamic temporal, asymmetric spatial, and high-level fusion layers for simultaneous learning in time and channel dimensions. Conversely, EEGNet utilizes temporal and spatial convolutional layers to learn similar discriminative representations. Both have shown superior classification accuracy compared to other methods.

EEG emotion recognition can also be improved by incorporating graph-based methods (34). Graph neural networks (GNNs) are particularly effective in capturing EEG signals’ spatial and temporal features (35). For instance, Li et al. developed a fusion graph convolutional network (FGCN) that extracts diverse relationships between EEG channels for a more detailed representation (36). GNNs have been observed to discern global and local EEG channel relationships, thereby improving classification accuracy (37). The application of GCNNs, leveraging functional connectivity among EEG features, has demonstrated superior emotion recognition capabilities compared to traditional techniques (38). Moreover, integrating GCNs with other models, like 1D CNNs, enhances depth in feature classification (39). These advancements highlight the potential of graph-based approaches in EEG emotion recognition, providing a deeper understanding of brain dynamics and boosting classification efficacy.

2.2 Synthetic EEG Data

In EEG emotion recognition, where diverse algorithms are utilized, DL’s need for extensive datasets is a significant challenge. Synthetic data generation addresses this issue and improves sample size. Notably, one study developed a framework for generating synthetic tabular data from cognitive psychology experiments using DL (40), while another demonstrated the creation of realistic biosignals, preserving domain-relevant features (41). Hernandez et al. (42) proposed using a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network and statistical stages to generate synthetic biomedical signals, including EEG. This process involves noise removal, segmentation, BiRNN application, and statistical analysis to produce similar, yet unique, signals. Another study demonstrates that multiple GPT-2 models can generate synthetic biological signals (EMG and EEG), enhancing real data classification (43).

A Deep Convolutional Generative Adversarial Network (DCGAN) was also proposed for synthetic EEG data generation in epileptic seizure prediction (44). It uses real EEG data and a patient-specific DCGAN, validated using SVM and Convolutional Epileptic Seizure Predictor (CESP). The CESP effectively associates features and labels, outperforming chance-level predictions. Ari et al. (11) propose a hybrid approach using Wavelet CNNs weighted layers and Multi-SVM to recognize emotions and increase dataset. The Extreme Learning Machine Wavelet Auto Encoder (ELM-W-AE) is used for data augmentation and ResNet18 for classification.

Several studies have explored the utility of GANs for EEG data augmentation, demonstrating their effectiveness in improving model performance and addressing the challenge of limited EEG data (45). Therefore, this work uses the diffusion model, which has proven its superiority over GANs in image and audio-related tasks for generating synthetic EEG data.

3 Methodology

This section describes the methodology of this study, which involves training a diffusion model using real EEG data to generate synthetic data. Both real and synthetic EEG data are then used to train the classifier. The classifier is tested only on the test set of real data. The diffusion process is shown in fig. 1. The methodology is divided into two main parts: the diffusion model and classifiers. The first part comprises five subsections; the first two discuss the conventional diffusion process and optimization of the denoising model, and the third subsection details iterative refinement, following Saharia et al.’s approach (46). The fourth subsection describes the adapted model architecture and noise scheduling. The last one introduces the augmentation module developed for this study.

3.1 Conditional Denoising Diffusion Model

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs), introduced in 2015 (47) and popularized by Ho et al. in 2020 (48), show great promise in image generation. They utilize forward and reverse diffusion processes; the former generates a noisy image sequence from a clean image, and the latter denoises this sequence to retrieve the clean image. This study adopts a conditional denoising diffusion model, a modification of Saharia et al.’s model (46) for image super-resolution, to generate conditional and synthetic signals. Using a U-Net architecture (49) with a denoising objective, it transforms a normal distribution into empirical data distribution through refinement steps resembling Langevin dynamics.

3.1.4 Model Architecture and Noise Schedulers

The model adopts a U-Net architecture similar to the one in DDPM (48), integrating self-attention and modifications from (52). Specifically, it replaces DDPM’s original residual blocks with those from BigGAN and skip connections re-scaled by 1212\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}. The number of residual blocks and channel multipliers at various resolutions are increased to enhance the model. The model is conditioned on input 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x} by introducing noise to the source sample and concatenated with 𝒚tsubscript𝒚𝑡\boldsymbol{y}_{t} along the channel dimension. The training noise schedule follows (51), employing a piece-wise distribution for γ𝛾\gamma, p​(γ)=∑t=1T1T​U​(γt−1,γt)𝑝𝛾superscriptsubscript𝑡1𝑇1𝑇𝑈subscript𝛾𝑡1subscript𝛾𝑡p(\gamma)=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\frac{1}{T}U\left(\gamma_{t-1},\gamma_{t}\right). This study adopts a technique from (51), which efficiently generates samples by directly conditioning on γ𝛾\gamma rather than on t𝑡t as in (48), allowing flexibility in choosing diffusion steps and noise schedules. Assuming a linear noise schedule, the maximum diffusion steps is set to 100. An inexpensive hyper-parameter search over start and end noise levels was performed, avoiding the need for model retraining (51). The diffusion model uses a denoising U-Net (fig. 2) to process noisy target and source conditioning samples.

3.1.5 Augmentation Module

The diffusion process involves two samples: a source sample 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x} and a target sample 𝒚𝒚\boldsymbol{y}. To modify the source sample, an augmentation module is employed that adds noise from a standard normal distribution Z𝑍Z, thus creating a condition sample as shown in fig. 1. This is formalized by:

𝒙Δ=𝒙+Δ×Z,subscript𝒙Δ𝒙Δ𝑍\boldsymbol{x}_{\Delta}=\boldsymbol{x}+\Delta\times Z, (15)

where, ΔΔ\Delta exhibits variability. These variations play a crucial role in the diffusion model as they lead to the generation of samples affected by noise. While the model relies on real samples as a basis for learning, it adapts to produce new samples with subtle differences.

3.2 Classifiers

Several classifiers are available in the literature for EEG-based emotion classification, the most popular utilized in this work. TSception (33) serves as a primary classifier, but for a balanced evaluation of the quality of the generated data, SVM (53) and EEGNet (32) are also employed.

  • •

    SVM: These are supervised learning models used for classification and regression, known for categorizing new examples by maximizing the class margin. They are capable of non-linear classification through the kernel trick, mapping inputs into high-dimensional spaces.

  • •

    EEGNet: A CNN-based approach that achieves comparable accuracy using deep and separable convolutions. Its architecture incorporates temporal convolution for frequency filter learning, depth-wise convolution for frequency-specific spatial filters, and separable convolution for effective feature map combinations.

  • •

    TSception: It includes a dynamic temporal layer for learning temporal and frequency representations from EEG channels, an asymmetric spatial layer for global spatial and emotional asymmetry patterns, a high-level fusion layer, and a classifier, utilizing varied convolutional kernel scales for spatial analysis.

4 Results and Discussion

The efficacy of this study is evaluated using real EEG data and synthetic data generated with varying noise levels (ΔΔ\Delta). Evaluation is done on SVM, EEGNet, and TSception classifiers, encompassing the following key experimental aspects.

  • •

    Training classifiers on purely real data, followed by a combination of real and synthetic data.

  • •

    Investigating accuracy changes relative to the number of synthetic EEG samples included in the training.

  • •

    Assessing classification accuracy variations with synthetic data generated with different noise levels (ΔΔ\Delta) in the diffusion model.

  • •

    Comparing performance outcomes when using synthetic data generated by vanilla diffusion and GAN.

All classifiers undergo testing on a dedicated real data test set.

4.1 Experimental Data

DEAP (55) is a multi-modal dataset for analyzing human affective states, widely used in research for emotion recognition, affective computing, and brain-computer interface (BCI) applications. It features EEG and peripheral physiological signals from 32 participants, recorded while watching 40 one-minute music videos. These videos were rated on a one to nine scale, which were binarized using a threshold of five, dividing emotional responses into ’high’ and ’low’ affective states. Originally collected at a 512 Hz sampling rate, the preprocessed dataset provides downsampled EEG signals at 128 Hz. The study utilizes 32 EEG channels, with EOG artefacts removed and a band-pass frequency filter applied. Data was referenced to a common average and reordered according to the Geneva convention. Segmentation into 60-second trials was done post the removal of a 3-second pre-trial baseline, followed by epoching into one-second intervals, resulting in data shaped as (1, channel count, EEG length), i.e., (1, 32, 128), yielding a total of 76800 samples. The dataset is split into 70:15:15 ratios for train, validation, and test sets.

4.2 Implementation Details

The experimental setup involved a DELL Precision 7820 Tower Workstation, with Ubuntu 22.04 OS, Intel Core(TM) Xeon Silver 4216 CPU, and an NVIDIA RTX A2000 12GB GPU. This hardware facilitated the implementation of DL models using Python 3.10 and the PyTorch library. The Adam optimizer, known for its computational efficiency, was used with default parameters (η𝜂\eta = 0.001, β1subscript𝛽1\beta_{1} = 0.9, β2subscript𝛽2\beta_{2} = 0.999), and a linear warmup was applied over 10,000 training steps. EEGNet and TSception were trained for 100 epochs, with batches of 16 and a learning rate of 1​e−41𝑒41e-4. For SVM, the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel from scikit-learn (56) was used with default settings. Classification accuracy was determined through stratified five-fold cross-validation, averaging the results for comprehensive assessment.

For diffusion experiments, T𝑇T = 500 was maintained and the γtsubscript𝛾𝑡\gamma_{t} are uniformly spaced. While larger T𝑇T values can improve model performance, using T𝑇T = 2000 as in (46) results in noiseless samples, contrary to this study’s goals. Additionally, this setting also accelerates sample generation (or inference) compared to earlier models (48, 52), which required between 1000 and 2000 diffusion steps. Additionally, the diffusion model training involved batches of 32 and a 0.2 dropout rate for 1 million steps, utilizing the latest checkpoint.

4.3 Effect of Synthetic Data in Classification Performance

Synthetic samples from the diffusion model are created by introducing slight variations to real samples through augmented noise (ΔΔ\Delta), aiming to resemble the original data closely but not identical. Synthetic samples created through diffusion, correspond one-to-one with real samples, ensuring equal quantities of both. The classifier was trained on a mix of real and synthetic data and then tested on real data alone. This approach was compared to training exclusively with real data, under the hypothesis that if the synthetic data offered no additional information, the performances would be identical. table 1 presents a comprehensive comparison of classifier performance (SVM, EEGNet, and TSception) on the DEAP dataset within a 95% confidence interval, adding robustness to the findings, under various data augmentation conditions (ΔΔ\Delta = 0.01, 0.05, 0.1). The ’Real’ column shows the baseline accuracy of each classifier when trained and tested on real data only. This serves as a reference point for evaluating the impact of synthetic data. The columns under ‘Vanilla Diffusion,’ ‘Proposed Method,’ and ‘GAN’ show the accuracy and gain (or loss) when classifiers are trained on a combination of real and synthetic data. The gain values indicate the improvement or decline in performance compared to the baseline. They refer to synthetic data generated via vanilla diffusion model, our approach, and GAN, respectively. All classifiers were tested only on real dataset.

SVM shows modest improvements in accuracy with synthetic data. The highest gain is observed at ΔΔ\Delta = 0.05 for Arousal and ΔΔ\Delta = 0.01 for Dominance, Liking, and Valence. Generally, EEGNet benefits more from including synthetic data, particularly at lower noise levels (ΔΔ\Delta = 0.01 and 0.05). However, the performance drops when trained with GAN-generated data. TSception shows significant improvements in accuracy with synthetic data, especially at ΔΔ\Delta = 0.01 across all affective states. Like EEGNet, its performance decreases with GAN-generated data. When trained with ΔΔ\Delta = 0, implying no noise addition, the generated synthetic data is identical to real data, and using it for training is the same as using real data twice, which did not alter classifier accuracy.

Training with synthetic data alongside real data enhances classifier performance on real data tests. During training, the combination of real and synthetic data (ΔΔ\Delta = 0.01) yields the highest accuracy improvements. Lower noise (ΔΔ\Delta) levels consistently result in more significant accuracy gains. All classifiers show a decrease in performance when trained with GAN-generated data compared to the proposed method, indicating the efficacy of the proposed diffusion approach in generating synthetic data to boost classifier training. Overall, synthetic data from the proposed diffusion method improves EEG-based emotion recognition accuracy, particularly at lower noise levels. The gains vary across classifiers and affective states, with TSception generally showing most improvements.

In another experiment, samples comprising purely Gaussian noise, unrelated to EEG data, were generated without utilizing the diffusion model. table 2 presents the training results on a combination of real EEG samples and these Gaussian noise samples, with testing conducted on real EEG samples. The accuracy for arousal and dominance decreases slightly and the gain is negative, indicating a drop in performance. There is negligible change in the classifier’s performance for liking and valence. Using mere Gaussian noise instead of diffusion-generated samples leads to decreased classification performance due to the lack of relevant information contributing to the classifier’s training. In contrast, when using diffusion-generated data for training, the samples are closely similar to real samples, with minor variations from noise augmentation. The proposed approach enriches the classifier’s feature learning, enhancing its classification performance.

4.4 Effect of the Number of Synthetic Samples Used in Training

The diffusion model-generated data was scrutinized in a study that adjusted the volume of synthetic data during the classifier training. The study opted for data with a noise level ΔΔ\Delta = 0.01, which had previously demonstrated superior performance over other noise variants. As shown in fig. 3, the model’s accuracy correlates with the percentage of synthetic data employed during classifier training, with data proportions ranging from 50% to 100%. The x-axis represents the synthetic data percentage, while the y-axis indicates accuracy, ranging from 65% to 72%. The baseline accuracy (dotted lines) serves as a reference to show the relative improvement gained by including synthetic data during classifier training.

Notably, arousal (blue) exhibits the highest accuracy, followed by dominance (red), liking (green), and valence (yellow). The results highlight a direct relationship between increased synthetic data inclusion and improved classifier performance, with the most significant accuracy observed at an equal mix of synthetic and real data (100% synthetic). Overall, the chart suggests that adding synthetic data generated with a slight noise (ΔΔ\Delta = 0.01) to the classifier’s training improves its ability for emotion recognition from EEG data.

5 Conclusion

This study addresses the scarcity of high-quality, accessible raw EEG data by proposing a conditional denoising diffusion model to create synthetic EEG samples by augmenting noise to real data to create similar but unique samples. Using synthetic data enhances classifier training when mixed with real data. Using SVM, EEGNet, and TSception for emotion classification, the study finds that EEGNet and TSception outperform SVM, with up to a 1.94% accuracy increase when incorporating synthetic data. Synthetic data, containing unique information, can be used to advance EEG applications and shared without privacy issues. This study confirms that augmenting training with more synthetic data improves classification performance while random noise reduces it. The potential of the proposed method in generating synthetic EEG data using a conditional denoising diffusion model is promising, indicating a future direction to refine these techniques for emotion recognition and synthetic data generation.
