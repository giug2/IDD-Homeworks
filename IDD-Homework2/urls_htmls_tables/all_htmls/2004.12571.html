Abstract

As a decentralized model training method, federated learning is designed to integrate the isolated data islands and protect data privacy. Recent studies, however, have demonstrated that the Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) based attacks can be used in federated learning to learn the distribution of the victimâ€™s private dataset and accordingly reconstruct human-distinguishable images. In this paper, we exploit defenses against GAN-based attacks in federated learning, and propose a framework, Anti-GAN, to prevent attackers from learning the real distribution of the victimâ€™s data. The core idea of Anti-GANÂ is to corrupt the visual features of the victimâ€™s private training images, such that the images restored by the attacker are indistinguishable to human eyes. Specifically, in Anti-GAN, the victim first projects the personal dataset onto a GANâ€™s generator, then mixes the fake images generated by the generator with the real images to obtain the training dataset, which will be fed into the federated model for training. We redesign the structure of the victimâ€™s GAN to encourage it to learn the classification features (instead of the visual features) of the real images. We further introduce an unsupervised task to the GAN model for obfuscating the visual features of the generated images. The experiments demonstrate that Anti-GANÂ can effectively prevent the attacker from learning the distribution of the private images, meanwhile causing little harm to the accuracy of the federated model.

1 Introduction

Nowadays, Machine Learning (ML), especially deep learning, is playing a critical role in real-life applications, ranging from face recognition to robotics. Despite the popularity and impressive performance, ML algorithms are vulnerable to various attacks, such as model inversion (Fredrikson etÂ al. (2015)), membership inference (Shokri etÂ al. (2017); Salem etÂ al. (2018)) and model extraction (TramÃ¨r etÂ al. (2016)). To protect the user-level privacy, as well as alleviate the problem of data fragmentation and isolation, McMahan etÂ al. (2016) propose a distributed machine learning framework called federated learning (FL), which is performed among one server and multiple clients. The training process of FL iteratively proceeds in multiple rounds of communication to enable the clients to collaboratively train a shared model. Recent studies, however, have shown that feature inference attacks (Melis etÂ al. (2019); Hitaj etÂ al. (2017); Wang etÂ al. (2019); Luo etÂ al. (2020)) can be effectively applied to federated learning, causing unintended information leakage and model poisoning.

Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) is proposed by Goodfellow etÂ al. (2014) to learn the distribution of training datasets. Given the impressive performance of GAN on data reconstruction, Hitaj etÂ al. (2017) propose a GAN-based attack on federated learning, where an adversarial client locally trains a GAN model to deliberately learn the distribution of the victimâ€™s private dataset based on the shared model gradients, then accordingly reconstruct the private images (e.g., face pictures). Wang etÂ al. (2019) further exploit user-level privacy leakage by training a multi-task GAN model in a malicious central server. Hitaj etÂ al. (2017) claim that it is difficult to defense such attacks as long as the classification accuracy of the victimâ€™s local model is high enough. In this paper, we exploit defenses against GAN-based feature inference attacks in federated learning. For simplicity, we focus on deep neural networks, which is consistent with Hitaj etÂ al. (2017) and Wang etÂ al. (2019).

There are two main challenges to design defenses against GAN-based attacks in federated learning: the first is that the traditional cryptographic methods (e.g., homomorphic encryption and secure multiparty computation) consume considerable computational time and power, thus barely useful for edge devices (e.g., mobiles and wearable devices) in federated learning; the second is that adding noise to the shared gradients (e.g., differential privacy) can harm the convergence and performance of the federated model. Besides, Hitaj etÂ al. (2017) have experimentally proved that differential privacy can hardly prevent the attacker from learning data distribution of the private dataset. To address these challenges, we propose a defense mechanism, Anti-GAN, to protect the victimâ€™s private dataset from the GAN-based inference attacks. The core idea of Anti-GANÂ is to generate the training images via a GAN model, where the visual features of the generated images are obfuscated by a specifically designed loss function, such that the attackerâ€™s GAN model can only learn the distribution of these visually indistinguishable images.

Considering that the distribution of the obfuscated images could be different from that of the real images, this strategy could degrade the accuracy (tested on the real images) of the federated model trained on the obfuscated images. To solve this problem, we redesign the structure of the victimâ€™s GAN model such that the classification features, instead of the visual features, of the real images, will be learned. To further improve the performance of the federated model, we use a modified Mixup scheme (Zhang etÂ al. (2017)) to generate the final training images by mixing the generated fake images with the real images. Specifically, the victim first trains a GAN model based on the private dataset Xğ‘‹X, then accordingly generates the fake images Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime}, such that Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} and Xğ‘‹X have similar classification features while the visual features of Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} are obfuscated. The generated Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} is then mixed with Xğ‘‹X to obtain the training dataset X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X}, and X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} is finally used to train the federated model. The framework of Anti-GANÂ is shown in Fig.Â 1. We conduct extensive experiments on MNIST (Yann etÂ al. (1998)), CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky etÂ al. (2009)) to evaluate the validity of Anti-GAN. The results demonstrate that Anti-GANÂ can effectively reduce the plausibility of the images reconstructed by the attackerâ€™s GAN model, meanwhile causing little harm to the accuracy of the global federated model.

2.3 Attacks and Defenses in Machine Learning

Machine learning algorithms are vulnerable to various attacks, such as membership inference (Shokri etÂ al. (2017); Salem etÂ al. (2018); Nasr etÂ al. (2019)), feature inference (Fredrikson etÂ al. (2015)) and model extraction (TramÃ¨r etÂ al. (2016)). In this paper, we focus on the feature inference (or model inversion) attacks.

Feature inference attacks aim to restore the feature values from the private training datasets. Fredrikson etÂ al. (2015) explore model inversion attacks in decision trees and neural networks based on labels and black-box access to the target model. Luo etÂ al. (2020) propose to infer the training data in federated learning by model predictions. Zhu and Han (2020) and Zhao etÂ al. (2020) restore the private training data by only the model gradients. In federated learning, some newest studies exploit feature inference attacks based on GANs. Hitaj etÂ al. (2017) propose a GAN-based threat model, in which an adversary tries to extract the training information of a class that is not owned by her. By taking the global model as the discriminator of GAN, the adversary can further mislabel the pseudo-samples generated by the generator so as to trick other participants to leak more private information. Wang etÂ al. (2019) assume the server is malicious and utilize GAN to explore user-level privacy leakage, which is similar to Hitaj etÂ al. (2017)â€™s work.

Several defenses have been proposed to alleviate the risks of data leakage, such as InstaHide (Huang etÂ al. (2020)), anomaly detection (Bagdasaryan etÂ al. (2018)), dropout (Melis etÂ al. (2019); Salem etÂ al. (2018)), differential privacy (Shokri and Shmatikov (2015); Bagdasaryan etÂ al. (2018)), secure multiparty computation (Bonawitz etÂ al. (2017)) and model stacking (Salem etÂ al. (2018)), among which InstaHide is most similar to our scheme, i.e., using Mixup (Zhang etÂ al. (2017)) to corrupt the visual features of private training images. The difference is that InstaHide mixes multiple real images into one training image, while we first use a GAN model to generate multiple fake images and then mix fake images with real images. In addition, Carlini etÂ al. (2020) and Luo etÂ al. (2021) have experimentally proved that InstaHide can hardly protect private images, whereas our scheme can provide better privacy protection compared to InstaHide. Besides, few methods have been proposed to defense the attacks in Hitaj etÂ al. (2017) and Wang etÂ al. (2019). To our best knowledge, this is also the first paper to exploit defenses against GAN-based feature inference attacks.

3 Approach

The proposed scheme, Anti-GAN, is used to defense GAN-based feature inference attacks in federated learning (Hitaj etÂ al. (2017); Wang etÂ al. (2019)). In this section, we first introduce the threat model of GAN-based attacks, then detail the framework of Anti-GAN.

3.1 Threat Model

There are generally two types of threat models in GAN-based attacks: one or multiple participants are adversaries, e.g., Hitaj etÂ al. (2017); the central server is the adversary, e.g., Wang etÂ al. (2019). Nevertheless, the basic ideas under these attacks are similar, i.e., the attacker takes the global shared model as a discriminator, then trains a generator to reconstruct the victimâ€™s private training images. For simplicity, we focus on the attack model inÂ Hitaj etÂ al. (2017).

In federated learning, all participants agree on a common global model before training. Suppose the central server is authoritative and can not be compromised by any adversaries. One participant Ağ´A, acting as an adversary, owns data with labels [b,c]ğ‘ğ‘[b,c], and the victim Vğ‘‰V owns data with labels [a,b]ğ‘ğ‘[a,b]. The objective of Ağ´A is to reconstruct the data with label ağ‘a owned by Vğ‘‰V. The attacking process is as follows: first, Vğ‘‰V honestly trains a local model and uploads the model gradients to the central server; second, Ağ´A downloads these shared gradients and accordingly updates the discriminator of his GAN model. Then Ağ´A generates a data point of label ağ‘a from GAN and labels it as class cğ‘c. Ağ´A will train his local model with these pseudo-samples and share the parameters to the global model to encourage Vğ‘‰V providing more information about ağ‘a class. In the end, Ağ´A can reconstruct an image of class ağ‘a which is indistinguishable from Vğ‘‰Vâ€™s original images. Hitaj etÂ al. (2017) claim that it is difficult to defense such attacks as long as the accuracy of the victimâ€™s local model is high enough.

3.2 The framework of Anti-GAN

We design a defense mechanism, Anti-GAN, to eliminate the data leakage risks incurred by GAN-based inference attacks. Note that the victim Vğ‘‰V is also called the defender in this paper since the defense method is performed by Vğ‘‰V. In Anti-GAN, we propose to obfuscate the visual features of training images fed into the federated classifier. The problem is how to incur little accuracy degradation when training the federated classifier on the obfuscated images.

In our scheme, to protect the private dataset Xğ‘‹X, the defender first trains a CGAN model based on Xğ‘‹X, then generates a shadow image set Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} via this CGAN and mixes Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} with Xğ‘‹X to generate the final training images X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} (Fig.Â 1). We design new loss functions for the defenderâ€™s GAN to guarantee that the classification feature maps of Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} are similar to that of Xğ‘‹X, meanwhile the visual features of Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} are hardly distinguishable to human, such that the images restored by the attackâ€™s GAN will demonstrate little plausible visual features.

4 Security Analysis

We now consider the security of Anti-GANÂ in federated learning.

Attack scenario: the defender generates the training dataset X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} by mixing the real dataset Xğ‘‹X and the dataset Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} produced by a CGAN model. In each epoch, the defender first feeds the (x^,y^)^ğ‘¥^ğ‘¦(\hat{x},\hat{y}) pairs into the local federated model fğ‘“f to obtain the model gradients âˆ‡fâˆ‡ğ‘“\nabla f, then uploads a percentage of gradients [âˆ‡f]delimited-[]âˆ‡ğ‘“[\nabla f] to the parameter server (Hitaj etÂ al. (2017)). The attacker downloads [âˆ‡f]delimited-[]âˆ‡ğ‘“[\nabla f] from the server and infers the information of the private dataset Xğ‘‹X from [âˆ‡f]delimited-[]âˆ‡ğ‘“[\nabla f].

The security arguments consist of two halves: (1) to restore the information of Xğ‘‹X, the attacker has to recover the distribution of X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X}; (2) restoring Xğ‘‹X from X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} is difficult.

For the first argument, since the gradients [âˆ‡f]delimited-[]âˆ‡ğ‘“[\nabla f] are computed based on the training images X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X}, an attacker with unlimited capacity can accurately recover the training images X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X}; while a computationally limited attacker can only infer the distribution of X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} via a GAN model. Nevertheless, both of these two attackers can not directly infer the information of Xğ‘‹X from [âˆ‡f]delimited-[]âˆ‡ğ‘“[\nabla f] because X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} is the only input for the computation of [âˆ‡f]delimited-[]âˆ‡ğ‘“[\nabla f]. Note that the X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} is generated by mixing Xğ‘‹X and Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime}. The visual features of Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime} are corrupted by â„’obfsubscriptâ„’obf\mathcal{L}_{\text{obf}} (Eq.Â 4), and the visual features of Xğ‘‹X are also corrupted by mixing with Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime}. The attackers with or without unlimited capacity can not infer the visual features of Xğ‘‹X from X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X}.

For the second argument, suppose the attacker has limited computational power, then this attacker can obtain the distribution of X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X}. As mentioned before, the visual features of X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} are corrupted, thus the images restored by the attackerâ€™s GAN model are indistinguishable to human eyes. Separating the distribution of Xğ‘‹X from X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X} is difficult since the attacker knows nothing about the mixing parameter Î¼ğœ‡\mu and the distribution of Xâ€²superscriptğ‘‹â€²X^{\prime}. Suppose the attacker has unlimited capacity, then this attacker can restore the original images of X^^ğ‘‹\hat{X}. We convert the attackerâ€™s objective to the one that, given x^^ğ‘¥\hat{x}, restore the information of xğ‘¥x. Although the x^^ğ‘¥\hat{x} is generated based on a linear transformation on xğ‘¥x (Eq.Â 7), we mix each real image only once, which means that the xâ€²superscriptğ‘¥â€²x^{\prime} acts as a one-time mask on the real image xğ‘¥x. The attacker can not infer the information of xğ‘¥x from x^^ğ‘¥\hat{x} as long as the mask xâ€²superscriptğ‘¥â€²x^{\prime} is private. In addition, our mix-only-once strategy can defense the state-of-the-art attacks on Mixup (Carlini etÂ al. (2020); Huang etÂ al. (2020); Luo etÂ al. (2021)) because these attacks only consider the scenario that one image is mixed multiple times.

6 Conclusion

Federated learning is vulnerable to GAN-base feature inference attacks, which are difficult to defense as long as the victimâ€™s local model has good performance. In this paper, we propose a defense framework, Anti-GAN, to protect the victimâ€™s private data from GAN-based attacks. Anti-GANÂ can distort the original distribution of the private training data to prevent attackers from generating distinguishable images, meanwhile causing little harm to the performance of the federated classifier.
