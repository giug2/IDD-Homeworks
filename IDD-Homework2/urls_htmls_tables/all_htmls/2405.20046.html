Abstract

Federated learning benefits from cross-training strategies, which enables models to train on data from distinct sources to improve the generalization capability. However, the data heterogeneity between sources may lead models to gradually forget previously acquired knowledge when undergoing cross-training to adapt to new tasks or data sources. We argue that integrating personalized and global knowledge to gather information from multiple perspectives could potentially improve performance. To achieve this goal, this paper presents a novel approach that enhances federated learning through a cross-training scheme incorporating multi-view information. Specifically, the proposed method, termed FedCT, includes three main modules, where the consistency-aware knowledge broadcasting module aims to optimize model assignment strategies, which enhances collaborative advantages between clients and achieves an efficient federated learning process. The multi-view knowledge-guided representation learning module leverages fused prototypical knowledge from both global and local views to enhance the preservation of local knowledge before and after model exchange, as well as to ensure consistency between local and global knowledge. The mixup-based feature augmentation module aggregates rich information to further increase the diversity of feature spaces, which enables the model to better discriminate complex samples. Extensive experiments were conducted on four datasets in terms of performance comparison, ablation study, in-depth analysis and case study. The results demonstrated that FedCT alleviates knowledge forgetting from both local and global views, which enables it outperform state-of-the-art methods.

I Introduction

Federated learning aims to address the challenge of data privacy leakage posed by traditional centralized machine learning methods in scenarios where data is distributed across multiple devices or organizations [1, 2, 3]. It enables collaborative model training across distributed devices or data sources while respecting data privacy and security [4, 5, 6, 7]. Existing studies leverage model-level interactions and aggregation between participants and the server to achieve updates of the global model, which facilitates each participant to benefit from the training knowledge of others and collectively enhance the generalization of the global model [8, 9, 10]. However, recent studies have revealed the fact that federated learning encounters a decline in the performance of the global model when confronted with variations in data distribution among different data sources [11, 12, 13, 14]. This is because the heterogeneous data distribution leads to different optimization goals in local models, resulting in significant differences in the representation distributions within their learned feature spaces, which causes local model to fit only to its specific data source.

To mitigate the issue of data distribution heterogeneity, existing methods typically focus on improving the performance of local models on multi-source datasets, rather than on a single data source. They either integrate cross-training strategies to assist local models in training on multi-source datasets [15, 16, 17], which can expand the knowledge base that local models can learn from, or use external knowledge to guide the training of local models. This adjustment in their optimization objectives enhances the cooperative modeling capabilities among local models [13, 18, 19]. For example, FedExg is recognized as the first cross-training method, which achieves an impressive performance [15]. Moreover, FedMe employs mutual learning techniques to enable client models to acquire knowledge from other data sources [16]. And PGCT utilizes prototypes to guide local models in learning a consistent feature space across different clients, which alleviates the issue of knowledge forgetting in cross-training processes [17]. They typically only consider whether the knowledge learned by a local model within an exchange group is consistent, that is, focusing on the issue of knowledge forgetting from local view. However, the number of clients within an exchange group is minuscule compared to the entire federated learning system, which is insufficient for them to acquire a comprehensive of global knowledge.

To alleviate the aforementioned problem, this paper proposes a novel multi-view knowledge-guided cross training method, termed FedCT, which expands the local knowledge base from the local view and enhances the consistency of knowledge among different clients from the global view. Figure 1 shows the framework. Specifically, FedCT has three main modules: the consistency-aware knowledge broadcasting (CAKB) module, the multi-view knowledge-guided representation learning (MVKGRL) module and the mixup-based feature augmentation (MFA) module. Considering the privacy concerns related to exposing private data, FedCT memorizes a prototypical representation for each class. The CAKB module has developed a local model assignment strategy based on prototypical knowledge consistency, which explores the fitting capability of local models to the prototypes. This can enhance the collaboration benefits between clients. The MVKGRL module enriches the local knowledge base and guides clients in constructing as homogeneous as possible class-level representations in the latent space to alleviate the challenge of knowledge forgetting from multiple views, which facilitates a more comprehensive understanding of global distributions and inter-class relationships. The MFA module focuses on enhancing the decision phase, and it aggregates rich information from both prototypes and features, which increases the diversity of the feature space. This reduces the risk of overfitting and contributes to improving the generalization performance and robustness of local models.

Extensive experiments are conducted on four commonly used datasets in terms of performance comparison, ablation study of the key components of FedCT, and case study for the effectiveness of knowledge broadcasting and representation learning. The results demonstrate that multi-view knowledge-guided cross-training can expand the learnable knowledge of local models and alleviate dataset bias from the perspective of representation consistency. To summarize, this paper makes three main contributions:

  • •

    This paper presents a model-agnostic cross-training strategy, named FedCT, to mitigate the problem of knowledge forgetting caused by dataset bias. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first method that combines cross-training and multi-view knowledge distillation in federated learning.

  • •

    This paper presents a plug-and-play technique, modeling the cooperative relationship between clients based on the prototypical prediction loss, that can enhance collaboration benefits between clients in scenarios with heterogeneous data distributions and can be easily incorporated into existing methods to improve communication efficiency.

  • •

    Experimental findings have revealed that local and global knowledge forgetting diminishes the benefits that cross-training strategies bring to federated learning. These findings ensure the effectiveness of the proposed multi-view knowledge-guided representation learning module, which mitigates the issue of multi-view knowledge forgetting.

II Related Work

II-A Federated Learning with Non-IID Data

Existing studies on federated learning has made efforts from two complementary perspectives to mitigate the issue of data distribution heterogeneity, as illustrated below.

II-A1 Methods that optimizing the local training on the client-side.

To alleviate the issue of dataset biases, extensive efforts have been made in existing studies on the client-side. This line of research includes regularization-based methods and cross-training strategies. These methods both aim to facilitate the learning of consistent knowledge among clients. Regularization-based methods have carefully designed regularizers to regulate local training from three perspectives, including model weights or gradient [11, 8, 20, 21, 22], features space [23, 24, 13, 25, 18, 26, 27, 28, 29, 30, 31], and model predictions [24, 32, 33, 34]. The weight-based regularization is commonly employed to ensure that the parameters of local models do not deviate significantly from the global model, which could prevent weight divergence. For example, FedProx use Euclidean distance as a regularizer [8] and FedDC introduces a correction term to overcome the gap at the parameter level between local and global models [11]. The feature-based regularization focuses on penalizing inconsistencies in feature space. For example, MOON uses a model-contrastive term to maximize the similarity between local and global features [13], FedProc leverages class-aware prototypes to design a prototypical contrastive term to guide clients in learning aligned representations [18]. The prediction-based regularization utilizes the final predicted probability generated by the global model as supervision i nformation to guide local training, such as FedNTD conducts distillation only for the not-true classes to mitigate forgetting [32]. It has been shown that regularization methods offer a means of addressing the challenge of data heterogeneity.

Moreover, cross-training has emerged as a promising strategy for addressing the problem of dataset bias. It aims to perform model exchange in a privacy-preserving manner to re-train a local model across different clients [17, 15, 16]. This expands the training data for local models, which enables them to learn comprehensive knowledge. Notably, the cross-training strategy, as an orthogonal approach to regularization techniques, can be combined with them to further enhance performance gains. For instance, FedExg [15] and FedMe [16] leverage cross-training strategies to enhance the generalization of the global model and the personalized capabilities of the local models, respectively. PGCT alleviates the problem of local knowledge forgetting due to data heterogeneity and employs prototypes to guide clients in learning similar decision boundaries [17]. Despite they all expand the local knowledge base, the consistency of knowledge across clients should be of greater concern, as shown in Figure 2.

II-A2 Methods that enhancing the aggregation efficacy on the server-side.

Research on the aggregation phase is also crucial for federated learning, which is the key steps for model update. Existing methods for enhancing the aggregation efficacy lie in three main categories, which include designing novel rules to replace weighted averaging [35, 36, 37, 38, 39, 40], fine-tuning or retraining model after aggregation [41, 42, 43, 44, 45], and clustering-based grouping [46, 47, 48, 49]. Regarding research on designing novel aggregation rules, FedNova employs normalization averaging technique prior to aggregation to eliminate target inconsistencies and maintain rapid error convergence [39], FedBE performs sampling to obtain higher-quality global models, and then leverages Bayesian model averaging to combine global models [36], PFNM proposes a probabilistic federated neural matching module to assist in constructing the global model without the need for additional supervision information [50].

In addition, fine-tuning or retraining the model after aggregation has also demonstrated improved performance. It aims to integrate knowledge from multiple data sources to construct a stable global model. For example, FedFTG first models the input space of multiple parties to generate pseudo-data, and then utilizes knowledge distillation to correct the biases introduced after model aggregation [45]. CCVR [41] and CReFF [43] believe that the biases in the model primarily stem from the classifier, and therefore, it is not necessary to fully retrain the entire model. CCVR tunes the classifier using virtual representations sampled from an approximate Gaussian mixture model. CReFF re-trains the classifier by utilizing a set of learnable federated features available on the server and optimizes the gradients of these federated features to align with the gradients of real data.

Another commonly used approach is clustering-based client grouping, which aims to group clients into clusters based on their similarities in data or model characteristics to reduce the intra-cluster weight divergences. A straightforward method for clustered federated learning is to group models based on their loss values on local datasets [51]. However, the significant computational cost hinders the deployment of this method. Some works measure the cosine similarity of local model parameters to form groups, but this approach may be inefficient in large-scale federated settings [48]. Recent study has also developed a method to assess the differences in the feature space, which reduces computational costs while improving performance [49].

II-B Contrastive learning in Federated Learning

Contrastive learning has demonstrated impressive representation learning capabilities in both supervised and unsupervised learning settings. Its main idea is to maximize the distance between target samples and their positive samples in the latent space while minimizing the distance between target samples and negative samples. For example, SimCLR [52] and Simsiam [53] are two classic self-supervised learning methods. PCL combines contrastive learning and clustering for unsupervised representation learning [54]. SCL utilizes label information for fully supervised representation learning, enabling advanced classification performance [55].

Recent works have employed contrastive learning to overcome heterogeneity challenges in federated learning. They typically employ contrastive loss to compare global and local representations, leveraging the comprehensive global information to guide the learning of local models, such as MOON [13] develops a model-level contrastive learning, which maximizes the similarity between the representations of the current round’s local model and the global model’s output while minimizing the similarity with the representations of the previous round’s local model’s output and FedProc [18] maximizes the consistency between sample representations and the corresponding prototype. And FedCA [56] and FedX [24] focus on the unsupervised learning cases, they aim to learn unbiased representation from distributed and heterogeneous data.

IV Method

IV-A Overall Framework

The proposed cross-training scheme (FedCT) in federated learning, as described in Figure 3, has four main phases, including local training, knowledge broadcasting strategy development, multi-view knowledge-guided cross training, and global aggregation. In phase I, FedCT optimizes the local model and retains a class-specific prototypical representation for per class. In phase II, FedCT generates a group of global prototypes and introduces a consistency-aware knowledge broadcasting strategy, which can facilitate better cooperation among local models without compromising privacy. In phase III, each client obtains the model learned in the phase I from another client and then re-trains it. It has two key modules, the multi-view knowledge-guided representation learning module and the mixup-based feature augmentation module. In phase IV, FedCT collects all the local models learned in phase III and aggregates them to build a global model.

FedCT optimizes local models and provides the prototypical information to the server without privacy leakage in the Local Training phase. IV-D Phase III: Multi-View Knowledge-Guided Cross Training

FedCT retrains local models under the guidance of multi-view prototypical knowledge in Cross Train phase, which helps the local model learn comprehensive knowledge. In this phase, each client obtains the local model, local prototype and global prototype from other client based on consistency-aware knowledge broadcast rules. It contains two main modules in the training process: the multi-view knowledge-guided representation learning module and the mixup-based feature augmentation module.

IV-D1 Multi-View Knowledge-Guided Representation Learning (MVKGRL).

The MVKGRL module employs multi-view prototypes to guide all clients to learn comprehensive knowledge, which expands the knowledge repository that local models can learn from and strives to impose consistent knowledge learning among different local models to the greatest extent possible. Specifically, it first fuses personalized and global prototypes, and then extends traditional contrastive learning, and leverages augmented samples to enhance the effectiveness of representation learning, which aligns the distribution of samples representations and the corresponding prototypes.

VI Conclusion

This paper proposes a multi-view knowledge-guided cross-training approach, termed FedCT, to tackle the issue of knowledge forgetting. Specifically, FedCT first introduces a consistency-aware knowledge broadcasting strategy, which is capable of enhancing collaboration between clients. Subsequently, FedCT develops a multi-view knowledge-guided representation learning method to guide client learn comprehensive knowledge, which extends the local training set and builds a unified knowledge base across clients as comprehensively as possible. And FedCT then leverages mixup-based feature augmentation to aggregate information, which increases the diversity of the feature space. Experimental results show that FedCT is capable of effectively mitigating knowledge forgetting from both local and global views, which leads to FedCT outperforming existing methods.

Despite FedCT has achieved impressive improvements, there are still two directions that can be further explored in future work. First, stronger local representation learning methods can guide feature alignment across clients, which can significantly enhance performance. Second, the investigation into more optimal exchange strategies is imperative, as it holds the potential to expedite model convergence.
