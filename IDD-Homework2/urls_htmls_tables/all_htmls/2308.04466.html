Abstract

Federated learning (FL) has been widely deployed to enable machine learning training on sensitive data across distributed devices. However, the decentralized learning paradigm and heterogeneity of FL further extend the attack surface for backdoor attacks. Existing FL attack and defense methodologies typically focus on the whole model. None of them recognizes the existence of backdoor-critical (BC) layersâ€”a small subset of layers that dominate the model vulnerabilities. Attacking the BC layers achieves equivalent effects as attacking the whole model but at a far smaller chance of being detected by state-of-the-art (SOTA) defenses. This paper proposes a general in-situ approach that identifies and verifies BC layers from the perspective of attackers. Based on the identified BC layers, we carefully craft a new backdoor attack methodology that adaptively seeks a fundamental balance between attacking effects and stealthiness under various defense strategies. Extensive experiments show that our BC layer-aware backdoor attacks can successfully backdoor FL under seven SOTA defenses with only 10% malicious clients and outperform latest backdoor attack methods.

1 Introduction

Federated learning (FL)Â [1] enables machine learning across large-scale distributed clients without violating data privacy. However, such decentralized learning paradigm and heterogeneity in data distribution and client systems extensively enlarge FLâ€™s attack surface. A small set of malicious clients can easily manipulate the training process by uploading poisoned local models to the FL server.

Increasing numbers of attack methods have been developed to either slow down the convergence of FL training (i.e., untargeted attacksÂ [2, 3, 4, 5]) or enforce the model to intentionally misclassify specific categories of data (i.e., targeted attacksÂ [6, 7, 8, 9, 10]). As a subset of targeted attacks, backdoor attacksÂ [6, 7, 9, 11, 10] are one of the stealthiest attacks for FL, which train models on data with special triggers embedded, such as pixels, textures, and even patterns in the frequency domainÂ [12]. Models compromised by backdoor attacks typically have high accuracy on general data samples (i.e., main task) except that samples with triggers embedded activate the â€œbackdoorâ€ inside the model (i.e., backdoor task), leading to misclassification targeted to specific labels (e.g., recognizing a stop sign as a speed limit sign).

Several defense methods have been proposed to detect backdoor attacks and mitigate their impacts, which can be classified into three types based on their key techniques: distance-based, inversion-based, and sign-based defense. Distance-based defenses, such as FLTrustÂ [13] and FoolsGoldÂ [14], calculate the cosine similarity distance and euclidean distance between the local models to detect potential malicious clients. Inversion-based defenses, such asÂ [15], utilize trigger inversion and backdoor unlearning to mitigate backdoors in global models. Sign-based defenses, such as RLRÂ [16], detect the sign change directions of each parameter in the local model updates uploaded by clients and adjust the learning rate of each parameter to mitigate backdoor attacks.

However, existing backdoor attack and defense methods ignore the correlation between the expression of backdoor tasks and model structuresâ€”the effectiveness of backdoor attacks is only related to a small subset of model layersâ€”backdoor-critical (BC) layers. To demonstrate the existence of BC layers, we train a benign five-layer CNN model on a clean dataset until it has converged. Then, we train a copy of the benign model on poisoned data (with triggers embedded) and obtain a malicious model. We substitute each layer in the benign model for the same layer in the malicious model and measure the backdoor attack success rate, which denotes the accuracy of recognizing samples with trigger embedded as the targeted label. Fig.Â 1(a) shows that the absence of layers in the malicious model does not degrade the BSR except for the fc1.weight layer. Fig.Â 1(b) shows the reversed layer substitution that only the fc1.weight layer from the malicious model enables successful backdoor tasks. Therefore, we argue that a small set of layers, such as fc1.weight, are backdoor-criticalâ€”the absence of even one BC layer leads to a low Backdoor Success Rate. BC layers as a small subset of models can be observed in large models like ResNet18 and VGG19 (refer to Fig.Â 15). Intuitively, deeper layers are more BC because shallower layers learn simple, low-level features such as edges and textures, and deeper layers combine these features to learn more complex, high-level concepts such as objects and their partsÂ [17, 18, 19].

This paper proposes a Layer Substitution Analysis, a general in-situ approach that identifies BC layers using forward and backward layer substitutions. We further craft two new backdoor attack methods: layer-wise poisoning attack and layer-wise flipping attack. These two backdoor attack methods leverage the identified BC layers to bypass state-of-the-art (SOTA) distance-based, inversion-based, and sign-based defense methods by carefully attacking the BC layers with minimal model poisoning and a small number of clients (i.e., 10% of the participating clients).

2.2 A Brief Taxonomy of Attacks on FL

Based on the attackersâ€™ goal, existing attacks on FL can be classified into two types: untargeted and targeted attacks. Untargeted attacks aim to prevent the global model from converging or minimizing the accuracy of the global modelÂ [2, 3, 4, 5], while targeted attacks attempt to decrease the accuracy on specific test inputs, and keep a high accuracy on the remaining test inputs Â [6, 7, 8, 9]. As a subset of targeted attacks, backdoor attacksÂ [6, 7, 9] force the model to classify images to targeted incorrect labels by embedding special triggers to training data samples. DBAÂ [6] designs a distributed backdoor attack method that splits a complete trigger into multiple partial triggers. DBA exploits such small triggers to stay stealthy. Scaling attackÂ [7] scales up the updates of malicious models, which are trained on trigger-embedded examples, to strengthen their impacts on the global model. The constraint loss module is introduced by 3DFedÂ [10] to minimize the gap between malicious models and benign models. In this work, we present a distinct distance reduction approach that leverages BC layers and conducts attacks in the layer space.

2.3 FL Defense Methods Against Backdoors

To address the vulnerabilities of FL, a rich literature of defense methods against backdoor attacks have been proposed. We classify them into three categories based on their methodology: distance-basedÂ [13, 20, 14, 21], inversion-basedÂ [15] and sign-based defensesÂ [22, 16].

Distance-based defense methods measure the distance between model updates to detect the outliers as malicious clients. For example, FLTrustÂ [13] exploits a root datasetâ€”a small set of clean samples on the serverâ€”to train a model in each round and obtain its update norm. FLTrust calculates the cosine similarity between the server model update norm and client modelsâ€™ update norm as the trust scores and performs robust FL aggregation to filter out malicious updates according to the trust scores. FLAMEÂ [20] calculates the cosine distance between each local model and uses the HDBSCAN algorithm to filter potential malicious models. After selecting more than half of the models as benign models, FLAME clips the updates of each model and adds noise to the final global models. DeepSightÂ [21] focuses on the parameters of output layers and uses random input to detect malicious updates by HDBSCAN algorithm. FLDetectorÂ [23] predicts all clientsâ€™ gradients by summing up their previous gradients and the predicted vector calculated by L-BFGS algorithm. Then it clusters all clients by K-means algorithm by calculating suspicious scores using Euclidean distance between their predicted gradient and the gradient from clients. FoolsGoldÂ [14] presumes that output layers are most related to backdoor tasks so only calculates cosine similarity by parameters in output layers. FLAREÂ [24] calculates distance-maximum mean discrepancy based on penultimate layer representations from the root dataset.

Sign-based defense methods distinguish benign and malicious clients based on the signs of their gradient updates. For example, Robust learning rate (RLR)Â [16] presumes that updates from benign clients should achieve consensus on the direction of each parameter, and mitigates backdoor attacks by flipping the learning rate on parameters, where malicious clientsâ€™ updates break the consensus.

Inversion-based defense methods aim to invert potential triggers in each class and use adversarial training to eliminate backdoor tasks in the global model. Prior works, such as Neural CleanseÂ [25] and AbsÂ [26], have explored the potential of inversion-based defense techniques. More recently, FlipÂ [15] has introduced asymmetric trigger inversion, adversarial training, and confidence threshold into FL to enhance its robustness against backdoor attacks.

7 Related Work

Backdoor Attack in Deployment Stage. The deployment stage of a backdoor attack aims to misclassify a set of crafted samples into a targeted label by modifying a small number of model weights. Several studies, such as [40, 41, 42, 43], have considered the manipulation of binary-form parameters stored in computer memory to inject backdoors into models. Other studies, such as [34, 44, 45], have proposed modifying a subnet of the model to identify triggers. [44, 45] need to modify the model architecture and program, while [34] selects a path from the input layer to the output layer to craft a subnet without modifying model architecture and knowing the parameters or training data in models. Despite these approaches having the same goal as us that only changing a limited number of parameters or bits, they have been found to be susceptible to be detected by FL defense strategies, as they do not take the distance as a part of the optimization.

Backdoor Critical Parameters. Recent research has demonstrated that certain neurons within neural networks are particularly susceptible to backdoor attacks. Several studies, includingÂ [46, 47, 25, 26, 48], have proposed pruning these critical neurons as a means of mitigating such attacks. Adversarial Neuron Perturbation Â [46] is a gradient-based technique that injects backdoors with a limited perturbation budget, however, it is computationally expensive.[49] propose a method for injecting backdoors into transfer learning by targeting shallow layers of the network.

Backdoor Attack in Federated Learning. LIEÂ [3] clips models updates in each parameter by calculating the mean and variance, but this attack fails in classical setting FedAvg and new defense strategies like FLAME and FLDetector with a relatively large number of attackers (20%). Scaling attack [7] scales up malicious clients weights to overcome the affect of other clients, which is easily detected by defense strategies. Edge-case backdoor attackÂ [9] aims to attack a set of samples with low predicted probability, which is hard to detect. But this attack has disadvantages in that targeted inputs are decided by the model and it is also defended by SOTA defense likeÂ [20, 21]. 3DFedÂ [10] proposes a constraint loss module for distance-based defenses, a noise masks module for bypassing update energies detection, a decoy model module for deceiving dimensionality reduction techniques, and an indicator module for fine-tuning hyperparameters. However, the indicator module and decoy model module require multiple clients to collaborate in attacks, which conflicts with our experimental setup, where only one malicious client is allowed in each round. Consequently, these modules are not considered in the experiment section.

Model compression and lottery tickets. Like iterative model pruning techniques widely applied to identify lottery ticketsÂ [50, 51, 52], we can also iteratively prune a DNN model on poisoned data to seek for a key subnetwork that dominates its vulnerability. However, iterative model pruning requires large volumes of training data, and the identified structures (i.e., the lottery ticket) vary with different initial model weights. Instead, this paper proposes a general in-situ approach that directly searches BC layers.

8 Conclusion

This paper proposes Layer Substitution Analysis, an algorithm that verifies and identifies the existence of backdoor-critical layers. We further design two layer-wise backdoor attack methods, LP Attack and LF Attack that utilize the knowledge of backdoor-critical layers to craft effective and stealthy backdoor attacks with minimal model poisoning. We evaluate the relationship between backdoor tasks and layers under an extensive range of settings and show our attacks can successfully bypass SOTA defense methods and inject backdoor into models with a small number of compromised clients.
