Abstract

The proposed IncidentResponseGPT framework - a novel system that applies generative artificial intelligence (AI) to potentially enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of traffic incident response. This model allows for synthesis of region-specific incident response guidelines and generates incident response plans adapted to specific area, aiming to expedite decision-making for traffic management authorities. This approach aims to accelerate incident resolution times by suggesting various recommendations (e.g. optimal rerouting strategies, estimating resource needs) to minimize the overall impact on the urban traffic network. The system suggests specific actions, including dynamic lane closures, optimized rerouting and dispatching appropriate emergency resources. IncidentResponseGPT employs the Technique for Order Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS) to rank generated response plans based on criteria like impact minimization and resource efficiency based on their proximity to an human-proposed solution.

1 Introduction

Traffic congestion caused by road incidents represents a critical challenge in urban environments, leading to a cascade of detrimental impacts. Increased travel times, elevated pollution levels, and significant economic losses due to delays are just a few of the consequences faced by commuters and cities alike. While existing traffic management centers (TMCs) employ a mix of automated and manual processes [1, 2], these systems often struggle to keep pace with the increasing complexity and real-time demands of todayâ€™s traffic conditions.

Addressing this critical need, we introduce IncidentResponseGPT, a novel system designed to revolutionize traffic incident response by leveraging the power of generative artificial intelligence (AI). Unlike traditional approaches, IncidentResponseGPT goes beyond simple automation. It analyzes real-time traffic incident reports and integrates region-specific operational guidelines to rapidly generate adaptable response plans. This empowers traffic management authorities with a proactive and intelligent tool to significantly improve the efficiency and effectiveness of their incident response strategies. This is achieved by incorporating a Generative AI [3] with traffic incident reports and area-specific operational guidelines.

The novelty of IncidentResponseGPT lies in its unique application of generative AI within the domain of traffic management. Our system can synthesize incident response guidelines and propose solutions that are adapted to the specific circumstances of each traffic incident. This approach holds the potential to not only accelerate incident resolution times but also to minimize the overall impact of incidents on the entire urban traffic network.

While promising, the integration of AI into traffic management presents inherent challenges. These include effectively representing the complexity of urban road networks, accounting for the wide variety of potential incident types, ensuring real-time decision-making capabilities, and adhering to local traffic laws and regulations:

  1. 1.

    Complexity of Urban Traffic Network: Urban traffic networks are extraordinarily complex systems with various intersecting roads, bridges, and tunnels. This complexity makes it difficult to predict and manage traffic during incidents effectively.

  2. 2.

    Variety of Traffic Incidents: The nature and severity of traffic incidents can greatly vary, including minor accidents, major crashes, natural disasters, road works, etc. Each type requires a different and suitable response strategy.

  3. 3.

    Real-Time Decision Making: Traffic management authorities must make quick decisions. Any delay can escalate the situation, making the problem of generating accurate and timely response plans even more challenging.

  4. 4.

    Alignment with Local Laws and Regulations: Different regions have different response guidelines and roadway regulations. The system must align with these, requiring a good understanding of the rules.

overall, IncidentResponseGPT presents a novel approach with the capability to revolutionize traffic incident management in urban environments. By using the power of generative AI, our proposed system aims to overcome the limitations of traditional approaches by offering rapid, adaptable, and informed response plans. The potential benefits are significant, promising to reduce congestion, minimize economic losses, and enhance overall safety for all stakeholders within the urban traffic ecosystem.

2 Related Works

LLMs have shown promise in disaster management, demonstrating their ability to generate rapid response plans and disseminate information [4, 5]. However, these models often struggle with real-time data integration and may lack the domain-specific knowledge required for accurate traffic incident management. Similarly, existing AI-based traffic management systems often rely on techniques like Case-Based Reasoning (CBR) or Bayesian Theory [6, 7]. While these approaches have shown some success, they often struggle with the scalability and adaptability required for real-time incident response. CBR systems are heavily reliant on the quality and quantity of historical cases, which can limit their effectiveness in novel situations. Bayesian methods, while powerful, can become computationally expensive in real-time scenarios, especially with large datasets [6].

ChatGPT previously has been explored for its capabilities in natural disaster management and demonstrate the applicability of LLM models in simplifying and accelerating the disaster management process through stages such as pre-disaster, during-disaster, and post-disaster scenarios [4]. These models prove effective in generating rapid response plans and disseminating crucial information during the event, with inherent limitations concerning real-time data processing and professional accuracy. The model addresses the limitations in real-time data handling and expert-level accuracy in complex disaster management scenarios. Also, a recently proposed DisasterResponseGPT framework [5] relies on LLMs to generate rapid action plans in disaster situations with limitations in handling visual and real-time data inputs. The discussion on using simulation models like Visual Interactive System for Transport Algorithms (VISTA) for simulating traffic conditions and test incident management strategies further underscores the benefits of dynamic modeling in responding to varying traffic conditions effectively [8]. These simulations help in preplanning which, as their findings suggest, is essential for reducing response times and managing congestion effectively during traffic incidents.

Through integrating Geographic Information System (GIS) capabilities with AI [9], the efficiency of incident responses can be markedly improved by optimizing resource allocation and managing traffic disruptions in real-time scenarios. AI models are capable of facilitating quick and effective decision-making processes by processing and analyzing large volumes of data swiftly [9]. Works that tackle the integration of AI in traffic systems further affirm the potential of LLMs in transforming urban traffic management [10]. The integration of real-time data, the need for continuous model training, and the development of robust frameworks to handle complex emergency situations are recurrent themes across studies. As indicated by safety analytics in transport and construction sectors, there remains a crucial gap in applying AI to dynamically interpret complex environmental data sets and interact seamlessly across diverse operational platforms.

The integration of data-driven models in ERM systems was previously extensively reviewed [11] and highlighted the disconnect between traditional static models and the dynamic requirements of real-world emergency management. The authors proposed a transition towards more adaptive models, capable of real-time adjustments in response strategies based on live data inputs. Key challenges identified include the ethical implications which remain paramount as these technologies become more relevant in critical areas affecting public safety of predictive policing and the limitations of current technology in accurately modeling complex urban behaviors. Current study aims to address AI capabilities with a consideration of ethical implications, striving for transparency and reliability of AI-driven methodology in traffic incident management. The application of dynamic resource allocation has been demonstrated in a similar case of response planning: crime response, with strategies like predictive analytics and hotspot policing gaining prominence. The continuous-time Markov decision process (CTMDP) uses real-time crime data to optimize police patrol allocations, significantly improving response times in urban scenarios by minimizing the duration between crime occurrence and police arrival.

The potential of AI to enhance traffic incident management significantly revolves around its ability to process and model the incident data. Real-time traffic incident management demands rapid assessment and response, which can be significantly optimized using AI. There is a critical need for integrating real-time data with AI systems to manage incidents effectively on complex traffic networks [12]. The introduction of IncidentResponseGPT addresses these challenges by combining generative AI with incident report data with the consideration of the importance of real-time adaptable plans in managing urban disasters [5].

Previous studies have demonstrated AIâ€™s capabilities in performing complex text analysis, including the examination of accident reports to enhance the prediction of various aspects of traffic incidents [13]. However, the potential of Generative AI in generating immediate incident response plans remains largely unexplored.

Despite advancements, several challenges persist in AI-driven incident management. Recent study on data-driven models in emergency response management identified the need for adaptive models capable of real-time adjustments based on live data inputs [11]. Trust and transparency in AI-generated content during emergencies are crucial [14], emphasizing the need for transparent decision-making processes, which IncidentResponseGPT addresses through its chain-of-thought visualization and validation process.

Ravish and Swamyâ€™s review discusses the integration of machine learning and computational intelligence in managing traffic congestion, highlighting significant advancements in Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS). They stress the importance of real-time data processing and accident detection mechanisms for improving traffic flow and safety [[7]]. The review identifies challenges such as the increasing number of vehicles and insufficient infrastructure expansion. Although machine learning and computational intelligence offer promising solutions, real-time data processing, system complexity, adaptability, and scalability, as well as accident detection, remain critical concerns for the advancement and expansion of traffic systems.

Case-Based Reasoning (CBR) and Bayesian Theory have been employed to automatically generate traffic incident response plans [[6]]. The effectiveness of CBR depends on the quality and quantity of historical cases in the database, as limited or biased data can lead to suboptimal decision-making. Moreover, calculating posterior probabilities using Bayesian Theory in real time can be resource-intensive, particularly with complex models and large data sets.

The use of generative AI addresses the limitations of static area-specific models [11] through the flexibility of using information represented as a text. It allows to promptly change guidelines for incident response or even try guidelines from other countries or areas to compare their efficiency. Also, it allows for adaptation of insights from safety guidelines and analytics from other sectors, like transportation construction area [15], to the domain of traffic incident response (e.g. the description of a highway architecture can help to derive more efficient plans with consideration of its operational and structural specifics).

Overall, this study underscores the capacity of LLMs to process complex information from the area of traffic maangement and generate actionable safety recommendations, illustrating the broader applicability of AI in understanding and mitigating hazards.

3 Case Study

Our IncidentResponseGPT framework (Traffic Incident Response generation framework utilising GPT models) aims to utilize generative AI to rapidly generate and propose response plans for traffic incidents based on incoming incident reports and region-specific incident response guidelines, providing traffic management authorities with powerful tools to enhance efficiency, safety, and streamline operations with a potential to dramatically enhance their operational effectiveness.

The US-Accidents dataset is a comprehensive countrywide traffic accident dataset for the United States, excluding Alaska and Hawaii, that includes about 1.5 million traffic accident records collected continuously since February 2016 from multiple data providers. The data captures real-time traffic events through several APIs sourced from different entities such as US and state departments of transportation, law enforcement agencies, and traffic cameras.

The dataset includes a number of attributes for each accident record:

  • 1.

    Location Details: Latitude and longitude, city, county, state, and zip code.

  • 2.

    Time Details: Accident start and end time, timezone, and timestamps for weather observations.

  • 3.

    Environment: Weather conditions such as temperature, wind chill, humidity, visibility, and precipitation.

  • 4.

    Accident Details: Severity, description, and distance affected.

  • 5.

    Nearby Features: Presence of traffic signals, crossings, junctions, and other points of interest.

  • 6.

    Daylight Conditions: Based on sunrise, sunset, and various levels of twilight.

We use â€Traffic Incident Management Handbookâ€ as incident response guidelines. These guidelines were prepared for the Federal Highway Administration in November 2000, serve as a comprehensive guide for agencies involved in managing traffic incidents on public roadways. It targets two key audiences: 1) Managers responsible for developing and improving incident management programs, 2) Field Practitioners tasked with the day-to-day execution of these programs. The handbook addresses a wide range of topics related to incident management, including: 1) definitions for incident management, outlines its significance, and identifies the key agencies involved, 2) organization, planning, and design of an Incident Management, 3) provides a step-by-step process for establishing or enhancing programs, emphasizing the importance of collaboration, data analysis, goal setting, and evaluation, 4) outlines operational and technical approaches to improving the incident management process with the mention of specific techniques and strategies for each stage of incident management, such as detection, verification, motorist information, response, site management, traffic management, and clearance, 5) combines process-oriented guidance for program development with a toolbox of strategies and techniques applicable to real-world incident response.

Within our framework, these guidelines pre-processed using Generative AI models to extract key instructions related to incident severity estimation, responder safety, traffic control, and information dissemination. The page length of these guidelines is 106 pages excluding references. These extracted guidelines are then converted into a structured format. These steps are necessary to allow the AI model to utilize instructions effectively with the consideration for inherent limitation for context window of Generative AI models.

In a real-world implementation, IncidentResponseGPT has a property of high adaptability and scalability to available data sources due to its inherent ability to process any information represented as text. This could potentially allow the system to continuously adapt its response plan based on real-time updates from the field, traffic sensors, and other data sources.

4 Guideline synthesis

The core operation of guideline synthesis procedure consists of multiple chunk processing and synthesis cycles (S-cycle). This procedure can be used as a component of a larger chain-of-thought schema and allows to process large amounts of text. S-cycle consists of the following steps (see Figure 2):

  • 1.

    Text Chunking: The first step involves breaking down the given full text of incident response guidelines (guidelines can be extensive and include up to 300 pages) into manageable segments, or â€chunks,â€ to facilitate processing. This step acknowledges the limitations in processing capacity of generative models for large texts and ensures that each segment can be individually summarized. For our current research, the size of these chunks is defined at 6000 tokens to address limited token space of various LLM models.

  • 2.

    Iterative Processing: The core of the process involves iterative steps where each text chunk is processed individually. This iterative process is initiated with a â€For Each Chunkâ€ node, indicating the beginning of a loop that applies to every chunk derived from the original text.

  • 3.

    Query Application: For each chunk, a query, specifically â€summarize as a table,â€ is applied using ChatGPT technology. This step leverages the natural language processing and generative capabilities of ChatGPT to convert textual information into a structured table format, summarizing the key points of each chunk.

  • 4.

    Result Acquisition: Following the application of the query, the result, which is the ChatGPT-generated table summarizing the chunk, is obtained.

  • 5.

    Loop Continuation Decision: After processing a chunk, a decision node â€End of Text?â€ checks if there are more chunks to process. If more chunks remain (â€noâ€), the process loops back to continue with the next chunk. This iterative cycle ensures that every chunk of the original text is processed and summarized.

  • 6.

    Response Combination and Final Synthesis: Once all chunks have been processed and summarized, the resulting tables from each chunk are combined. A final query, â€synthesize the table,â€ is applied to this combined data, aiming to integrate the content into a single, coherent table. This step is crucial for synthesizing the fragmented summaries into a unified representation of the original text.

  • 7.

    Final Output: The process is finished with obtaining the final table, which represents a synthesized summary of the entire original text in a structured table format.

Our research outlines a strategic approach for processing and synthesizing methodologies related to traffic incident response management, structured around four types of queries.

5 Incident response plan comparison methodology

During the incident management, decision-makers are frequently confronted with the need to evaluate and select from a multitude of response actions under conditions of uncertainty and time constraints. The commonly used method is Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) which serves as a robust multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) tool that assists in organizing and analyzing these complex decisions. By breaking down the decision-making problem into a hierarchy of more easily comprehended sub-problems, AHP enables decision-makers to systematically evaluate the relative importance of various criteria and actions. While the Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) provides a structured approach for multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM), in this study, we leverage the Technique for Order Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS) [16, 17] method to compare incident response strategies. TOPSIS is another robust MCDM technique that ranks alternatives based on their relative closeness to an ideal solution. Unlike AHP, which involves pairwise comparisons and eigenvector calculations, TOPSIS evaluates alternatives based on their geometric distance to a positive ideal solution (PIS) and a negative ideal solution (NIS).
