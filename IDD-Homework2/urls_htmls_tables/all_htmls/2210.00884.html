Generating Synthetic Data with The Nearest Neighbors Algorithm

Ali Furkan Kalay a.kalay@uq.edu.au, School of Economics, University of Queensland, Australia.
Abstract

The k𝑘k nearest neighbor algorithm (k𝑘kNN) is one of the most popular nonparametric methods used for various purposes, such as treatment effect estimation, missing value imputation, classification, and clustering. The main advantage of k𝑘kNN is its simplicity of hyperparameter optimization. It often produces favorable results with minimal effort. This paper proposes a generic semiparametric (or nonparametric if required) approach named Local Resampler (LR). LR utilizes k𝑘kNN to create subsamples from the original sample and then generates synthetic values that are drawn from locally estimated distributions. LR can accurately create synthetic samples, even if the original sample has a non-convex distribution. Moreover, LR shows better or similar performance to other popular synthetic data methods with minimal model optimization with parametric distributional assumptions.

Keywords: synthetic data, nearest neighbor algorithm, disclosure control, semiparametric methods.

1 Introduction

The k𝑘k nearest neighbor algorithm (k𝑘kNN) is used for various purposes such as causal inference (Abadie \BBA Imbens, \APACyear2006), missing value imputation (Little \BBA Rubin, \APACyear2019), and classification (Cover \BBA Hart, \APACyear1967). Unlike most model-based approaches, k𝑘kNN produces robust outcomes in most contexts without effortful hyperparameter tuning. The nearest neighbor algorithm could also be combined with modeling assumptions to increase the precision and/or to achieve desired asymptotic properties111Such as de-biased matching estimator (Abadie \BBA Imbens, \APACyear2011), matching with estimated balance scores (Kalay, \APACyear2021), predictive mean matching (Little \BBA Rubin, \APACyear2019), and propensity score matching (Rosenbaum \BBA Rubin, \APACyear1983). This paper proposes an approach named Local Resampler (LR) utilizing k𝑘kNN to create synthetic samples. LR produces efficient results with minimal efforts for hyperparameter tuning222i.e., the analyst need to choose the value for k𝑘k and the type of the estimated distribution, and it can replicate nonlinear and non-convex distributions.333The implementations (and extensions) of LR algorithm and paper simulations are available: github.com/alfurka/synloc.

Rubin (\APACyear1993) proposed to use the multiple imputation approach to create synthetic versions of the microdata to “honor the confidentiality constraints.” The multiple imputation approach is further developed since then.444See Raghunathan (\APACyear2021) for a literature review. Various software packages are available to create synthetic data today, such as synthpop (Nowok \BOthers., \APACyear2016) and SDV (Patki \BOthers., \APACyear2016). The synthpop adapts the multiple imputation approach suggested by Rubin (\APACyear1993), and allows incorporating both parametric and nonparametric models.555The package allows quite a flexibility to users, such as handling and synthesizing missing values, selecting synthesis sequences, stratifying the sample, sampling from predictive posterior distributions of the models, and so on. The theoretical aspects of synthpop package were discussed in Raab \BOthers. (\APACyear2014). SDV, on the other hand, is an environment rather than a package. It includes different copula and deep learning methods to synthesize different data types.666Although there are other numerous methods and software to create a synthetic sample, this paper will use SDV and synthpop as a benchmark because of their simplicity and popularity.

Technically, the multiple imputation methods replace the observed values with the model-predicted synthetic values as if they were missing. The theoretical and practical aspects of the imputation methods are well-established for the missing value problem (Van Buuren \BBA Oudshoorn, \APACyear1999; Stekhoven \BBA Bühlmann, \APACyear2012). Nevertheless, its implementation differs from missing value imputation problem to some extent. For example, the multiple imputation method creates synthetic values from a predictive posterior distribution (PPD) conditional on the “previously generated” variables rather than all variables. The analyst can choose seperate PPD models for each variable. The nonparametric models (especially, decision trees) are preferred over parametric models to account for the nonlinearity of the original sample (Raab \BOthers., \APACyear2014; Drechsler \BBA Reiter, \APACyear2011).

The more straightforward approach is to estimate a distribution and draw synthetic values from the estimated distribution to create the synthetic values instead of sequentially estimating PPDs for each variable. One of the preferred solutions is using copulas because of their flexibility, allowing both parametric (e.g., Gaussian copula) and nonparametric distributions (e.g., Vine copula (Sun \BOthers., \APACyear2019)). However, copulas-based models are often difficult to generalize. They require proper specification for marginal distributions of each variable (if possible), and it is challenging to synthesize continuous and discrete variables simultaneously.

The deep learning algorithms are also commonly used to create synthetic samples, such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Goodfellow \BOthers., \APACyear2014) and Variational Auto Encoders (VAE) (Kingma \BBA Welling, \APACyear2013). Both methods, like most deep learning algorithms, are computationally heavy-burden and are difficult to optimize. Kamthe \BOthers. (\APACyear2021) utilize normalizing flows to synthesize tabular data.777The normalizing flows are used various data types such as time series (e.g., Rasul \BOthers. (\APACyear2020); de Bézenac \BOthers. (\APACyear2020); Feng \BOthers. (\APACyear2022)), images (e.g., Dinh \BOthers. (\APACyear2014); Kingma \BBA Dhariwal (\APACyear2018); Hoogeboom \BOthers. (\APACyear2019); Nagar \BOthers. (\APACyear2021)) and sounds (e.g., Prenger \BOthers. (\APACyear2019); Valle \BOthers. (\APACyear2020); Natsiou \BBA O’Leary (\APACyear2021); Bilinski \BOthers. (\APACyear2022). Their method can synthesize the mixed data types and replicate non-convex distributions.

The iterative imputation, arguably, has an easier implementation than other approaches. Even with large samples, the computational burden is not a concern. The decision trees can replicate asymmetric and nonlinear distributions efficiently for both discrete and continuous variables (Drechsler \BBA Reiter, \APACyear2011). Moreover, Raab \BOthers. (\APACyear2014) established the theoretical aspects of making inferences with synthetic samples - which is a missing aspect in most of the other works.888Although the validity of inference relies on strong assumptions that most likely fails with the use of the decision trees.

Using the k𝑘kNN to create synthetic data is not entirely new. k𝑘kNN had been used for differential privacy purpose in specific contexts (e.g., Zhu \BOthers. (\APACyear2020)). Chawla \BOthers. (\APACyear2002) developed a sampling technique named “synthetic minority over-sampling technique” (SMOTE) to synthesize the rare cases. The synthetic values are created by interpolating the neighboring values. This technique had been mostly used for classification problems. Later, Torgo \BOthers. (\APACyear2013) extended SMOTE to balance the rare and frequent cases of continuous variables. Recently, Sivakumar \BOthers. (\APACyear2022) proposed a method that combining Mega-Trend Diffusion approach(Li \BOthers., \APACyear2007) and k𝑘kNN named k-Nearest Neighbor Mega-Trend Diffusion. Their approach resembles most of this paper but differs from LR in the sense that they find neighbors of each variable separately.

This paper proposes a generic approach named Local Resampler (LR). LR is a sequential algorithm similar to the multiple imputation, instead, LR iterates through the observations (subsamples). This paper uses k𝑘kNN only to define the subsamples from neighbors, but it is possible to use a different approach, such as clustering. The synthetic values are drawn from locally estimated distributions. LR can be further extended and developed using nonparametric distributions and more efficient algorithms. LR outperforms most copula-based methods and deep learning algorithms even with strong parametric distributional assumptions, i.e., multivariate normal distribution.

The next section explains the proposed methodology. The third section discusses the theoretical and practical concerns regarding LR. The fourth section shows an implementation of LR in real data sets. The fifth section makes concluding remarks.

3 Discussions

This section addresses the theoretical and practical implications of the proposed methodology.

3.1 Assumptions

Assumption 1.

Sisubscript𝑆𝑖S_{i} is drawn from Fi​(𝛂i)subscript𝐹𝑖subscript𝛂𝑖F_{i}(\bm{\alpha}_{i}) with parameters 𝛂isubscript𝛂𝑖\bm{\alpha}_{i}. F^i​(𝛂i^)subscript^𝐹𝑖^subscript𝛂𝑖\hat{F}_{i}(\hat{\bm{\alpha}_{i}}) is an unbiased estimator of Fi​(𝛂i)subscript𝐹𝑖subscript𝛂𝑖F_{i}(\bm{\alpha}_{i}) for all i∈S′𝑖superscript𝑆′i\in S^{\prime} where 𝛂i^^subscript𝛂𝑖\hat{\bm{\alpha}_{i}} is estimated with subsample Sisubscript𝑆𝑖S_{i}.

Under the assumption 1, the target inference from the synthetic sample will be an unbiased estimator of the target inference from the original sample. Assumption 1 is in fact a set of n′superscript𝑛′n^{\prime} nested distributional assumptions and likely to fail, at least, for some i∈S′𝑖superscript𝑆′i\in S^{\prime}. However, it is possible to test the validity of the distributional assumption for each i∈S′𝑖superscript𝑆′i\in S^{\prime} or use nonparametric distributions, if not unfeasible due to high dimensionality. This paper argues and shows that assumption 1 is weaker than assuming the entire sample is drawn from a specific parametric/nonparametric distribution.

Nevertheless, the selection of the distribution model could be crucial for the quality of the synthesis. Figure 1 shows the estimated probability density functions with uniform and multivariate normal (MVN) distributions for a small sample. Based on a visual inspection, the MVN is expected to replicate the original subsample better than the uniform distribution as subsample variables are seemingly correlated. The correlation will be overlooked if the analyst uses a univariate distribution.

k𝑘kNN estimator is assumed to have fixed k𝑘k for the asymptotic consistency of the prediction. At least, k𝑘k is not chosen proportional to the sample size. It is well known that k𝑘kNN suffers from the curse of dimensionality. However, the proposed methodology uses k𝑘kNN only to define subsamples and k𝑘kNN is not required to make asymptotically consistent predictions. Therefore, we assume that k𝑘k goes to infinity at the same rate as the sample size.

Assumption 2.

k/n→C→𝑘𝑛𝐶k/n\to C as n→∞→𝑛n\to\infty where C∈(0,1)𝐶01C\in(0,1).

Assumption 2 implies that the estimated distribution parameters should converge true distributions fast. Under some parametric assumptions and assumption 1, the estimated distributions could converge at rate Op​(n−1/2)subscript𝑂𝑝superscript𝑛12O_{p}(n^{-1/2}) (such as MVN).

The selection of k𝑘k is a crucial decision for the analyst. The larger the k𝑘k becomes, assumption 2 becomes stronger and efficiency decreases. Furthermore, large k𝑘k makes the synthetic sample biased towards the sample mean (or cluster means).999Such bias could be desirable for privacy concern because the bias towards sample mean, in a way, trims outliers. The analysts often trim outliers before creating synthetic data to minimize the disclosure risk. Consider the following scenarios:

3.2 Discrete and Censored Values

Arguably, the biggest challenge in estimating a multivariate distribution is handling the mixed type variables, e.g., continuous and discrete variables (Onken \BBA Panzeri, \APACyear2016; Ziegler \BBA Rush, \APACyear2019; Tran \BOthers., \APACyear2019; Kamthe \BOthers., \APACyear2021). Although it is a computationally heavy burden, it is possible to estimate a mixed variable type copula and draw values from the estimated distributions.101010Some software packages are readily available to estimate mixed copula, e.g., a Python package named mixedvines estimate copulas with both discrete and continuous marginal distributions (Onken \BBA Panzeri, \APACyear2016)

Indeed, such practice would lead to heavy tails in the synthetic sample distribution.

3.3 Missing Value Imputation

Most real data sets contain missing values. The proposed method does not naturally handle missing values. The missing values must be imputed beforehand. However, the analyst may require missing values in the synthetic data as in the real data. In that case, missing values can be imputed, for example, with the MICE algorithm (Van Buuren \BBA Oudshoorn, \APACyear1999), and the missingness propensities of each variable can be converted into variables. The missingness propensity scores, then, can be treated as another variable in the 𝒙isubscript𝒙𝑖\bm{x}_{i} vector, and they can be synthesized. From the synthesized missingness indicators, we can stochastically generate missing values.111111This methodology is efficiently implemented by an R package named synthpop (Nowok \BOthers., \APACyear2016).

3.4 Identical Neighbors

The analyst can encounter cases where k𝑘k neighbors are identical for some discrete variables. Hence, estimating zero-variance distributions will be redundant. Nevertheless, this is not necessarily a concern if k𝑘k is large “enough”. If there are identical neighbor variables, then the synthetic values will be created from a stratified subsample. Stratification of sample is a common practice in creating synthetic data (e.g., Nowok \BOthers. (\APACyear2016)).121212Also, having identical k𝑘k observations would imply that there is no disclosure risk.

5 Application

We replicate a real data set that contains more than three variables. We will, then, compare regression models estimated with the original and synthetic samples. The data set has median house values in California districts generated from the 1990 US Census along with other explanatory variables such as median income, house age, the average number of rooms/bedrooms, population, and the average number of household members (Pace \BBA Barry, \APACyear1997).

The data set contains 20,640 observations. We set k=15𝑘15k=15 and estimate multivariate normal distribution for each subsample as in the previous section. Table 1 shows the descriptive statistics of the original and the synthetic samples. The table also highlights one of the drawbacks of LR method. The variance of the synthetic samples is smaller than the original sample because LR tends to be biased toward the sample mean. Subsamples are defined with k𝑘kNN, hence some observations appear more frequently than others as a “neighbor”. The observations that are more frequently used as a neighbor are (expectedly) closer to the sample mean (or to the cluster means). This problem can be mitigated by defining subsamples with a different method such as unsupervised clustering methods; by weighting the observations depending on their frequency of use as neighbors141414The observations will have smaller weights if they appear more frequently, vice versa.; or by allowing k𝑘k to vary based on the distance metrics.

Lastly, we compare LR with other methods to create synthetic data. We will generate synthetic samples using SDV software (Patki \BOthers., \APACyear2016) and using synthpop (Nowok \BOthers., \APACyear2016). Both methods are extensively used in the literature. We use a preset model named as “FAST_ML” in SDV and use Classification and Regression Trees (CART) model with synthpop.151515CART is one of the most popular choices both for imputing missing values (e.g., Stekhoven \BBA Bühlmann (\APACyear2012)) and generating synthetic data (e.g., Drechsler \BBA Reiter (\APACyear2011)) due to its robustness and flexibility. Note that both SDV and synthpop have been developed and optimized over the years. Nevertheless, LR can achieve similar and arguably better results with minimal model tuning.

Figure 4 shows a scatter plots for some variables and table 2 shows the regression coefficients estimated with the original and synthetic samples. LR and synthpop contestably produce visually more accurate synthetic samples than FAST_ML. Furthermore, we compare the Kolmogorov-Smirnov Distance statistic for each variable (Massey Jr, \APACyear1951).161616The distance metric ranges from 0 to 1. Smaller distance implies that the reference and test samples have similar cumulative distribution functions. The average distance is approximately 2% for LR, 17% for FAST_ML, and less than 1% for synthpop. While synthpop performs best in terms of Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance, the regression results do not reveal a clear winner.171717Note that most coefficients are statistically different from the original sample estimations for all methods. Surprisingly, we see a similar pattern for all methods, in terms of divergence from the original sample estimations.

However, LR outperforms both methods in replicating non-convex distributions simulated in the previous section. The synthpop performs better than FAST_ML but worse than LR in synthesizing non-convex distributions. Figures 5 and 6 in the appendix show the distributions of the synthpop-created synthetic samples from the first and the second simulation, respectively.

6 Conclusion

This paper proposes an approach using k𝑘kNN to create synthetic samples, called Local Resampler (LR). LR uses k𝑘kNN to define subsamples and estimates distributions for each subsample sequentially. The synthetic values are drawn from the locally estimated distributions. The proposed method allows using both parametric and nonparametric distributions.

LR requires only two hyperparameter selections: (1) k𝑘k and (2) distribution (to be estimated). k𝑘k asymptotically goes to infinity at the same rate as n𝑛n, it is also allowed to vary for each subsample though this paper uses fixed value. The second assumption is not a primary concern if k𝑘k is chosen small enough181818It depends on the sample size, and there is no rule-of-thumb value.. Although LR can be further extended and optimized, LR with multivariate normal distribution replicates real samples very efficiently.
