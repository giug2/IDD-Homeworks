Abstract

Federated Learning (FL) is a collaborative learning performed by a moderator that protects data privacy. Existing cross-silo FL solutions seldom address the absence of participating clients during training which can seriously degrade model performances, particularly for unbalanced and non-IID client data. We address this issue by generating secure data digests from the raw data and using them to guide model training at the FL moderator. The proposed FL with data digest (FedDig) framework can tolerate unexpected client absence while preserving data privacy. This is achieved by de-identifying digests by mixing and perturbing the encoded features of the raw data in the feature space. The feature perturbing is performed following the Laplace mechanism of Differential Privacy. We evaluate FedDig on EMNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 datasets. The results consistently outperform three baseline algorithms (FedAvg, FedProx, and FedNova) by large margins in multiple client absence scenarios.

I Introduction

Federated Learning (FL) has shown great success in multi-modal collaborative learning via distributed training with a central orchestration [1] that addresses many privacy and data sharing issues [2, 1]. Existing FL methods mostly assume a collaborative setting among clients and can tolerate temporary client disconnection from the moderator [2]. In practice, however, extended data absence or client departure can happen due to business competitions or other non-technical reasons. Performance degradation caused by client absence can be severe in the cross-silo FL scenario, especially when the client data are unbalanced, skewed, or non-independent-and-identically-distributed (non-IID) [3]. These scenarios can occur commonly in the real-world applications of FL, as the collaborative entities (companies or production lines) are usually scarce and hold distinctive data.

Another issue arises when the moderator needs to fine-tune or evaluate a released model on a new consumer side after the main FL training phase is completed. As private client data are not accessible by the moderator, the representative data become inaccessible when the clients are not available, resulting in largely biased FL updates and evaluation results. We will describe this scenario in further detail in section III.

A naive solution of memorizing gradients at the FL moderator during training is not suitable, as the gradients become unrepresentative as iterations progress. We hypothesize that a privacy-preserving data memorizing and sharing mechanism is required to handle the cross-silo FL scenario effectively. Without violating the principle of FL— the client’s raw data should not be shareable, the sharing between the clients and the FL moderator is bound by an additional requirement that the identity of data samples should not be revealed during the data memorizing and sharing process.

We propose the Federated Learning using Data Digests (FedDig) framework to address the aforementioned data absence problem by synthesizing representative updates for the absent data at the moderator. Figure 1 shows an overview of the FedDig framework. It includes: (1) a method to generate data digests to represent the private client data, in which the probability for privacy leakage or recovery of raw samples is low enough for practical use, and (2) a method to synthesize trainable samples from the digests to mitigate the FL performance drop with client absence. We name the synthesized samples the training guidance that is generated from the digests. Specifically, each client uses a fixed digest producer combined with a feature mixing (mixup) and perturbing method to generate privacy-preserving digests. Combining feature mixing and perturbing can effectively increase the difficulty to recover the raw data from the digests under adversarial attacks. The proposed feature perturbing method greatly reduces the privacy concerns of our previous work [4]. During training, the FL moderator will learn a suitable sample generator that synthesizes trainable guidance from the digests. The synthesized samples can be used for the model updates in case any client data are absent.

Our framework is end-to-end trainable to ensure that our models can learn to synthesize training guidance from the digests (see Figure 2). FedDig can work hand-in-hand with many existing FL algorithms. The design is orthogonal to them and has the potential to support new FL gradient aggregation algorithms against client absence. The proposed data digest design can also be applied to non-FL applications, such as using digests for model tuning or deployment.

We note that the definition of privacy in machine learning is subject to legislation and conditions [5]. To this end, we let the users control the trade-off between the desired security level and FL model performance, by considering both privacy mitigation approaches of Differential Privacy (DP) [6] and feature mixing [7]. We believe that the proposed framework can effectively address the FL robustness as well as privacy and deployment issues in real-world applications.

The contributions of this paper are in the following:

  • •

    We propose a robust FL framework named FedDig to handle various client/data absence scenarios in non-IID settings using secure data digests.

  • •

    FedDig can effectively trade-off between the desired privacy mitigation and FL model performance.

  • •

    Our experiments show that raw data cannot be recovered from digests under malicious adversarial attacks, even if the feature producer is revealed or reverse-engineered.

II Related Work

Federated Learning (FL) provides solutions to address critical issues including data security, privacy, access rights, and heterogeneous data training for distributed and collaborative learning. A FL moderating server aggregates the training updates in terms of model gradients from individual clients and merge them wisely, where various data privacy and security issues can be addressed in a variety of settings [8].

Effective model aggregation for FL training with non-IID client data distribution has been extensively studied. FedAvg [9] is a simple but perhaps the most widely used algorithm by aggregating local client updates via a weighted average. However, non-IID data harm FedAvg in leading to inferior performance and making convergence analysis harder [10]. FedProx [11] addresses this issue by adding an ℓ2subscriptℓ2\ell_{2} regularization term with a new hyper-parameter to restrict aggressive local client updates. FedNova [12] modifies the aggregation of FedAvg by normalizing the update w.r.t. the number of training steps in each client. The Scaffold method [13] estimates the gradient update direction and then uses it to tune the direction of local updates to avoid client drifts. FedDyn [14] adds a regularization term in local training based on a global model and the local model from the previous iteration. In FedBN [15], BN layers are updated locally without uploading to the moderator. In [16], a monitoring scheme infers the composition of training data in each FL training round, and the impact of data imbalance is mitigated using Ratio Loss. In [17], representations learned by the global and local models in both the current and previous rounds are compared to correct the local training. The experience-driven Q-learning of FAVOR [18] intelligently controls the client devices in their participation of the FL rounds, such that bias introduced by non-IID data is counterbalanced and training convergence can speeds up.

FL vulnerabilities. The FL model parameter sharing and the increased training iterations and communications expose the federated environment to a new set of risks. Hacking and adversarial attacks can unveil FL vulnerabilities such as model manipulation and the leaking of sensitive user data. Numerous works address the FL training with adversarial clients [19], insufficient client computational or communication bandwidth [20], and irresponsible or non-contributing clients with malicious intents that exploit the vulnerability of FL in adversarial or free-riding attacks [21].

Client absence in FL. Most methods surveyed in [19] identify client leaves and thus adaptively re-weight client gradients in the aggregation accordingly. Such passive approaches cannot address long-term client absence. DeepSA [19] deals with aggressive adversarial clients by secure aggregation. FedCCEA [22] uses the client’s data quality to estimate the client contribution and removes the least contributors to test the estimation. To the best of our knowledge, there is barely any literature addressing the leaving of FL clients for a long duration, which can well occur in practice and seriously impact FL training. In this paper, we present a practical solution to tackle this very issue.

Feature mixing for privacy protection. In the literature, training data privacy is preserved by image mixing, data distillation, and augmentation [7]; see works of InstaHide [23], DataMix [24], XORMixUp [25], and FedMix [26]. The core idea of XORMixUp is to collect encoded samples from other clients, then decode them to provide synthetic samples until an IID set is obtained for model training. Both the encoding and decoding procedures follow bit-wise XOR to ensure data privacy. The FedMix framework merges multiple raw training data at each client by mixup. Then, each client sends and receives mixed raw data to and from the other participants, respectively, against the non-IID data setting. However, sending averaged data may cause privacy leakage but the authors did not provide in-depth privacy definition nor discuss privacy-related issues.

Differential Privacy (DP) [27] is a widely used privacy-preserving technique with mathematical guarantees, where data privacy can be preserved by adding noise to sensitive attributes during training.

We note that privacy infringement is subject to the user or subject involved. For corporate data, privacy infringement is usually up to legal discretion e.g. whether business secrets are leaking or not. Those definitions are case-by-case; a rigorous definition across disciplines is still lacking at this time.

The proposed FedDig framework ensures data privacy by leveraging both DP and feature mixing. Raw client data are kept locally, and only data digests in the form of perturbed and mixed low-dimensional representations are sent out. Our method ensures that each sample is accessed only once during feature averaging and mixing, such that statistical adversarial attacks cannot reveal sufficient information to identify the original samples. FedDig can address FL client absence issues in a principled way.

III Preliminary: The Data Absence Issues

We target the data absence in the cross-silo federated learning scenario with non-IID data distribution. We first conduct a pilot experiment to show how client data absence can impact FL model training. This pilot experiment is conducted in a severe non-IID setting to show the impact on CIFAR-10 classification performance. The training data is distributed by the Dirichlet distribution with μ=0.1𝜇0.1\mu=0.1 for 4 clients C1 to C4, each containing highly skewed non-IID distribution among image classes. During the training, the largest client (C2) who contains the largest training sample size leaves the FL training at iteration 50, and then rejoins the training at iteration 100. The original CIFAR-10 training data are divided into three subsets that contain 80%, 10%, and 10% of samples, respectively, where the first two subsets are used as training and validation sets for FL clients, and the last subset is used to monitor the convergence of the server model. The original CIFAR-10 test set is used to evaluate the resulting FL model performance. The evaluation metric is classification accuracy. Figure 3 shows the results of this preliminary experiment. Figure 3(a) shows the overall testing accuracy evaluated on the server model on three common FL algorithms. We observe instant accuracy drops after client absence, which shows that the server model rapidly forgets the learned knowledge after a few FL updates. Besides, a temporal data absence causes permanent accuracy degradation in general. Figure 3(b) shows the accuracy of the server model trained with FedAvg on the validation set held by each client. The leave of C2 directly decreases the accuracy of the validation set held by C2, while the accuracy of the set held by the other clients C0, C1, and C3 increases in this non-IID setting. However, the overall testing accuracy still drops. This preliminary experiment shows that client absence for a long period of time can seriously degrade FL training.

IV FedDig Framework

Our solution to deal with data absence during cross-silo FL is to extract sufficient information contained in the raw data as data digests, an alternative data representation with low privacy concern. On top of the feature extraction and encoding of raw data into the digests, data privacy can be further guarded by feature mixing and Differential Privacy noise perturbing during the digest generation process. Then, the digests from each client are sent and stored at the FL moderator. Once the moderator detected any client absence, the moderator generates synthesized samples named training guidance G𝐺G by decoding the digests to replace the missing data. Then, the moderator can continue model training as if the all clients are available.

In the proposed FedDig framework (Figures 1 and 2), we use two sub-networks, namely, a fixed feature producer PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} and a trainable guidance producer PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} with an additional process to jointly train PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} and server model M𝑀\mathcal{}M. This way, both the PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} and M𝑀\mathcal{}M are learnable to better generate suitable G𝐺G from D𝐷D. Again, during the digest production, we can tune feature mixing and perturbation to trade-off between model performance and privacy protection. In addition to these components, FedDig training operates based on the standard FedAvg [9, 10], with a small computation overhead of digest/guidance production and transmission. The feature producer PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} and client model Misuperscript𝑀𝑖{\mathcal{}M}^{i} belong to each FL client. The guidance producer PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} is protected at the moderator, as the sharing of PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} may introduce unnecessary vulnerability from adversarial attacks. Also, protecting PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} can avoid undesired access from unauthorized clients. Our design enforces identical architectures for M𝑀{\mathcal{}M} and Misuperscript𝑀𝑖{\mathcal{}M}^{i}, with the only difference of the inputs. When the client is available, its raw data are used to generate features. Whenever clients become absent, the moderator will take the digests and produce the guidance for the clients to synthesize the gradient, as shown in Figure 2. This way, FedDig training does not interrupt whether clients are available or not. Nevertheless, the design of digests and guidance is versatile and adaptable to many existing FL algorithms in various applications.

Privacy of the digests and the training guidance. The potential privacy issue of FedDig is the recoverability of the raw client data by reverse-engineering the digests or hacking the moderator to obtain the guidance. The privacy of the digests is highly demanded in avoiding infringement, as the digests will be transmitted outside the clients and stored at the moderator. Challenges for producing such privacy-preserving digests include: (1) the digests should contain sufficient information of the raw data for use in model training; (2) the definition of privacy infringement is subjective to conditions and tasks, and (3) private information should not be recoverable under malicious attacks. To the best of our knowledge, there exists no general and effective privacy infringement measurement; otherwise, such measurement can be directly leveraged to optimize and design a general privacy-preserving model.

In this paper, we target FedDig at the classification problem, but there is no limitation on extending the data digest idea to other machine learning problems. For the definition of privacy, we focus on protecting the intra-class, per-sample privacy (distinguishing samples within a class), rather than inter-class identification (distinguishing samples from two or more classes). This privacy definition is suitable for the classification problem, as removing class identifications from the digests will make them useless for classification training.

IV-C The FedDig Training Framework

The FedDig training framework is based on the FedAvg training and Differential Privacy with two new designs: (1) the computation and mixing of data digests at the client side and the generation of training guidance at the moderator side, and (2) a set of loss functions that utilize the data digests to jointly update the guidance producer PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} and the server model. The client and server models in FedDig. Figure 2(a) shows the client model that takes a raw data sample and an unmixed feature r𝑟r as input and predicts a hard classification label for this sample. V Experimental Results

We evaluate FedDig using standard datasets from image classification including EMNIST ByClass [32], CIFAR-10 [33], and CIFAR-100 [33] to simulate and investigate the FL client absence scenarios when training deep neural network models. FedDig is compared against three popular FL algorithms, namely, FedAvg [10], FedProx [11], and FedNova [12], as these algorithms are widely used for FL with different client contribution estimations.

V-A Experiment Settings

All experiments are conducted on a Linux server with six Intel® Xeon® CPU E5-2690 v4 2.60GHz, 115GB RAM, and a V100 GPU. We implement algorithms with PyTorch 1.10.1+cu102. The FL clients are trained sequentially due to hardware limitations. All client models are empirically trained with 300 iterations to ensure the convergence of the server model without early-stop strategies. For each iteration, each client model is trained using its raw data for 1 epoch. The batch size is set to 256 and 32 for the EMNIST and CIFAR-10/100 datasets, respectively. The learning rate is set to 0.001. Stochastic Gradient Descent with 0.9 momentum is used. The S​p​D𝑆𝑝𝐷SpD defaults to 4. The size of training samples S𝑆S is set to 20,000 and 10,000 for the EMNIST and CIFAR-10/100, respectively. The split of the training/validation/test set is the same as the pilot experiment in §§\SIII. The training samples are dispatched to clients following Dirichlet distribution with parameter ε=0.005𝜀0.005\varepsilon=0.005, a commonly used strategy to simulate a real-world non-IID client data distribution [34, 35]. The data skewness parameter μ𝜇\mu is set to 0.1 if not specially mentioned. The evaluation metric is the average test accuracy. Figure 5 shows the detailed network architectures.

V-B Impact of Client Leaving and Data Distribution

We conducted experiments to compare FL algorithms in highly-skewed, unbalanced, and non-IID scenarios to investigate the effectiveness of FedDig. Specifically, clients 0, 1, 2, and 3 own 34.1%, 23.6%, 23.8%, and 18.5% of unbalanced training samples, respectively. We first report the average test accuracy without client absence. The FedAvg, FedProx, FedNova, FedDig, and centralized training achieve 85% (1.0%), 85% (0.6%), 85% (1.0%), 84% (0.3%), and 87% (0.02%) average test accuracy (standard deviation), respectively, from five repeated experiments with different seeds.

We target four client absence scenarios on the EMNIST experiments: (1) the largest client (who owns the largest amount of samples) leaves temporarily; (2) the largest client leaves forever; (3) all clients leave sequentially; and (4) two groups of clients join the training at different time. The resulting test accuracy is shown in Figure 6 at each iteration to illustrate the impact of client absence.

Figure 6(a) shows the temporary client leaving scenario. The largest client (C0) leaves at iteration 100 and rejoins training at iteration 200. Observe that the FedDig test accuracy curve is almost unaltered, while the curves of the other baseline models drop significantly as C0 leaves. This is because the server models trained by the baseline algorithms suffer from catastrophic knowledge forgetting due to the non-IID training samples after the training data absence.

Figure 6(b) shows the result of the permanent leaving of a single client. Figure 6(c) shows a worse scenario of the permanent client leaving in sequence until all clients are absent. In both scenarios, the FedDig server models consistently achieve stable test accuracy as if all clients remain online. This show the efficacy of FedDig using the data digests and training guidance to handle client absence.

Figure 6(d) shows an interesting group training scenario, where only C0 and C1 join the FL training process in the beginning. Then, the two clients leave at iteration 100. Immediately, the second group of clients C2 and C3 join the training. During the first 100 iterations, all algorithms perform poorly on the test set due to the lack of training samples for a certain classes. All algorithms achieve only about 73% test accuracy. Once the second group of clients joins the training, the accuracy of FedDig boosts largely thanks to the stored digests and the training guidance, which push the server model training as if C0 and C1 are still available. In comparison, for the baseline algorithms, the training of the server model suffers from serious data absence. The improvement after C2 and C3 join the training is limited due to the high skewness of non-IID client data distribution.

We next investigate the effectiveness of FedDig in handling different levels of data distribution. The skewness parameter μ𝜇\mu of Dirichlet distribution is set to 0.10.10.1, 0.50.50.5, and 1.01.01.0. Figure 7(a) shows the average test accuracy on the sequential leaving scenario of five repeated experiments, where the error bar represents the standard deviation from the experiments. We calculate the average test accuracy from iteration 251 to 255 after all clients leave at iteration 250. We observe a stable test accuracy of FedDig. In comparison, the other base algorithms suffer from different levels of accuracy degradation as the label skewness increases. FedDig outperforms all baseline algorithms by a large margin when μ=0.1𝜇0.1\mu=0.1.

V-C Design Trade-off for Privacy-Preserving Digests

We conduct a set of experiments to comprehensively investigate the trade-off between the level of privacy infringement and the parameters of digest feature mixing. Starting from this section, all experiments are conducted on the sequential leaving scenario, where the S​p​D𝑆𝑝𝐷SpD is set to four, and the Laplace mechanism is disabled to highlight the performance impact and produce better visual observations, if not specifically mentioned.

DP Laplace mechanism vs. Feature mixing. We investigate the performance impact caused by two feature perturbations, DP Laplace Mechanism and Feature Mixing. We have made our best effort in selecting the best settings for both methods in balancing the resulting model performance and privacy protection in this experiment. Results in Figure 7(b) show that the DP-only perturbation achieves better test accuracy. However, high model performance comes with inferior privacy protection when ε𝜀\varepsilon is small. How to select a suitable ε𝜀\varepsilon value that can guarantee data privacy protection in practice is still an open question for all DP-based methods. The rightmost bar in Figure 7(b) shows the accuracy of the combination of feature mixing and DP. The accuracy does not show a significant drop when combining the two perturbing methods.

We further look into the visual appearance of the produced guidance, as shown in Figure 8. Observe that the raw data is visually recoverable by the guidance producer PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} in Figure 8(b), which indicates privacy leakage. Recovering raw data at the moderator is unacceptable due to privacy invasion. Setting a small ε𝜀\varepsilon value could mitigate the privacy issue, such as in Figure 8(c). However, such data noise could still be reduced by the noise removal algorithms and the characters will become recognizable. Selecting a suitable parameter for the noise-adding method is difficult because the parameter depends on the characteristic of the data and the application scenario.

In comparison, our experiments show that feature mixing can trade accuracy with better privacy protection over DP Laplace noise adding. Figure 9 shows the guidance of produced from feature mixing with S​p​D=4𝑆𝑝𝐷4SpD=4. Observe that the characters are barely identifiable or recognizable from the reconstructed guidance. In summary, this experiment shows that DP Laplace noise achieves better model performance with inferior privacy protection, while feature mixing comes with better privacy protection and inferior model performance. FedDig supports both DP and mixing perturbation methods, and Figure 9 2nd column shows the visual privacy protection from applying both perturbations.

Samples per Digest. We investigate the impact of S​p​D𝑆𝑝𝐷SpD values in FedDig. Figure 7(c) shows the accuracy scores. Larger S​p​D𝑆𝑝𝐷SpD leads to lower test accuracy, which is intuitive because the level of data perturbation increases when more features are mixed together. The S​p​D𝑆𝑝𝐷SpD value controls the privacy protection of the digest. In the trivial case of S​p​D=1𝑆𝑝𝐷1SpD=1, the digest is almost unprotected and raw data is recoverable by decoding, which is usable against client absence when privacy is not a concern. S​p​D=2𝑆𝑝𝐷2SpD=2 or 444 yields a good trade-off between privacy protection and model performance.

We next explore the effect of weight combination in feature mixing. Experiments are conducted with S​p​D=2𝑆𝑝𝐷2SpD=2 for better guidance visualization. Figure 10 shows the training guidance produced with weight pairs ranging from (1,0)10(1,0) to (0,1)01(0,1). For the mixing weights of (1,0)10(1,0) or (0,1)01(0,1), the produced guidance is equivalent to the result of S​p​D=1𝑆𝑝𝐷1SpD=1, which is essentially the raw data after encoding and decoding (with some perturbation caused by the dimension reduction in PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R}). When the weights become balanced, namely (0.5,0.5)0.50.5(0.5,0.5), the produced guidance is most distinct from either side of the raw data. As a result, such balance yields the best digest for privacy protection.

Random vs. within-class mixing. We investigate two feature mixing strategies: (1) mixup random features regardless of classes, and (2) mixup random features within each class (within-cls). Figure 7(d) shows the average test accuracy. The model performance difference between the two mixing methods is not large, but mixing within-cls could potentially cause more privacy leaks. Figure 8(d) shows the guidance produced by the within-cls mixing, where the characters are visually recognizable although the guidance may not be recovered w.r.t. a certain identifiable raw data. In contrast, random mixing produces much better digests in terms of data privacy protection. It is again a trade-off: the security protection of mixing across classes comes with a slight performance drop.

The number of clients. We investigate the impact of the number of clients in FedDig by increasing the number of clients from four to eight in this experiment. We randomly select two clients to sequentially leave the training at iterations 100, 150, 200, and 250. Figure 7(e) shows the result, where the impact on accuracy is not significant as the number of clients increases. Our interpretation is that the cross-silo FL performance depends less on the number of clients but more on the amount absent samples in the non-IID data distribution. Results from Figure 7(a) and (e) show that FedDig is robust and stable (with low standard deviation) against the percentages of intermittent absent data during training, and FedDig outperforms the other baselines by a large margin. There is no theoretical limit on the number of clients that FedDig can handle.

V-D Experiments on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100

FedDig is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 to verify its scalability and generalizability. Figure 11(a) and 11(b) show the mean and standard deviations of test accuracy from five repeated sequential leaving experiments.

In the experiment of CIFAR-10, the client and server models are configured similarly to the EMNIST experiments to prevent unnecessary bias across the network architectures. The kernel size in the first conv layer of FRsubscript𝐹𝑅F_{R} is 3×\times3×\times3. The result shows that FedDig outperforms the baselines by a large margin.

In the experiment of CIFAR-100, we select a deeper feature producer PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} to obtain highly refined features. Generally, raw data are harder to be recovered from such features, because information loss from a deeper network is larger. We use the network layers before the avgpool operation of a pre-trained MobileNetV2 model [36] as PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R}. This model achieves 88%percent8888\% and 676767% test accuracy on the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 datasets, respectively. Then, PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} is constructed with three deconvolutional layers with ReLU and sigmoid functions. We further test FedDig using a representative and an under-representative PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R}. We define the term representative by whether the PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} is pre-trained with the same dataset (representative) or not (under-representative). The Dig10 and Dig100 in Figure 11(b) indicate the cases where PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} is pre-trained on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, respectively. Figure 11(b) shows that FedDig still outperforms the baselines with under-representative PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} by a margin of 4%percent44\%. For the case of using representative PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R}, FedDig even outperforms the centralized training and the accuracy of the original pre-trained model on CIFAR-100, which shows the combination of the highly refined features and the raw images could potentially further improve the model performance. Figure 11(c) shows the test accuracy curves obtained along the training iterations. Representative digests can produce training guidance that better represents the absent samples (orange and red lines in Figure 11(c)).

Overall, our CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 experiments show the effectiveness of using data digests to handle client absence, even if an under-representative PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} is used for FL training.

V-E Malicious Attacks on Recovering Samples from Digests

We investigate to what extent the feature-mixing digests of FedDig can protect data privacy under malicious attacks. Recall that we use a fixed feature producer PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R}. We assume that at the beginning of FedDig training, the moderator must transmit PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} to all clients. Suppose an attacker monitors this transmission and hacks PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} to obtain its pseudo-inverse PR−1superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑅1{\mathcal{}P}_{R}^{-1} and tries to recover the raw data.

We simulate this attack on the EMNIST dataset by training autoencoders with the same structures of PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} and PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} described in Figure 5; and the CIFAR-10 dataset by the MobileNetV2-based PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} and the corresponding PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} used in the CIFAR-100 experiment described in §§\SV-D. Then, we use the encoder part as PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} and the decoder part as PR−1superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑅1{\mathcal{}P}_{R}^{-1}. After training the guidance producer PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} and the server model following the FedDig training process, we visualize the produced guidance and the results recovered by the PR−1superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑅1{\mathcal{}P}_{R}^{-1}.

Figure 9 and Figure 12 show examples of the obtained training guidance and the recovered results. Observe that the guidance G𝐺G and the recovered results are visually unrecognizable. In the EMNIST experiment, although the recovered results PR−1​(D)superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑅1𝐷{\mathcal{}P}_{R}^{-1}(D) reveal some patterns, individual raw data are far from identifiable from these pattern-like samples, thanks to the unrecoverable feature mixing. We further set the S​p​D𝑆𝑝𝐷SpD value to 111 to simulate a relatively vulnerable scenario compared with S​p​D=4𝑆𝑝𝐷4SpD=4. Figure 13 shows the results. In this scenario, we cannot successfully obtain a suitable PR−1superscriptsubscript𝑃𝑅1{\mathcal{}P}_{R}^{-1} that can recover meaningful recovered results from a highly refined feature because of the information loss caused by the deep model structure. Interestingly, the recovered results show different patterns that can be used to distinguish different classes. This result also suggests the effectiveness of adopting a deeper digest producer. Overall, recovering raw data from digests is difficult due to the permanent information loss caused by the feature perturbation and the steps passing through deep non-linear layers.

VI Conclusion

We present FedDig, a robust Federated Learning framework that handles data absence during FL training while preserving data privacy. The core idea is to store sufficient information for solving a task in the form of privacy-preserving digests, where the digests capture shareable information of the raw data and provide useful training guidance. We develop the client and server models of FedDig by adopting the encoder/decoder structure for low-dimensional feature extracting as well as enabling trainable guidance producer and the FL models, guided by a set of new loss functions. We evaluate FedDig on four client-leaving scenarios over three open datasets. Results show that FedDig outperforms FedAvg, FedProx, and FedNova in all test scenarios. We further investigate the potential privacy threats under malicious attacks that try to recover data from the digests. Results show that the proposed feature mixing and noise perturbation methods can provide strong protection under attack, such that reconstructed training guidance is not recognizable compared to the raw data.

Future Work. We believe FedDig represents a step forward for privacy-preserving cross-silo FL. With FedDig, the FL participant clients can join and leave the distributed training freely, without degrading the joint training performance. FedDig solves the issues of data absence on unbalanced data, which significantly affects the performance of the resulting ML models. The choice of PRsubscript𝑃𝑅{\mathcal{}P}_{R} and PGsubscript𝑃𝐺{\mathcal{}P}_{G} affects the model accuracy on heterogeneous datasets and client privacy, which deserve more research attention moving forward. Future work includes (1) performing FL on additional tasks such as object detection and segmentation, (2) the deployment of FedDig on real-world industrial FL applications, and (3) further investigating the opportunities to utilize the digest and the guidance, such as controlling the information contained in the digest by Shannon’s Entropy to further protect the privacy.
