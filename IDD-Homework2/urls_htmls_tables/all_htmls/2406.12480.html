Abstract

Stance detection holds great potential for enhancing the quality of online political discussions, as it has shown to be useful for summarizing discussions, detecting misinformation, and evaluating opinion distributions. Usually, transformer-based models are used directly for stance detection, which require large amounts of data. However, the broad range of debate questions in online political discussion creates a variety of possible scenarios that the model is faced with and thus makes data acquisition for model training difficult. In this work, we show how to leverage LLM-generated synthetic data to train and improve stance detection agents for online political discussions: (i) We generate synthetic data for specific debate questions by prompting a Mistral-7B model and show that fine-tuning with the generated synthetic data can substantially improve the performance of stance detection. (ii) We examine the impact of combining synthetic data with the most informative samples from an unlabelled dataset. First, we use the synthetic data to select the most informative samples, second, we combine both these samples and the synthetic data for fine-tuning. This approach reduces labelling effort and consistently surpasses the performance of the baseline model that is trained with fully labeled data. Overall, we show in comprehensive experiments that LLM-generated data greatly improves stance detection performance for online political discussions.

1 Introduction

With the recent advent of powerful generative Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT111https://chat.openai.com/, Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), investigating how synthetic data from LLMs can be leveraged for downstream tasks has become a very important and relevant topic of research. It is well known that Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks based on single-label or multi-label classification such as stance and sentiment detection benefit immensely from large amounts of labelled data (Mehrafarin et al., 2022). However, in many cases, labelled data is scarce, especially in the realm of online political discussions: one issue is that gathering labelled data may not be allowed for privacy reasons. Another issue is that especially in stance detection for online political discussions comments and their stances are intrinsically linked to a question. There are a potentially infinite number of specific questions open for debate for which different types of comments (and their stances) arise. This can be a problem, since the samples may not be varied or pertain to only one class, hence usually yielding an unbalanced dataset. For instance, performance drops severely for questions in the test dataset, that are not present in the training dataset (Vamvas and Sennrich, 2020).

An obvious approach to stance detection might be to apply LLMs directly, which has been tried with mixed results (see Section 2). Furthermore, LLMs are not yet fast enough at inference time for smaller real world interactive deployment in online discussions, unless adequate hardware infrastructure is provided. Also, using public hosting infrastructure may not be feasible when use cases require anonymity and privacy.

While LLMs might not be directly useful for stance detection, we show in this paper, that they can be used to generate synthetic training data for more specific (and smaller) NLP models, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), to create powerful stance detection for online political discussions that potentially work for any type of question. How exactly synthetic data from an LLM should be combined with a small pool of unlabelled training data will be investigated along the following questions:

Question 1 (Q1): Does fine-tuning with synthetic data improve the stance detection model? Answer: Yes, and the more the better!

One problem of stance detection datasets for online political discussions is the lack of (balanced) data for specific questions (sometimes also called issues). While fine-tuning with synthetic data is possible for BERT-based models, it was previously found to be challenging (Mahmoudi et al., 2024). Using more powerful LLMs, we can show that fine-tuning with synthetic data does improve stance detection performance in online political discussions. This also holds when augmenting existing labelled data.

Question 2 (Q2): Can the labelling effort be reduced by controlling what example to manually label using active learning with synthetic data? Answer: Yes, even if we don’t have real true labels initially.

Another challenge in stance detection for online political discussions is labelling data, which is expensive and time consuming. Active learning can help us to identify the most informative samples that are then labelled manually, even if there are initially no labelled samples. For this we use the synthetic samples which have labels by construction, to select the most ambiguous (and thus most informative) samples in the unlabelled dataset.

Question (Q3): Can fine-tuning using the most informative samples plus the synthetic data outperform fine-tuning with a completely manually labelled dataset? Answer: Yes, it can!

Our study reveals that the most effective way to improve stance detection performance is to combine both synthetic data and the most informative samples obtained via active learning. Surprisingly, this is even better than using a full dataset that has been completely manually labelled.

2 Background

Stance Detection for Online Political Discussions.

Stance detection is a sub-task of sentiment analysis (Romberg and Escher, 2023) and opinion mining (ALDayel and Magdy, 2021) that aims to automatically detect the stance (e.g., favor, against, or neutral) of the author of a contribution towards a discussed issue, or target. In online political discussions, stances can be defined as the side that the author is on, e.g., for or against an increase in value added tax. Research has identified stance detection as an important yet challenging task; it is important because the automated detection of stances can, e.g., improve discussion summarisation (Chowanda et al., 2017), facilitate the detection of misinformation (Hardalov et al., 2022), and provide more comprehensive evaluations of opinion distributions in online political discussions and participation processes (Romberg and Escher, 2023). Stance detection is also implemented as a feature in many recommender systems and discussion platforms (Küçük and Can, 2020). Still, due to its dependency on context, stance detection is a highly challenging task. In online political discussions, for example, detecting the stance of a contribution towards a question requires a proper identification of both the question under discussion and the position of the contributor towards it. However, users regularly deviate from the original question and discuss multiple topics in the same thread (Ziegele et al., 2014), resulting in a high number of potential questions with often very few instances for training purposes. Fine-tuning transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017) to solve stance detection is a common practice, but training these models requires a large amount of annotated data, which for the large variety of questions in online political discussions is unfeasible to acquire. We therefore in our work, show how to improve stance detection for online political discussions with synthetic data.

Active Learning.

The aim of active learning is to minimize the effort of labelling data, while simultaneously maximizing the model’s performance. This is achieved by selecting a query strategy that chooses the most interesting samples from a set of unlabelled data points, which we refer to as most informative samples. These samples are then passed to, e.g., a human annotator for labelling. The usual setup consists of iteratively training a model on a small subset of manually labelled data that stems from a pool of unlabelled data. There exist many different query strategies such as Query By Comittee (QBC, (Seung et al., 1992)), Minimum Expected Entropy (MEE, Holub et al. (2008) or Contrastive Active Learning (CAL, Margatina et al. (2021)). By actively choosing samples and asking for the correct labelling, the model is able to learn from few labelled data points, which is advantageous especially when annotated datasets are not available. Within the domain of political text analysis, many different tasks lack large amounts of annotated data. It has been already shown in the past that these tasks can benefit from the active learning: e.g., stance detection (Kucher et al., 2017), topic modeling (Romberg and Escher, 2022), speech act classification (Schmidt et al., 2023) or toxic comment classification (Miller et al., 2020). In this work, we examine how LLM-generated synthetic data can be used instead of real labelled data to select the most informative samples to be manually labelled.

Using LLM-generated synthetic data for fine tuning.

Recent work has shown that synthetic data generated from LLMs can be used to improve the performance of a model on downstream tasks. Møller et al. (2023) showed that synthetic data can be used to improve the performance of a model on downstream classification tasks by comparing the performance of a model finetuned on LLM-generated data to crowd annotated data. In many cases the model finetuned on LLM-generated data outperforms the model finetuned on crowd annotated data. Mahmoudi et al. (2024) study the use of synthetic data for data augmentation in stance-detection. The authors use GPT-3 to generate synthetic data for a specific topic with mixed results due to the inability of GPT-3 to generate good data samples. In our work,we use a newer LLM model, Mistral-7B, which generates better synthetic data samples and show that we can generate synthetic data that matches the real data distribution. Veselovsky et al. (2023) analyse in which ways synthetic data is best generated for tasks like sarcasm detection and sentiment analysis. The authors reach the conclusion that grounding the prompts to generate the synthetic data to real samples helps improve the quality of the synthetic data. Similarly, Li et al. (2023) argue that subjectivity in the classification task determines whether synthetic data can be used effectively.

Using LLMs directly for stance detection.

It has been shown that LLMs can be used directly for stance detection such as (Cruickshank and Xian Ng, 2023), (Burnham, 2023). However, the general conclusion of these studies is that while LLMs are competitive with other transformer models such as BERT, especially for edge cases, they exhibit replication issues. (Burnham, 2023) also discuss the posibility of pre-training models on more specific data to improve the generalisation capability of the model. We therefore focus on using LLMs to generate synthetic data to solve key challenges in stance detection such as the lack of available data for specific topics and labelling large amounts of data, rather than using LLMs directly for the task.

3 Method

In line with our declared contributions, we present our core ideas to improve the performance of the stance detection model: (i) To fine-tune the model with synthetic data, we first define the synthetic dataset and show our pipeline to generate it. The baseline model is then further fine-tuned on the synthetic data. (ii) We then present our synthetic data-based approach to identify the most informative samples from a batch of unlabelled data. We propose a synthetic extension to the QBC (Query by Commitee, (Seung et al., 1992)) method, where the synthetic data act as an ensemble of experts.

3.3 Synthetic Data-driven Query By Comittee (SQBC)

In the typical active learning scenario, we have a set of unlabelled data, where the goal is to choose the most informative samples to label. Our approach is inspired by the two methods Query by Comittee (QBC) (Seung et al., 1992) and Contrastive Active Learning (CAL, Margatina et al. (2021)): instead of using QBC’s ensemble of experts and the KL-divergence based information score in CAL, we directly use the synthetic data and its labels to produce classification scores for the unlabelled data using k𝑘k nearest neighbors. The most informative samples are then the data points with the most indecisive scores. Since we query a synthetic dataset instead of an ensemble of experts (as in QBC), we call our method Synthetic data-driven QBC or short SQBC.

4.1 Datasets

X-Stance dataset.

To evaluate our methods, we use the X-Stance dataset (Vamvas and Sennrich, 2020), a multilingual stance detection dataset, which contains a total of 67,0006700067,000 annotated comments on 194194194 policy-related questions, answered by Swiss election candidates. X-stance includes comments in German (48,6004860048,600), French (17,2001720017,200) and Italian (1,40014001,400). For this paper we only included the German comments, but as mentioned above, other languages can be easily included by automatic translation. The data has been extracted from smartvote, a Swiss voting advice platform. The comments are labelled either as being in favor (positive) or against (negative) the posed question, which is a typical labelling scheme for stance detection datasets. The X-Stance dataset is split in training and testing questions, i.e,. a question in the training dataset does not appear in the test dataset. Furthermore, for each question q𝑞q from the training data, there are several annotated comments, which form the dataset 𝒟train(q)superscriptsubscript𝒟train𝑞\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}^{(q)}. To limit computation time, we selected 10 questions from the test dataset to evaluate our method. The selection was done to best reflect the variability of the data (see Appendix D.1): we choose questions with small amount of comments, unbalanced comments and also balanced comments. Q1: Fine-tuning with synthetic data reaches on average the performance of the model with all true labels.

4.5 Interpreting the role of the synthetic data

The synthetic data is at the core of our work. To get a better understanding we analyse the role of synthetic data when using it for data augmentation and for the selection of the most informative samples. To study whether the synthetic data matches the real data, we visualize embeddings of the synthetic data together with the embeddings of the real world data using T-SNE projection (van der Maaten and Hinton, 2008) in Figures 5, and 6. To gain insights into the selection mechanism for different query strategies, we add the T-SNE embeddings of the selected samples in Figures 8 and 7. Again, we discuss our insights along the questions Q1, Q2 and Q3.

Q1: The synthetic data matches the distribution of the real world data.

We visualize the positive and negative synthetic data samples together with the means (cluster centers) of the real world data in Figures 5 and 6. We observe that the synthetic data extends the real world data, which we believe is the reason why fine-tuning with synthetic data is effective in online political discussions. Also, the larger the synthetic dataset size, the more the synthetic data matches the distribution of the real world data. Furthermore, the positive and negative samples are well separated, which we attribute to having pre-trained the BERT-model on 𝒟trainsubscript𝒟train\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}} of the X-Stance dataset, providing prior knowledge about the stance detection task.

Q2: SQBC selects samples that are not contained in the synthetic data distribution.

We visualize the selected samples of the SQBC and compare them to the selected samples by CAL and Random in Figures 7 and 8. We see that SQBC selects the unlabelled samples that are mostly in between the two classes of the synthetic data. This is the expected behaviour since we select the samples where the classification score is ambiguous. For random selection, the range of selected samples is broad: some similar samples between the two classes like SQBC are selected, but also within class samples that are not covered by the synthetic data set. This explains why random selection works well with a large synthetic dataset, since it further extends the decision boundary of the model. Finally, CAL selects samples similar to SQBC, but mostly tends to select samples from only one class, resulting in worse performance.

Q3: The synthetic data extends the unlabelled real data, while the most informative samples improve classification by selecting samples not well covered by the synthetic data.

Combining the insights from Q1 and Q2, we see that the synthetic data effectively extends the real world data thus providing a better estimate of the underlying distribution. When adding the most informative samples from the active learning methods, we add samples that are not in the more general synthetic data distribution thus extending the decision boundary of the classifier. We argue that this is why the combination of most informative samples and synthetic samples is so effective.

5 Discussion

Limitations and future work.

One limitation of our approach is the fact that we have to fine-tune a separate model for each question. While this leads to excellent results, a common approach is to fine-tune a single (and thus more general) model for several questions (like Baseline). Having observed that the synthetic data distribution differs (sometimes greatly) for each question, we feel that the extra training is justifiable and necessary. The computing time is not a concern in our case, since fine-tuning the BERT model takes less than a minute even with a synthetic dataset size of M=1000𝑀1000M=1000 on a reasonable GPU (NVIDIA A100). Future work, could study whether the underlying distribution of the synthetic data can serve as a proxy to combine real unlabelled data from different questions with similar (compatible) underlying distributions. This might enable us to train capable general stance detection models.

Conclusion.

In this work, we presented how to improve stance detection models for online political discussions utilizing LLM-generated synthetic data: (i) we showed that fine-tuning with synthetic data related to the question improves the performance of the stance detection model. After analysing the embeddings of the synthetic data, we attribute this to the LLM-generated synthetic data aligning well with the real data for the given question. (ii) We reduce the labelling effort by selecting the most informative samples informed by synthetic data with SQBC. The synthetic data are separated by construction into the positive and negative classes, which makes finding the most informative samples more straightforward, since we can count the labels of the k-nearest synthetic neighbours to determine classification ambiguity. (iii) Fine-tuning with synthetic data can be further enhanced by adding the most informative samples, outperforming the method that uses all true labels. Concluding, our findings demonstrate the potential of synthetic data to significantly improve the efficiency and effectiveness of stance detection in online political discussions.

Appendix A Broader Impact

Our work has the potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of stance detection in online political discussions. This can be beneficial for social media platforms, news outlets, and political campaigns, where the detection of stance can help to understand the sentiment of the users. The use of synthetic data in this case is not critical for the user, since the synthetic data is only used to improve the model’s performance. Stance detection could also be used to for bad purposes, e.g., to manipulate the public opinion. However, our work does not contribute to this, since we only focus on improving the model’s performance, i.e., the model’s ability to detect stance.

Appendix C Additional Experimental Details

C.1 Compute and Runtime

We conduct our experiments on a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU and a 32 core CPU. With this setup, for Mistral-7B, the generation of synthetic data takes approximately 333 hours per question for a synthetic dataset size of M=1000𝑀1000M=1000. Fine-tuning the BERT model with the synthetic data takes less than a minute. For the active learning methods, the selection of the most informative samples takes less than a minute. Hence the largest computational effort is the generation of the synthetic data.
