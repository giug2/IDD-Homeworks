3DArticCyclists: Generating Simulated Dynamic 3D Cyclists for Human-Object Interaction (HOI) and Autonomous Driving Applications

Eduardo R. Corral-Soto, Yang Liu, Tongtong Cao, Yuan Ren, Liu Bingbing All authors are with Huawei Noahâ€™s Ark Lab, Canada at the time of writing. {eduardo.corral.soto, yang.liu9, caotongtong, yuan.ren3, liu.bingbing }@huawei.com
Abstract

Human-object interaction (HOI) and human-scene interaction (HSI) are crucial for human-centric scene understanding applications in Embodied Artificial Intelligence (EAI), robotics, and augmented reality (AR). A common limitation faced in these research areas is the data scarcity problem: insufficient labeled human-scene object pairs on the input images, and limited interaction complexity and granularity between them. Recent HOI and HSI methods have addressed this issue by generating dynamic interactions with rigid objects. But more complex dynamic interactions such as a human rider pedaling an articulated bicycle have been unexplored. To address this limitation, and to enable research on complex dynamic human-articulated object interactions, in this paper we propose a method to generate simulated 3D dynamic cyclist assets and interactions. We designed a methodology for creating a new part-based multi-view articulated synthetic 3D bicycle dataset that we call 3DArticBikes that can be used to train NeRF and 3DGS-based 3D reconstruction methods. We then propose a 3DGS-based parametric bicycle composition model to assemble 8-DoF pose-controllable 3D bicycles. Finally, using dynamic information from cyclist videos, we build a complete synthetic dynamic 3D cyclist (rider pedaling a bicycle) by re-posing a selectable synthetic 3D person while automatically placing the rider onto one of our new articulated 3D bicycles using a proposed 3D Keypoint optimization-based Inverse Kinematics pose refinement. We present both, qualitative and quantitative results where we compare our generated cyclists against those from a recent stable diffusion-based method.

I Introduction

Human-object interaction (HOI) and human-scene interaction (HSI) are crucial for human-centric scene understanding applications in Embodied Artificial Intelligence (EAI) research, robotics, and augmented reality (AR). A common limitation faced in these research areas is the data scarcity problem: insufficient labeled human-scene object pairs on the input images, and limited interaction complexity and granularity between them. To overcome the data sparsity problem, recent methods have exploited Visual Language Models (VLM) Â [1], Â [2] and CLIP Â [3] to obtain semantic textual interaction descriptions from images that contain humans interacting with objects. Other methods address the data scarcity problem by creating new datasets for HOI. NeuralDome Â [4] introduced a multi-view dataset of humans interacting with 23232323 rigid objects such as chairs, tables, rackets, and cooking tools. Instant-NVR Â [5] uses monocular RGBD cameras and human/object tracking and key frame selection, coupled with neural radial fields to reconstruct and render interactions between humans and rigid objects such as toys, backpacks, and a â€œbalance carâ€. Iâ€™m HOI Â [6] uses a combination of RGB camera with object-mounted IMU to capture 3D motions of both the human and rigid objects such as skateboard, gym weights, baseball bats, frying pans and umbrellas. On the other hand, the Compositional Conditional Neural Radiance Field method from Â [7] and CG-HOI Â [8] generate animatable HOI interactions between humans and home objects such as chairs, tables, and boxes. PHYSCENE Â [9] presents a method to synthesize physically-plausible dynamic interaction scenes for Embodied AI, but focuses on objects moving within a scene rather than interactions at the human body-articulated object level. All these methods can generate interactions (possibly dynamic) between humans and rigid, non-articulated objects. However, more complex dynamic interactions between humans and articulated objects, such as a human rider pedaling a bicycle, have been left under-explored.

Our contributions: To address the 3D cyclist data scarcity problem, and to enable research on complex dynamic human-object interactions, in this paper we propose a method to generate simulated 3D dynamic cyclist objects and interactions. The following are our contributions: 1) We propose a methodology for creating a new part-based multi-view articulated synthetic 3D bicycle dataset that we call 3DArticBikes, which can be used to train NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)-based 3D reconstruction methods. 2) We propose a 3DGS-based parametric bicycle composition model to assemble pose-controllable (8-DoF) 3D bicycles (without a rider). 3) We generate complete synthetic animatable dynamic 3D cyclists (rider pedaling a bicycle) by re-posing a selectable 3D person following the dynamics of a cyclist video, while automatically placing the rider onto one of our new articulated bicycles using a proposed 3D Keypoint optimization-based Inverse Kinematics pose refinement. We present both, qualitative and quantitative results where we compare our generated cyclists against those from a recent stable-diffusion method.

II Related Work

3D Reconstruction and Rendering of Novel Views: NeRF-based Â [10] 3D Scene reconstruction methods can be roughly classified into Multi-view, which require either multiple synchronized real camera, motion capture (MoCap) images or synthetic 3D data, and Monocular, which operate on images captured by a single camera. The multi-view approaches can be further divided into methods that reconstruct stationary Scenes (e.g. buildings, background) Â [10], Nerfing-MVS Â [11], Â [12], and methods that reconstruct stationary rigid foreground objects: Neus Â [13], and S-NeRF Â [14]. NeRF-from-image Â [15], and Zero-1-to-3 Â [16], which are trained using multiple views rendered from rigid 3D objects. 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) Â [17]-based methods have gained popularity recently, mainly because of their faster training speed and their reasonably high reconstruction and rendering quality of rigid objects and static scenes. Some notable NeRF-based methods for dynamic, deformable 3D human body reconstruction are Pifu Â [18], NeuMan Â [19], HumaNeRFÂ [20], and SHERF Â [21]. Most of these methods use the Skinned Multi-Person Linear model (SMPL) Â [22] for human body representation and pose manipulation. HuGS Â [23] and GaussianAvatar Â [24] are recent 3DGS-based methods for 3D human body reconstruction and rendering, which are also based on SMPL. Typical datasets used to train these methods include THuman Â [25], ZJU MoCap Â [26], which images are captured using a rig or multiple synchronized cameras in a controlled environment indoor lab, and RenderPeople Â [27] which provides multi-view images of synthetic 3D persons.

Bicycle Reconstruction: The BIKED method Â [28] introduces a technique to represent bicycle features using PCA from bicycle models obtained from the BikeCAD website Â [29]. The technique can generate novel 2D bicycle shapes, but does not generate 3D bicycles. ShapeNet Â [30] contains around 60606060 3D bicycles but most of their shapes are either not realistic or have no colored texture. The CO3D dataset Â [31] includes a few rigid 3D bicycles and motorcycles reconstructed from real images, but the quality and number of of instances is insufficient for our project. Both, the AKB-48 Â [32] and Gapartnet Â [33] datasets include articulated 3D objects of indoor home and office object categories, such as Scissors, Eyeglasses, Book, Bottle, but do not include 3D models of articulated bicycles. GSO Â [34] includes 3D models of scanned real rigid objects, but it does not include bicycles. A limited number of commercial articulated dynamic cyclist 3D models are available for purchase from websites like Â [35], which come with specific pre-selected (and fixed) rider and bicycle models. As of the time of writing, we are not aware of the existence of any real-data multi-view motion-capture (MoCap) dataset for 3D dynamic bicycles or cyclists.

VIII Conclusions

We have presented a method to generate 8-DoF pose-controllable 3D dynamic bicycles and cyclists that can be used in human-object interaction and autonomous driving perception applications.
