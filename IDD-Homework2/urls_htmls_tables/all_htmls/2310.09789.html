Abstract.

Federated learning (FL) achieves great popularity in broad areas as a powerful interface to offer intelligent services to customers while maintaining data privacy. Nevertheless, FL faces communication and computation bottlenecks due to limited bandwidth and resource constraints of edge devices. To comprehensively address the bottlenecks, the technique of dropout is introduced, where resource-constrained edge devices are allowed to collaboratively train a subset of the global model parameters. However, dropout impedes the learning efficiency of FL under unbalanced local data distributions. As a result, FL requires more rounds to achieve appropriate accuracy, consuming more communication and computation resources.

In this paper, we present FLrce, an efficient FL framework with a relationship-based client selection and early-stopping strategy. FLrce accelerates the FL process by selecting clients with more significant effects, enabling the global model to converge to a high accuracy in fewer rounds. FLrce also leverages an early stopping mechanism to terminate FL in advance to save communication and computation resources. Experiment results show that FLrce increases the communication and computation efficiency by 6% to 73.9% and 20% to 79.5%, respectively, while maintaining competitive accuracy.

1. Introduction

In the era of big data, edge devices have garnered extensive utilization in efficiently capturing and retaining vast volumes of data at the periphery of a network. Owing to privacy concerns and limited network bandwidth, it is desired that edge devices process high-volume and privacy-sensitive data locally rather than upload them to a remote server (Li etÂ al., 2020b). To this end, federated learning (FL) emerges. FL is a distributed machine learning paradigm that allows edge devices to collaboratively train a global model without sharing any private data (McMahan etÂ al., 2017). In an FL process, devices (also named clients) learn a machine learning model locally from their personal datasets, and exchange the model parameters/gradients with the server. Compared with raw data, the communication overheads and the risk of data leakage for transmitting the parameters/gradients become smaller (McMahan etÂ al., 2017; Wang etÂ al., 2021; Zhang etÂ al., 2022b). This capability enables FL to train a universal global model that can provide high-quality services to end users in a wide range of privacy-sensitive applications, such as voice assisting (Leroy etÂ al., 2019), healthcare (Qayyum etÂ al., 2022; Guo etÂ al., 2022; Nguyen etÂ al., 2022) and Internet of Things (IoT) (Zhang etÂ al., 2022b).

Even though FL remarkably upholds data privacy, in real-world applications, the effectiveness of FL is compromised by the limited capacities of edge devices and unbalanced local data distributions. Due to the system heterogeneity of edge devices, a high variability exists in edge devicesâ€™ communication and computation resources, including bandwidth, processor, memory, and battery life (Imteaj etÂ al., 2022; Smith etÂ al., 2017; Li etÂ al., 2020b), presenting a critical challenge to address communication and computation bottlenecks in FL. First, tremendous communication costs can occur when large-scale deep learning (DL) models, such as large image processing models and language models, are transmitted across edge devices with limited bandwidths. Second, training a complex DL model on resource-constrained devices may result in a long latency, as these devices cannot complete the training in time due to deficient hardware support. Subsequently, the resource-constrained devices need more time to complete the local training, and extensive computation resources are consumed in the long-run training process, like compute units (HorvÃ¡th etÂ al., 2021) and energy (Imteaj etÂ al., 2022; Li etÂ al., 2021). To address this problem, we expect an efficient FL framework that can effectively reduce computation and communication costs while maintaining accuracy.

Data imbalance is another bottleneck in FL. In real life, edge devices are inherently distributed, generating non-identical independent (non-iid) data that may exhibit unbalanced characteristics and unequal training contributions [2]. On the one hand, some devices are important to FL, as their local data distributions are closer to the global distribution. On the other hand, some devices are non-important or even harmful to FL due to reasons like data poisoning, noise, or local biases. In this scenario, it becomes crucially important to select which devices to participate in the collaborative learning. Selecting the right devices will fully utilize the limited computation and communication resources and make the global model reach a high accuracy quickly while selecting unimportant devices may degrade the global modelâ€™s accuracy and waste resources (Li etÂ al., 2022; Chen etÂ al., 2022; Lai etÂ al., 2021).

Status Quo and Limitations. To comprehensively address the computation and communication bottlenecks, the dropout technique has been proposed. Dropout methods (Wen etÂ al., 2022; Caldas etÂ al., 2018; HorvÃ¡th etÂ al., 2021; Li etÂ al., 2021; Diao etÂ al., 2021) let resource-constrained devices train a â€sub-modelâ€ in local training, which is a subset of the global model parameters. During the upstream and downstream global communications, the resource-constrained devices communicate the sub-model parameters with the server as well. Given a drop rate, dropout methods extract a sub-model by pruning neurons in the global model. For example, studies in (Caldas etÂ al., 2018; Wen etÂ al., 2022) generate a sub-model by randomly pruning neurons from the global model. Studies in (HorvÃ¡th etÂ al., 2021; Diao etÂ al., 2021) fixedly prune the rightmost neurons in the global model. Study in (Li etÂ al., 2021) prunes the unimportant neurons, which are identified by adding a regularization term to a deviceâ€™s local objective function. Compared with the full global model, the complexities of training and transmitting a sub-model become smaller, enabling dropout to achieve computation and communication efficiency simultaneously.

However, dropout impedes the learning efficiency under local data imbalance. For one thing, dropout implicitly allocates sub-models to edge devices without considering data importance. When a sub-model is allocated to a device whose data are important to FL, the learning efficiency of FL will be compromised. To be straightforward, a common example is shown in Figure 1. Suppose two edge devices: a mobile phone and a laptop. The phone has fewer resources and has been used to take many photos, the laptop has more resources but stores very few images. In this case, the resource-constrained mobile phone owns the data that are important to FL, but dropout can only acquire limited update information from the data by allocating a sub-model to the phone. For another thing, most dropout methods use a random client selection strategy (Wen etÂ al., 2022; Caldas etÂ al., 2018; HorvÃ¡th etÂ al., 2021; Li etÂ al., 2021). If unfortunately, the non-important devices are selected too many times, the FL process can easily become slow.

With degraded learning efficiency, whether dropout methods can reduce the total resource consumption is yet to be explored. Even though dropout reduces the communication and computation costs in every single training round, it does not necessarily reduce the total resource consumption. This is because, compared with traditional full-model based training methods (e.g. FedAvg (McMahan etÂ al., 2017)), dropout methods may require more rounds to achieve a high accuracy owing to a deteriorating learning speed (Caldas etÂ al., 2018), and more communication and computation resources are required to support the additional rounds of training.

Challenges and solutions. In summary, within a heterogeneous edge network where devices train with unbalanced local datasets, dropout may degrade the learning efficiency of FL. Consequently, FL requires more rounds, and thus more communication and computation resources to train an accurate global model. To address this limitation, we expect an FL framework that can achieve communication and computation efficiency under data imbalance, meanwhile maintaining accuracy. We notice that: (1) Dropout degrades the learning efficiency by allocating sub-models to devices with the important data and a random client selection strategy; (2) With lower learning efficiency, dropout demands more training rounds and thus more communication and computation resources to train a global model with desirable accuracy; (3) Compared with dropout, training with a full model is less vulnerable to data imbalance but consumes more communication and computation resources in every single training round. Based on (1), (2) and (3), we raise the question: If we conduct full-model training on every device and design a client strategy to achieve high accuracy in fewer rounds, ending up with less communication and computation consumption overall, why do we even need dropout?

Solution Overview. Motivated by the question above, we propose a novel efficient FL framework â€“ FLrce (Federated Learning with relationship-based client selection and early stopping). The logical line of FLrce is presented in Figure 2. First, FLrce conducts full-model training on every edge device. This overcomes the vulnerability of dropout to real-world scenarios where local datasets are naturally unbalanced, such as an IoT network (Abeysekara etÂ al., 2021). Second, as full-model training causes higher consumption of communication and computation resources in each round, to reduce the overall consumption, FLrce utilizes an early stopping mechanism inspired by (Prechelt, 2002), which terminates FL in advance to reduce the rounds of training. We notice that when the global model â€oscillatesâ€ between edge devicesâ€™ local optima, it would be very hard for the model to reach the global optimal solution, making FL extremely inefficient. Accordingly, FLrce detects the oscillation between the devicesâ€™ parameter updates using cosine similarity, when a large degree of oscillation happens, i.e. over 50% of the update pairs have a negative cosine similarity, the early stopping mechanism will be triggered. Subsequently, the overall resource consumption can be significantly reduced, including computation resources (e.g. energy) and communication resources (e.g. bandwidth). Finally, to mitigate the accuracy loss emanating from fewer training rounds, inspired by (Li etÂ al., 2022), FLrce leverages an innovative client selection strategy that tends to select the devices that are more â€importantâ€ to the collaborative learning to improve the learning efficiency. The importance of a device is the sum of its relationship degree on other devices, where the relationship degree between two devices is computed by the cosine similarity or orthogonal distance between their parameter updates. The client selection strategy enables the global model to converge to high accuracy in fewer rounds, which greatly improves the efficiency of FLrce in resource-constrained application scenarios, such as a mobile edge network with limited communication and computation resources.

5. related work

Efficient Federated Learning. To curtail the excessive communication and computation costs in FL, substantial studies have been conducted. To the best of our knowledge, the prior art can be divided into three categories: (1) message compression, (2) accuracy relaxation, and (3) dropout.

Message compression methods reduce the communication cost by decreasing the volume of transmitted messages, including quantization methods (Alistarh etÂ al., 2017; Dettmers, 2016; Fu etÂ al., 2020; Wen etÂ al., 2017) and compression methods (Sattler etÂ al., 2020; Aji and Heafield, 2017; Dutta etÂ al., 2020). Quantization methods reduce the number of bits of transmitted messages to reduce bandwidth usage. For example, quantized SGD (Alistarh etÂ al., 2017) and TernGrad (Wen etÂ al., 2017) map the continuous elements of a gradient to discrete values without changing the statistical property. (Dettmers, 2016) replaces the 32-bit gradients with 8-bit approximations. Tinyscript (Fu etÂ al., 2020) proposes a distribution-aware quantization framework that minimizes the quantization variance of gradients to mitigate accuracy sacrifice. Compression methods let edge devices only communicate a subset of parameters or gradients with the server. In (Sattler etÂ al., 2020; Aji and Heafield, 2017), elements that are less than a pre-defined threshold are removed. In (Dutta etÂ al., 2020), a model is compressed in a layer-wise manner, within each layer, a certain percentage of neurons are removed before transmission. However, message compression does not address the computation bottleneck. On the worst case, deploying compression on the client side might bring additional computation overheads to resource-constrained devices, entailing extra consumption of computation resources.

Accuracy relaxation methods save computation resources by allocating simpler tasks to edge devices. Fedprox (Li etÂ al., 2020c) and Fedparl (Imteaj and Amini, 2021) allows resource-constrained devices to train a sub-optimal solution during local training, which requires less computation effort. PyramidFL (Li etÂ al., 2022) reduces the local epoch if a device cannot complete the training in time. However, the communication bottleneck is neglected in this kind of approach, as devices still communicate the full model architecture with the server.

Dropout simultaneously realizes communication and computation efficiency by allowing clients to train and transmit light-sized sub-models. There exist multiple ways to extract a sub-model from the original model. Federated dropout (Wen etÂ al., 2022; Caldas etÂ al., 2018) generates sub-models by randomly pruning neurons in the original neural network. Fjord (HorvÃ¡th etÂ al., 2021) and HeteroFL (Diao etÂ al., 2021) prune neurons in a fixed order. In Hermes (Li etÂ al., 2021), each client prunes the unimportant neurons personally, where the unimportant neurons are determined by a group-lasso-based regularization.

All methods above improve efficiency by reducing the consumption of computation and communication resources in every single iteration. FLrce, however, works from a different perspective. Utilizing an ES mechanism, FLrce reduces the overall resource computation resources by reducing the total rounds of training.

Federated Learning with Local Data Imbalance. Due to the intrinsic statistical heterogeneity of IoT devices, clients contribute unequally to the FL task. Some clients promote FL by contributing important parameter updates, while others may hinder FL by sending malicious or noisy updates. It has been well studied on how to overcome data imbalance and maintain performance. Existing works mainly adopt two ways. One way is to mitigate the negative effects of malicious updates in the aggregation phase. For example, FLtrust (Cao etÂ al., 2020) designs a trust mechanism that assigns higher weights to clients whose gradients are more consistent with the global update. RHFL (Fang and Ye, 2022) minimizes the negative impact of noisy datasets by increasing the weights of clients with larger training loss reduction. (Li etÂ al., 2020a) designs an anomaly-detection-based FL framework that can identify malicious updates through a variation auto-encoder (VAE). All updates are fed into the VAE before aggregation, and those with a large construction error will be considered malicious and removed from the aggregation. DS-FL (Itahara etÂ al., 2023) uses an entropy-reduction aggregation strategy to improve the modelâ€™s robustness against low-entropy logits from noisy clients.

Another way is to identify and discard malicious clients during the phase of client selection. Fed-CBS (Zhang etÂ al., 2023) invents a metric named Quadratic Class-Imbalance Degree (QCID) that measures the distance between clientsâ€™ local data distribution to an ideal uniform distribution. By selecting clients with the lowest QCID values, FedCBS can improve the modelâ€™s accuracy when the class distributions among clients are unbalanced. FLDetector (Zhang etÂ al., 2022a) detects malicious clients based on update inconsistency to defend FL against large-scale model poisoning attacks. Similarly, in FLrce, clients who hold the lowest relationship degrees with others are considered to be unimportant and are unlikely to be selected.

Very few of these works consider the communication and computation bottlenecks. Two exceptions are (Itahara etÂ al., 2023) and (Fang and Ye, 2022) which transmit light-sized logits to realize communication efficiency. Comparatively, FLrce simultaneously achieves computation and communication efficiency.

6. Conclusion

In this paper, we propose FLrce, an FL framework that comprehensively achieves overall communication and computation efficiency, meanwhile maintaining accuracy and convergence under local data imbalance. Experiment results show that FLrce maintains competitive accuracy under unbalanced data distribution and significantly improves communication and computation efficiency. In the future, we target enhancing FLrce with strict theoretical proof and improving efficiency from the perspective of every single client.
