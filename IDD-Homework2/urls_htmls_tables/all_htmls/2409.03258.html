Abstract

Although Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated potential in processing graphs, they struggle with comprehending graphical structure information through prompts of graph description sequences, especially as the graph size increases. We attribute this challenge to the uneven memory performance of LLMs across different positions in graph description sequences, known as â€œpositional biasesâ€. To address this, we propose GraphInsight, a novel framework aimed at improving LLMsâ€™ comprehension of both macro- and micro-level graphical information. GraphInsight is grounded in two key strategies: 1) placing critical graphical information in positions where LLMs exhibit stronger memory performance, and 2) investigating a lightweight external knowledge base for regions with weaker memory performance, inspired by retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). Moreover, GraphInsight explores integrating these two strategies into LLM agent processes for composite graph tasks that require multi-step reasoning. Extensive empirical studies on benchmarks with a wide range of evaluation tasks show that GraphInsight significantly outperforms all other graph description methods (e.g., prompting techniques and reordering strategies) in understanding graph structures of varying sizes.

2 Related Work

2.1 LLMsâ€™ Understanding of Graph Structures Through Description Sequences

Research on the ability of LLMs to understand graph structures by inputting natural language descriptions of graphs into these models has become an emerging area of study. Existing work has primarily focused on designing evaluation benchmarks for graph structure understanding tasks and analyzing the results. For example, NLGraph and GPT4Graph provided preliminary empirical assessments of LLMsâ€™ performance on coarse-grained graph structure tasks, indicating that this research is still in its early stages. Subsequently, benchmarks such as GraphEval2000 Wu etÂ al. (2024b), GraphArena Tang etÂ al. (2024), GraphInstruct Luo etÂ al. (2024), and GraCoRe Yuan etÂ al. (2024) have concentrated on fine-grained tasks, such as graph theory problems. Ge etÂ al. also explored the impact of the sequence of graph descriptions on LLMs comprehension, though their study was limited to a few tasks and lacked deep analysis. Unlike previous work, our framework aims to enhance LLMsâ€™ capabilities in graph structure understanding tasks.

2.2 Positional Bias in Long Sequences for LLMs

Extensive research Tang etÂ al. (2023); Hsieh etÂ al. (2024) has shown that the position of inputs and the order of answer choices can significantly impact LLMsâ€™ performance and output generation (i.e., â€œpositional biasâ€). Liu etÂ al. analyzed how LLMs utilize information in long input sequences and identified the â€œlost-in-the-middleâ€ problem. Additionally, Xiao etÂ al. discovered that attention scores tend to be biased towards initial tokens of input sequences due to the Softmax operation.

Some recent works have attempted to mitigate the positional bias issue in LLMs. For instance, Zhang etÂ al. and Hsieh etÂ al. introduced attention instructions to guide the LLMs to focus on specific sequence segments. An etÂ al. proposed a training solution using a synthesized long- sequence question-answer dataset to alleviate the â€œlost-in-the-middleâ€ problem. Additionally, Wu etÂ al. suggested interpolating positional encodings by manipulating position indices, a method that requires fine-tuning within the pre-trained context window. Note that these broad strategies are not specifically designed for graphs and do not directly apply to graph description sequences; instead, they are generally implemented during the training/fine-tuning phases of LLMs. Therefore, these methods are orthogonal to our framework and could further enhance its performance if computational resources permit.
