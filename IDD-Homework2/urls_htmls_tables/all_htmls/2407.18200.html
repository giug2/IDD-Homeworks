Abstract

This paper investigates federated learning (FL) in a multi-hop communication setup, such as in constellations with inter-satellite links. In this setup, part of the FL clients are responsible for forwarding other clientâ€™s results to the parameter server. Instead of using conventional routing, the communication efficiency can be improved significantly by using in-network model aggregation at each intermediate hop, known as incremental aggregation (IA). Prior works [1] have indicated diminishing gains for IA under gradient sparsification. Here we study this issue and propose several novel correlated sparsification methods for IA. Numerical results show that, for some of these algorithms, the full potential of IA is still available under sparsification without impairing convergence. We demonstrate a 15Ã—\times improvement in communication efficiency over conventional routing and a 11Ã—\times improvement over state-of-the-art (SoA) sparse IA.

I Introduction

Federated learning (FL) [2] is an instance of distributed machine learning (ML) over bandwidth-restricted communication networks, in which a large number of clients use the local sets and collaboratively train \pglsml model. This process is orchestrated by a central parameter server (PS) , which is responsible for synchronizing intermediate results among the clients. This involves collecting intermediate results {ğ’ˆkt}ksubscriptsuperscriptsubscriptğ’ˆğ‘˜ğ‘¡ğ‘˜\{\bm{g}_{k}^{t}\}_{k} from all clients, aggregating these values into a new global iterate ğ’˜t+1superscriptğ’˜ğ‘¡1\bm{w}^{t+1} of the ML model parameters, and distributing ğ’˜t+1superscriptğ’˜ğ‘¡1\bm{w}^{t+1} back to all clients. As contemporary ML models can consist of billions of parameters [3], communication efficiency in this synchronization phase is paramount.

Here, we are interested in FL over multi-hop (MH) networks, where some of the FL clients are responsible for forwarding other clientâ€™s results to the PS . Such a setup arises in the implementation of FL in satellite constellations [4, 5], when inter-satellite links are used for communicating with the PS [6, 1]. Similar setups are also applicable to FL in wireless mesh and multi-hop sensor networks [7]. It is shown in [6, 1, 7] that a network topology-aware implementation of FL , leveraging in-network computing, leads to a massive reduction in the communication load during the result-collection phase. However, it has been observed in [1] that the efficiency of this incremental aggregation (IA) procedure is reduced massively when combined with gradient sparsification methods such as Top-Qğ‘„Q [8, 9]. This is because individual gradient sparsification at each node leads to (almost) uncorrelated sparsification supports. The result is that the number of non-zero elements in the partial aggregate increases with each hop.

In this paper, we consider sparse IA and propose several approaches to the issues outlined above. After establishing the system model in SectionÂ II, we analyze the shortcomings of state-of-the-art (SoA) sparse IA and take two different angles towards improving it in SectionÂ III. Both methods rely on intentionally creating correlation in the sparsification procedure. In SectionÂ IV, we combine our methods with time correlated sparsification (TCS) [10] to further increase this correlation. The communication cost of the proposed algorithms is discussed in SectionÂ V, and evaluated numerically in SectionÂ VI.

IV Time-Correlated Sparse IA

In SectionÂ III, we improved upon AlgorithmÂ 1 in two different ways. AlgorithmÂ 2 has exactly the same communication cost as AlgorithmÂ 1, but utilizes the bandwidth more efficiently. VII Conclusions

We have considered efficient communication for FL in MH networks. The SoA approach for collecting intermediate FL results in such a system is IA [5, 7], which shows diminishing returns under gradient sparsification [1]. We have developed four novel algorithms to address this issue and evaluated their performance numerically. These results show a distinct advantage of AlgorithmÂ 3 over all other methods, both in terms of communication cost and FL performance. However, our results also show a minor gap in final test accuracy over the SoA . This will be subject to further investigation in future work, starting with a rigorous convergence analysis of AlgorithmÂ 3.
