Abstract

Automated persistent and fine-grained monitoring of orchards at the individual tree or fruit level helps maximize crop yield and optimize resources such as water, fertilizers, and pesticides while preventing agricultural waste. Towards this goal, we present a 4D spatio-temporal metric-semantic mapping method that fuses data from multiple sensors, including LiDAR, RGB camera, and IMU, to monitor the fruits in an orchard across their growth season. A LiDAR-RGB fusion module is designed for 3D fruit tracking and localization, which first segments fruits using a deep neural network and then tracks them using the Hungarian Assignment algorithm. Additionally, the 4D data association module aligns data from different growth stages into a common reference frame and tracks fruits spatio-temporally, providing information such as fruit counts, sizes, and positions. We demonstrate our method’s accuracy in 4D metric-semantic mapping using data collected from a real orchard under natural, uncontrolled conditions with seasonal variations. We achieve a 3.1 percent error in total fruit count estimation for over 1,790 fruits across 60 apple trees, along with accurate size estimation results with a mean error of 1.1 cm. The datasets, consisting of LiDAR, RGB, and IMU data of five fruit species captured across their growth seasons, along with corresponding ground truth data, will be made publicly available at: https://4d-metric-semantic-mapping.org/.

I Introduction

Automated persistent monitoring of orchards to assess the growth of fruits is crucial for farmers to optimize cultivation and yield. This involves obtaining actionable information regarding the present development of fruits and accordingly taking decisions such as using chemical thinning to prune under-developed fruits, managing irrigation levels, protecting against diseases, and optimizing labor requirements for harvesting. Therefore, taking timely and streamlined actions at each stage of fruit growth is essential for farmers, especially in the current age of rising agriculture costs and food shortage. Spatio-temporal mapping of an orchard can also help enhance plant phenotyping [4, 5] by monitoring important traits of plants and providing insights into how they change over the entire growth season. Hence, this motivates the problem of 4D orchard mapping, which in this context is defined as 3D orchard mapping with temporal information. The actionable information obtained from 4D orchard mapping is that each fruit is associated with a timestamp, and its state is monitored across its growth season.

To track fruit changes over time, an accurate measurement from a single time session is crucial for spatio-temporal data association. Most works focus on fruit tracking and counting during a single time session, like during the harvest season. [6, 7] used deep learning algorithms to directly count fruits from images and obtain a yield estimate. Our prior work [8] used a Fully Convolutional Network (FCN) [9] to segment fruits from video streams. Fruits were later tracked robustly using a combination of the Hungarian Assignment algorithm [10], Kalman Filter, optical flow tracking [11], and Structure for Motion (SfM) algorithm [12] for data association and outlier rejection. Building upon this work, our follow-up work [13] used the fruit instances as features to replace SIFT [14] features, which directly constructed a map of fruits and estimated the camera trajectory at a fraction of the computational cost.

While these works demonstrate impressive fruit-counting accuracy, they fall short of providing information for monitoring across multiple time sessions. To this end, we extend the 3D fruit detection and tracking problem to a 4D fruit monitoring problem where the fruits are detected, tracked, and monitored across the entire growth season.

The key component extending 3D mapping to 4D mapping is data association across time. In [15], Carlone et al. register the point clouds of crops via expectation-maximization. Dong et al. in [16] and Nived et al. in [17] both propose to extract 2D image features that are robust to seasonal variation for image registration. [18] combines both 2D and 3D visual features to find temporal correspondences. While these approaches successfully monitor changes in a plot of crops or individual plants, they lack the granularity needed in tracking fruit instances that are subject to occlusions from canopy and changes in position, size, and appearance. In more recent works, [19] and [20] propose to match the skeletons or keypoints extracted from high-precision point clouds of plants for growth tracking and phenotyping. As for application in fruits, [21] uses terrestrial laser scanners for colored and precise fruit point clouds, and a descriptor encoding both positions and neighboring fruit layout is designed for fruit matching. However, such high-precision point clouds are slow and costly to acquire, especially in large-scale natural and uncontrolled orchards. In such environments, changes in illumination, view angles, and long tracking duration introduce more challenges.

To address the challenges in 4D metric-semantic mapping in orchards, we leverage deep learning for fruit detection from a monocular camera, LiDAR-inertial odometry for pose estimation, and LiDAR-RGB fusion for fruit position and size estimation. In each time session, fruits are tracked using the overlap of segmentation masks between frames during the ego-motion of the sensors. Fruit positions are then optimized by minimizing re-projection error. The Hungarian assignment algorithm based on 3D Euclidean distance cost is used to track fruits between two consecutive time sessions. In our 4D metric-semantic map, as seen from Fig. 1, the semantics involve detecting the presence of fruits, while metrics such as fruits’ counts, sizes, and positions can be obtained.

To support such research for the broad community, we also release a comprehensive dataset that includes data on different fruit species across multiple growth stages. The fruit species included are apples, pears, cherries, peaches, and pistachios. Unlike most existing datasets such as [22, 23, 24, 25, 26], which typically capture only a single growth stage, our dataset spans the entire growth cycle, from the bud stage through to harvest.

Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

  1. 1.

    Method: A complete framework that takes in LiDAR-visual-inertial measurements across multiple time sessions and constructs a 4D metric-semantic map, along with the estimates of the count and size of fruits. It includes spatial and temporal data association modules to track fruits, and aggregate information from multiple sensors and time sessions.

  2. 2.

    Experiments: Experiments and results in real-world natural orchards demonstrating the framework’s ability to track and monitor fruit growth over time.

  3. 3.

    Datasets: We publicly release datasets capturing multimodal measurements across multiple seasons, along with instance segmentation annotations, pre-trained models, and ground truth fruit counts and sizes for benchmarking.

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that proposes a method and multi-modal, multi-orchard dataset for 4D metric-semantic mapping of natural, uncontrolled, large-scale orchards to monitor fruit-level changes across the entire growth season. A demo video can be found at: https://youtu.be/uzHNjeqAk-0?si=cBCWybSB6dDNQZrc.

III Proposed Approach

In this section, we describe each module of our framework for 4D metric-semantic mapping. A visualization of the framework is shown in Fig. 2.

III-A Spatial (3D) Tracking

III-A1 Fruit Instance Point Cloud Extraction

To obtain fruit instance point clouds, we fuse LiDAR and RGB data. For each image, we first preprocess the image using the Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) algorithm [27] to correct for any exposure problems from varied lighting conditions in the orchard. Later, we run inference using a YOLOv8 [3] instance segmentation model fine-tuned on our annotated images to obtain the instance mask of each fruit. Meanwhile, we use Faster-LIO [28], a LIO algorithm, to estimate the pose of the sensor. Our LiDAR and RGB camera are time synchronized, and their extrinsic (rigid body transformation) and intrinsic parameters are calibrated before the experiments. Based on such information, each image’s corresponding camera pose is obtained. We then project the point clouds onto the image plane to obtain each fruit’s instance point cloud.

However, the point cloud from a single scan is sparse. To remedy this, we use undistorted LiDAR scans in the fixed frame from Faster-LIO to accumulate point clouds over time. Accumulated point clouds from recent k𝑘k seconds are used to obtain fruit instance point clouds. In our implementation, we set k=1.5𝑘1.5k=1.5 to get a suitable point cloud density while reducing the computational burden and adverse effects of noise from the odometry.

We further filter out outlier background points by rejecting points that are too far from the sensor. The position of each fruit is then simply the centroid of this instance point cloud.

From this step, we obtain a set of tracks. For clarity, a “track” refers to a sequence of associated fruit detections across multiple frames. To avoid overcounting due to potential false positive detections, we only count the fruits that have been observed in more than three consecutive frames.

Due to canopy occlusions and missing detections from the neural network, some fruits may be initially tracked, lost, and then re-tracked again in later frames, resulting in double counts of the same fruit. It is thus necessary to re-identify such previously tracked fruits. However, directly identifying such tracks using their 3D positions will be less reliable due to noise in depth estimation. Therefore, for each track and its nearby tracks within a certain distance threshold, we project their 3D point cloud into the same image plane. Two conditions must then be met to re-associate the two tracks as one: (1) IoU between the two masks is higher than a predefined threshold. III-B Spatial-Temporal (4D) Tracking

The 4D tracking problem can be formulated as follows. Once the 3D positions of the fruits are accurately estimated from the previous step, we start associating the fruits from multiple time sessions. For this purpose, we use the Hungarian assignment algorithm with a 3D Euclidean distance cost. We only rely on the 3D positions of fruits instead of image-based feature descriptors for data association because, as mentioned before, there are drastic changes in lighting conditions, canopy growth, and fruit density across different time sessions. Finding reliable image features for data association in such perceptually aliased situations is challenging.

The process starts with finding a common reference frame for data from different time sessions. We achieve this by aligning the point clouds from two successive temporal sessions using ICP algorithm [29], supported by an initial guess of the transformation. This initial guess comes from starting the data collection process every time from a fixed marked location in the real world. Each of the point clouds and its corresponding RGB data is then processed using the framework detailed in the previous sub-section to obtain robust 3D positions of fruits. The relative transformation obtained from ICP is then used to transform the position of fruits into a common reference frame. The 3D Euclidean distance is used as the cost function. IV 4D Multi-Modal Datasets in Orchards

Our datasets consist of multimodal data collected from various orchards covering a variety of fruits. The datasets were sampled during different time sessions throughout the entire growth season of these fruits. In this section, we describe the details of datasets and the sensor suite used to collect them.

IV-A Sensor Suite and Datasets

Our sensor suite is shown in the leftmost (inputs) panel of Fig. 2. It consists of an Ouster OS0-128 LiDAR (Rev 6) along with a FLIR Chameleon global-shutter RGB camera and an Intel RealSense D435i RGB-Depth (RGB-D) camera. It also consists of a high-quality VectorNav VN-100 IMU and UBlox ZED-F9P for GPS measurements. We compute the extrinsic calibration between the LiDAR and the global shutter RGB camera by using an initial estimate from the sensor tower’s CAD model. We further refine the calibration by manually aligning the projected point cloud with an RGB image using a calibration board. The intrinsic calibration of the RGB camera is done using the standard checkerboard pattern calibration technique. We utilize the Kalibr software [30] for this operation. More details on the sensor suite can be found in [31] and [32].

The datasets consist of LiDAR point clouds, RGB images, RGB-D data, IMU measurements, and GPS measurements. The seasons covered by the dataset start from April 2024 and end in August 2024. The size of the data is over 4 TeraBytes (TB). There are 284 trees in total, which include 120 apple trees, 63 pear trees, 50 peach trees, 34 cherry trees, and 17 pistachio trees.

IV-B Orchards Overview

We collected data from orchards in Pennsylvania, Maryland, and California to create a diverse and comprehensive dataset. Data was collected every two or three weeks from the start of the growing season (early April) until late August. While the fruits were typically too small to detect early on, as illustrated in Fig. 1, reliable fruit detection became feasible once they had grown to a reasonable size (e.g., early June for apples). However, we utilized the early-season data to construct mesh models of tree trunks and branches, which were most visible during this period. All collected data has been included to support future research in improving early-season fruit detection and persistent fruit growth monitoring.

The types of orchards from which we collected datasets include apple, peach, pear, cherry, and pistachio orchards. The sites where we collected apple and pear datasets are located at the Hands on Earth Orchard, Lititz, Pennsylvania, USA. The apple, peach, and cherry datasets were collected from the Shaw Orchards, Harford, Maryland, USA. Finally, the pistachio dataset was collected at the IoT4Ag Merced testbed [33], Merced, California, USA.

V Results and Analysis

This section presents our results and analysis. We first describe the method used to obtain the ground truth data, such as total fruit count and per-tree batch fruit count. Later, detailed statistics are provided on how well our method performs in 3D fruit tracking and counting against the obtained ground truth. Finally, we provide qualitative and quantitative results, such as monitoring the change in size of fruits, which evaluates our 4D fruit monitoring method.

V-A Ground Truth Generation

Obtaining accurate ground truth by directly counting the fruits in the orchard is a challenging problem for humans due to orchards being an extremely perceptually aliased environment. This results in the double-counting of fruits, which decreases ground truth accuracy. Hence, we obtain the ground truth by manually counting the fruits on each tree from image frames extracted from video streams. Counting on images allows one to use programs and applications that can help track previously counted fruits (by placing a digital mark on them) and prevent the issue of double-counting. Even so, double-counting can still happen in this approach, especially when a particular tree is visible in multiple image frames. One approach to alleviate this is to stitch together multiple images and count the fruits in the resulting panorama [8]. However, this method can sometimes induce artifacts in the panoramas during the mosaicing operation, making fruits blurry and difficult to count.

Instead, we choose a more reliable method by carefully selecting a subset of image frames. In such images, only the trees that are at the center and completely visible have their fruits counted. To avoid double-counting, we mark the fruits that have already been counted and visually track the boundaries of each tree.

V-B 3D Fruit Tracking and Counting

Our 3D fruit tracking results are detailed in Table I. For a total of 60 apple trees, we obtain a 3.1% total count error. We estimate 1846 apples from our algorithm, and when compared to the ground truth of 1790 apples, it gives us an absolute total count error of 56 apples. Compared to ground truth, our method tends to overestimate counts, primarily due to (1) double counting some fruits due to noisy fruit instance point clouds and (2) counting fruits fallen on the ground, which are excluded when we generate the ground truth. In particular, noisy fruit instance point clouds are obtained because some small and partially occluded fruits cannot accurately be fused from the RGB detections and the point clouds. Hence, they contain noisy background information, which induces errors in their estimated 3D positions.

Fig. 4 shows a per tree-batch count estimation compared to the corresponding ground truth. A total of 22 batches, each with two or three trees, was created from 60 apple trees, and their per-batch fruit count was compared with the per-batch ground truth. Trees were separated into these batches based on how easily human annotators can distinguish tree boundaries to generate accurate ground truth counts. We decided to use tree batches for method evaluation instead of individual trees because, when trees are very close together, it is challenging to accurately assign fruits to one or the other. As seen from the above-mentioned figure, our method consistently estimates fruit counts for trees despite the varying fruit densities and canopy structures of the trees. Similarly, the low standard deviation of absolute count error (5 apples) and the small mean of absolute count error (6 apples) shown in Table I demonstrates the precision and consistency of our method across the entire data sequence. Note that per tree-batch count errors may include human-induced inaccuracies, which may skew the results, as even human annotators can face ambiguity when assigning fruits to specific trees.

V-C 4D Metric-Semantic Mapping and Fruit Size Estimation

A visualization of our 4D tracking results on one example tree is shown in Fig. 6. Our method is able to track and associate fruits across multiple time sessions. Since our method relies on fruit landmark position information rather than image features, it is invariant to changes in lighting conditions and viewpoints. Spatio-temporal tracking of fruits allows for close monitoring of fruit size and appearance changes. An example of this can be seen in the left panel of Fig. 3 where each individual fruit is tracked and visualized across its growth progress.

The resulting 4D metric-semantic map of the orchard, shown in Fig. 1, illustrates how fruit sizes, canopy structure, and the overall appearance of both fruits and trees change over time.

To quantitatively evaluate the 4D data association, we manually match the fruits from five trees using images. We exclude a minority (less than 5 percent) of fruits that are ambiguous to match for humans. The total number of fruits is approximately 150 for each time session. For each of the three consecutive time sessions—June 12th to July 4th, July 4th to July 19th, and July 19th to August 13th—the precisions are 75.96%percent\%, 88.89%percent\%, and 75.21%percent\%, respectively. The recalls are 87.78%percent\%, 85.95%percent\%, and 84.25%percent\%. The F1 scores are 81.44%percent\%, 87.39%percent\%, and 79.47%percent\%.

In Fig. 5, we also show that we can accurately estimate the size of fruits up to a mean absolute error of 1.1 cm. For this experiment, we created a physical, real-world simulation of an apple tree in an orchard by manually attaching 19 apples to a similar tree. This was done since hand measuring and obtaining ground truth sizes of fruits in orchards is challenging due to difficulties in establishing a precise one-to-one association between ground truth and estimates. Data was then collected for one time session and processed to obtain the sizes of the fruits. The fruits were hand-measured using a Vernier caliper to obtain their size ground truths. As seen from Fig. 5, the size estimates closely follow the ground truth measurements. The errors induced in the size measurements are mainly due to the presence of background points such as leaves and stems in the segmentation mask of the fruits, which in turn inflates the extracted 3D fruit point clouds, leading to slightly larger size estimates compared to the ground truth. Further, we also show results on using our method to track the changes in the growth of fruits in uncontrolled orchards over multiple time sessions, as shown in the right panel of Fig. 3.

VI Conclusion and Future Work

This paper presents methods and datasets for 4D metric-semantic mapping of orchards. Real-world experiments and results demonstrate that the proposed method is accurate in counting and estimating the sizes of fruits. The fruit detection and spatial 3D tracking accuracy are also validated via manual fruit counts. The absolute percentage error in total fruit count is 3.1%percent\% over 1790 fruits, while the standard deviation of the absolute percentage errors in per-tree-batch counts is 6.4%percent\% over 22 batches of trees. The accuracy of the metric mapping is validated by the accurate size estimation results, which show absolute errors with a mean of 1.10 cm and a standard deviation of 0.45 cm. The spatio-temporal 4D tracking accuracy is supported by both qualitative and quantitative results of fruit data association across multiple consecutive time sessions. Together, these results demonstrate that our method is able to effectively construct accurate 4D metric-semantic maps of orchards. Such accurate maps, scalable to include entire orchards while maintaining fine-grained details at the individual fruit level, provide actionable information for downstream tasks such as automated harvesting, precision irrigation, and targeted fertilizer and pesticide application.

Future work involves optimizing the method and deploying it onboard robots, enabling them to leverage the 4D metric-semantic maps for real-time decision-making.
