Abstract

Federated Learning (FL) is becoming a popular paradigm for leveraging distributed data and preserving data privacy. However, due to the distributed characteristic, FL systems are vulnerable to Byzantine attacks that compromised clients attack the global model by uploading malicious model updates. Most existing Byzantine-robust FL systems statistically analyze the weights of whole individual model updates uploaded by clients to defend against Byzantine attacks. With the development of layer-level and parameter-level fine-grained attacks, the attacksâ€™ stealthiness and effectiveness have been significantly improved. Due to unawareness or overreaction, the existing model-level defense methods degrade the training efficiency and model performance. To address this problem, we proposeÂ SkyMask, a new attack-agnostic robust FL system that leverages fine-grained learnable masks to identify malicious model updates at the parameter-level. Specifically, the FL server applies parameter-level masks to model updates uploaded by clients and trains the masks over a small clean dataset (i.e., root dataset) to learn the subtle difference between benign and malicious model updates in a high-dimension space. Our extensive experiments involve different models on three public datasets under state-of-the-art (SOTA) attacks, where the results show that SkyMask achieves up to 10% higher testing accuracy compared with SOTA defense strategies and successfully defends against attacks with malicious clients of a high fraction up to 80%. In the meantime, the experimental results demonstrate the scalability of our approach and the weak dependence on the data distribution of the root dataset.

I Introduction

Every user click is an oil drop of the digital era that powers up todayâ€™s information industry. Massive volumes of data generated by users and collected by online services (e.g., social networks, online sales, and streaming platforms) are used to train machine learning (ML) models (e.g., recommendation algorithms) that enhance user experience and boost profits. However, with increasingly strict data and privacy policies, such as the GDPR by EU and ATT by Apple, web services tracking and collecting user data for centralized training becomes inappropriate. The emergence of federated learning (FL) has addressed such conflicts between ML training and data privacyÂ [1]. Instead of centralizing training data, FL distributes a model to clients, trains the model locally, and aggregates the local models into a global model without leaking any clientsâ€™ local data, which has been applied in many web applications, including online recommendation systems and personal healthcare.

However, due to FLâ€™s distributed design, attackers can easily attack the FL system by compromising client participants and smuggling malicious model updates, known as Byzantine attacksÂ [2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7]. Based on whether the compromised clients poison their local data or models, Byzantine attacks can be classified into data poisoning and model poisoning attacks. Data poisoning attacks corrupt the global model by injecting contaminated training samples to the local datasetÂ [2, 7]. Model poisoning attacksÂ [3, 5] directly manipulate local model updates to compromise the global model. To tackle these attacks, researchers have explored various defense strategies, most of which leverage coarse-grained model-level statistics, distances, and cosine-similarities to detect outlier model updates (i.e., Multi-KrumÂ [8] and FLTrustÂ [9]) or greedily filter out outlier parameters (i.e., TrimÂ [10] and BulyanÂ [11]).

Unfortunately, research on fine-grained attacking methodologies is becoming trendy due to their precise balancing between attacking stealthiness and effectiveness. Fine-grained model poisoning attacksÂ [4, 12] leverage the varying sensitivities of different neural network layers and parameters to craft adaptive and stealthy attacks. FIBAÂ [13] injects small pixel-wise perturbations in the frequency domain to conduct data poisoning. Existing model-level defense strategies either fail to prevent such fine-grained attacks or spill over the benign clients, which extensively sacrifices training efficiency and model quality.

This paper proposes SkyMask, a new Byzantine-robust FL system that defends against agnostic attacks with fine-grained learnable masks at the parameter-level. Specifically, after collecting local models from participant clients, the FL server freezes and multiplies each of the local model parameters with a learnable mask variable. Then, all masked local models are trained on a small and clean data set (i.e., the root datasetÂ [9]) together. During the training on the root dataset, the mask variables applied to the frozen local model parameters learn to improve the model accuracy. Particularly, the masks applied to malicious models are trained to correct their potential misbehaviors at the parameter-level. Since utilizing fine-grained learnable masks does not enforce customization for specific attacks or refactoring the FL training objective functions, SkyMask gains three superior capabilities beyond existing defense strategies: 1) effective and efficient detection of fine-grained stealthy attacks, 2) attack-agnostic defense against a wide spectrum of attack methods, and 3) strong compatibility and complementarity with existing FL and defense methodology. Our main contributions are as follows:

  • â€¢

    We design fine-grained and computation-efficiency learnable masks that can capture malicious modelsâ€™ characteristics in a uniform high-dimension space.

  • â€¢

    We develop SkyMask, a new attack-agnostic Byzantine-robust FL system, which is the first work applying fine-grained learnable masks to detect malicious clients and defend against Byzantine attacks.

  • â€¢

    We empirically evaluate SkyMask on various benchmarks under six SOTA attacking methods and compare them with existing defense methods, where experimental results show that SkyMask achieves up to 10% higher testing accuracy compared with existing defenses.

II-B Threat Model & Byzantine Attacks on FL

Malicious clients compromised by an attacker participate in the FL process and have access to the knowledge of neural network models, learning rates, and objective functions. Besides, the attacker can fully control these clientsâ€™ activities (e.g., modifying the model updates) and has complete access to their local datasetsÂ [4, 9, 12].

Existing Byzantine attacks on FL can be categorized into untargeted, targeted, or backdoor based on the adversaryâ€™s goal: 1) Untargeted attacks seek to corrupt the global model and minimize its accuracy on any test inputÂ [2, 4, 12, 5]. For example, FangÂ [4] adaptively trades off the attacking stealthiness and effectiveness at a fine granularity as an optimization problem. 2) Targeted attacks aim to minimize the accuracy on the specific test inputs, and maintain high accuracy on the restÂ [3, 14]. 3) Backdoor attacks reduce the utility of the global model on test inputs that contain a specific signal called triggerÂ [15, 6].

II-C Byzantine-robust FL Algorithms

We classify existing representative Byzantine robust algorithms into two categories based on their strategies:

II-C1 Model-level defense strategies

FLDetectorÂ [16] determines whether a client is malicious by performing the consistency analysis on its model updates. Specifically, FLDetector predicts a clientâ€™s model update for each iteration based on historical records and compares it with the received model update. If they are inconsistent in multiple iterations, the client is probably malicious. FLTrustÂ [9] trains a root model with a small clean root dataset at the server in each round, and measures the cosine similarity between a local model and the root model. The similarity metric dictates the weight in global model aggregation. TolpeginÂ [2] detects malicious participants by analyzing the characteristics of model updates. It standardizes all model updates, uses the principal component analysis (PCA) for dimension reduction, and identifies malicious clients via clustering algorithms. However, existing robust FL strategies can hardly defend against those fine-grained attacks without sacrificing training efficiency and model quality by utilizing coarse-grained similarity checking or greedy parameter-wise filtering, which motivates us to explore malicious client detection with fine-grained learnable masks.

II-D Motivating Learnable Masks

Binary masks have been widely used in deep learning and demonstrated the capability to capture neural network modelsâ€™ intrinsic characteristics at the parameter-level. For example, ZhouÂ et al.Â [17] prune deep neural network models with binarized masks to improve training efficiency. FedMaskÂ [18] utilizes learnable binary masks to enable each device to learn a personalized and structured sparse neural network model that runs efficiently on devices.

We use a realistic experiment to show how learnable masks can be applied to detect malicious clients. In a swarm of 100 FL clients, we compromise 20 as malicious clients to apply Min-Sum attack. We train a four-layer CNN model on Fashion-MNIST dataset with the non-IID setting described in Â§IV-A1. We visualize the CNN model updates of all clients in one iteration using PCA in FigureÂ 1(a), which shows how the fine-grained Min-Sum attack hides the malicious model updates among the benign updates. Hence, analyzing local model updates with PCA is insufficient to distinguish benign and malicious clients.

To detect such stealthy attacks, we apply fine-grained learnable masks that capture the parameter-level characteristics of local models. We aggregate all masked local models and train the masks together on a small root dataset, where the learnable masks learn to enhance the benign models and correct the malicious models (details in Â§III-B). FigureÂ 1(b) shows the visualization of trained masks using PCA, where the masks applied to the malicious model updates are easily distinguished among all masks. This phenomenon reveals the capability of learnable masks that captures the difference between benign and malicious model updates.

II-E Root Dataset

Considering that the server has no root of trust to decide whether a model update is malicious or not, FLTrustÂ [9] was the first to introduce the concept of root dataset. It can take advantage of the root dataset even if the distribution diverges from the overall data distribution or the size is less than one hundred. Therefore, it is easy for the server and FL manager to manually collect or generate a small root dataset. The rationality for using root datasets has been verified in several other approachesÂ [19, 20].

III SkyMaskâ€™s Design

III-A Goals

We aim to design a server-based robust FL system. The server has a small representative root dataset following a previous workÂ [9]. Moreover, this root dataset can be imbalanced without a major bias (analyzed in Â§IV-D1). We allow reasonable additional computation costs and extra storage space to defend against Byzantine attacks. We aim to detect all the malicious model updates and filter them out at each communication round to eliminate the impact of attacks. We should also keep as many benign clients as possible in the filtered FL client set to train a final global model with sufficient local data for satisfactory accuracy. For the proposed Byzantine-robust FL system, it should promise the following three features:

  • â€¢

    Robustness. The system should preserve the global model accuracy when attacked by malicious clients. In particular, we should maintain a high malicious client detection accuracy and a low misidentification rate of benign ones (i.e., false positive rate). The system should also stay effective on different datasets and model structures under different attacks.

  • â€¢

    Efficiency. The system should not introduce extra computation and communications overhead to the clients, which are often resource-constrained devices in FL. Besides, when performing malicious client detection and defense on the server, the system should only incur a proper computation complexity.

  • â€¢

    Scalability. The system should still support a large scale of clients and work under a high fraction of malicious clients (e.g., 80%) since FL systems usually involve a large number of client devices.

III-B SkyMask: Overview

When initializing the FL process, the server creates an initial global model and builds a root dataset asÂ [9]. FigureÂ 2 presents SkyMaskâ€™s workflow, where each communication round has six steps: Step 1, the server distributes the global model parameters to each client. Step 2, the clients load the parameters, train the local models and send back these model updates. Step 3, the server freezes received local models and applies learnable masks to each local model update. Step 4, all the masked local models are trained together on the root dataset to converge. Step 5, the server detects and removes the malicious clients by clustering all trained masks. Step 6, the server aggregates the remaining model updates into a new global model.

The key component of SkyMask is the detection mechanism based on learnable masks. Specifically, SkyMask constructs a same-size fine-grained mask for each local model update and trains these masks by optimizing the aggregation result of masked local models until they converge. Byzantine attacks take impairing global model performance as an optimization objective, so the poisoned model updates tend to make the global model far from normal behavior. Therefore, to correct potential misbehaviors of malicious models, the masks applied to the malicious models learn to mitigate the side effects of poisoned parameters through training on the root dataset. In this way, the learnable masks form a high-dimensional representation space to capture the characteristics of both malicious and benign model updates. Then, the clusters formed by masks are the basis for determining whether a client is malicious. After removing all the detected malicious model updates, SkyMask calculates the average of remaining model updates and uses it to update the global model.

III-C The Complete SkyMask Algorithm

Most existing defense strategies are based on the local model updatesâ€™ coarse-grained statistics or greedily filtering out outlier parameters. However, fine-grained attacks corrupt a small set of particular layers or parameters to work around existing defense strategies easily. Thus, we apply fine-grained learnable masks and clustering analysis to detect and defend against malicious clients. Our defense strategy has two stages: 1) mask initialization and training stage; 2) mask clustering and classification stage.

IV Evaluation

We implement a prototype of SkyMask based on PyTorch and evaluate our algorithm with existing Byzantine-robust FL aggregation algorithms and two malicious client detection algorithms. To verify the generalization of our method, we conduct experiments using several popular datasets and pair them with different models, respectively. We evaluate our approach with six popular attacks, explore the impact of the fraction of malicious clients, and verify its scalability. In sensitivity analysis, we discuss the influence of binarization threshold value, the number of features after dimensionality reduction, and the root datasetâ€™s data distribution on our detection methodâ€™s performance. We repeat each experiment three times and average the results. All experiments run on a cloud server with eight NVIDIA RTX 2080 GPUs.

On Fashion-MNIST and HAR datasets, SkyMask improves the testing accuracy by 1â€“3% compared with the best result of all five Byzantine-robust algorithms under the same attacks. More significantly, our method gains a 10% improvement in testing accuracy on CIFAR-10 dataset under Min-Max attack.

IV-A7 Metrics

Our experiments compare the testing accuracy of global models of various FL defenses under different attacks, obtained from the prediction accuracy of the global model on the test dataset. IV-B2 Under no attack

Some attacks send poisoned models to the server only at certain communication rounds, and some model updates are often mistaken for poisoning by existing defense methods due to data heterogeneity. In Table I, almost all other robust aggregation algorithms affect the convergence prediction accuracy in the no-attack case. The existing robust aggregation algorithms either choose a subset of model updates that seem benign or use model updatesâ€™ statistics to correct the impact. So the server selects most clients and has the same or very similar convergence with FedAvg in both IID and non-IID cases.

V Discussion and Limitations

The current Byzantine attacks are launched with either a single client or multiple clients executing the same attack strategy, which may only account for an increased proportion of the model aggregation stage on the server, but the malicious clients do not collaborate. Increasingly, some researchers find that some specific layers are more vulnerable to Byzantine attackÂ [24]. We also observe that some Byzantine attacks modify parameters near the output layer significantly more than those far from the output layer.

Therefore, each client can be responsible for a different part of model poisoning, using the philosophy of â€œnot putting eggs in the same basket.â€ The attacker hides poisoning factors in all the uploaded models of the malicious clients it compromises. When most or all malicious model updates bypass the malicious client detection mechanism and get aggregated on the server, it can form a powerful attack on the global model, which is truly a collaborative attack by multiple malicious clients.

The damage to the characteristics of local model updates by this collaborative attack is unknown, and it remains to be verified whether SkyMask can efficiently detect these malicious attacks. For other malicious client detection algorithms, detecting this abnormal pattern in a single model update may be challenging. Theoretically, using our mask mechanism, the collaborative impact of all malicious clients on the global model could be shown after masked aggregation so that the optimization process can change their corresponding masksâ€™ pattern. Actually, Fang attack and AGR-agnostic attack have attempted to attack collaboratively, which weakens the anomaly level of model updates uploaded by each malicious client but influences the performance of the global model when all attacks are combined. Hence, there is a chance for our SkyMask to detect this kind of malicious client.

In the future, we will continue to study this topic in-depth, first verify the effectiveness of this collaborative attack, and deepen the ability of our learnable binary mask to extract the features of model updates and realize the defense capability for this unknown attack. In addition to collaborative attack, the performance of SkyMask under multiple attack types in one communication round is unknown. We should explore how many clusters will the corresponding masks obtained by SkyMask form, and we should also adapt the settings of SkyMask according to this.

Furthermore, SkyMask can be seen as an additional module to the aggregation algorithm. Not only the basic FedAvg algorithm, the SOTA FL algorithms, such as FedProxÂ [25], FedBNÂ [26], or FedAMPÂ [27], can also be equipped with our malicious client detection module. This feature lets the FL system retain the heterogeneity-handling, high convergence rate, or personalization features the original aggregation algorithm may have. It is also possible to enjoy the defensive capability of our malicious client detection strategy, and we will explore the merits of various combinations in future work.

VI Conclusion

We propose a new attack-agnostic robust FL framework called SkyMask to defend against Byzantine attacks. By training parameter-level learnable binary masks on a clean root dataset, SkyMask is the first to implement a fine-grained detection of the poisoned elements of local model updates. Because SkyMask analyzes the mask as a whole, it avoids accidentally removing outliers caused by data heterogeneity on some elements of model updates and precisely removes the malicious clients. Extensive experiments on IID and non-IID data under different attacks prove the effectiveness of SkyMask. Our method shows a solid defensive capability, better than various robust aggregation algorithms and existing malicious client detection methods. Moreover, it also tackles the problem that most clients are malicious and can defend against attacks with a fraction of malicious clients up to 80%percent8080\%.
