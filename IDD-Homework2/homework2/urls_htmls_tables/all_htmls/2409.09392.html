Topological Eigenvalue Theorems for Tensor Analysis in Multi-Modal Data Fusion

Ronald Katende
Abstract

This paper presents a novel framework for tensor eigenvalue analysis in the context of multi-modal data fusion, leveraging topological invariants such as Betti numbers. Traditional approaches to tensor eigenvalue analysis often extend matrix theory, whereas this work introduces a topological perspective to enhance the understanding of tensor structures. By establishing new theorems that link eigenvalues to topological features, the proposed framework provides deeper insights into the latent structure of data, improving both interpretability and robustness. Applications in data fusion demonstrate the theoretical and practical significance of this approach, with potential for broad impact in machine learning and data science.

Keyword:Tensor eigenvalues; Topological invariants; Betti numbers; Multi-modal data fusion; Algebraic topology

1 Introduction

The study of eigenvalues and eigenvectors in matrix theory is well-established; however, extending these concepts to tensors, particularly in the context of multi-modal data fusion, remains less developed [1]. Tensors, as higher-dimensional generalizations of matrices, are increasingly used to model complex, multi-way data structures in machine learning and data science [2, 3]. Current methods for tensor eigenvalue analysis typically extend matrix-based approaches [4, 5], leaving a significant gap in formulating algebraically rigorous frameworks that leverage the unique properties of tensors [1, 6]. This manuscript addresses this gap by introducing novel topological methods for analyzing tensor eigenvalues, particularly in multi-modal data fusion [2, 7]. Unlike traditional techniques, which rely on algebraic methods derived from matrix theory [8], our approach utilizes algebraic topology to establish new eigenvalue theorems that are independent of classical linear algebra. 4 Implications of Topological Invariance

Corollary 1 asserts that the eigenvalues of homotopy-equivalent tensors are identical. This result has significant implications for data fusion, as it guarantees the stability of the eigenvalue spectrum under continuous deformations of the underlying data [10]. In practical applications, where multi-modal data is often subject to noise [12], perturbations [13], or other continuous variations, such stability is critical. The corollary ensures that as long as the underlying topological structure of the data is preserved (i.e., the tensors are homotopy equivalent), the eigenvalues remain unchanged. This robustness increases the reliability of tensor-based methods in data fusion by protecting against small variations that might otherwise lead to incorrect interpretations [14].

In data fusion, this result can be utilized to maintain consistency across multiple data sources [15]. If two multi-modal datasets are homotopy equivalent, their spectral properties will remain identical, enabling reliable cross-source validation and consistency checks [16]. This feature can also assist in aligning or synchronizing datasets, ensuring that spectral analysis remains robust to minor discrepancies between data sources.

Theoretical Validation and Data Fusion Implications

In data fusion, these results underscore how topological invariants like Betti numbers influence the spectral properties of tensors. The manuscript posits that tensor eigenvalues can be expressed as a weighted sum of topological features, and this hypothesis is validated through the numerical results. While the theoretical sum of eigenvalues is consistently 2, practical results exhibit some deviation due to approximation errors in CP and Tucker decompositions. MSE values shed light on these approximations. For random tensors, both CP and Tucker methods display high MSEs (4.43 and 4.81, respectively), indicating that in arbitrary, unstructured data, the tensor decompositions struggle to capture the precise topological structure [13, 17]. This suggests that random data introduces noise into eigenvalue computations, reducing the robustness of data fusion in such cases. For low-rank tensors, CP decomposition yields a lower MSE (0.165), while Tucker decomposition shows a slightly higher error (0.95). This indicates that for structured data with clear topological patterns, CP decomposition performs well in aligning with theoretical expectations. This suggests CP is more effective when the data exhibits distinct multi-modal relationships with low complexity. In the rank-deficient tensor scenario, CP decomposition has a higher MSE (1.53), but Tucker decomposition achieves a remarkably low error (0.005), showing that Tucker is better suited for handling rank-deficient data. Its ability to capture the core structure makes it ideal for data fusion tasks involving noisy or incomplete datasets [20].

Insights and Implications

These results offer valuable insights into the practical application of topological tensor eigenvalue theorems. The relationship between tensor eigenvalues and topological features holds up in structured and rank-deficient data scenarios [18], as shown by the low MSE values. This validates the utility of topological invariants as stable descriptors in data fusion. However, the high MSE in random data indicates that the topological framework is sensitive to the structure within the data. In the absence of meaningful structure, tensor decomposition results deviate significantly from theoretical predictions. This highlights the importance of preprocessing to enhance the topological structure of the data, ensuring reliable results in practical applications. Overall, the framework confirms the utility of topological tensor eigenvalue analysis for structured and rank-deficient data [23]. The discrepancies in random data stress the need for considering the dataâ€™s inherent structure in real-world scenarios. This provides a novel approach to understanding the interplay between topological invariants and spectral properties in multi-modal data fusion.

Insights for Multi-Modal Data Fusion

The presented results provide a theoretical basis for applying tensor eigenvalue analysis in multi-modal data fusion. Topological invariants guarantee consistent spectral behavior across tensors derived from different data sources, provided the underlying topologies are homotopy equivalent. This is vital for developing resilient data fusion algorithms, especially in noisy or incomplete datasets. Exploring these topological principles in high-dimensional contexts could expand their applicability [10, 19]in fields like neuroscience, physics, and machine learning.

6 Conclusion

This paper introduces a novel framework that merges tensor analysis with topological invariants, offering a new perspective on tensor eigenvalue problems in data fusion. The results extend traditional tensor eigenvalue theory by incorporating topological insights, particularly Betti numbers, to capture the structural complexity of multi-modal data. The findings represent a significant advancement in tensor eigenvalue analysis, providing valuable tools for multi-modal data fusion. By integrating topological invariants into the tensor eigenvalue problem, this framework opens the door to theoretical advancements and practical applications in data analysis. The results lay a rigorous foundation for future work at the intersection of tensor analysis, algebraic topology, and data fusion, with promising implications for various fields.
