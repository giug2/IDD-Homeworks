Abstract.

Collaborative filtering on user-item interaction graphs has achieved success in the industrial recommendation. However, recommending users’ truly fascinated items poses a seesaw dilemma for collaborative filtering models learned from the interaction graph. On the one hand, not all items that users interact with are equally appealing. Some items are genuinely fascinating to users, while others are unfascinated. Training graph collaborative filtering models in the absence of distinction between them can lead to the recommendation of unfascinating items to users. On the other hand, disregarding the interacted but unfascinating items during graph collaborative filtering will result in an incomplete representation of users’ interaction intent, leading to a decline in the model’s recommendation capabilities. To address this seesaw problem, we propose Feedback Reciprocal Graph Collaborative Filtering (FRGCF), which emphasizes the recommendation of fascinating items while attenuating the recommendation of unfascinating items. Specifically, FRGCF first partitions the entire interaction graph into the Interacted & Fascinated (I&F) graph and the Interacted & Unfascinated (I&U) graph based on the user feedback. Then, FRGCF introduces separate collaborative filtering on the I&F graph and the I&U graph with feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning and macro-level feedback modeling. This enables the I&F graph recommender to learn multi-grained interaction characteristics from the I&U graph without being misdirected by it. Extensive experiments on four benchmark datasets and a billion-scale industrial dataset demonstrate that FRGCF improves the performance by recommending more fascinating items and fewer unfascinating items. Besides, online A/B tests on Taobao’s recommender system verify the superiority of FRGCF.

1. Introduction

Motivated by the success of graph neural networks (GNNs) in tasks on the relational data (Kipf and Welling, 2017; Hamilton et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2022b; Shengyuan et al., 2023; Bei et al., 2024), graph-based collaborative filtering (GCF) has formulated user-item historical interactions as user-item graphs and then recursively aggregated interacted neighbors’ representations to obtain the refined embeddings of each user and item (He et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023).

However, learning meaningful representations from the interaction graph to recommend items that truly fascinate users remains a seesaw dilemma. On the one hand, not all interacted items are attractive to users. For example, as shown in Figure 1-(a), the recommended short videos with exquisite content are more likely to fascinate users and receive higher ratings or likes feedback (Xie et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023b) (short as I&F items), while those videos that users are not actually interested in but with appealing covers and tiresome content may also be clicked by them but receive low rating scores or quick-glanced (Ajesh et al., 2016; Margaris et al., 2020) (short as I&U items). Existing studies mainly directly train on the entire interaction graph, which does not consider and distinguish the difference between fascinated and unfascinated interactions. In this manner, GCF models aggregate all interacted neighbors’ information to represent users or items, where the information of unfascinated neighbors can also be aggregated. Then, during the recommendation process, the aggregated user embeddings have close relationships with the unfascinated items, and thus the users will be recommended a certain number of unfascinating items, leading to a negative user experience. As shown in Figure 1-(b), the recommendation results of GCF models trained on the entire interaction graph contain a high ratio of user-unfascinated items. On the other hand, disregarding the I&U interactions leads to an incomplete understanding of user-item interactions. Although the unfascinated items may bore users, they also reflect some intent of users, and directly ignoring all these items will lose potentially helpful information and their positive attributes that attract user interaction. As shown in Figure 1-(c), the GCF models only trained on the I&F graph have performance drops compared with the performance of models trained on the entire graph. Therefore, it is vital to comprehensively consider users’ interaction intent from both interactions with fascinated and unfascinated feedback while recommending users with more fascinated items and fewer unfascinated items.

Although important, building GCF models of this target presents two main challenges: (1) Joint modeling of I&F and I&U interactions: Both I&F and I&U interactions contain valuable information for user modeling. Simply separating interactions according to fascinated and unfascinated feedback to build separate models would greatly reduce the training data and negatively impact the performance (Tang et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2022). Thus, it remains a challenge to comprehensively consider both two views of interactions. (2) Building connections between I&F and I&U interactions: Besides the joint modeling, there is a further challenge to establish an effective connection between the I&F perspective and the I&U perspective without being affected by the negative factors of I&U interactions.

To address these issues, we propose a novel Feedback Reciprocal Graph Collaborative Filtering (FRGCF). Specifically, we first utilize two separate feedback-partitioned graph collaborative filtering modules for the partitioned I&F graph and I&U graph. Further, we design a feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning to connect the I&F graph and I&U graph, enabling better modeling of the user’s interaction intent through the exchange of information while avoiding the model being misled by the I&U interaction. Then, we design a macro-level feedback modeling scheme to alleviate the data incompleteness caused by separately modeling I&F graph and I&U graph. Finally, the recommender constructs on the I&F graph can capture the interaction character of the I&U graph without aggregating the information of the I&U items, and thus is adopted for inference. The main contributions are listed as follows:

  • •

    We highlight the seesaw dilemma in current graph collaborative filtering methods, which is largely neglected by previous works and poses difficulties in taking into account both the complete modeling of user intent and recommending more items that users are truly fascinated by.

  • •

    We propose FRGCF, a novel approach using two different-intent subgraphs: I&F graph and I&U graph. FRGCF separately models the I&F graph and the I&U graph with feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning and macro-level feedback modeling for meaningful feedback information learning and exchanging while avoiding the negative impacts of I&U interactions.

  • •

    Extensive experiments on four benchmark datasets and a billion-scale industrial dataset demonstrate that FRGCF can outperform existing models while reducing the recommendation of unfascinating items. Online A/B studies verify the effectiveness of FRGCF in Taobao’s industrial recommender system.

2. Related Works

2.1. Graph Collaborative Filtering

Recently, the success of GNNs in modeling complex relational data has attracted widespread attention in various applications (Kipf and Welling, 2017; Hamilton et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2024b; Bei et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). Given that user-item interactions can inherently be perceived as a graph structure, numerous studies have focused on graph collaborative filtering (GCF) and achieved good recommendation performance (Wang et al., 2019; He et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2024c). Further, in recent endeavors to address the intricacies of noise and scarcity within GCF, a variety of self-supervised Learning (SSL) techniques have been explored and obtained state-of-the-art performance (Yang et al., 2021; Xia et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022). Current SSL-based GCF models can be mainly organized into two main paradigms: contrastive-based GCF and predictive-based GCF, all of which mainly share the common trait of employing augmented data views (Wu et al., 2021; Xia et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022). The defined views can take the form of two embedding views in contrastive GCF or data views utilized for estimating mutual information in predictive GCF.

However, the dilemma of these GCF models remains, with some unfascinating items being recommended to users due to the training manner, with the entire interaction graph undistinguishing between the fascinating and unfascinating items. This will lead to the recommendation of unfascinating items to users and affect the user experience in real-world applications. In this paper, our designed FRGCF is an SSL-based GCF model to tackle the highlighted seesaw dilemma with cooperative modeling of the I&F and I&U feedbacked interactions.

2.2. User Feedback Enhanced Recommendation

User feedback is an important signal for recommendation evaluation (Chen et al., 2022a; Huang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2022b). Positive feedback from users indicates satisfaction with the item being interacted with, reflecting the users’ genuine interest. While negative feedback represents dissatisfaction with the product, and continuing to recommend such products may affect the user experience. In recent years, the explicit consideration of user feedback has received more and more attention in sequential recommendations and click-through rate prediction (Xie et al., 2021; Gong and Zhu, 2022; Fan et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2023). When modeling historical sequences, these models consider different weights for items with different user feedback. For GCF models, in recent years, a small amount of work has started to design customized models to take into account user feedback (Seo et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023b; Hennekes and Frasincar, 2023). They incorporate both positive and negative encoders for different user feedback considerations.

However, the seesaw dilemma in GCF of using both I&F and I&U graphs simultaneously or using only the I&F graph and ignoring the I&U graph has not yet been solved. Therefore, we design FRGCF for addressing the seesaw problem in this paper.

4. Experiments

In this section, we conduct comprehensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed FRGCF method. Specifically, we aim to answer the following research questions: RQ1: How does FRGCF perform compared with state-of-the-art interaction recommendation models? RQ2: Whether FRGCF can recommend more fascinated items and less unfascinated items? RQ3: If each tailored component can positively impact the recommendation performance? RQ4: How does FRGCF perform in the real-world industrial recommender system?

4.1. Experimental Setup

4.1.1. Datasets

We evaluate our method on four benchmark datasets: MovieLens (Harper and Konstan, 2015), Douban (Wu et al., 2017), Beauty (McAuley et al., 2015), KuaiRec (Gao et al., 2022), and a billion-scale industrial dataset with 0.17 billion users and 0.31 billion items collected from Taobao platform. The statistics of these datasets are shown in Table 1. We follow the commonly adopted settings in previous works (Wang et al., 2019; He et al., 2020) to randomly split the datasets into training and testing sets. To partition the interaction graph, for KuaiRec, we consider interactions with a completion rate greater than 50% as I&F and those less than 50% as I&U feedback. For the other public benchmark datasets, we regard the interactions with ratings greater than or equal to 4 as I&F feedback and those less than 4 as I&U feedback (Zhou et al., 2018). For the industrial dataset, we view the interactions with page stay time more than 4 seconds as I&F feedback and those less or equal to 4 seconds as I&U feedback.

4.1.2. Baselines

We compare the proposed FRGCF with seven representative state-of-the-art graph collaborative filtering recommendation models as follows: (i) Representative GCF models: BUIR (Lee et al., 2021), DirectAU (Wang et al., 2022), and LightGCN (He et al., 2020). (ii) SSL-enhanced GCF models: SGL (Wu et al., 2021), SimGCL (Yu et al., 2022), and NCL (Lin et al., 2022). (iii) User feedback-based GCF model: SiReN (Seo et al., 2022).

4.1.3. Parameter Setting & Evaluation Metrics

The embedding size is fixed to 64 for all models (He et al., 2020), and the embedding parameters are initialized with the Xavier method (Glorot and Bengio, 2010). We adopt the Adam optimizer (Kingma and Ba, 2014) and a default batch size of 2048. We choose the widely used all-rank Recall@N and NDCG@N (N=20) as the evaluation metrics (Wang et al., 2019; He et al., 2020). For fair comparisons, we run the experiments five times with different random seeds and report the average results to prevent extreme cases.

4.2. Offline Evaluation (RQ1)

To verify the superiority of FRGCF, we conduct the offline experiment on the interaction recommendation performance of FRGCF, baseline methods, and their variants that are directly trained ignoring the I&U graph. Table 2 illustrates the offline evaluation results. From these results, we have the following observations:

FRGCF outperforms all state-of-the-art methods on all datasets. Specifically, Recall and NDCG metrics are improved over 6.28% and 6.37% for the experimental datasets on average, respectively. Note that FRGCF gains 6.55% and 7.82% on Recall and NDCG respectively of the billion-scale industrial dataset. These observations indicate that comprehensively considering fascinated interactions and unfascinated interactions can better model users’ interaction intent. Besides, incorporating the feedback-partitioned graph collaborative filtering, feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning, and macro-level feedback modeling with FRGCF can address the highlighted dilemma and performance decays caused by splitting the complete graph.

The consideration of I&U graph is helpful to the model performance. Compared with training on the whole interacted graph, LightGCN, SGL, SimGCL, and NCL only trained on the I&F graph are more likely to lead to a significant decline in model performance. These results verify that simply removing all the I&U interactions for model training will result in sub-optimal performance. Thus, an effective solution for modeling user feedback through the I&U graph and the I&F graph simultaneously is needed, which verifies the necessity of FRGCF’s feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning and macro-level feedback modeling.

4.3. Recommendation Preference (RQ2)

We further conduct the case study on Douban to observe the recommendation performance of FRGCF on fascinated and unfascinated items. The recommendation comparison results of the user-fascinated interactions and the user-unfascinated interactions are presented in Table 3.

From these results, we can find that FRGCF can accurately recommend more user-fascinated items while effectively reducing the recommendation of user-unfascinated items. Specifically, compared with the top-performed baselines (SGL, SimGCL, and NCL), FRGCF can improve the overall recommendation performance by recalling more fascinating items. Then, for the unfascinated recommendation, FRGCF recalls less unfascinating items and diminishes their influence in the ultimate recommendation outcomes, indicating that with the help of feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning and macro-level feedback modeling, FRGCF can effectively model the unfascinated interactions while reducing the recommendation of them.

4.4. Ablation Study (RQ3)

In order to verify the effectiveness of the designed components of FRGCF, we conduct the ablation study on FRGCF by comparing with its three variants: FRGCF without feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning (FRGCF-w/o frcl), FRGCF without macro-level feedback modeling (FRGCF-w/o macro) and FRGCF without distance regularization (FRGCF-w/o dis).

Upon analyzing the results presented in Figure 3, it can be inferred that when compared to the FRGCF-w/o frcl and w/o macro variant, it is evident that FRGCF yields significant improvement, thereby substantiating the need for feedback-reciprocal information exchange between the I&F graph and I&U graph and the support from macro-level to micro-level information. Furthermore, compared with FRGCF-w/o dis, the enhancement derived from distance regularization substantiates the imperative of representation disentanglement between the I&F graph and the I&U graph.

4.5. Online Evaluation (RQ4)

In order to verify the capability of FRGCF in real system application, we further conducted an online A/B test for FRGCF on Taobao’s industrial recommender system, where FRGCF serves as a recall model, replacing the currently online recalling baseline model. Table 4 displays the average relative performance gain over four consecutive weeks for twenty million platform users.

From the results, we can have the following observations: Firstly, compared to the baseline model, FRGCF demonstrates a performance improvement of 1.35% for PCTR and 0.91% for UCTR, suggesting that users are more willing to click since FRGCF recommends items that truly fascinate them. Furthermore, GMV and Stay Time increase by 1.61% and 2.89%, respectively, indicating that FRGCF encourages users to engage more deeply with the recommended items, such as making purchases or exploring item details and videos. Lastly, FRGCF needs only slightly additional response time cost as the baseline model, demonstrating that FRGCF significantly improves performance while maintaining efficiency.

5. Conclusion

In this paper, we first highlight the seesaw dilemma in graph collaborative filtering models of considering only the I&F interactions or both the I&F and I&U interactions. To address this issue, we proposed FRGCF, the Feedback Reciprocal Graph Collaborative Filtering model, which introduced novel feedback-partitioned graph collaborative filtering, feedback-reciprocal contrastive learning, and macro-level feedback modeling architectures for user and item representation learning to recommend more user-fascinated items and fewer user-unfascinated items. Extensive offline and online experiments verify the effectiveness of FRGCF, which demonstrated significant recommendation improvement compared to state-of-the-art methods and the applicability of FRGCF in industrial utilization.
