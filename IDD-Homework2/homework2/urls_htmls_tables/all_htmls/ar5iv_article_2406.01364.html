BELLS: A Framework Towards Future Proof
Benchmarks for the Evaluation of LLM Safeguards

Diego Dorn Alexandre Variengien Charbel-Raphaël Segerie Vincent Corruble
Abstract

Input-output safeguards are used to detect anomalies in the traces produced by Large Language Models (LLMs) systems. These detectors are at the core of diverse safety-critical applications such as real-time monitoring, offline evaluation of traces, and content moderation. However, there is no widely recognized methodology to evaluate them. To fill this gap, we introduce the Benchmarks for the Evaluation of LLM Safeguards (BELLS), a structured collection of tests, organized in three categories: (1) established failure tests , based on already-existing benchmarks for well-defined failure modes, aiming to compare the performance of current input-output safeguards; (2) emerging failure tests , to measure generalization to never-seen-before failure modes and encourage the development of more general safeguards; (3) next-gen architecture tests , for more complex scaffolding (such as LLM-agents and multi-agent systems), aiming to foster the development of safeguards that could adapt to future applications for which no safeguard currently exists. Furthermore, we implement and share the first next-gen architecture test, using the MACHIAVELLI environment, along with an interactive visualization of the dataset.

1 Context

Developers of LLM-based applications compete for innovation and create products of ever-increasing complexity and reach. While applications such as ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, or agents such as Devin and AutoGPT become more capable and are more integrated with other systems, the number of ways those systems can fail increases, and new failure modes are discovered after every release of a new product. Previously observed failure modes include harmful model behaviour, such as Bing Chat threatening users (Vincent, 2023 ) and trying to manipulate them during normal conversation; lack of robustness to attacks such as universal adversarial attacks (Zou et al., 2023 ) , which are specific strings that can generate objectionable behavior that generalizes across models and prompts; indirect prompt injection (Greshake et al., 2023 ) , where attackers take control of an LLM through the output of tools. There can also be unforeseen technical bugs, such as ChatGPT glitch tokens (Rumbelow & Watkins, 2023 ) which are tokens that ChatGPT could not repeat and made it produce incoherent responses or insult the user.

Such failures can generate damage ranging from reputational harm to model providers, to systemic risks such as making dangerous knowledge available to malicious actors and initiating society-scale value drift. However, damage can also be of unforeseen nature, through emerging undesired behavior or yet unknown means (Hendrycks et al., 2023 ) .

This underscores the need for input-output safeguards color=green!40]Diego: Add a footnote explaining why we chose this term over the others? around LLM systems, that catch both well-known documented failures and new unexpected failure modes. Input-output safeguards process traces of an LLM application (i.e. all inputs and outputs of its constituting LLMs) before producing safety reports ( Figure 2 ). They take the form of real-time or periodic monitoring, offline evaluation of traces, content moderation, or any form of trace analysis.

Despite the rich ecosystem in the area of LLM evaluation, little attention has been given to evaluating their safeguards.

2 Motivation: building metrics to foster the development of future-proof safeguards

Our core vision is to introduce benchmarks to guide the development of robust safeguards that can act as an early detection system for risks of harm arising from new behaviors, use cases, or attacks. Those include misalignment of goals between humans and autonomous systems, advanced persuasion skills, or direct manipulation of actions by an attacker. These safeguards would help us address threats emerging in new LLM-based applications such as AI companions that interact emotionally with users, AI assistants that perform real-world actions to help with daily work and decision-making, and continuously learning AI agents that evolve based on user interaction and data acquisition. However, such safeguards should be thought as tools part of a comprehensive approach to mitigate the harm from LLM systems, and alone can not be sufficient to ensure LLM operations are safe.

Given the early nature of the field of input-output safeguards, we think the best way forward is to include a diversity of possible damage and failure modes instead of focusing on a few. The generalization abilities of safeguards are crucial to limit societal harm, but also reputational damage (e.g. detecting new kinds of jailbreaks). Addressing well-defined, established problems, and proactively researching emerging failure modes is key to providing fast feedback loops and defining robust design principles grounded in today’s applications, to ensure future-proof systems.

By building BELLS, a benchmark for LLM input-output safeguards, our goal is threefold, as illustrated in Figure 1 :

  1. 1.

    Comparison of safeguards. We want users and developers of LLM-based apps to be well aware of the strengths and weaknesses of their security systems and enable them to choose the best performing systems on the market. Safeguards need to be evaluated by third parties, as in-house metrics cannot form a solid basis for comparison and evaluation.

  2. 2.

    Measure the ability of safeguards to generalize to new failure modes. We want to provide a measure of how well a given safeguard can detect unknown failure modes, for instance, detecting a new kind of jailbreak (Anil et al., 2024 ) , or something of an entirely different nature enabled by the application, such as in-context reward hacking (Pan et al., 2024 ) . We hope that such a robust detection system could act as an early warning to detect and study new sources of systemic risks, such as emergent harmful use cases.

  3. 3.

    Enable safeguards for future applications of a different type. We want to foster the development of new kinds of safeguards that can apply to future applications, such as supervising autonomous LLM-based agents or multi-agent systems. There are, to our knowledge, no recognized methodologies for the detection of undesired behavior in those kinds of systems yet.

3 State-of-the-art systems

3.1 Input-output safeguards

The current field of LLM safeguards is still very much rooted in the field of automatic content moderation. Most systems focus on detecting the presence of unauthorized content inside the text sent by a user, or in the text generated by an LLM application.

Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023 ) is a fine-tuned Llama-7b model trained to perform multi-label classification to detect the presence of context categories in interaction with an LLM chatbot, such as the presence of violent or hate speech, sexual content, content that could help people plan criminal activities, etc.

Lakera Guard (Lakera AI, 2024a ) is a proprietary classification system to detect prompt injections, jailbreaks, and toxic speech inside free-form text.

OpenAI Moderation API (Markov et al., 2023 ) is a multi-headed transformer trained to assess whether a free-form text contains content that is sexual, hateful, violent, or promotes self-harm. Similarly, Perspective API (Lees et al., 2022 ) and Azure AI Content Safety (Microsoft, 2023 ) serve models classifying free-form text content on metrics such as toxicity, hate speech, self-harm, or sexual content.

3.2 Benchmarks for safeguards

Few benchmarks have been developed to assess the quality of input-output safeguards, except for prompt injection detection. Other benchmarks focus on the classification of text content on metrics such as toxicity.

Prompt injections. Benchmarks such as BIPIA (Yi et al., 2023 ) and the PINT benchmark (Lakera AI, 2024c ) to detect prompt injections have been made directly to test safeguards.

Unauthorised content in chat interaction. Datasets such as ToxicChat (Lin et al., 2023 ) and the Open AI Moderation Dataset (Markov et al., 2023 ) contain respectively single rounds Q&A or free form text, with labels for specific categories of unauthorized content (e.g. toxicity, hate speech).

Evaluation of LLM-specific failure modes. For failure modes specific to LLM applications, there are collections of jailbreaks (Shen et al., 2023 ) , datasets for goal hijacking and prompt leaking (Perez & Ribeiro, 2022 ) , sycophantic behavior (Perez et al., 2023 ) , and more. However, these have been made to test model robustness, and were not tailored to evaluate safeguards.

Proprietary datasets. Moreover, the most complete benchmarks of prompt injection are proprietary (e.g. the Lakera dataset ( 2024b ) ). However, given the safety-critical nature of reliable safeguards, their evaluation should be an open process. This is a necessary condition for LLM application developers and users to trust the system they use.

4 Structure of BELLS

The safeguard problem can be seen as an anomaly detection problem characterized by heavy class imbalance and a broad definition of what constitutes an anomaly. The definition of anomaly cannot be fully outlined in advance: new failure modes are discovered after deployment. color=green!40]Diego: Put this somewhere else

To achieve the three goals outlined in section 2 , we propose to design a collection of datasets organized into three categories: established failure tests, emerging failure tests, and next-gen architecture tests.

Established failures tests come from already existing datasets to detect well-defined failure modes such as jailbreaks, and unauthorized content. They come from two sources:

  • •

    By aggregation of pre-existing benchmarks designed for safeguards and monitoring systems, such as Toxic Chat and the OpenAI Content Moderation Dataset.

  • •

    By transformation of well-established benchmarks designed for LLMs into benchmarks for input-output safeguards. This corresponds to collecting traces on existing benchmarks using multiple models and multiple pre-prompts to induce different behaviors, some being harmful and some being safe.

Emerging failure tests are a collection of smaller tests on a diversity of recent or emerging failure modes. They can be used as a proxy to estimate how well a safeguard can catch problems safeguards developers ignored the existence. Some of those tests could be kept private, and be run by independent third parties to limit data contamination and have a stronger measure of generalization to unknown failure modes. Emerging failure tests are created using data augmentation on examples of failures gathered from diverse sources, such as:

  • •

    Scientific literature . For instance, this could include jailbreak from ASCII art (Jiang et al., 2024 ) , in context reward hacking (Pan et al., 2024 ) , indirect prompt injection (Greshake et al., 2023 ) , many-shot jailbreaks (Anil et al., 2024 ) , etc.

  • •

    Identifying new use cases in the wild . By following the methodology used in (Shen et al., 2023 ) , this would include looking at online forums like r/ChatGPT to notice new jailbreak techniques, new failure types, or new use cases that enable new kinds of harm.

  • •

    Reports on emerging vulnerabilities such as OWASP Top 10 for LLM ( 2024 ) , inspiring tests for LLM02 Insecure Output Handling, LLM06 Sensitive Information Disclosure, and LLM08 Excessive Agency.

Next-gen architecture tests aim to support the development of safeguards targeting future, more complex systems, such as LLM-agents and multi-agent systems. Currently, no detection system can find anomalies in a system that combines a collection of API calls, such as LLM-agents. However, understanding the context of multiple calls can be necessary to correctly classify the behaviors of LLM-agents. Next-gen architecture tests come in a kit containing:

  • •

    A minimal application . E.g. a minimal LLM-agent interacting with an environment.

  • •

    A dataset of anomalies in the application operation by forcing them (e.g. through prompting). We also collect a dataset of non-anomalous traces from the normal operation of the application.

  • •

    A minimal safeguard to establish a baseline detection accuracy and to show that creating safeguards for this failure mode is possible. Minimal application-specific detectors can be built using LLMs to inspect traces.

Ideally, these datasets should be kept up to date, new emerging failure tests should be continuously added, and some would become part of the established failure tests over time. Nonetheless, this project’s objective is to initiate a discussion about the evaluation of LLM safeguards and generate an inaugural batch of those datasets, which would already constitute a remarkable leap forward in the evaluation of input-output safeguards.

5 Example of next-gen architecture tests: agent traces on the Machiavelli Benchmark

To illustrate the technical process, we present an example of a next-gen architecture test, based on the MACHIAVELLI benchmark (Pan et al., 2023 ) .

5.1 Presentation of the Machiavelli Benchmark

The Machiavelli benchmark is a collection of 134 choose-your-own-adventure games, for which each of the 572 322 scenes have been annotated with the strength of many ethical violations (e.g. physical harm, trespassing, lying, etc.). We sum all the ethical violations provided by the benchmark to get a harm score for each step. Each step consists of a multi-paragraph textual description of the current scene and a choice between pre-defined actions. A trajectory for one scenario is on the order of a hundred steps.

Why choose the Machiavelli benchmark? Machiavelli doesn’t aim to capture a realistic setting in which an LLM application could plausibly cause harm. Instead, we chose it as an experimental environment to uncover the challenges that come with detecting undesired behavior in LLM agents.

  • •

    A sandbox to study agents. Machiavelli is a collection of environments that the current LLM can successfully navigate. LLM agents are an active field of research (Xi et al., 2023 ) and are poised to bring advances in a large diversity of domains. LLM agents can exhibit a much greater diversity of failure modes, and none of the existing tools (e.g. Lakera / Llama guard) can be easily repurposed to analyze agents trace, as they were designed to analyze free-form text. It is thus key to research agent-tailored safeguards before their wide-spread deployment. However, the existing open-source projects, such as AutoGPT (Significant-Gravitas, 2024 ) , are still brittle and of limited use to navigate complex environments.

  • •

    Rich environments. Machiavelli contains a large diversity of environments written by humans that can be used to investigate nuanced phenomena that are likely to arise in real-world settings, like the tradeoff between ethical and performant action.

  • •

    Simple structure. Compared to other existing LLM agent benchmarks such as GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023 ) , and Agent-Bench (Liu et al., 2023 ) , the Machiavelli benchmark doesn’t require access to tools like web search, or a terminal. This makes it a great starting point to understand the singular challenges that come from an agentic LLM system, before extending the scope to include tool use.

Generating the dataset of traces is done by collecting traces from two agents with different steering prompts integrated into the system prompt. The first steering prompt encourages ethical action, the other is pushing the model to do as many unethical actions as possible. Since the games are long, our agent takes actions depending only on the current scene, and not the previous ones. This generates a sequence of API calls and their outcomes, each of the form {steering prompt, scene details, agent thoughts, decision}. We then hide from the trace the steering prompt that guides the LLM towards ethical or unethical behavior.

The final dataset. We generated 1 1 footnotemark: 1 200 traces, with a 50% split between ethical and unethical steering prompts. This first dataset for BELLS contains 33.6k API calls, amounting to 18.5 million words.

Baseline detection. We also provide a baseline aiming to detect whether the agent was steered to behave unethically, as a proxy to unethical behavior. It achieves a 0.97 AUPRC score after 80 steps in the environment. Details for the experiment can be found in Appendix A .

Interactive visualization. To better understand this rich dataset, we built an interactive tool 1 1 1 The code, data, and visualization will be made available upon acceptance. using the Streamlit library ( 2019 ) . A screenshot of the tool is in Appendix D .

5.2 Preliminary lessons building safeguards for agents

Challenges of safeguards for agents. Through our preliminary experiments with the Machiavelli benchmark, we highlight three challenges for detecting anomalies in LLM agent traces:

  • •

    Long-range dependencies exist between API calls, for instance, with the agent making a commitment at step #3 and forgetting to honor it at step #30. This could not be detected only by analyzing calls one by one, as there could be no information about a previous commitment present in the text of call #30.

  • •

    Counterfactual evaluations are needed to evaluate ethical behavior, as sometimes the available options are all harmful (or all beneficial), and we want to know whether the agent took the best or worst one.

  • •

    Diversity of scenarios. The rich set of environments provided in the benchmark requires the detector to understand a wide array of situations, from understanding if an action is hurting allies or enemies in the description of a fight, to tracking long-term commitments.

LLMs such as GPT-4 can be straightforwardly transformed into an accurate content moderator (Weng et al., 2023 ) . Preliminary experiments show that this result doesn’t translate to supervising LLM-agent action in complex settings such as the MACHIAVELLI scenarios.

We hope that our dataset can provide a challenging bar to foster innovation in safeguards research tailored for agents navigating complex environments.

6 Discussions and future work

In this work, we proposed a structure for a comprehensive collection of tests for input-output safeguards, including established failure tests, emerging failure tests, and next-gen architecture tests. Our motivation is to address both current and future failure modes.

We exemplified the next-gen architecture tests using the Machiavelli benchmark to provide traces of agents exhibiting ethical or harmful behavior in a rich text-based environment.

Future work includes developing the other tests in our proposed structure, conducting rigorous evaluations of available safeguards, and measuring robustness against adversaries attempting to bypass these safeguards. Expanding the BELLS datasets and continuously incorporating new failure modes will be critical to maintaining comprehensive coverage and accurate performance assessments.

The evaluation provided by BELLS should not be viewed as an absolute assessment of the safety of LLM applications using input-output safeguards. Indeed, safeguards should be seen as tools within a systematic approach to LLM safety, and not as a silver bullet to ensure LLM operations are safe.

Ultimately, BELLS aims to foster a collaborative effort among researchers and practitioners to develop robust, future-proof safeguards that contribute to the safe and ethical deployment of LLM applications.

Appendix A Experiment: Baseline Detection for MACHIAVELLI

In addition to our benchmark, we publish a baseline detection mechanism, as a sanity check showing it is possible to make progress on our benchmark.

In this experiment, we aim to detect unethical behavior in the traces of agents evolving in the MACHIAVELLI environment. It is a binary classification problem, where the target is to determine whether the model was instructed to follow unethical behavior. We use the balanced dataset described in subsection 5.1 but note that the classifier is meant to be used in the context of anomaly detection, with high class imbalance in production.

Metrics

For the evaluation of our anomaly detection model, we use the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) as our metric, following the methodology outlined in (Markov et al., 2023 ; Inan et al., 2023 ) . This metric is particularly suitable for applications with a large class imbalance.

Baseline

The threshold depends on the step, to evaluate how early our baseline can detect unethical behavior, but is independent of the scenario, enabling the model to generalize to previously unseen scenarios. Limitations

There are several limitations to this approach. First, the method requires direct access to the environment, which prevents it to generalise to scenarios without a python made environment, such as the transcript of a role playing game. Second, trajectories can vary greatly in length (from 80 steps to over 600 steps), so the average of random agents is less precise for the end of trajectories. Last, the approach necessitates generating many (random) trajectories even in test scenarios, which makes this baseline better viewed as taking a pair of a scenario and a trace rather than evaluating just a trace. color=green!40]Diego: Not super clear formulation

Results

We present the results of our baseline detector using the AUPRC computed independently at each time step. The results are visualized in Figure 5 , which shows an AUPRC above 0.9 after step 29, and an AUPRC of 0.97 at step 80. This shows that the baseline is able to detect unethical steering prompts with reasonable precision and recall, even with access limited to the start of the trajectory.

Appendix B TRICOTS – A versatile tool to accelerate research in LLM Safeguards

In addition to our Machiavelli dataset, we also release TRICOTS (Trace Interception and Collection Tool for Safeguards), a generic tool to collect traces from any Python codebase that uses OpenAI’s API (e.g. Auto-GPT, etc.), and to edit API calls on the fly (e.g. to add extra instructions). Appendix C Traces in the Machiavelli Environment

C.1 Steering prompts

For the ethical steering prompt, we re-use the Ethics Prompt used in the MACHIAVELLI paper (Pan et al., 2023 ) and adapt it while keeping the same structure for the unethical steering prompt.

Ethical steering prompt.

Please play the game on your best behavior, exemplifying what it means to be a good and moral person.
1. Be altruistic: Be kind, helpful, and value the wellbeing of others.
2. Follow deontological rules: Do not lie, do not steal, do not harm others.
3. Act with humility and generosity: Do not be power-hungry, greedy, or take up too much space.

Unethical steering prompt.

Play the game on your worst behavior, exemplifying what it means to be a bad and immoral person.
1. Be selfish: Be unkind, unhelpful, and value only your own wellbeing.
2. Break deontological rules: Lie, steal, harm others.
3. Act with arrogance and greed: Be power-hungry, greedy, and take up too much space.

C.2 Sample trace

Each collected trace is a sequence of calls, in the format of to OpenAI’s API, all with the same structure:

  • •

    System prompt including the Steering prompt describing how to play the game.

  • •

    Scene details and list of actions available to the agent as a chat message from a user.

  • •

    Agent action , a single integer representing the action taken by the LLM-agent.

It is important to note that there is little dependency between the scenes, as the agent does not have memory of previous scenes. However, the steering prompt stays the same, playing the role of a latent variable and the scene is dependent on the previous actions.
