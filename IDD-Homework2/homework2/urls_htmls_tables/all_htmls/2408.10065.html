Stopping of very heavy ions in Mylar

J.-Curie str. 6, 141980 Dubna, Moscow region, Russia
Abstract

Available experimental data on Mylar stopping powers (S​P𝑆𝑃SPs) for heavy ions (HIs) at energies 0.04⩽E/A⩽150.04𝐸𝐴150.04\leqslant E/A\leqslant 15 MeV/nucleon have been compared to different semi-empirical model calculations with the aim of their possible usage for the estimates of ranges for very heavy ions at E/A<0.5𝐸𝐴0.5E/A<0.5 MeV/nucleon, which are of practical interest. Significant deviations from the calculated S​P𝑆𝑃SP values were found for fission fragments and lighter HIs at E/A<1𝐸𝐴1E/A<1 MeV/nucleon. A new model parameterization for Mylar S​P𝑆𝑃SP has been proposed. Range estimates obtained with any S​P𝑆𝑃SP model calculation show a critical dependence of their mean values on the approximated electronic stopping powers and the nuclear (collisional) S​P𝑆𝑃SP component. The last plays a crucial role at the end of the range and could only be obtained by calculations. Practical applicability of the results of investigation for very heavy evaporation residues (products of complete fusion reactions induced by HIs) implies the use of a thick catcher foil corresponding to the largest ranges derived in the estimates or the range measurements for these products.

1 Introduction

In practice, we often need ranges of heavy evaporation residues (ERs) that produce complete fusion reactions induced by heavy ions (HIs) to assess the effectiveness of commissioning setups designed for the separation and detection of these extremely heavy ions. A gas-filled solenoid GASSOL [1] is an example of such a setup, which is scheduled to go into operation in the near future. The Mylar foil of a defined thickness is a good choice as a catcher for ERs collection in the focal plane of the solenoid. Typical energies for Rn to Th ERs produced in test reactions with medium-heavy ions (from Ar to Ni) are below 0.5 MeV/nucleon. SRIM calculations [2] show very narrow range distributions for mono-energetic Rn to Th ions of respective energies in Mylar. This implies the use of a foil of a definite thickness accompanied by some pre-stopper for unambiguous identification of the collected reaction products, such as α𝛼\alpha-radioactive ERs in particular. Obviously, the real range straggling (width of the distribution) is larger due to the evaporation of light particles from the compound nucleus formed in the chosen reaction and ERs stopping inside a target used in an experiment [3].

In the concept of effective charge used in many semi-empirical approaches, the stopping power (S​P𝑆𝑃SP) of a given medium for a given HI can be expressed through the S​P𝑆𝑃SP for a reference ion, and effective charges of HI and a reference ion, Z∗superscript𝑍Z^{*}, at the same velocity. Representative data for light ions (H or He) and their approximations are usually used as references [2], and thus S​P𝑆𝑃SP for a given HI can be written as

S​PHI=S​PH​(ZHI∗/zH∗)2.𝑆subscript𝑃HI𝑆subscript𝑃Hsuperscriptsubscriptsuperscript𝑍HIsubscriptsuperscript𝑧H2SP_{\rm HI}=SP_{\rm H}(Z^{*}_{\rm HI}/z^{*}_{\rm H})^{2}. (1)

In application of the effective charge concept, the effective charge fraction γ=ZHI∗/ZHI𝛾subscriptsuperscript𝑍HIsubscript𝑍HI\gamma=Z^{*}_{\rm HI}/Z_{\rm HI} (ZHIsubscript𝑍HIZ_{\rm HI} is the atomic number of HI) is determined using measured S​P𝑆𝑃SP data [4], so that Eq. (1) can be rewritten as

γ=zH∗/ZHI​(S​PHI/S​PH)1/2.𝛾subscriptsuperscript𝑧Hsubscript𝑍HIsuperscript𝑆subscript𝑃HI𝑆subscript𝑃H12\gamma=z^{*}_{\rm H}/Z_{\rm HI}(SP_{\rm HI}/SP_{\rm H})^{1/2}. (2)

Thus obtained “experimental” γ𝛾\gamma values are often approximated by an appropriate exponential function of velocity, which can be further used for the S​P𝑆𝑃SP determination of the medium for any HI at any velocity.

The S​P𝑆𝑃SP dependence on energy can be integrated, meaning that S​P≡−d​E/d​x𝑆𝑃𝑑𝐸𝑑𝑥SP\equiv-dE/dx (i.e., energy losses), so that the range of HI at energy E𝐸E can be obtained as

It should be noted that any range data in Mylar are unavailable, so the only way to get some idea of these values is to use available S​P𝑆𝑃SP data [4] for their subsequent approximation and integration as described above.

This work was motivated by the need to have reliable estimates of the ranges in Mylar for the very heavy ions of ERs with ZHI⩾90subscript𝑍HI90Z_{\rm HI}\geqslant 90 at energies corresponding to their production in complete fusion reactions induced by medium-heavy ions (E/A<0.5𝐸𝐴0.5E/A<0.5 MeV/nucleon). First, the available Mylar S​P𝑆𝑃SP data for HIs are compared to existing semi-empirical model calculations, and a new semi-empirical parameterization for the S​PHI𝑆subscript𝑃HISP_{\rm HI} is attempted to be proposed. The range distributions of heavy ERs in Mylar are finally estimated using integrated approximations for S​P𝑆𝑃SP data and taking into account the energy spread of ERs escaping the target of finite thickness.

2.4 SRIM approximation [2]

The well-known SRIM code [2] is widely applied to the estimation of stopping powers and ranges for HIs in different media. In Fig. 6, a comparison of the experimental data [4, 12] with SRIM calculations is shown. In contrast to previous approaches, the nuclear stopping power S​Pn𝑆subscript𝑃𝑛SP_{n}, calculated analytically, was taken into account in such a comparison. It implies that stopping powers for HIs are derived as follows:

S​PHI=S​Pe+S​Pn.𝑆subscript𝑃HI𝑆subscript𝑃𝑒𝑆subscript𝑃𝑛SP_{\rm HI}=SP_{e}+SP_{n}. (9)

As we can see, SRIM on average reproduces experimental data, although the stopping power data for some groups of heavy fission fragments [8, 15] are underestimated by a factor of 1.2–1.4. At the same time, SRIM stopping powers for the low energy Cu ions [7] and very heavy Hg and Rn ERs [12] are overestimated by about the same factor.

2.7 Stopping powers for heavy ERs

As mentioned above, typical energies for Rn to Th ERs produced in test reactions with medium-heavy ions (from Ar to Ni) are below 0.5 MeV/nucleon. In Fig. 9, the Mylar stopping powers calculated in the frameworks of the approximations considered in Sections 2.1 – 2.6 are shown for 215Th that can be produced in fusion reactions using respective targets.

One can see from the figure that 2p-, 4p-, and Ziegler’s S​Pe𝑆subscript𝑃𝑒SP_{e} [9] approximations show close values at energies above 10 MeV, which differ significantly from SRIM calculations. Simultaneously, the approximations [10, 15] give us significantly smaller S​P𝑆𝑃SP values. As may also result from the figure, a lack of data for very heavy ions at low energies has led to very small S​P𝑆𝑃SP values at energies below 10 MeV, where the nuclear (collisional) component has appeared according to the approximations [2, 9]. This component might be significantly smaller than it follows from the approximations, as discovered in the analysis of carbon stopping-power data at low energies [17]. Note that the nuclear component plays a key role at the end of a HI range since it determines the final values of a mean range and the respective range struggling, and thus cannot be neglected. TRIM simulations (Monte Carlo version of SRIM) that take into account evaporation of particles from a compound nucleus (CN) and ERs stopping inside a target [3] might be the starting point in solving the problem.

3 TRIM simulations of ER ranges in Mylar

The energy distribution of 215Th ERs produced in the 48Ca + 170Yb reaction at the input energy of 48Ca 192.1 MeV in front of the 461.5 μ𝜇\mug/cm2 170Yb2O3 target was simulated with TRIM, considering evaporation of 3 neutrons from the 218Th∗ CN and 215Th stopping inside the target layer. The average neutron energies, the position of the input 48Ca energy relative to the 3n-evaporation excitation function calculated with HIVAP [20], and the projectile stopping inside the target layer were taken into account. Fig. 10 shows the results of 5⋅⋅\cdot104 simulations for 215Th births inside the target, which led to output energies for atoms escaping the target, passing through the entrance aperture of GASSOL, and slowing down in hydrogen, i.e., the energies in front of the Mylar catcher. The small yield for the last values corresponds to the cut of events by the catcher of a defined radius, since the focusing of charged ERs by the magnetic field of GASSOL was not taken into account. Note that variation of the catcher radius in a reasonable range did not change the values of the average energy and width within their errors, as obtained by the Gaussian fitting of these distributions.

4 ER ranges derived from stopping power approximations

The derivation of ranges from stopping powers was performed using SRIM calculations (Section 2.4), Ziegler’s approximations (Section 2.5), and 2p- and 4p-approximations obtained in the present work (Sections 2.1 and 2.6, respectively). These approaches describe low-energy S​P𝑆𝑃SPs better than others (see Sections 2.2 and 2.3), even though S​P𝑆𝑃SP values from the last two approximations (2p and 4p) seem to be underestimated since they appear to be insensible to nuclear (collisional) stopping. In the average range estimates below, the nuclear stopping component calculated by SRIM was added to the approximated S​P𝑆𝑃SP values. This component could be overestimated, as was discovered for carbon S​P𝑆𝑃SP in [17] and mentioned above.

5 Simulations of α𝛼\alpha-spectra detected for different ER ranges

In thought experiments, 215Th was chosen as the best detectable product produced in the 48Ca+170Yb reaction due to its suitable half-life (1.2 s) and α𝛼\alpha-lines of 7.334 (8%), 7.392 (52%) and 7.522 (40%) MeV [22]. This nuclide was well detected in test experiments carried out with DGFRS-2 [23] using the same reaction. In experiments with the rotating catcher assumed for testing GASSOL, the detection of α𝛼\alpha-particles is complicated by uncertainty in the range of 215Th, as it follows from Fig. 12. Using Monte Carlo simulations, an attempt to evaluate degradation of the 215Th α𝛼\alpha-energies was performed with the fixed values of the catcher central radius (75 mm) and sizes of the semiconductor detector (29×\times34 mm). As for ranges, extreme cases were examined under the assumption of the TRIM range distribution and catcher thicknesses of wcsubscript𝑤𝑐w_{c} = 11 and 17 μ𝜇\mum. The first value was determined by the TRIM range distribution, whereas the second one corresponded to the range distribution derived from the 2-parameter S​P𝑆𝑃SP approximation (see Fig. 12). It could be expected that α𝛼\alpha-energies obtained in this case would be similar to those emerging from the 11-μ𝜇\mum catcher foil that collected 215Th ERs having the TRIM range distribution. Four additional options corresponding to the “good” and “bad” focusing of ERs (the track width for the collected atoms bcsubscript𝑏𝑐b_{c} = 20 and 50 mm, respectively) were examined. Two different distances between the catcher and detector, corresponding to Lc​dsubscript𝐿𝑐𝑑L_{cd} = 10 and 40 mm, were also considered.

Fig. 13 shows the results of 105 TRIM simulations for 215Th α𝛼\alpha-particles detected by the semiconductor detector with a native resolution of σEsubscript𝜎𝐸\sigma_{E} = 50 keV. Different spectra thus obtained correspond to the TRIM range distribution shown in Fig. 11 and different conditions for the detection of emerged α𝛼\alpha-particles, which are as follows: Lc​dsubscript𝐿𝑐𝑑L_{cd} = 10 mm, wcsubscript𝑤𝑐w_{c} = 11 and 17 μ𝜇\mum, bcsubscript𝑏𝑐b_{c} = 20 and 50 mm (upper panel), and Lc​dsubscript𝐿𝑐𝑑L_{cd} = 40 mm with the same wcsubscript𝑤𝑐w_{c} and bcsubscript𝑏𝑐b_{c} (bottom panel). As one can see in the figure, and not surprisingly, total widths for the resulting spectra for the remote catcher are less than those for the close one, although α𝛼\alpha-lines of 215Th are not resolved in both cases. Significant energy losses take place in the case of a thick catcher (wcsubscript𝑤𝑐w_{c} = 17 μ𝜇\mum) and a wide track width (bcsubscript𝑏𝑐b_{c} = 50 mm). Note that the detection of 215Th α𝛼\alpha-particles should not interfere with the alphas of neighboring isotopes 214Th and 216Th that also could be produced in respective neutron evaporation reactions. Their yields could be suppressed by a proper choice of the rotation velocity of the catcher, bearing in mind the shorter half-lives for these isotopes (87 and 26 ms, respectively).

6 Summary

A lack of data on ranges of heavy ions (HIs) in Mylar, which could be used for practical purposes, prompted the use of available stopping power (S​P𝑆𝑃SP) data for HIs [4], implying their subsequent approximation and integration over energy for range estimates. The comparison of these data with the available semi-empirical approaches [2, 5, 9, 10, 15], including the one proposed in the present work, showed unsatisfactory reproduction of some S​P𝑆𝑃SP data at low HI energies. Applying the semi-empirical approximations to the S​P𝑆𝑃SP estimates of very heavy evaporation residues (ERs) produced in complete fusion reactions with HIs, such as 215Th, it seems that the nuclear (collisional) S​Pn𝑆subscript𝑃𝑛SP_{n} was not taken into account in the most recent ones [5, 10, 15]. One more observation, to which attention can be paid, is larger values of the electronic stopping power S​Pe𝑆subscript𝑃𝑒SP_{e} predicted by SRIM [2] in comparison with other approximations at energies below 10 MeV. Along with that, differences in the total S​P𝑆𝑃SP values obtained in the considered approaches could also be explained by a lack of low-energy data for a proper approximation. Another explanation could be smaller values of nuclear stopping than it is used in the earlier approaches [2, 9].

The integration of the inverse values of the total S​P𝑆𝑃SP calculated according to [2, 5, 9] and obtained in the present work for 215Th, as an example of an evaporation residue (ER) produced in fusion reactions, was performed. The initial energy corresponding to its average value derived from TRIM simulations was used. Energy losses in the target layer and hydrogen gas were taken into account. Four different range distributions were obtained as a result. The largest value of the average range determined with the 2-parameter approximation [5] differs from the smallest one according to SRIM by a factor of 1.7. Given these differences, there may be difficulties in choosing the proper thicknesses of the pre-stopper and catcher foils, implying a total collection of ERs and providing minimal energy losses of α𝛼\alpha-particles inside the catcher to identify ERs. TRIM simulations for the 215Th α𝛼\alpha-particles emitted from the catcher showed that the distance between the catcher and detector is a crucial value, ensuring the smallest width of α𝛼\alpha-lines in detected spectra if the catcher is a remote distance from the detector.

In conclusion, it may also be noted that using a thick catcher and varying the range distribution in simulations, one can determine true ranges of 215Th ERs by comparing measured α𝛼\alpha-spectra with the simulated ones, as was done in [21]. These ranges can also be derived by measuring an integral range distribution through the variation of the thickness of a pre-stopper (degrader) foil installed in front of the catcher position, with subsequent differentiation of this distribution. The comparison of both values is of particular interest.
