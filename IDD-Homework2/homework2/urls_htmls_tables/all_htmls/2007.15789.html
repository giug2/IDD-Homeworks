LDP-FL: Practical Private Aggregation in Federated Learning
with Local Differential Privacy

Anonymous Authors
(2018)
Abstract.

Training deep learning models on sensitive user data has raised increasing privacy concerns in many areas. Federated learning is a popular approach for privacy protection that collects the local gradient information instead of raw data. One way to achieve a strict privacy guarantee is to apply local differential privacy into federated learning. However, previous works do not give a practical solution due to three issues. First, the noisy data is close to its original value with high probability, increasing the risk of information exposure. Second, a large variance is introduced to the estimated average, causing poor accuracy. Last, the privacy budget explodes due to the high dimensionality of weights in deep learning models and many query iterations of federated learning. In this paper, we proposed a novel design of local differential privacy mechanism for federated learning to address the abovementioned issues. It is capable of making the data more distinct from its original value and introducing lower variance. Moreover, the proposed mechanism bypasses the curse of dimensionality by splitting and shuffling model updates. A series of empirical evaluations on three commonly used datasets, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST and CIFAR-10, demonstrate that our solution can not only achieve superior deep learning performance but also provide a strong privacy guarantee at the same time.

1. Introduction

Many attractive applications involve training models on highly sensitive data, to name a few, diagnosis of diseases with medical records, or genetic sequencesÂ (alipanahi2015predicting). In order to protect the privacy of the training data, various privacy protection works have been proposed in the literature (ma2018pdlm; michie1994machine; nissim2007smooth; samangouei2018defense). The federated learning framework is of particular interest since it can provide a well-trained model without touching any sensitive data directly (konevcny2016federated; mcmahan2016communication). The original purpose of the federated learning framework is to share the weights of the model trained on sensitive data instead of data directly. However, some studies show that the weights also can leak privacy and recover the original sensitive data (papernot2017semi). In order to solve the problem, recent works start to use differential privacy to protect the private data in federated learning (bhowmick2018protection; geyer2017differentially; nguyen2016collecting), but most of them can not give a practical solution for deep learning on complex dataset due to the trade-off between privacy budget and performance.

In previous approaches, there are several apparent concerns and challenges. First, the noisy data is close to its original value with high probability, increasing the risk of information exposure. For example, the price of a pen is 5. After data perturbation, the protected information is close to 5, such as 5.3, still leaking information. Second, a large variance is introduced to the estimated average, causing poor accuracy. As a result, it requires more communication between the cloud and clients for the learning convergence. Third, the privacy budget explodes due to the high dimensionality of weights in deep learning models. Last, to the best of our knowledge, no existing works have shown an excellent deep learning performance on popular datasets such as MNIST (lecun2010mnist), Fashion-MNIST (xiao2017fashion) and CIFAR-10 (krizhevsky2009learning), with a reasonably small privacy budget.

In this paper, we proposed Locally Differential Private Federated Learning (LDP-FL), a new local differential privacy (LDP) mechanism to solve the above issues. As shown in Fig. 1, novelly designed local data perturbation and local model weights splitting and shuffling are applied to a typical federated learning system.

Our main contribution is multifold. First, we propose a new LDP mechanism, and show how to apply it to federated learning. We prove our LDP mechanism is securer than existing mechanisms with a much reduced risk of information exposure by making the perturbed data more distinct from its original value. Second, we apply splitting and shuffling to each clientsâ€™ gradients to mitigate the privacy degradation caused by high data dimension and many query iterations. Third, we prove that our solution introduces less variance to average calculation, making it capable of presenting a better model training performance. Last, we evaluate LDP-FLÂ on three commonly used dataset in previous works, MNIST (lecun2010mnist), Fashion-MNIST (xiao2017fashion) and CIFAR-10 (krizhevsky2009learning). The proposed mechanism achieves a privacy budget of Ïµ=1italic-Ïµ1\epsilon=1 with 0.97% accuracy loss on MNIST, a privacy budget of Ïµ=4italic-Ïµ4\epsilon=4 with 1.32% accuracy loss on Fashion-MNIST, and a privacy budget of Ïµ=10italic-Ïµ10\epsilon=10 with 1.09% accuracy loss on CIFAR-10, respectively. To our best knowledge, this work gives the first practical LDP solution for federated deep learning with a superior performance.

2.2. Federated Learning

Federated learning (mcmahan2016communication; konevcny2016federated) has been proposed and widely used in different approaches. The motivation is to share the model weights instead of the private data for better privacy protection. Each client, the owner of private training data, updates a model locally, and sends all gradients or weights information to the cloud. The cloud aggregates such information from clients, updates a new central model (e.g., averaging all clientsâ€™ weights), and then distributes it back to a fraction of clients for another round of model update. Such process is continued iteratively until a satisfying performance is achieved. Note that, to minimize communication, each client might take several mini-batch gradient descent steps in local model computation. To enhance the privacy protection, differential privacy has been applied to federated learning (bhowmick2018protection; geyer2017differentially). Previous works mostly focus on either the differential privacy mechanism that requires a central trusted party or the theoretical analysis of LDP techniques. Practical solutions for deep learning tasks data and tasks have not been well studied.

3. Our Approach

In the framework of LDP-FLÂ as shown in Fig.1, the cloud is an aggregator that collects a set of weights of local client models from the local side and averages the weights after each communication round. The goal is to maximize the accuracy of both cloud and local client models while preserving usersâ€™ privacy. In our proposed algorithm, we will alter and approximate each local information with a randomized mechanism. This is done to totally hide a single clientâ€™s contribution within the aggregation and thus within the entire learning procedure.

3.1. Threat Model

Clients are honest-but-curious. They follow the training protocol and may try to infer information about other clients. In our design, clients do not share information with each other so they are unable to compromise each otherâ€™s privacy.

Cloud is not trusted. It may try to infer clientsâ€™ private data based on model updates or weights collected from clients. It may utilize some auxiliary background knowledge to increase its inference capability. We assume the majority of clients do not collude with the cloud.

Note that we do not intend to defend against active attacks such as data poisoning, which is a common problem in FL, but is out of the scope of this work.

In this section, we introduce the federated learning approach with LDP consists of two steps, as shown in Algorithm 1.

In fact, prior knowledge is a very strong assumption and all clients having a consensus at the beginning is not very practical in most scenarios . Assuming there is no prior knowledge for all clients, a naive solution is to set a very large range which can probability cover all clients. In order to tackle this issue, we propose adaptive range setting during the federated learning. Splitting & Shuffling. As pointed in (dwork2014algorithmic), a sophisticated privacy-preserving system composites multiple locally differentiate algorithms leads to a composition of privacy cost of such algorithms. That is, a combination of locally differentiate algorithm with privacy budget Ïµ1subscriptitalic-Ïµ1\epsilon_{1} and Ïµ2subscriptitalic-Ïµ2\epsilon_{2} consumes a privacy budget of Ïµ1+Ïµ2subscriptitalic-Ïµ1subscriptitalic-Ïµ2\epsilon_{1}+\epsilon_{2}. Training a deep neural network using federated learning requires clients to upload gradient update to the cloud in multiple iterations. If location differential privacy is applied at each iteration, along the iterative training process, the privacy budget will accumulate and case the explosion of the total privacy budget. In order to control the total privacy budget, a larger noise has to be added in the algorithm which inevitably hurts the modelâ€™s accuracy for complex learning task.

Bittau et al. designed the Encode, Shuffle, Analyze (ESA) architecture which leverages trustworthy shufflers to anonyomize LDP reports from users in order to enhance privacy protection. This idea was further formalized by Erlingsson et al. (erlingsson2019amplification). It is shown that when LDP reports are properly anonymized at each timestep and unlinkable over the time, the overall privacy guarantee can be greatly improved. Inspired by their work, we proposed to add anonymity into the proposed protocol so that the cloud is unable to directly link the received data with a specific client, for the benefits of better privacy protection. Such anonymization can be achieved by multiple existing mechanisms, depending on how the server tracks clients in the specific scenario. As a typically best practice for privacy, a certain level of anonymity of each client to the server is applied to disassociate clientsâ€™ individually-identifiable information from their weights update. For example, if the server tracks clients by IP address, each client can adopt an untraceable IP address by using a network proxy, a VPN service (OperaVPN), a public WiFi access, and/or the Tor (Dingledine04tor). For another example, if the server tracks clients by a software-generated metadata such as an ID, each client can randomize this metadata before sending it to the server. More advanced anonymization techniques have also been proposed such as using secure multiparty computation (Bonawitz2017).

However, we argue that client anonymity is not sufficient to prevent side-channel linkage attack, e.g., the cloud can still link a large number of data together if the client uploaded them at the same time. Therefore, we design the local splitting and shuffling mechanism break the updates into pieces. The primary purpose is to give stronger privacy protection while using LDP on federated learning. Compared to traditional machine learning, deep learning needs more communications and weights for better performance, causing more privacy leakage during traditional federated learning. So, splitting and shuffling can greatly reduce privacy budget during the training phase.

Splitting breaks the private connections of each local client modelâ€™s weights, and shuffling mixes all clientsâ€™ weights together and makes them indistinguishable from each other. An approach is given in AlgorithmÂ 3, where each client samples a small random latency tğ‘¡t for each weight and waits tğ‘¡t before sending the weight to the cloud. Fig.Â 2 shows the idea of splitting and shuffling of the weights of each local client model that contains two main steps. For local model A,B,Cğ´ğµğ¶A,B,C, each model has the same structure but with different weight values. Original federated learning is to send the modelsâ€™ information to the cloud, as shown in Fig.Â 2 (1). In our approach, first, for each model, we split the weights of each local model. Then, for each client, we shuffle the weights though random latency and send each weight with its id to the cloud, where id indicates the location of the weight value in the network structure. Finally, splitting and shuffling can ensure the cloud collects the right weight values to update the central model without any knowledge about the relation of each weight and local clients. Note that LDP-FLÂ supports each client to finish the split and shuffling locally instead of using a third party.

Splitting and shuffling breaks the linkage between model updates from the same clients and hides them among updates from other clients, making it harder for the cloud to combine more than one piece of updates to infer more information about any client. Note that the shuffling inÂ (erlingsson2019amplification) is achieved by a semi-trusted third-party, for the purpose of achieving centralized DP on the cloud providing statistics for an untrusted analyst. On the contrary, our shuffling is performed locally to strength local DP.

5. Experiments

In this section, image classification tasks and a real mobile application are used as experimental examples to evaluate the effectiveness of LDP-FL. We first examine the effect of different weights based on the image benchmark datasets, MNIST (LeCun1998), Fashion-MNIST (xiao2017fashion) and then verify the performance improvement based on CIFAR-10 (krizhevsky2009learning) and the preceding three datasets.

For MNIST and Fashion-MNIST, we implement a two-layer CNN for image classification. However, for CIFAR-10, the default network from Pytorch library only achieves around 50% accuracy without data perturbation, and we re-design a small VGG (simonyan2014very) for the task. The training data and the testing data are fed into the network directly in each client, and for each client, the size of the training data is the total number of the training samples divides the number of the clients. In this case, a larger number of clients implicits the small size of the training data of each client. For each weight, we clip them in a fixed range. In this work, we set (c,r)=(0,0.075)ğ‘ğ‘Ÿ00.075(c,r)=(0,0.075) and (0,0.015)00.015(0,0.015) for MNIST and Fashion-MNIST, respectively. For CIFAR-10, due to the complexity of the network, we set c=0ğ‘0c=0 and rğ‘Ÿr is adaptively set by the weight range of each layer, using the max value minus min value of each layer. The learning rate Î³ğ›¾\gamma is set as 0.03 for MNIST/Fashion-MNIST and 0.015 for CIFAR-10. Considering the randomness during perturbation, we run the test experiments ten times independently to obtain an averaged value. In order to evaluate the performance of different learning approaches, we use different metrics including accuracy for utility, Ïµitalic-Ïµ\epsilon for privacy cost and nğ‘›n communication rounds for communication cost. Any approach with a high accuracy, a small Ïµitalic-Ïµ\epsilon and a small communication round indicates a good and practical solution.

The proposed models are implemented using Pytorch, and all experiments are done with a single GPU NVIDIA Tesla V100 on the local server. MNSIT and Fashion-MNIST can be within an hour with 10 communication rounds, and CIFAR-10 needs about 2 hours with 15 communication rounds.

6. Related Work

Differential privacy (dwork2014algorithmic) has been widely studied in last ten years. For stronger privacy, several local differential privacy mechanisms were proposed, e.g.,Â (duchi2013local; duchi2014privacy; erlingsson2014rappor; duchi2018minimax; wang2019collecting).

Recently, more works studies how to use DP in federated learning (geyer2017differentially; mcmahan2017learning) or use LDP in the federated learning (bhowmick2018protection; nguyen2016collecting; seif2020wireless; truex2020ldp). However, existing approaches can not be applied practically on deep learning. Some of them (nguyen2016collecting; seif2020wireless) only focus on small tasks and simple datasets and do not support deep learning approaches yet. The other works (bhowmick2018protection; truex2020ldp) studied the LDP on federated learning problem. However, as we discussed in the experiments part, both of them hardly achieve a good performance with a small limited budget. Bhowmick et al. (bhowmick2018protection) is the first work focus on the LDP on federated learning with deep learning models. However, it first need a strong assumption that attacker has little prior acknowledge about user data. Meanwhile, it spends Ïµ=500italic-Ïµ500\epsilon=500 for MINST and Ïµ=5000italic-Ïµ5000\epsilon=5000 for CIFAR-10, which means the LDP is not practical and needs traditional differential privacy for second protection. Last, they did not report the absolute performance on MNIST and CIFAR-10. Truex et al. (truex2020ldp) is another recent work. It uses It contains two obvious problems. First, it replaces LDP by Condensed LDP (CLDP), which spends a large privacy budget Ïµitalic-Ïµ\epsilon with a large clip bound reported in the work. Second, this work does not provide a complete privacy utility analysis of the number of weights and communication rounds. Due to the high variance of the noises in their approaches, it requires more communication rounds between cloud and clients, which spends more privacy cost in their framework. Our approach focus on solving the weakness of all previous approaches and accelerates the practical solution on complex dataset.

Besides FL communication framework, there are other communication frameworks with differential privacy protection, such as teacher-student communication (papernot2017semi; papernot2018scalable). The teacher models are fixed model who want to privately transfer their knowledge to the student side, which is very different to FL communication. So, we do not compare them in this paper.

7. Conclusion

In this paper, we propose a new mechanism for LDP, show how to apply it to protect federated learning, and apply splitting and shuffling mechanism to each local clientsâ€™ gradients to mitigate the privacy degradation caused by high data dimension and many query iterations. Our empirical studies demonstrate the proposed system performs better than previous related works on the same image classification tasks. Hopefully, our work can considerably accelerate the practical applications of LDP in federated learning.
