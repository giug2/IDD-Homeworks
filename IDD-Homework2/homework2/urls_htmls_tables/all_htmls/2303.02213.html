Backdoor Attacks and Defenses in FL: A Survey

Abstract

Federated learning (FL) is a machine learning (ML) approach that allows for the use of distributed data without compromising personal privacy. However, the heterogeneous distribution of data among clients in FL can make it difficult for the orchestration server to validate the integrity of local model updates, making FL vulnerable to various threats, including backdoor attacks. Backdoor attacks involve the insertion of malicious functionality into a global model through poisoned updates from malicious clients. These attacks can cause the global model to behave improperly on specific inputs while appearing normal in other cases. Backdoor attacks have received significant attention in the literature due to their potential to impact real-world deep learning systems. However, they have not been thoroughly studied in the context of FL. In this survey, we provide a comprehensive overview of current backdoor attack strategies and defenses in FL, including a comparison and analysis of different approaches. We also discuss the challenges and potential future directions for both attacks and defenses in the context of FL.

Federated Learning, Backdoor Attack, Backdoor Defense

1 Introduction

1.1 Federated Learning

Federated learning (FL) [McMahan2016FederatedLO, Bonawitz2019TowardsFL] is a collaborative training method that allows multiple devices to train a global model without compromising data privacy by storing data locally on end-user devices. The orchestration server collects and aggregates locally computed updates and analysis results to update the global model, which is then distributed to end-users. FL has been used in various fields such as natural language processing (NLP) [Chen2019FederatedLO, Lin2021FedNLPAR], healthcare [Xu2021FederatedLF, Prayitno2021ASR, Gupta2021HierarchicalFL, Liu2021FederatedLA], Internet of Things (IoT) [Liu2021DeepAD, Kholod2021OpenSourceFL, Nguyen2021EfficientFL]. However, FL is still subject to adversarial attacks, including untargeted attacks [blanchard2017machine, bernstein2018signsgd] which aim to cause the model to converge to a sub-optimal local minima or diverge completely (e.g., causing a model to misclassify a handwritten “7” as any other number), and targeted attacks [bhagoji2019analyzing, chen2017targeted, liu2017trojaning], also known as backdoor attacks, which seek to cause the model to misclassify a specific group of samples while minimizing the impact on its performance on the main task (e.g., using a model to categorize a handwritten “7” as a ”4”). Privacy and security in FL have been studied in [Abdulrahman2021ASO, Yin2021ACS, Mothukuri2021ASO, Gosselin2022PrivacyAS].

1.2 Backdoor Attacks in FL

Backdoor attacks, in which a trigger in a test-time input causes a model to behave in a specific manner that the attacker desires, have been well-studied in machine learning [Chen2017TargetedBA, Salem2022DynamicBA, Goldblum2022DatasetSF]. In FL, an adversary can insert poisons at various stages of the training pipeline and attacks are not constrained to be ”clean-label”[bagdasaryan2020backdoor, wang2020attack, Xie2020DBADB, Neurotoxin]. Most existing attacks focus on Centralized FL, in which the orchestration server is assumed to be honest and there may be one to many malicious participants. These attacks can target different stages of the training pipeline and can leverage techniques such as GANs and posterior probability. However, FL is more resilient to backdoor attacks due to the dilution of malicious updates by benign updates from non-adversarial participants. Nevertheless, techniques have been proposed to prolong the durability of the backdoor in FL [Neurotoxin, Zhou2021DeepMP, Liu2022TechnicalRA].

1.3 Defenses against the Backdoor Attack

Defense mechanisms against backdoor attacks in FL can be conducted in different phases of the learning process, including pre-aggregation, in-aggregation, and post-aggregation, illustrated in Figure 1. Pre-aggregation techniques [fung2018mitigating, Nguyen2022-FLAME, rieger2022deepsight, shen2016auror] aim to identify and remove or reduce the impact of malicious updates. In-aggregation techniques [Pillutla2022RobustAF, Sun2019CanYR, Blanchard2017MachineLW, Ozdayi2021DefendingAB] use more robust aggregation operators to alleviate the effects of backdoors. Post-aggregation techniques [Wu2022FederatedUL, Wu2020MitigatingBA] aim to repair backdoored models. These techniques differ from those used in traditional ML, as FL training data is private and methods that require data investigation, such as major voting and statistical mechanisms [Wu2020MitigatingBA, Weber2020RABPR, Yun2019CutMixRS], are not applicable. This survey aims to thoroughly review existing works on backdoor attacks and defenses specialized for FL.

1.4 Related Surveys

Existing surveys on backdoor attacks in FL have primarily focused on viewing them as a specific instance of target poisoning attacks [Abdulrahman2021ASO, Yin2021ACS, Mothukuri2021ASO, Gosselin2022PrivacyAS] or as a special example of robustness threats [Lyu2020ThreatsTF]. Other surveys [Li2022BackdoorLA, Goldblum2022-wq, Chen2017TargetedBA] have treated FL backdoor attacks as a special case of those in deep learning, but they do not fully address the unique characteristics of FL systems, such as the absence of clean-label attacks [Goldblum2022-wq] and the lack of discussion on model poisoning attacks in FL. The authors of [ba-fl-survey-2022] have investigated FL backdoor attacks and cutting-edge defenses, but their survey is primarily technique-driven and does not fully analyze or demonstrate the interconnection between backdoor attacks or the relationship between attacks and defenses. Additionally, evaluation metrics and their applicability to physical attacks have not been discussed.

In contrast, this survey aims to provide a comprehensive overview of FL backdoor attacks and defenses by thoroughly reviewing and analyzing existing works, as summarized in Table 1. Our survey differs from previous ones in that we focus on the functioning mechanism and evolution of FL backdoor attacks, as well as the interconnection between different attack strategies and their limitations. We also evaluate the effectiveness of defense mechanisms in terms of their robustness against various attack schemes, including physical attacks. Through this analysis, we aim to improve the understanding of FL backdoor attacks and their consequences, and to assist academia and industry in developing more robust FL systems.

1.5 Our Motivation and Main Contributions

The motivation for this survey is twofold. Firstly, despite the increasing popularity of FL, little work has been done to systematically analyze and understand the properties and characteristics of backdoor attacks in FL. While existing surveys provide an overview of backdoor attacks in ML or FL, they do not provide a comprehensive analysis of the techniques used, their limitations, and the connection between different attack strategies. Secondly, the rapid advancement of FL has led to the development of various defense mechanisms. However, these methods have not been thoroughly compared and analyzed in terms of their efficiency against different types of attacks, their limitations, and their suitability for different scenarios.

The contribution of this work can be summarized as follows:

  • 1.

    We present a systematic taxonomy of backdoor attacks in FL based on different perspectives on their manner and purpose of attacks. We also illustrate the connection among attacking techniques via their common features and advancement to help readers better understand the assumptions, principles, reasons, and differences among these attacking strategies.

  • 2.

    We present the state-of-the-art defense strategies and categorize them based on their common objectives and methodologies. In addition, we provide a comprehensive comparison and analysis of their efficiency against existing backdoor attacks and their applicability.

  • 3.

    We discuss the challenges for both backdoor attacks and defenses in FL, followed by possible future works in different aspects, and demonstrate significant missing gaps that need to be addressed.

  • 4.

    To the best of our knowledge, this is the first survey to assess and analyze backdoor attacks and defenses utilizing FL-specific criteria and perspectives. Our survey will facilitate the development of more advanced methods and promote a deeper understanding of backdoor threats and countermeasures, hence facilitating the construction of more secure FL systems.

In this survey, we delve into the topic of backdoor attacks in FL. The rest of the paper is divided into six chapters: Chapter 2 provides an introduction to FL and the concept of backdoor attacks, Chapter 3 classifies backdoor attacks in FL based on data poisoning and model poisoning, with a focus on HFL, Chapter 4 reviews defense strategies against backdoor attacks in HFL, Chapter 5 examines evaluation metrics, compares attack and defense strategies, and discusses the unique challenges and considerations present in the setting of VFL, Chapter 6 discusses challenges and future directions in the field, and Chapter 7 provides a summary of findings and conclusion.

2 Background

2.1 Definition of Technical Terms

This section presents concise definitions and descriptions of technical terms used in FL systems, backdoor attacks, and defenses in Table 2. These definitions will be consistently referred to throughout the remainder of the survey.

2.2 Overview of Federated Learning

FL is a distributed ML paradigm that enables the training of a ML model on decentralized data. Instead of bringing all of the data to a central location for training, FL allows the model to be trained directly on the data distributed across multiple devices or clients. This decentralized approach to training ML models has numerous advantages, including the ability to preserve the privacy and security of sensitive data, the ability to operate on large-scale datasets, and the ability to incorporate data from multiple sources. The main challenge in FL is to coordinate the training process across multiple clients while ensuring privacy and security. To overcome this challenge, FL algorithms such as Federated Averaging (FedAvg) [mcmahan2017communication] and SimFL [li2020federatedNPI] have been developed to facilitate the training process.

FedAvg is the de facto algorithm for centralized FL, where a central server aggregates model updates from multiple clients and uses these updates to update the global model. In contrast, SimFL is a decentralized federated learning framework that allows clients to communicate with each other directly to exchange model updates and train a shared model without the need for a central server. FedAvg has the advantage of faster training and simpler communication, but it also has the limitation of being reliant on a central server. SimFL has the advantage of not being reliant on a central server and allowing clients to have greater control over their data, but it also has the limitation of requiring more frequent communication and being susceptible to straggler clients slowing training.

Based on the distribution of data features and samples among clients, FL can be classified into horizontal federated learning (HFL) [smith2017federated], vertical federated learning (VFL) [wu2020privacy, yang2019parallel], and federated transfer learning (FTL) [chen2020fedhealth]. HFL is used when different datasets share the same feature space but differ in sample IDs. VFL is used when different datasets share the same sample IDs but differ in feature space. FTL is used when different datasets do not share the same sample IDs or feature space, and involves transferring knowledge from a source domain to a target domain to improve learning outcomes.

2.3 Basis of Backdoor Attack in Federated Learning

In FL, there are two main strategies for implementing backdoor attacks: data poisoning and model poisoning. In data poisoning, the attacker adds manipulated data to the training datasets of compromised clients, such as by flipping labels or adding triggers to data samples. Examples of data poisoning attacks include labeling 30 km/h signs as 80 km/h in a street sign database [shen2016auror] and adding a specific pixel pattern to images [xie2019dba]. In model poisoning, the attacker manipulates the training process of compromised clients by changing parameters or scaling the resulting model update to maximize the attack impact while evading the anomaly detection of the aggregator [bagdasaryan2020backdoor, wang2020attack].

3 Backdoor Attack Strategies

There are many significant dimensions associated with a backdoor attack strategy, which are depicted in Tab. 3. Based on different criteria, a number of ways to categorize backdoor attacks in FL were proposed. In particular, authors in  [wang2020attack], [2021-AdvancesOPFL], [Nguyen2022-FLAME], [RODRIGUEZBARROSO2022-FL-threat-survey], [Neurotoxin] mentioned Data Poisoning and Model Poisoning as two main taxonomies of backdoor attack in FL, which characterizes for the two manners of inserting backdoor. This approach is known as the most widely-used way to categorize the backdoor attack in the literature. On the other hand,  [Shejwalkar2022-BackDB], [bagdasaryan2020backdoor] divided backdoor attacks on FL into Semantic backdoor attack and Artificial backdoor attack or Pixel backdoor attack based on the discriminate property of the backdoor inserted. Along with the line, [wang2020attack] introduced Black-box attack and White-box attack, which is representative for the capability of the attacker. Moreover, many other perspectives are employed to classify backdoor attack on FL such as: attack frequency [bagdasaryan2020backdoor], distributed property [Liu2022-EvilvsEvil], etc.

In our work, we follow the majority of previous studies and roughly classify the backdoor attack strategies in FL into two primary categories: Data poisoning attack and Model poisoning attack. This systemizing approach is associated with two sub-phases of the FL training pipeline in, which are training data collection and learning procedure. Training data collection is to gather a training dataset, and the learning procedure generates a model based on the training dataset. From this, further categories are studied based on the different techniques to implant the backdoor. The taxonomies of backdoor attacks in FL are summarized in Figure 3.

3.1 Data Poisoning

In data poisoning attacks, it is assumed that the adversary has complete control over the training data collection process of compromised clients. In particular, the adversary manipulates the training data such that, following the completion of the local training procedure, any compromised client’s local update is poisoned. Most of the time, the poisoned training dataset has both clean samples and a subset of data with a backdoor trigger. Interestingly, the purposes of the attacker when trying to insert the backdoor are different, which is clearly presented via the characteristic of the targeted samples that the attacker leads the model to misclassify. Based on this point, data poisoning attack is further classified into semantic backdoor and artificial backdoor attacks.

3.1.1 Semantic backdoor.

The attacker employs semantic backdoor targets at misleading the aggregated model to classify a set of samples with the specific properties as his targeted label (e.g., cars with certain attributes are classified as birds [bagdasaryan2020backdoor]). In this category of attack, the key of the backdoor is “input-instance” and no modification is necessary on the features of backdoored samples. It is worth noting that semantic backdoor attack covers the term “physical backdoor attack” well-known in ML [Wenger2021PhysicalBA] to some extend. In specific, a physical backdoor attack leverages the physical objects as triggers, thus making them a credible threat against deep learning systems, i.e., the trigger is “white tape” on the forehead in the face recognition task. To achieve his goal, the adversary often manipulates the poisoning data of one or many compromised clients. However, the method to manipulate the poisoned training data that the adversary leverages vary significantly and could be roughly classified into three main approaches as follows.

Label-flipping attack. In [fung2018mitigating, SemiTargetedMP-Sun2022, tolpegin2020data], the adversary’s poisoning strategy is directly flipping the label of the benign samples belonging to compromised clients into their targeted ones. For instance, the all images of class “1” are labeled as “0” in [fung2018mitigating] and images of “dog” are flipped to “cat” in [tolpegin2020data]. On the other hand, in  [bagdasaryan2020backdoor], the attacker only targets the set of samples with specific features lying in the originality of the samples. These features may be the unusual car color (e.g., green), the presence of a special object in the scene (e.g., stripped pattern), or a trigger sentence ending with an attacker-chosen target word in a word prediction problem.  [Nguyen2020PoisoningIoT] proposed an attack scenario to backdoor FL-based IoT Intrusion Detection System, in which the adversary targets that packet sequences of malicious traffic from specific malware (e.g., IoT malware like Mirai malware) are not detected as malicious traffic. To achieve this purpose, the adversary moderately inserts malicious network traffic patterns into the training set including benign traffic data. The key common underlying these techniques is that the targeted set of samples contains specific characteristics, belongs to the same raw class, and these samples may be likely to appear in the training set of benign clients.

Out-of-distribution attack. In the label-flipping attack method mentioned above, the effect of the backdoor may be diluted since other benign clients contain a clean sample from the targeted class [Gong2022-survey]. Therefore, [wang2020attack, rare-Yoo2022BackdoorAI] employ the samples far from the global distribution and not likely to appear on the validation set of the training set of benign clients to backdoor the model. Specifically, the author in [wang2020attack] proposed an edge-case backdoor attack, in which the adversary targeted the edge-case samples (e.g., Southwest airplane images), which are not likely to appear in the benign clients’ training data, that is, they are located in the tail of the input distribution (e.g., CIFAR-10 [CIFAR-10]). Besides, authors in [rare-Yoo2022BackdoorAI] proposed ultimate rare word embedding to trigger the backdoor in Natural Language Processing domain. The rationale behind it is that rare words do not occur in the train or test sets of the clean sample by definition, which means it has little to no effect on learning the main task while it sufficiently influences the model output when present in the input. The main difference between this approach and the previous one is that the targeted set samples often lie in the tail of data distribution of the benign clients so that the impact of the backdoor will not be easily diluted.

Noise-instance attack. Different from the two mentioned above approaches, [PoisonGAN] proposed to train a GAN network during the local training process and employ the shared model to generate crafted samples and leverage these samples to backdoor the model. Since the adversary may be not known about the data distribution of benign clients, so leveraging the GAN network to mimic other participants’ training samples helps the attack conduct a stealthier backdoor attack [PoisonGAN]. In recent years, the idea of employing GAN to generate these poisoned samples has become popular  [zhang2019poisoning, Zhao2022CLPACP], and it opens a new potential direction to conduct backdoor attacks in FL.

3.1.2 Artificial backdoor.

In this strategy, the “trigger key” does not naturally exist in the samples, but is often intentionally inserted during the training process. Therefore, the targeted set of samples that the adversary tries to lead the model to misclassify is the set of samples associated with one or more backdoor patterns. The key idea of this strategy of attack is to poison a model such that in the presence of a backdoor trigger (e.g., a small square in the image), the model will misbehave while maintaining normal behaviors in the absence of a trigger. This strategy of attack is similar to a ”digital attack” in ML, in which the adversary digitally inserts a random pixel block into an input [Wenger2021PhysicalBA]. To achieve his goal, at the data preparing procedure, the adversary manipulates the poisoned training data of compromised clients by injecting trigger pattern(s) to clean images and flipping the corresponding labels to the targeted one, then attackers submit their updates to the server for model aggregating. In the inference stage, the attackers use the predetermined trigger pattern(s) to trigger the learned model to perform abnormally. Based on the homogeneous property of the trigger inserted, we can roughly classify it into three main approaches as follows.

Single-trigger attack. Existing backdoor attacks against FL are mainly based on a single backdoor trigger, that is, all the compromised clients inject the same trigger into their local training dataset [Gu2019BadNetsEB, bagdasaryan2020backdoor, Sun2019CanYR, Liu2021BatchLI-VFL]. The trigger used in this approach is often set randomly and determinedly (e.g., square, cross patterns at the redundant pixels of images). At the inference process, the inserted trigger(s) to malicious clients are employed to trigger the aggregated model. Although the effectiveness of the backdoor inserted is proved to be significant [bagdasaryan2020backdoor], the above works have not fully exploited the decentralized nature of the FL as they embedded the same trigger(s) to all adversarial parties [Xie2020DBADB].

Distributed-trigger attack. Observing the shortage of the previous approach, [Xie2020DBADB] decomposed the objective trigger into multiple local triggers and insert them into the corresponding compromised parties. This kind of attack was named a Distributed backdoor attack (DBA) and proved to be more effective than the single-trigger attack mentioned above. Specifically, each adversarial party uses the training data poisoned with its local trigger and sends the poisoned update to the server after completing local training. Unlike the previous approach, rather than directly using the local triggers independently, the adversary uses them to form a global trigger to trigger the model. Interestingly, even though the global model has not appeared during the training process, DBA could achieve a higher attack success rate and remain stealthier than the single-trigger attack strategy.

Coordinated-trigger attack. As observed from the two previous approaches, the trigger selected by the adversary is often generated independently from the learning model and not associated with the learning process (e.g., a logo, a sticker, or a pixel perturbation). Therefore, such single-trigger backdoor attacks do not fully exploit the collaboration among multiple malicious users during the training process [Gong2022CoordinatedBA]. To address this shortage, [Gong2022CoordinatedBA] newly introduced model-dependent trigger to inject the backdoor more efficiently. The model-dependent trigger is generated by seeking the optimal value assignment of the trigger (shape, size, and placement) through a sub-training process with the objective is minimizing the difference between the activation value with the expected one of a chosen neuron. After the local trigger is generated by the adversarial party, the local training dataset will be poisoned based on the trigger. At the inference step, the global trigger is constructed by the combination of local triggers, this idea is analogous to [Xie2020DBADB]. To this end, the model-dependent trigger is proven to be more efficient than the standard random trigger in previous works.

3.2 Model Poisoning

In FL, even after successfully inserting the backdoor into the aggregated model, the impact of the backdoor is diluted due to the aggregation process involving many benign updates [bagdasaryan2020backdoor, Neurotoxin, Gong2022CoordinatedBA]. As a result, to boost the effect of the backdoor attacks, many works were proposed to poison the model before submitting the local updates for aggregation. This strategy requires that the adversary has complete control over the training procedure and the hyperparameters (e.g., number of epochs and learning rate) and is free to modify the model parameters before submitting [bagdasaryan2020backdoor]. This technique demonstrates its efficiency when combined with data poisoning attacks in various scenarios in the literature [bagdasaryan2020backdoor, wang2020attack, Liu2021BatchLI-VFL, rare-Yoo2022BackdoorAI]. Based on the range of poisoned parts in model parameters, we can categorize existing works into Fully poisoning attack and Partially poisoning attack as followings.

3.3 Fully poisoning attack.

Since the most common way of aggregating the local updates from clients is the average method, the most naive way to strengthen the effect of backdoors is scaling the updates from adversarial clients to dominate the updates from the benign ones. Therefore, most of the state-of-the-art model poisoning attacks mainly utilize a hyperparameter to scale up the effectiveness of the client’s malicious mode. Moreover, the malicious models submitted are often fully poisoned by training procedures on poisoning data before scaling. [bagdasaryan2020backdoor] first introduced the Model replacement method, in which the attacker ambitiously attempts to substitute the new global model with the poisoned model by scaling the poisoned update by a wisely-chosen factor. This technique requires careful estimation of global parameters and is effective when the global model is close to convergence  [bagdasaryan2020backdoor]. This technique is widely used in later works and demonstrates its success in intensifying the backdoor [Sun2019CanYR, wang2020attack]. However, directly scaling seems to be naive to be successful due to the variety of FL defenses in the literature, and the robustness of FL is more seriously considered. This raises a need for stealthier model poisoning attack strategies, and as a result, many efforts were taken to address this necessity.
Constraint-based. In this approach, the adversary constraints the local updates from the malicious clients such that they are not identified by the anomaly detector at the server by making assumptions by feasible anomaly detectors might be employed. [bagdasaryan2020backdoor, Bhagoji2019AnalyzingFL] proposed to modify the objective (loss) function by adding anomaly detection terms. The terms considered are formulated from the assumptions of any type of anomaly detection (e.g., the p-norm distance between weight matrices, validation accuracy). In [Sun2019CanYR, wang2020attack], PGD (projected gradient descent) attack is introduced to be more resistant to many defense mechanisms. In a PGD attack, the attacker periodically projects their model on a small ball, centered around the global model of the previous iteration so that at every FL round the attacker’s model does not deviate significantly from the global model. Along with the line, [ALittleIsEnough2019] established a method to calculate a perturbation range in which the attacker can change the parameters without being detected even in i.i.d. settings. From this perturbation range, an additional clipping step is conducted to better cover the malicious updates.
Information-leakage based. More recently, [Liu2022TechnicalRA] reported that an early-injected single-shot backdoor attack is ineffective, so they introduced a novel information leakage-assisted two-phase FL backdoor attack to strengthen the early-inserted backdoor attack. In specific, the adversary generates a crafted auxiliary dataset by leveraging information leakage about global data distribution and conducts training on this auxiliary dataset to facilitate the convergence of the FL model. The rationale behind it is that training on the auxiliary dataset whose distribution aligned with the inferred whole population distribution can facilitate the convergence of the FL model and reduce the magnitude of local model updates from normal clients, and further boost the performance of the backdoor attack.
Gradient-replacement based. The model poisoning attack strategies mentioned above originate from the design of Horizontal FL, wherein the participating parties own the labels of their data training samples. However, to the best of our knowledge, these techniques have not been verified or fully investigated in the Vertical FL scheme. Due to this fact, [Liu2020BackdoorAA, Liu2021BatchLI-VFL] introduced Gradient-replacement backdoor attack, which is applicable to VFL even when the adversary owns only one clean sample belonging to the targeted class. Specifically, the attacker in [Liu2020BackdoorAA] records the intermediate gradients of clean samples of the targeted class and replaces the gradients of poisoned samples with these ones and uses these poisoned gradients to update the model. [Zou2022VFL] shown that even with HE-protected communication, the backdoor attack can also be conducted by directly replacing encrypted communicated messages without decryption using gradient replacement method.

3.4 Partially poisoning attack.

Different from the previous direction, which is fully poisoning the model parameters of the malicious clients, [Zhou2021DeepMP] demonstrated that the backdoor insertion could be conducted effectively without fully poisoning the whole space of model parameters. Specifically, they proposed an optimization-based model poisoning attack that injects adversarial neurons in the redundant space of a neural network to keep the stealth and persistence of an attack. To determine the redundant space, the Hessian matrix is leveraged to measure the distance and direction (i.e., “important”) of the update for the main task for each neuron. Then, an additional term is added to the loss function to avoid injecting poisoning neurons in positions that are particularly relevant to the main task. More recently, [Neurotoxin] proposed a Neurotoxin attack, wherein the adversary employs the coordinates that the benign agents are unlikely to update to implant the backdoored model to prolong the durability of the backdoor. In Neurotoxin, instead of directly updating the poisoned model by gradient computed on poisoning data, the attacker projects gradient onto coordinatewise constraint, that is the bottom−k%percent𝑘-k\% coordinates of the observed benign gradient. The common objective of partially poisoning attacks is to prevent catastrophic forgetting of the adversarial task and prolong the durability of the backdoor’s impact.

4 Backdoor Defense Methodologies

Researchers have developed various strategies to defend against the significant impact of backdoor attacks in FL. These strategies can be dedicated or non-dedicated, and implemented on the server or client side. While some strategies were specifically designed for FL backdoor attacks, others, such as Krum [blanchard2017machine] and geometric mean [RFA2022] for mitigating Byzantine attacks, have also been effective in defending against such attacks despite having strong assumptions (e.g., independent and identically distributed data distribution) and specific limitations. Server-side defenses assume the orchestration server aggregating local updates from clients can be trusted, while client-side defenses aim to protect the robustness of FL when this assumption is not met. These defenses can be implemented at different stages of the FL training process, resulting in various methods and approaches. FL backdoor defenses can be grouped into three categories based on methodology: previous-aggregation defense (Pre-AD), which uses anomaly detection techniques; in-aggregation defense (In-AD), which relies on robust training techniques; and post-aggregation defense (Post-AD), which involves model restoration. More information about these defense methodologies in HFL can be found in Figure 4.

4.1 Previous-aggregation defense

Pre-AD methods are implemented before the server aggregates updates from client models. These methods identify adversarial clients as anomalous data in the distribution of local model updates and exclude them from the aggregation.

Pre-AD methods rely on the assumption that malicious client model updates are similar and use either unsupervised or supervised ML techniques to differentiate between benign and malicious updates. Examples include Krum [blanchard2017machine], AFA [munoz2019byzantine], and Auror [shen2016auror], which use distance measurements such as the Mahalanobis Distance and Cosine Similarity under the assumption of either IID or non-IID data distribution. However, model updates are often high dimensional, making it difficult to apply anomaly detection techniques effectively. To address this, some Pre-AD methods, such as [tolpegin2020data], use dimensional reduction techniques such as PCA to make the data more manageable. These approaches typically rely on the Euclidean Distance for clustering, which can be vulnerable to stealthy attacks like constraint-based attacks [HTBD2018, Bhagoji2019AnalyzingFL]. Other Pre-AD methods, like FoolsGold [fung2018mitigating], inspect client updates based on the similarity of their model updates, with the assumption that malicious updates will behave more similarly to benign updates. By identifying updates that are too similar, it is possible to identify and exclude malicious clients.

Anomaly detection can be performed using machine learning techniques, such as clustering and graph-based methods. For example, in [sattler2020byzantine], model updates are divided into clusters based on Cosine Distance, and in [preuveneers2018chained], an unsupervised deep learning anomaly detection system is integrated into a blockchain process. Graph-based anomaly detection is proposed in [cao2019understanding], where the authors build a graph of local model updates and identify benign models by solving a maximum clique problem. Anomaly-based systems based on Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) have been tested on IoT-specific datasets in [nguyen2019diot]. Li et al [li2020learning] propose a spectral anomaly detection framework using a latent space and an encoder-decoder model. Malicious updates are identified as those that produce higher generation errors than benign ones.

Defenses against malicious clients in FL can be vulnerable to certain attack scenarios and impose strong assumptions about the adversary’s capabilities. Multi-krum [Blanchard2017MachineLW] fails to mitigate edge-case backdoor attacks [wang2020attack] in non-IID data distributions, and FoolGold [fung2018mitigating] is vulnerable to constrain-and-scale attacks [bagdasaryan2020backdoor]. To address this, Nguyen et al. [Nguyen2022-FLAME, nguyen2021flguard] studied multi-target backdoor attacks, which do not assume a fixed number of adversaries or data distribution. FLAME [Nguyen2022-FLAME] uses the HDBSCAN algorithm to detect malicious updates, and Flguard [nguyen2021flguard] combines model filtering with poison elimination to detect and remove malicious updates and is robust against inference attacks. However, Flguard requires more computational resources than traditional FL processes. Li et al. [li2020learning]’s method is effective at detecting multi-trigger backdoor attacks while maintaining high prediction accuracy for the benign main task.

There are two main approaches for addressing malicious clients in FL: total exclusion and impact reduction. The first approach removes poisoned updates from malicious clients before aggregating updates from all clients [Blanchard2017MachineLW, Nguyen2022-FLAME], and is effective when the proportion of malicious clients is high. However, its effectiveness against multi-target backdoor attacks is unknown, and it relies on the assumption that malicious clients will behave similarly at each round or that benign clients will have similar data distributions, which may not hold in certain cases such as fixed-frequency attacks. The second approach reduces the impact of malicious clients on the aggregated model, such as decreasing the learning rate of suspicious clients in FoolsGold [fung2018mitigating]. There is a risk of incorrectly detecting anomalous updates in cases where these assumptions do not hold.

4.2 In-aggregation defense

In-AD mechanism for FL operates while the server is aggregating local models, using techniques such as differential privacy, robust learning rates, smoothness and perturbation, and local validation to mitigate the effects of backdoors.

Differential Privacy (DP). DP has been shown to be effective against backdoors [Sun2019CanYR, naseri2020toward], but it can compromise model performance under data imbalance [2021-AdvancesOPFL, bagdasaryan2019differential], which is common in federated learning. DP-FedAvg [mcmahan2017learning] (Central-DP) is a differentially private aggregation strategy that removes extreme values by clipping the norm of model updates and adding Gaussian noise, but the required amount of noise significantly reduces task accuracy. Sun et al. [Sun2019CanYR] proposed Weak-DP, which adds sufficient Gaussian noise to defeat backdoors and preserve task accuracy, but it is not effective against constrain-based backdoor attacks [wang2020attack]. Additionally, differential privacy-based defenses can potentially affect the benign performance of the global model, as the clipping factors also change the weights of benign model updates [bagdasaryan2020backdoor, wang2020attack].

Model Smoothness and Perturbation. Despite the lack of robustness certification in previous defense approaches, Xie et al. [xie2021crfl] proposed the first general defense framework, CRFL, for training certifiable robust FL models against backdoor attacks. CRFL employs cropping and smoothing of model parameters to control model smoothness and generate sample robustness certification against backdoor attacks with limited amplitude. The smoothness and perturbation method is also used as an additional component to limit the L2-norm of individual updates to improve defense performance [Nguyen2022-FLAME, rieger2022deepsight].

Robust Aggregation Rule. Several approaches have been proposed to address the vulnerability of standard aggregation methods, such as FedAvg [mcmahan2017communication], to backdoor attacks. For example, the use of the geometric median of local parameters as the global model has been proposed in [yin2018byzantine, guerraoui2018hidden]. Another approach is the use of the Median and α𝛼\alpha-trimmed mean, which replaces the arithmetic mean with the median of model updates to increase robustness against attacks [pmlr-v80-yin18a]. Additionally, Ozdayi et al. [ozdayi2021defending] propose the use of Robust Learning Rate (RLR) as an improvement of signSGD [bernstein2018signsgd], which adjusts the server’s learning rate based on the agreement of client updates.

Local validation. BaFFle [andreina2021baffle] is a decentralized feedback-based mechanism that eliminates backdoors by using clients’ data to validate the global model through a supernumerary validation process. Selected clients check the global model by calculating a validation function on secret data and report whether it is backdoored to the orchestration server. The server then decides whether to reject the global model based on inconsistency of misclassification rates per class between the local model and the global model. BaFFle is compliant with secure aggregation, but has limitations: it requires trigger data to activate the backdoor, does not work in non-IID data scenarios with a small number of clients, and is not effective against continuous attacks that corrupt FL training.

In-aggregation defenses, which are applicable in various FL schemes and preserve privacy, have little impact on the training process and are effective against artificial backdoor attacks [Xie2020DBADB, zhang2020defending, Sun2019CanYR]. However, they primarily resist convergence attacks and do not completely discard poisoned local updates, allowing a significant percentage of compromised updates to impact the aggregated model. For example, the geometric median (RFA)[RFA2022] is vulnerable to distributed backdoor attacks[Xie2020DBADB], and RLR [ozdayi2021defending] can cause a trade-off between defense efficiency and performance on the main task. The effectiveness of these defenses and the trade-offs they incur under severe conditions such as a high ratio of malicious clients or non-IID data needs further evaluation.

4.3 Post-aggregation defense

To ensure the integrity of the global model, a protective procedure is implemented after local models from clients, potentially including malicious ones, have been aggregated. The orchestration server subsequently reviews and amends the global model, maintaining valuable information and removing any corrupt updates from malicious clients.

Wu et al. [wu2020mitigating] introduced the first post-aggregation defense strategy for FL against backdoor attacks. Their approach involves identifying and removing neurons with low activation when presented with benign samples, as these neurons are likely to be dormant without the presence of the trigger. To address the issue of the server not having access to private training data, Wu et al. [wu2020mitigating] proposed a distributed pruning strategy. The server asks clients to record neuron activations using their local data and create a local pruning list, which is then used to determine a global pruning sequence. The server can adjust the pruning rate based on the current model’s performance on a validation dataset and gather feedback from clients to finalize the pruning list.

Unlearning has recently gained attention in the field of machine learning [Bourtoule2021MachineU, Neel2021DescenttoDeleteGM, Gupta2021AdaptiveMU], and its application to defend against backdoor attacks in FL has been explored by Wu et al. [Wu2022FederatedUL]. Wu et al. demonstrated the use of Federated Unlearning for removing the effects of single-trigger backdoor attacks without significantly affecting overall performance (e.g., BA = 0%). However, this method requires identifying malicious clients to be unlearned and has only been tested on artificial backdoor attacks, leaving its effectiveness against semantic backdoor attacks unknown.

5 Backdoor Attacks and Defenses Assessment

5.1 Evaluation Metrics

The objective of an adversary’s backdoor attack is to mislead the global model to produce incorrect outputs on backdoored inputs (e.g., the global model classifies images of ”green cars” as ”frogs” in an image classification task). Therefore, the metrics used to evaluate the effectiveness of a backdoor attack are related to the attack’s objective. One metric, called attack success rate (ASR), measures the probability that the output of the backdoored model on targeted inputs matches the adversary’s preference. In general, a higher backdoor accuracy corresponds to a higher attack success rate.

5.2 Backdoor Attacks in Different FL Schemes

5.2.1 Horizontal Federated Learning

We first compare the existing attacks in the following eight dimensions in Table 3. Then, we explain in detail each dimension considered to compare in Table 4.

Backdoor Characteristics. Most of state-of-the-art attacks follow the common attack strategy which combines data poisoning and model poisoning to intensify the backdoor effect. For example, as reported in [Xie2020DBADB], the distributed backdoor attack without scaling achieve low attack success rate (i.e., below 5%); meanwhile, using scale factor wisely can boost the ASR to approximately 100%. Other attack with solely data poisoning requires the continuous attack to achieve successfully backdoor effect (i.e., the malicious partipants are selected in multiple rounds). For instance, in [wang2020attack], with the assumption that one malicious client can join the FL training every 10 rounds, the adversary can achieve almost 80% of backdoor task accuracy in digit classification on EMNIST. The attack which requires only data poisoning is easy to conduct for even non-tech adversary with limited capability and knowledge about the training protocol as well as aggregation operator.

Adversary Capability. In addition to the adversary’s in common threat model, authors in [PoisonGAN, SemiTargetedMP-Sun2022, Liu2022TechnicalRA] proposed using customized local training mechanism instead of simply varying number of local epochs or training parameters, i.e., training additional GAN network to generate poisoning samples in alignment with the data population of the benign clients [PoisonGAN].
To conduct effective backdoor attack, the participant collusion is employed. The higher colluding ratio, the stronger attack (cf. [Sun2019CanYR]). As a result, the backdoor durability is also increased when the adversary owns large number of compromised clients. However, the malicious clients often account for just a small portion among total clients in practical scenario, i.e., [0.01,1]%percent0.011\left[0.01,1\right]\% (cf. [Shejwalkar2021BackTT]). Therefore, the assumption about the large ratio of malicious participants may not be hold. In most of the proposed attacks [fung2018mitigating, wang2020attack, Xie2020DBADB, Nguyen2020PoisoningIoT], the malicious clients are assumed to participate in multiple training rounds, i.e., continuous attack. However, this kind of attack may not be hold practically since the number of participants randomly selected in each training round is much more smaller than total clients, which means the probability that an adequate ratio of ℳℳ\mathcal{M} joining training is very small. The direct solution for this is an effective single-shot attack [bagdasaryan2020backdoor, Liu2022TechnicalRA]. This approach seems practical since each malicious client may be selected only once in the large-scale FL system (cf. [Shejwalkar2021BackTT]).

Attack efficiency. The efficiency of an attack is evaluated under three key dimensions: stealthiness, durability and flexibility.
One of the biggest challenges to backdoor attack designing is that the malicious clients often account for just a small portion among total clients in reality, i.e., [0.01,1]%percent0.011\left[0.01,1\right]\% (cf. [Shejwalkar2021BackTT]). Therefore, the poisoned updates may be easily diluted by the benign updates. This phenomenon is also known as “catastrophic forget” in machine learning. The techniques aim to replace the global model with the poisoned one [bagdasaryan2020backdoor] or continuously remind the global model about the backdoor task by continuous participation of malicious clients [Xie2020DBADB] can prolong the backdoor effect. Another approach tried to implant backdoor into the neurons which are least overlapped with the active neurons for the main task by employing the rare samples [rare-Yoo2022BackdoorAI, wang2020attack]. More recently, the durability of the backdoor is more seriously taken into account in [Neurotoxin, Liu2022TechnicalRA, Zhou2021DeepMP] and this opens a new novelty to design a robust and durable backdoor attack.
Conspicuous backdoor attack is hard to be practical since the various defenses available for aggregation server. It is trivial that the scaled malicious updates may have abnormally large values compared to the benign ones, which may easily detected.

Realizing this fact, many approaches consider utilizing additional techniques together with scaling the poisoned updates to make the backdoor more resistant against a range of defense mechanisms (i.e., PGD attack [Sun2019CanYR], constrain-and-scale [wang2020attack]). However, whether these techniques are robust against state-of-the-art defense mechanisms is still an open question.
The flexibility of a backdoor attack strategy is an important factor to be considered in real-world scenario. Therefore, an attack should not depend on many constraints such as training stage (i.e., early training rounds or converged training rounds), number of malicious participants, etc.

5.2.2 Vertical Federated Learning

Due to the difference in the mechanism between Horizontal FL and Vertical FL, the attack strategy is varied. The following attack scheme is considered: (i) the attacker has access to the training data of one or more data parties Dmsubscript𝐷𝑚D_{m} and it controls the training procedure and local hyperparameters of attacked parties. (ii) The attacker can modify local updates such as training weight and gradients before sending transmitted data to other parties. (iii) The attacker does not control any benign parties’ training nor does the attacker control the global communication and aggregation protocols. (iv) Contrary to traditional data poisoning attacks which focus on poisoning training data, the attacker in collaborative learning focus on poisoning gradients and model updates that are communicated among parties in the protocol. The main difference between backdoor attacks on Horizontal FL and Vertical FL is that: in VFL, the attacker only has access to a portion of the feature space of parties that it controls, and it may not have access to labels if it doesn’t control the active party. Moreover, it also has only a portion of the model parameters of the parties it controls.

VFL is under-studied for the lack of datasets and benchmarks [Cai2022AcceleratingVF], so there are not so much effort put for the backdoor attack on VFL. To the best of our knowledge, [Liu2021BatchLI-VFL] first introduced Gradient-replacement backdoor by wisely swap the gradients of benign and poisoned samples before sending back to the active party. This method is proved to be effective in the later work [Zou2022VFL] even when the communication between parties in FL is protected by Homomorphic encryption (HE) [HE2020].

5.3 Confrontation between Backdoor Attacks and Defenses

Adversaries and defenders are engaged in a never-ending battle. The conflict between them deepens our understanding of backdoor attacks. Attackers are always looking for ways to make poisoned attacks more covert, effective, and resistant to countermeasures. The primary objective for defenders is to successfully thwart as many kinds of unexpected backdoor attacks as possible. We summarize attack strategies and the corresponding applicable defenses in Table 5.

As shown in Table 5, most of defense strategies focus on the scenarios of label-flipping backdoor attacks, in which the adversary changes the label of targeted inputs into his expected one. Although a defense is often designed against multiple attacks, many attack strategies have not been addressed such as: noise-instance, coordinated-trigger, information leakage based, and partially poisoning backdoor attacks.

On the other hand, each countermeasure approach is often applicable to a group of attack strategies. Particularly, pre–aggregation methods (e.g., Krum, FoolsGold, AUROR) seems to be efficient under semantic backdoor attacks. Furthermore, in–aggregation methods are primarily utilized under artificial backdoor attacks, specifically single-trigger attacks. However, the more sophisticated attack strategies such as distributed-trigger and coordinated-trigger, have not been evaluated under the presence of these defenses.

6 Challenges and Future Research Directions

6.1 Realistic Backdoor Attack Scenarios

Backdoor attacks in FL can have serious consequences in a variety of real-world applications. For example, in the healthcare sector, a FL scenario is used to train a ML model to predict patient outcomes based on medical records. If an attacker were able to insert a backdoor, the model could potentially make incorrect predictions with serious consequences for patients’ health. In the banking sector, a FL scenario is used to train a ML model to identify fraudulent transactions. If an attacker were able to insert a backdoor, the model could potentially misclassify legitimate transactions as fraudulent, causing unnecessary disruptions and costs for banks and customers.

Other applications of FL that are vulnerable to backdoor attacks include smart cities, where FL is used to optimize traffic flow and reduce congestion; predictive maintenance, where FL is used to predict equipment failures; the Internet of Things (IoT), where FL is used to optimize energy consumption; supply chain management, where FL is used to optimize the flow of goods and materials; predictive analytics, where FL is used to predict customer demand and optimize product inventory; cybersecurity, where FL is used to detect and prevent cyber attacks; agriculture, where FL is used to optimize crop yields and reduce waste; environmental monitoring, where FL is used to monitor and predict environmental changes; and credit risk assessment, where FL is used to assess credit risk for loan applicants. In each of these scenarios, if an attacker were able to insert a backdoor, it could potentially lead to incorrect decisions and negative consequences.

6.2 Backdoor Design Issues

Limited Applicable Domains. Backdoor attacks in FL have been mostly studied for image classification [Sun2019CanYR, ALittleIsEnough2019, Xie2020DBADB, Gong2022CoordinatedBA] and next word prediction [HTBD2018] tasks. However, existing schemes may not be directly transferable to other domains due to differences in sample nature. Customized strategies may be needed to conduct backdoor attacks in specialized domains such as smart cities [Zheng2021FederatedLI] or IoT intrusion systems [Nguyen2020PoisoningIoT]. Some applications of FL, such as environmental monitoring [Hu2018FederatedRA] and reducing network congestion [Wang2019InEdgeAI], lack study on backdoor attacks and require further investigation.

Attacking with Limited Knowledge. It is common in FL backdoor attacks for the attacker to assume that they have knowledge of the distribution of the training dataset and that the majority of the training dataset for malicious clients is composed of clean samples [HTBD2018]. However, it is unclear whether these assumptions still hold in the rapidly developing field of FL. Additionally, it is uncertain if current attack strategies can succeed if these assumptions are not met. It would be interesting to explore the possibility of designing attack strategies that require limited assumptions and can be applied in various scenarios, such as in large-scale FL systems with limited knowledge about system operations.

Backdoor Effect Dilution. Designing a stealthy yet durable backdoor attack is a major challenge in the field of FL, as malicious clients often only make up a small proportion of total participating clients (typically between 0.01% and 1%). While continuous attack and scaling methods can help overcome this issue, they must also be evaluated for their stealthiness and feasibility in realistic scenarios. Some researchers have proposed using redundant space within a neural network architecture to covertly implant a backdoor, while still allowing the attacker to scale up the poisoned updates (e.g. [Zhou2021DeepMP, Neurotoxin]).

FL Attacking with Highly Heterogeneous Data. Backdoor attacks in HFL and VFL can be less effective when the data is highly diverse. In these cases, FTL can be a useful approach. In FTL, knowledge learned from one domain is transferred and fine-tuned for use in a related domain. It has been applied in various industries, particularly in the healthcare sector [Jing2019QuantifyingTP, Chen2020CommunicationEfficientFD], and has been successful in solving problems related to treatment and diagnosis between hospitals [Rahman2021ChallengesAA]. To date, there has not been any research on backdoor attacks in FTL.

FL with Heterogeneous Clients. In recent years, researchers have explored the use of edge computing platforms in FL systems, in which an edge server acts as a federal server and clients within its communication range collaborate to train a deep learning model [mao2017survey, nishio2019client, wang2019adaptive, tran2019federated]. Edge-based FL systems have been shown to reduce model training time and energy consumption compared to cloud-based FL systems [Briggs2020FederatedLW, Yang2021HFLAH, Lim2021DynamicEA]. However, the possibility of inserting backdoor attacks in hierarchical FL systems must be taken into consideration to ensure the privacy and trustworthiness of these systems in real-world applications.

Backdoor Attack in Physical World. Current attack strategies typically use an artificial procedure to insert a trigger for a backdoor, such as a small pattern in images during training and testing. However, these attacks can be affected by the loss of the trigger, such as when an image is captured by a camera from a display or printed photo. The effectiveness of such attacks depends on the location and appearance of the trigger, as discussed in [Wenger2021BackdoorAA]. Therefore, it is important to evaluate current backdoor threats in physical FL systems. A hybrid attack that works with both digital and physical triggers may be a promising approach for implementing effective backdoor attacks in FL.

6.3 Robustness of Backdoor Defenses

False detection of benign clients. Anomaly detection [fung2018mitigating, shen2016auror, li2020learning, nguyen2021flguard, nguyen2022flame] is a common technique for protecting against backdoor attacks in FL systems. However, it can sometimes incorrectly identify benign clients as malicious or vice versa, leading to false detection. This can occur when the model updates from many attackers are diverse, or when the model updates from benign clients are slightly different. To effectively protect against backdoor attacks, it is important for the defense to accurately distinguish between malicious and benign clients.

Differential Privacy in FL. DP is a framework that protects the privacy of individuals in a dataset by adding noise to the data before it is released or used for analysis. It has been proposed for use in FL [bagdasaryan2020backdoor, Sun2019CanYR] but has several limitations. DP requires a large number of clients to be effective, as the noise level needs to be high enough to mask the presence or absence of any individual client’s data. It may also degrade model performance and may not prevent all types of privacy attacks, such as attribute inference or model inversion. Additionally, DP may not be suitable for all FL scenarios depending on the data being used and the clients’ privacy requirements. It is important to consider these limitations and trade-offs when using DP in FL settings.

Backdoor in various AI-domains. Backdoor attacks are generally easier to detect and defend against in the CV domain than in the NLP domain, according to empirical studies. For example, Wan et al.[wan2021robust] found that ASR using the FedAvg algorithm was less than 75% effective with most defenses when one of ten clients was malicious in CV tasks. However, Yoo et al.[yoo2022backdoor] found that ASR was easily more than 95% effective on most attacks with most defenses when one of ten clients was malicious in NLP tasks. One reason for this difference may be that detecting NLP backdoors is more difficult. There is increasing interest in using FL in automatic speech recognition [cui2021federated, dimitriadis2020federated, mdhaffar2021study, guliani2021training], but the risk of backdoor attacks is a concern that needs to be addressed. Future research may focus on developing effective strategies for defending against and detecting backdoor attacks in the automatic speech recognition domain.

Fairness and Privacy Violation. It is important that the application of a defense mechanism in a FL setting does not impact the fairness among the participating clients. For example, efforts to improve the robustness of FL systems may result in the unfair treatment of honest clients, as their updates may be rejected from the aggregation process if they lie far from the distribution of other updates, as discussed in [wang2020attack]. This fact raises the question about the compensation between the fairness and robustness of FL systems in the presence of backdoor defenses. Additionally, some defense mechanisms rely on inspecting model updates to study the training data, which can increase the risk of membership inference and model inversion attacks [nguyen2022flame, rieger2022deepsight]. Therefore, it is important to carefully consider whether a specific defense mechanism is appropriate and to explore more secure defense strategies.

Rethinking Current Defenses. Secure aggregation [bonawitz2017practical] is a technique used in FL to enable model updates from multiple clients to be combined while preserving privacy. It uses cryptography techniques such as homomorphic encryption and secret sharing to allow clients to compute updates without revealing their data to other clients or the orchestration server. However, secure aggregation may make FL systems more vulnerable to poisoning attacks as individual updates cannot be inspected. The effectiveness of adversarial training in FL is uncertain, as it was developed for settings with IID data and its performance in non-IID settings is unknown. HFL, which involves data distributed across clients with similar characteristics, is the most common form of FL and has a wide range of applications. In contrast, VFL involves data distributed across clients with different characteristics (e.g., different types of devices or different organizations) and TFL involves transferring knowledge from one set of clients to another. Both VFL and TFL are relatively new areas of research, and there is still much to be explored in terms of potential backdoor attacks and how to defend against them.

7 Conclusion

In summary, backdoor attacks in FL pose a significant threat to the security and privacy of FL systems. These attacks can be triggered in various ways, including artificial and semantic triggers, and can be launched by a single client or a group of clients. To defend against these attacks, various approaches have been proposed, including pre-aggregation defenses, in-aggregation defenses, and post-aggregation defenses. Each of these approaches has its own advantages and limitations, and their effectiveness depends on the specific characteristics of the attack. Moreover, the robustness of these defenses in the face of various types of attacks, particularly in non-IID scenarios, remains an open research question. In the future, it will be important to continue developing more robust defense techniques that are effective against semantic backdoor attacks, improving the efficiency of defense techniques, studying the effectiveness of defenses under realistic attack scenarios, examining the impact of data heterogeneity on backdoor attacks and defenses, and investigating the impact of system-level factors on backdoor attacks and defenses. By addressing these research areas, it will be possible to make progress in understanding and addressing the risks of backdoor attacks in federated learning systems and to develop more secure and effective defense strategies against a wide range of attacks. It is also important to consider the potential for physical backdoor attacks and to explore potential defenses against these types of attacks. In addition, research on the effectiveness of backdoor defenses in specific AI domains, such as automatic speech recognition, could be valuable in developing targeted and effective protection mechanisms.
