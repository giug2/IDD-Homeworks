Abstract

Federated learning (FL) enables collaborative model training while preserving data privacy, making it suitable for decentralized human-centered AI applications. However, a significant research gap remains in ensuring fairness in these systems. Current fairness strategies in FL require knowledge of bias-creating/sensitive attributes, clashing with FL’s privacy principles. Moreover, in human-centered datasets, sensitive attributes may remain latent. To tackle these challenges, we present a novel bias mitigation approach inspired by “Fairness without Demographics" in machine learning. The presented approach achieves fairness without needing knowledge of sensitive attributes by minimizing the top eigenvalue of the Hessian matrix during training, ensuring equitable loss landscapes across FL participants. Notably, we introduce a novel FL aggregation scheme that promotes participating models based on error rates and loss landscape curvature attributes, fostering fairness across the FL system. This work represents the first approach to attaining “Fairness without Demographics" in human-centered FL. Through comprehensive evaluation, our approach demonstrates effectiveness in balancing fairness and efficacy across various real-world applications, FL setups, and scenarios involving single and multiple bias-inducing factors, representing a significant advancement in human-centered FL.

1 Introduction

The integration of advanced machine learning (ML) algorithms with ubiquitous sensing technologies has revolutionized human-centered computing li2021deep ; puccinelli2005wireless , enabling everyday devices, like smartphones and wearables in collecting rich sensory data, fueling myriad AI-driven applications from gesture recognition zhang2021widar3 to stress detection vos2023generalizable .

Edge and distributed computing have emerged as pivotal paradigms in this landscape zhou2019edge , with federated learning (FL) gaining prominence as a decentralized training approach mcmahan2017communication . FL facilitates model training across multiple edge devices while maintaining data localization, thereby respecting user privacy zhang2020fairfl and mitigating risks associated with centralized data repositories. This aligns with regulatory constraints such as the European General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), or the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) abay2020mitigating . FL’s suitability for practical human-centered AI applications has led to its adoption in various domains, including health monitoring epstein2020mapping , productivity tracking kim2016timeaware , and activity recognition shaik2022fedstack via wearable devices and smartphones.

Recent literature has drawn attention to the inadequate consideration of fairness in human-centered AI and sensing methodologies yfantidou2023beyond ; xu2023globem ; zhang2023framework . Notably, FL presents unique challenges in achieving fairness. The decentralized nature of data can result in varying data distributions across devices, potentially amplifying biases djebrouni2024bias . State-of-the-art FL fairness approaches encounter a significant challenge due to the inherent trade-off between fairness and privacy chen2023privacy . These approaches often necessitate access to individuals’ sensitive information, whether at the client or server level, thus contradicting FL’s privacy-preserving nature zeng2021improving ; papadaki2022minimax ; djebrouni2024bias ; zhang2020fairfl ; ezzeldin2023fairfed . Consequently, there arises a pressing need to enhance fairness in FL without explicitly requiring knowledge of sensitive or bias-creating attributes.

Moreover, human-centered FL may inadvertently perpetuate biased treatment of factors that are not explicitly recognized as requiring fairness protection during model training. For example, indoor sensing using Wi-Fi channel state information (CSI) is affected by environmental variables like building materials. In FL-based Wi-Fi CSI activity detection training, bias may arise if most users reside in concrete buildings. Since building material information is absent from most datasets, addressing such biases without explicit knowledge of the influencing attribute, such as building materials, is essential, a concept termed “Fairness without Demographics" lahoti2020fairness . Moreover, conventional human-centered sensing datasets typically lack individuals’ sensitive information, further underscoring the necessity of such fairness in human-centered FL yfantidou2023beyond ; mehrabi2021survey ; zhang2023framework .

While recent literature has explored “Fairness without Demographics" in centralized AI training lahoti2020fairness ; hashimoto2018fairness ; chai2022fairness , its compatibility with the decentralized FL paradigm has been deemed challenging due to the absence of knowledge regarding global data distributions during the local edge model training as discussed in Section 3. To our knowledge, this paper is the first to tackle the “Fairness without Demographics" challenge within the context of human-centered FL. The contributions of the paper are:

  • •

    Since centralized-based Fairness without Demographics methodologies are incompatible with FL due to non-shareable training instances, disjoint models, and loss landscapes, the paper addresses the challenge by introducing a novel approach, named Hessian-Aware Federated Learning (HA-FL). HA-FL leverages recent literature on loss-landscape curvature attributes, specifically sharpness, to promote fairness tran2022pruning ; wang2023robust . Unlike prior works, HA-FL not only utilizes loss-landscape sharpness to ensure fairness in locally trained models but also introduces a local models’ sharpness aware aggregation phase, attaining an improved fairness and efficacy balance. HA-FL offers a robust alternative to traditional bias mitigation schemes in FL, preserves privacy, and addresses challenges posed by human-centered data, requiring no knowledge of the bias-creating attribute that requires fairness protection.

  • •

    In human-centered FL, it’s typical for a single individual to use an edge platform like a smartphone. However, state-of-the-art (SOTA) FL approaches that achieve fairness by leveraging known sensitive information encounter challenges in such scenarios. Nonetheless, HA-FL exhibits equitable performance across FL systems with single individuals, multiple-person setups, or a combination of both on-edge platforms (referred to as clients in FL terms), thereby enhancing its practicality in real-world human-centered AI applications (Section 6.2, RQ3).

  • •

    Human-centered data may have multiple sensitive attributes at the same time, creating bias. Where SOTA FL approaches that achieve fairness by leveraging known sensitive information face challenges in improving fairness for multiple sensitive attributes simultaneously, HA-FL excels in attaining fairness for all, making it highly impactful. (Section 6.2, RQ4)

  • •

    We demonstrate the effectiveness of HA-FL using three real-world human sensing datasets, each involving distinct sensing technologies, applications, and sensitive attribute information. Our comprehensive assessments establish HA-FL’s superior performance in enhancing fairness and efficacy balance in human-centered AI, even without knowing the bias-creating factors.

3 Background and Related Work

Fairness in ML has gained significant attention zhang2019faht ; zhang2020crowd ; dwork2012fairness , initially focusing on removing or obfuscating sensitive attributes in raw data before training dwork2012fairness , but resulting in sub-optimal solutions zhang2020fairfl . Recent studies explore manipulating the loss function to enforce fairness constraints during model training zhang2019faht . However, these strategies face challenges in FL due to privacy constraints pessach2022review ; djebrouni2024bias .

Recent research has attempted to address bias mitigation in FL by ) by incorporating fairness constraints at the client side zeng2021improving ; papadaki2022minimax . However, achieving fairness solely on the client side does not ensure overall fairness ezzeldin2023fairfed ; djebrouni2024bias . Newer approaches propose bias mitigation during aggregation djebrouni2024bias ; ezzeldin2023fairfed ; li2019fair ; zhang2020fairfl or a hybrid approach combining client-side and aggregation-side bias mitigation abay2020mitigating ; cui2021addressing ; caldarola2022improving ; meerza2024glocalfair . Further details on them are in Appendix B.1. Notably, HA-FL, follows the hybrid framework.

Recent literature outside of FL explores fairness strategies without explicitly considering sensitive attribute information, known as ‘Fairness without Demographics’ lahoti2020fairness ; chai2022fairness ; chai2022self . One approach lahoti2020fairness identifies and boosts instances that are relatively less addressed without considering sensitive attributes. However, in FL, each client operates with its own training instances, making it challenging to identify such instances and apply this approach directly. Another approach chai2022fairness ; chai2022self involves knowledge distillation to enhance fairness starting from pre-trained biased models. However, in FL, models are not centrally shared, hindering the application of knowledge distillation approaches. Additionally, another recent study liu2024fairlisa introduced an approach requiring users’ private information as model input, conflicting with FL’s privacy-preserving nature. Further details are in Appendix B.2.

Hence, none of these approaches serve as true baselines for FL, and achieving ‘Fairness without Demographics’ in FL remains an unaddressed research question.

4 Motivation behind the Approach

First, this section presents a preliminary study investigating bias among FL clients based on various factors/attributes. We utilized recent FL approaches without fairness-awareness or constraints, such as FedAvg, FedSAM, and FedSWA, employing an off-the-shelf gesture recognition model on the WIDAR dataset zhang2021widar3 . Our analysis revealed bias in F1 scores based on sex across all approaches, as depicted in Figure 1.

As outlined in Section 3, current Fairness without Demographics methodologies are incompatible with FL due to the distinct characteristics of FL systems, including non-shareable training instances, disjoint models, and loss landscapes. To address this challenge, this paper draws upon recent literature examining the impact of loss curvature attributes, specifically its sharpness during model training/optimization on fairness tran2022pruning ; wang2023robust .

The maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian matrix H𝐻H, associated with the classification loss during model training, indicates the steepest direction on the loss surface jastrzkebski2018relation . A higher top eigenvalue implies a sharper minimum in the loss landscape, while a lower top eigenvalue suggests a flatter minimum, often preferred for the stability and generalizability of neural networks caldarola2022improving . Recent literature suggests that minimizing disparity in the top eigenvalue of H𝐻H across distributions/groups of sensitive attributes Djksubscriptsuperscript𝐷𝑘𝑗D^{k}_{j} mitigates unfairness due to these factors.

This section investigates whether FL training conforms to the literature discussed above. Figure 2 displays the top eigenvalues for the sensitive attribute ‘sex’ across two distributions, Dmalesexsubscriptsuperscript𝐷sexmaleD^{\text{sex}}_{\text{male}} and Dfemalesexsubscriptsuperscript𝐷sexfemaleD^{\text{sex}}_{\text{female}}, for three above-discussed non-fairness-aware FL approaches using the WIDAR dataset. The visualization exposes a disparity in the top eigenvalue between the two distributions for all FL approaches, indicating that the relationship between fairness and loss landscape attributes observed in the literature extends to FL systems.

This observation inspired the development of our loss-landscape sharpness aware HA-FL strategy, which utilizes the top eigenvalue of the Hessian to attain flatter and equitable loss-landscapes for all local client models, leading to achieving Fairness without Demographics.

5 Approach

Figure 3 provides an overview of HA-FL. At each round of the FL process, every client utilizes its own individuals’ data to train local models, aiming to optimize accuracy and ensure fairness. Subsequent to local training in each round, the server aggregates the individual client models, promoting increased fairness and accuracy, guided by our Sharpness-aware aggregation strategy. The aggregated global model is then redistributed to the clients for the next round of the FL process. The following sections discuss the design choices and details of the HA-FL approach.

6 Experimental Evaluation

In this section, we analyze the performance of HA-FL approach under different configurations and datasets.

6.1 Datasets, Models,and Metrics

Only a few human-sensing datasets encompass data from diverse demographic groups. This section performs comprehensive evaluations on three such human-centered datasets: EDA xiao2024reading with 48 participants, WIDAR zhang2021widar3 with 16 participants, and HAR (HugaDB) chereshnev2018hugadb with 18 participants (details in Appendix D.1). Each dataset comprises known specific bias contributing factors: ‘orientation’ relative to the sensor and ‘sex’ for WIDAR; the ‘hand’ in which the smartwatch is worn and ‘sex’ of the participant for EDA; and ‘sex’ alone for HugaDB. Although HA-FL does not require knowledge of sensitive attributes during training, we leveraged the aforementioned factors from each dataset as proxies for all unknown factors to evaluate the effectiveness of HA-FL.

The data partitioning for training and testing maintains a consistent 80:20 ratio across each client participant, ensuring balanced representation in our assessments. Each dataset is paired with its dedicated neural network model (detailed in Appendix D.3), adapted from off-the-shelf benchmark models in the literature yang2023benchmark ; sharma2022psychophysiological ; pirttikangas2006feature , tailored for FL classification tasks. Specifically, we conducted gesture recognition using WiFi signals on WIDAR, human activity recognition using accelerometer and gyroscope readings for Hugadb, and stress detection based on physiological parameters and accelerometer readings for the EDA dataset. We have meticulously selected and tuned multiple hyperparameters for each of the employed models, as detailed in Appendix D.4.

Evaluation Metrics: To measure the effectiveness of FL, we utilized F1 Scores and Accuracy, while for assessing fairness, we employed Equal Opportunity Gap (EO gap) metrics, in line with existing literature ezzeldin2023fairfed ; djebrouni2024bias . Further details can be found in Appendix A.

6.2.4 RQ4: Could HA-FL improve fairness for multiple sensitive attributes simultaneously?

7 Broader Impact and Limitation

This first of its kind paper addresses an important research gap lies at the intersection of fairness and federated learning in the context of human-centered applications and sets the stage for a more equitable and responsible future in decentralized human-centered computing, ensuring that the benefits of technological advancements are accessible and fair to all.

Limitation: Achieving fairness in FL systems requires attaining a balance between fairness and model performance gu2022privacy . The quest to ensure fairness for individual clients often results in reduced performance across the board, an issue highlighted in literature ezzeldin2023fairfed ; zhang2020fairfl ; al2014crowd ; wang2019age . Although HA-FL demonstrates significant effectiveness in achieving this balance, as discussed in Section 6.2, RQ1, it exhibits minor performance degradation in the WIDAR dataset. This could be attributed to our aggregation scheme, outlined in Section 5.1 Equation 3, which equally weighs low error rates and lower maximal uncertainty, thus limiting the ability to balance accuracy and fairness. The issue could be resolved by introducing a hyper-parameter as the weighting factor.

8 Conclusion

This paper introduces HA-FL, a novel bias mitigation strategy and an innovative Sharpness-aware aggregation scheme which addresses the challenge of incorporating fairness into human-centered FL without needing knowledge of sensitive information or bias-inducing attributes thereby upholding the privacy-preserving trait of FL. Through thorough evaluation, HA-FL proves its effectiveness in striking a balance between fairness and performance across a variety of real-world applications and FL setups, addressing both single and multiple bias factors without the need for knowledge of sensitive attributes. HA-FL paves the way for equitable and privacy-preserving decentralized human-centered AI learning, showcasing its potential for substantial impact.

A.1 A Primer on Fairness

This primer provides an overview of the algorithmic fairness dwork2012fairness in regard to machine learning applications and the commonly used metric for evaluating fairness. In machine learning (ML), where models learn from large datasets, any existing biases in the data can be amplified and perpetuated by the model hall2022systematic , causing unfair decision-making. There are multiple views of model fairness as per recent literature, like individual castelnovo2022clarification and group fairness raz2021group . In centralized ML, prior works on fairness without demographics hashimoto2018fairness ; lahoti2020fairness ; chai2022fairness ensure group fairness. Group fairness is inspired by the intuitive idea of equality castelnovo2022clarification , meaning that group fairness is focused on the equitable treatment of different groups, often defined by sensitive attributes like race, sex, age, etc. The scope of this work is achieving group fairness for FL systems, as outlined in Section 2, ‘Problem Statement’ of the main paper. The next section discusses a common fairness metric we have employed to measure the fairness of our system.

A.3 A Primer on Federated Learning

Federated learning (FL) mcmahan2017communication represents a paradigm shift in distributed machine learning, focusing on training algorithms across decentralized devices while keeping all the training data local djebrouni2024bias ; zeng2021improving . This method is fundamentally designed to address privacy concerns inherent in traditional centralized machine learning approaches zhang2020fairfl . In FL, a global model is initially distributed to all participants (in FL term clients), each of whom possesses a local dataset. These clients independently train the (local) model on their data and then send only the model parameters (or parameter updates), not the actual data, to a central server. The central server aggregates these updates to refine the global model and sends it back to the clients. This iterative process continues until the model achieves satisfactory performance. This approach ensures that sensitive data remains on the client’s local device mcmahan2017communication , thereby significantly mitigating privacy and security risks.

Mathematically, FL aims to minimize a global objective function, which is typically an aggregation of local objective functions corresponding to each client’s (participating entity’s) dataset. The formulation can be expressed as follows:

F​(w)=1K​∑k=1KFk​(w)𝐹𝑤1𝐾superscriptsubscript𝑘1𝐾subscript𝐹𝑘𝑤F(w)=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}F_{k}(w)

Here, F​(w)𝐹𝑤F(w) denotes the global objective function, w𝑤w represents the model parameters, K𝐾K is the number of clients participating in the learning process, and Fk​(w)subscript𝐹𝑘𝑤F_{k}(w) is the local objective function for the kt​hsuperscript𝑘𝑡ℎk^{th} client. The model parameters are updated based on the aggregated updates from all clients, reflecting the collaborative nature of learning in federated settings.

This privacy-preserving technique, coupled with its distributed nature, positions FL as a pivotal method in the field of distributed (i.e., de-centralized) machine learning, particularly for applications involving sensitive or personal data.

Appendix B Prior works

B.1 Bias Mitigation in FL

Bias in FL systems has been extensively studied by researchers cui2021addressing ; zeng2021improving ; djebrouni2024bias ; abay2020mitigating in recent years. Broadly, the bias mitigation for FL systems can be following types:

  1. 1.

    Performance-Based Fairness: This approach focuses on equitable decisions across all client models. It involves mechanisms at either the client or server level or both to enhance fairness across all participating entities (in human-centered AI, across all individuals irrespective of which client they belong to), particularly benefiting under-performing groups or clients abay2020mitigating ; zeng2021improving ; cui2021addressing ; mohri2019agnostic . This definition of fairness is in line with the focus of this paper.

  2. 2.

    Contribution Fairness: In traditional FL, due to bandwidth constraints and budget limitations, only a subset of clients is selected for each training round, leaving out some training data. One significant consequence is the potential for bias in the global model. When certain clients are consistently left out of the training process or have limited opportunities to contribute their data, their specific patterns or characteristics may be underrepresented or entirely absent in the global model. As a result, the global model may not adequately generalize to the data distributions of these excluded clients, leading to subpar efficacy for them. Several aggregation-time client selection strategies are developed to address such bias huang2022stochastic ; song2019profit , which are out of scope of this paper.

Our work focuses on achieving Performance-based Fairness for FL systems. The following paragraphs provide a detailed discussion of this.

B.1.1 Client-side Fairness Approaches in FL:

Client-side approaches put constraints on the local client-side model training to attain fairness. In client-based approaches, Li et al. li2021ditto introduced a framework for developing distinct models for each device aimed at enhancing fairness and robustness. This method addresses data heterogeneity at the client level by incorporating a regularization term in the training process. This term aligns the client model with the optimal global model at each training round, guiding each client towards the global model iteratively. Zeng et al. zeng2021improving introduced FedFB, a method that integrates FL with the Fair Batch technique roh2020fairbatch . This approach modifies the weight of samples during the client training phase to mitigate imbalances in data distribution among various groups. The objective of FedFB is to prevent the model from disproportionately benefiting one group over others. Du et al. du2021fairness ensured fairness at the client level by incorporating a fairness constraint as a hyperparameter directly in the client loss function. The constraint ensures that the client not only focuses on achieving higher accuracy but also on maintaining fairness across various data distributions. The fairness constraint acts as a regularizer in the optimization process and guides the models to avoid solutions that work well on average but perform poorly for specific groups or data distributions it encounters during the deployment. Agnostic FL mohri2019agnostic also works by regularization at the client level. It uses min-max optimization to minimize the worst-case client loss. Another work papadaki2022minimax used the min-max optimization strategy where each client calculates an empirical risk factor based on the sensitive attributes at the client level, which is then sent to the server where the server calculates an importance score based on client risk in order to reduce the worst-case risk. The Fairness-aware Agnostic FL du2021fairness also works on the min-max principle, where fairness is achieved by incorporating a fairness constraint in the client loss function. The fairness constraint acts as a regularizer in the optimization process, guiding the model to avoid solutions that would perform well on average but poorly for specific groups or distributions.

Notably, all the current state-of-the-art client-side fairness approaches in FL need knowledge of sensitive attributes li2021ditto ; zeng2021improving ; du2021fairness ; papadaki2022minimax ; du2021fairness or works under the assumption of clients having access to data from all sensitive attributes djebrouni2024bias ; mohri2019agnostic . Moreover, access to sensitive attributes might not be available due to privacy and ethical obligations abay2020mitigating in FL.

B.1.2 Server-side Fairness Approaches in FL:

Server-side approaches focus on the aggregation step in the FL process to promote system-wide fairness. Ezzeldin et al. ezzeldin2023fairfed propose an aggregation scheme for FL systems. Their approach, FairFed, which is agnostic to the local debiasing mechanism, works by each client performing local debiasing on its own local dataset, thus maintaining data decentralization and avoiding the exchange of any explicit information about its local data composition. To amplify the local debiasing performance, the clients evaluate the fairness of the global model on their local datasets in each FL round and collectively collaborate with the server to adjust its model aggregation weights. However, during their local debiasing mechanism and calculation of aggregation weights, FairFed requires access to information about the data distribution, such as sensitive group membership. FairFl zhang2020fairfl is another server-side fairness approach for FL systems that uses multi-agent reinforcement learning to optimize both fairness and accuracy under privacy constraints. They propose a client selection scheme based on team Markov game theory zhang2020fairfl . FairFL requires the calculation of an estimated Shapley value for different demographic groups, which represents the sensitivity of the model accuracy to different demographic groups in the client data distributions and requires access to sensitive information about the data. More recently, Djebrouni. et al. djebrouni2024bias propose Astral, which is an aggregation approach that selects clients for aggregation weights using mutation and crossover operators from the Differential Evolution algorithm. This process generates a diverse range of candidate solutions for aggregation weights, which are then used to compute global aggregated models. However, during the selection and aggregation of clients, Astral requires access to information about sensitive attributes of the data. Hence conflicting with the privacy-preserving nature of FL.

B.1.3 Hybrid (Client+Server) Fairness Approaches in FL:

There are several works that provide a hybrid bias mitigation strategy for FL systems that works at both the client and server side. Abay et al. abay2020mitigating proposed that the local fair training at the client level and local model re-weighting based on the knowledge of local sensitive attributes improves fairness at the client level. They also propose a global model re-weighting scheme if the client shares the data with the server. Cui et al. cui2021addressing proposed that each client maintains a fairness budget and local model weights are updated (by multiplying by factor alpha) such that the worst-case loss is minimized during aggregation. In the aggregation, they prioritize the worst-performing clients in order to minimize the worst-case performance.

We have compared the efficacy of HA-FL with FedSAM and FedSWA models, which were presented by a recent study caldarola2022improving that aims to improve the generalizability of FL systems by promoting optimal clients model by using Sharpness-aware minimization (SAM) at the client level and Stochastic-weight averaging (SWA) at the server level. They analyze the Hessian eigenvalues and show that using this training protocol in FL systems helps improve convergence and generalizability, specifically in cases of heterogeneous data distributions. Notably, we compared HA-FL to this approach as it improves model performance on different distributions in overall client data by enhancing model generalizability without accessing any sensitive information at the client or server level. During our evaluations, we observed that FedSAM and FedSWA did provide improvements to the model fairness as well. However, HA-FL outperforms both of these models consistently for all three datasets

B.2 Unknown Demographic Fairness

This section discusses prior works in machine learning aimed at enhancing fairness and reducing bias, particularly in scenarios where protected or unknown subgroups are involved. This means that the bias-creating factors or sensitive attributes are unknown. It highlights various methodologies and their implications in achieving equitable outcomes in diverse data-driven contexts.

Lahoti et al. lahoti2020fairness explored Rawlsian fairness by leveraging correlations between observed and protected attributes to safeguard fairness for unidentified protected subgroups. They employed adversarially reweighted learning (ARL) to minimize the disparity of hypothesized subgroups, enhancing classifier performance in three fairness datasets angwin2022machine ; misc_adult_2 ; wightman1998lsac . ARL uniquely assigns individual weights to each training sample, influencing their impact on the learner’s loss based on inferred subgroup membership.

Hashimoto et al. hashimoto2018fairness investigated the short and long-term effects of empirical risk management (ERM) on fairness regarding unknown subgroups. They showed that user attrition from disadvantaged groups leads to unfair models. To counteract this, they used distributionally robust optimization (DRO), which slowed disadvantaged user attrition and improved model performance post-attrition blodgett2016demographic . However, this approach assumes that users can opt-out, limiting its applicability in certain fairness contexts drago2011prison ; smith2023many .

Chai et al. chai2022fairness addressed the fairness issue for unknown protected subgroups differently, using distillation. This method trains an efficient model from a more complex one, using soft labels to encode class information hinton2015distilling ; papernot2016distillation . Applied to three fairness datasets ding2021retiring ; angwin2022machine ; yang2015facial , this approach showed enhanced accuracy and fairness compared to other methods.

B.4 Common Biases in Human-centered data

The issue of data bias and lack of benchmark datasets with sensitive attributes is a significant cause of concern for the human-centered AI and computing community mehrabi2021survey ; zhang2023framework . A recent study by Yfantidou et al. yfantidou2023state shows that there is a research gap in the study of bias in human-centered AI applications, with merely 5% of the works in the domain meeting current standards for fairness reporting, such as providing detailed demographic analyses. Additionally, recent works have found that measurement inaccuracies and the concept drift phenomena in sensor data lead to performance disparities across different sensitive attributes yfantidou2023beyond . The common biases associated with the modalities we evaluated in this work include:

Biases in Physiological Sensor Data : Wearable devices like smartwatches use sensors like photoplethysmography (PPG) to measure heart rate and other vital signs, but their accuracy can be influenced by device type, placement, skin tone, and individual differences bent2020investigating ; bayoumy2021smart ; colvonen2020limiting ; sharma2022psychophysiological . Studies have shown that physiological signals, notably electrodermal activity (EDA), can indicate racial bias nikseresht2021detection ; schimmack2021implicit and are affected by obesity and sex, with significant differences in EDA responses among obese individuals and between genders aldosky2019impact . This underscores the complexity and potential biases in physiological state monitoring technologies.

Biases in Inertial/Motion sensor data: A recent study alam2020ai explored biases in inertial measurement unit (IMU) data used for activity recognition in older adults. It identified that biases may arise due to the diverse abilities and functional disabilities among older adults. The study acknowledges that the same activity can present differently among individuals based on age and functional ability, even for the same person over time. These variations could lead to biased activity recognition, highlighting the importance of addressing AI fairness in this domain.

Biases in radar sensor data: Biases have also been in Doppler radars like millimeter-wave (mmWave) sensors, which are popular for tasks like presence detection nanzer2007human and pose estimation sengupta2020mm . The study by Park et al. park20213d investigated the use of mmWave radars for 3D motion estimation in robotics. They highlighted the challenges and biases associated with using these sensors, particularly in harsh or feature-poor environments like foggy conditions or uneven terrain, and developed a method to compensate for the limitations and biases of the radar sensors, such as their sensitivity to environmental factors and the casing’s impact on measurement quality.
