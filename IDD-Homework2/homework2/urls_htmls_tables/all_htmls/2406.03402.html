Abstract

Over-the-Air Federated Learning (OTA-FL) has been extensively investigated as a privacy-preserving distributed learning mechanism. Realistic systems will see FL clients with diverse size, weight, and power configurations. A critical research gap in existing OTA-FL research is the assumption of homogeneous client computational bit precision. Indeed, many clients may exploit approximate computing (AxC) where bit precisions are adjusted for energy and computational efficiency. The dynamic distribution of bit precision updates amongst FL clients poses an open challenge for OTA-FL, as is is incompatible in the wireless modulation superposition space.

Here, we propose an AxC-based OTA-FL framework of clients with multiple precisions, demonstrating the following innovations: (i) optimize the quantization-performance trade-off for both server and clients within the constraints of varying edge computing capabilities and learning accuracy requirements, and (ii) develop heterogeneous gradient resolution OTA-FL modulation schemes to ensure compatibility with physical layer OTA aggregation. Our findings indicate that we can design modulation schemes that enable AxC based OTA-FL, which can achieve 50% faster and smoother server convergence and a performance enhancement for the lowest precision clients compared to a homogeneous precision approach. This demonstrates the great potential of our AxC-based OTA-FL approach in heterogeneous edge computing environments.

I Introduction

Federated learning (FL), first proposed in [1], has been widely studied and applied as a secured and privacy-preserving distributed learning framework via its parameter sharing and aggregation designs instead of raw data sharing in centralized learning. From research perspectives, current literature focuses on (i) privacy and security enhancement, (ii) performance and efficiency improvement.

I-A Trade-offs in Computation and Communication Costs

The interplay between FL performance and computational cost has long been a focal point. Navigating this trade-off is crucial for optimizing system performance within client resource constraints. Approaches to this optimization span a broad spectrum, including hardware-accelerated solutions [2], quantization techniques for precision scaling [3], and software-driven network architecture optimizations [4]. Hardware and architecture-based Approximate Computing (AxC) methods prioritize end-to-end efficiency. Within it, quantization stands out as a universally applicable strategy, offering a direct balance between model accuracy and computational overhead [5].

On the communication front, optimizing FL systems involves not only minimizing the number of communication rounds but also reducing the data payload for each interaction. Strategies for managing asynchronous communication between clients and servers often depend on task urgency, with non-critical tasks being scheduled during low-traffic periods to make efficient use of available bandwidth. Moreover, leveraging computation-conserving measures, such as smaller model architectures or lower precision computations, can significantly reduce communication demands. Advanced techniques, such as gradient compression [6], further contribute to minimizing the bandwidth requirements for FL systems.

I-B Over-the-Air Federated Learning (OTA-FL)

Regarding privacy and security, cryptographic methods and differential privacy (DP) mechanisms have been explored, including but not limited to post-quantum cryptography [7], code-based [8], and differential privacy [9]. However, the computational and resource demands of these methods pose challenges, especially on resource-limited edge platforms.

In response, Yang et al. [10] introduced over-the-air federated learning (OTA-FL), leveraging the randomness of physical layer channel states and electromagnetic superposition to protect against information leakage. Recent advancements in OTA-FL encompass transmitting node precoding [11], server beamforming vector optimization [12], and reconfigurable intelligent surface (RIS) phase adjustment [13]. Compared to traditional cryptographic and DP methods, OTA-FL presents a viable, resource-efficient security framework for FL gradient-sharing processes.

I-C Novelty and Paper Structure

This paper underscores the significant yet under-explored potential of precision scaling in addressing both computational and communication trade-offs in FL. Our research is driven by the development of an OTA-FL system capable of accommodating clients of multiple computation bit precisions, with the long-term aim of optimizing efficiency, privacy, and security. The research is structured around several key questions: (i) what are the potential efficiency benefits of dynamically configuring client model parameters across different precision levels, and (ii) determining the a viable modulation scheme to allow OTA-FL to aggregate multi-precision client updates. In this paper, our contributions are threefold:

  1. 1.

    We propose an AxC-based OTA-FL framework for multi-precision clients, aimed at bolstering performance and efficiency.

  2. 2.

    We develop heterogeneous gradient resolution modulation schemes to ensure compatibility with physical-layer OTA aggregation.

  3. 3.

    Through the data-driven case study of intelligent transport, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach. Experimental outcomes indicate a notable enhancement in convergence speed and a performance improvement of approximately 5% for the lowest precision clients compared to conventional quantized FL. These findings underscore the potential of our AxC-based OTA-FL framework in resource-constrained, heterogeneous edge computing environments.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section II provides a detailed exploration of OTA-FL concepts. Section III delves into the design specifics of our AxC-based OTA-FL framework. Experimental validations and results are presented in Section IV. Finally, Section V concludes the paper, summarizing key findings and contributions.

II Preliminaries

II-A Federated EdgE Learning

Federated EdgE Learning (FEEL) represents a pivotal approach in AI applications, notably within the realms of mobile applications and the Internet of Things (IoT). As a distributed learning framework, FEEL enables a multitude of edge devices to collaboratively refine a unified global model, while preserving the privacy of their local data. This process is iterative, involving the training of local models on individual edge devices, followed by the aggregation of these models to update the global model. The refined global model is then disseminated back to the edge devices for subsequent predictions and further local training iterations, thereby progressively enhancing the global model’s accuracy.

II-C Approximate Computing

Approximate Computing (AC) encompasses diverse methodologies aimed at balancing computational performance with energy efficiency or computational resource savings. These methodologies span both hardware and software domains, offering versatile solutions for performance optimization. A pivotal inspiration within the domain of AC comes from the utilization of Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) as accelerators and the development of synthesizable algorithms that harness FPGAs’ reconfigurable nature [15, 16]. In contrast to the fixed computational architectures of Central Processing Units (CPUs) and Graphics Processing Units (GPUs), which operate within conventional pipelines at standard precision levels (e.g., 32-bit or 64-bit), FPGAs offer a paradigm of customizable computation. This unique capability enables FPGAs to perform calculations in a highly efficient manner tailored to specific tasks. Consequently, FPGAs find extensive applications across various fields, including networking, signal processing, multimedia codec operations, and, notably, machine learning tasks [17].

In the context of edge computing, FPGA accelerators becomes particularly advantageous, as they can be dynamically reprogrammed to custom computation precision. This flexibility allows for computations to be executed at lower precision levels (e.g., 8-bit or 4-bit) with minimal overhead, thereby significantly enhancing computational efficiency and energy conservation in resource-constrained environments [2].

III Approximated OTA Federated EdgE Learning

III-A Low-precision Machine Learning on Edge

Machine learning deployment on edge devices presents significant challenges, chiefly due to constrained resources in computation and power. In response, low-precision machine learning has emerged as a viable strategy to mitigate these limitations by reducing computational demands and memory footprint. Unlike traditional approaches that rely on 32-bit or 64-bit floating-point representations, low-precision techniques employ 16-bit, 8-bit, or even 4-bit fixed-point formats for storing, inferring, and training machine learning models directly on edge devices [18, 19]. As illustrated in Fig. 2, empirical benchmarks reveal that the degradation in model performance becomes significant only when quantization is applied below 8-bits. Remarkably, many models retain acceptable levels of accuracy even at 4-bit precision.

Adopting lower bit-depths not only conserves computational and memory resources but also enables the integration of larger and more complex model architectures within the same resource constraints as opposed to utilizing standard precision levels. Beyond this flexibility, the pursuit of low-precision machine learning unveils a critical trade-off between quantization depth and model performance. This observation underpins the essence of designing AC-based OTA-FL systems; it underscores the necessity of identifying the most suitable quantization level for each edge device, particularly those operating at the lowest feasible bit-depths [20]. This optimization is paramount in maximizing the efficiency and efficacy of machine learning models in resource-limited edge computing environments, highlighting the intricate balance between computational frugality and performance integrity.

IV Experiments

This section details the experiment setup and results addressing the research questions posited earlier, focusing on (i) the efficacy of multi-precision over-the-air (OTA) aggregation, and (ii) the formulation of client quantization schemes to optimize performance across both server and client dimensions.

IV-A Settings

Our experimental framework emulates an OTA FL system of 15 traffic sign recognizer clients, distributed evenly across three quantization levels, including repeated elements. All models are of ResNet152 structure and initialized with ImageNet [21] pre-trained weights, operating over 100 communication rounds with 5-30dB of emulated Gaussian noise.

IV-A1 Data

The German Traffic Sign Recognition Benchmark (GTSRB) [22] serves as the dataset for our experiments, comprising 39,209 training and 12,630 testing samples across 43 traffic sign classes. Each client receives an equal subset of data per round, working at their respective quantization levels. Given the uniform data distribution, the weighting factor wisubscript𝑤𝑖w_{i} in Equation 1 is consistent across clients, simplifying the aggregation equation by excluding it.

IV-A2 Quantization Schemes

Quantization levels are set at [32,24,16,12,8,6,4]32241612864[32,24,16,12,8,6,4], bifurcated into higher [32,24,16,12]32241612[32,24,16,12] and lower [8,6,4]864[8,6,4] sub-lists for two reasons: (i) model performance remains stable at 8-bit and above as evidenced by Fig. 2, and (ii) the GTSRB dataset images are in 8-bit RGB format. Each quantization scheme comprises a three-element permutation from these lists, with a focus on lower quantization levels. Specifically, permutations include one element from the higher and two from the lower sub-lists. The round-to-nearest (RTN) method is employed for quantization, with fixed-point and floating-point formats applicable to 8-bit and above. Below 8-bit, only fixed-point format is used due to the reduced dynamic range of floating-point representations.

IV-A3 Baselines

For comparative analysis, uni-precision FL systems operating at 16-bit, 8-bit, and 4-bit serve as baselines against our multi-precision approach. The 16-bit setup is considered standard precision for edge devices, while 8-bit and 4-bit setups represent uniform quantization baselines.

IV-A4 Metrics

Evaluation metrics for server include convergence speed, measured by the number of communication rounds required for convergence, and the performance of the aggregated model post-convergence. For clients, we assess their performances after aggregation and re-quantization.

IV-B Results

This section presents the empirical findings from our experiments, focusing on federated training convergence, server model accuracy, and client performance enhancements through re-quantization strategies.

IV-B1 Federated Training and Server Performance

The convergence velocity, as depicted in Fig. 3, indicates that setups of uniform 4-bit clients, or a mixed-precision schema of [12,4,4]1244[12,4,4] bits, exhibit slower and more erratic initial convergence. In contrast, setups incorporating clients with 16-bit precision or higher demonstrate more rapid and stable convergence, achieving approximately 95% accuracy within 20 communication rounds. Notably, the incremental convergence benefit diminishes when client precision exceeds 16-bits, suggesting that, for high-capacity clients, opting for 32-bit or 24-bit precision offers marginal gains compared to the computational and storage efficiencies achievable at 16-bit precision.

Server model performance analysis reveals that all quantization schemes surpass 97% top-1 accuracy within 100 communication rounds. The performance differential across schemes remains within a tight 0.3% margin, underscoring the effectiveness of the federated learning framework in achieving high accuracy irrespective of client precision variations.

IV-B2 Client Performance

Following 100 communication rounds, the global model, θ100superscript𝜃100\theta^{100}, is re-quantized to the designated precision levels and distributed to update client models. As illustrated in Fig. 4, client models under schemes incorporating 16-bit precision or higher, when re-quantized for 4-bit clients, attain a minimum of 3% higher top-1 accuracy. The performance boost for lower precision clients from higher precision counterparts shows diminishing returns beyond 16-bit precision. This phenomenon highlights the ’free lunch’ performance uplift, from higher precision clients, to their lower precision peers up to a certain threshold.

IV-C Future Work

Future investigations will focus on three primary areas to enhance the proposed AxC-based OTA-FL framework:

1) High-Precision Client Advantages: Explore the mechanism of benefits induced by higher precision clients in terms of their impact on convergence and accuracy.

2) Adversarial Attack Resilience: Assess the framework’s resilience to adversarial attacks and develop defence methods.

3) OTA Aggregation Optimization: Creating a more flexible and efficient OTA aggregation mechanism.

V Conclusion

In this study, we introduce a framework for Over-The-Air Federated Learning (OTA-FL) that incorporates Approximate Computing (AxC) principles to accommodate clients of varying precisions. This framework aims to enhance overall performance, computational efficiency, and privacy preservation within federated learning systems. A novel OTA aggregation mechanism is developed to ensure the integration of model parameters from clients at multiple precision levels. Our approach is empirically evaluated through a traffic sign recognition task, showcasing the framework’s efficacy.

Experimental outcomes indicate that clients operating with precision levels above 16-bit significantly expedite and stabilize convergence on the server side. Moreover, these higher precision clients contribute to an improvement of near 5% ’free lunch’ performance boost for the lowest precision clients when compared to a uniform precision federated learning setup maintained at this minimum precision level. Additionally, the employment of a mixed precision quantization scheme, which remains opaque to potential adversaries, inherently bolsters the security posture of the OTA-FL system against inference attacks during the aggregation process.

The preliminary findings presented herein underscore the potential of leveraging multi-precision client configurations in OTA-FL systems, particularly within the constraints of resource-diverse and heterogeneous edge computing environments. These insights may serve as a foundational guideline for the architectural design and operational optimization of future multi-precision OTA-FL systems.
