Rethinking Targeted Adversarial Attacks for Neural Machine Translation

Abstract

Targeted adversarial attacks are widely used to evaluate the robustness of neural machine translation systems. Unfortunately, this paper first identifies a critical issue in the existing settings of NMT targeted adversarial attacks, where their attacking results are largely overestimated. To this end, this paper presents a new setting for NMT targeted adversarial attacks that could lead to reliable attacking results. Under the new setting, it then proposes a Targeted Word Gradient adversarial Attack (TWGA) method to craft adversarial examples. Experimental results demonstrate that our proposed setting could provide faithful attacking results for targeted adversarial attacks on NMT systems, and the proposed TWGA method can effectively attack such victim NMT systems. In-depth analyses on a large-scale dataset further illustrate some valuable findings. 111This research has been made possible by funding support from the Research Grants Council of Hong Kong under the General Research Fund project 16204720 and the Research Impact Fund project R6003-21. Our code and data are available at https://github.com/wujunjie1998/TWGA.

Index Terms—  Targeted adversarial attack, neural machine translation, natural language processing, robustness.

1 Introduction

Neural machine translation (NMT) [1] has been the prevailing framework for machine translation in recent years. However, current NMT systems are still fragile to inputs with imperceptible perturbations, also known as adversarial examples, in that their translation qualities could be dramatically degraded [2, 3, 4, 5]. Among these attack methods, targeted adversarial attack that enforces targeted translation errors on some targeted words rather than the entire sentence has drew many research attention [6, 7, 8, 9] since it can provide more fine-grained attacking results.

Based on the types of targeted translation errors, we first frame the problem settings of existing NMT targeted adversarial attacks as: make a targeted word that is not in the original reference appear in the translation output [7, 9] (Setting 1); or let a targeted word in the original reference disappear in the translation output [6, 8] (Setting 2). The evaluation of targeted attacks under both settings relies on a label-preserving assumption, i.e., an adversarial example keeps the same semantic meaning of the original input sentence and thus it is rational to verify the effectiveness of this adversarial example using the reference of the original sentence, which is further illustrated in §2. However, we argue that this assumption is not always true since existing settings do not constrain the source tokens that can be perturbed, which makes many crafted adversarial examples invalid as shown in Fig. 1. Hence, the attack success rates obtained under both problem settings are largely overestimated (see the results in Table 1), making the obtained attacking results of those targeted attacks unreliable.

To solve the above issue, in this paper, we first propose a new setting for NMT targeted adversarial attacks such that the obtained attacking results are faithful. Specifically, given a source sentence including a targeted word, an attack method is applied to make the correct translation of this targeted word disappear in the output by only modifying non-targeted word tokens in the source sentence, which ensures the validity of the crafted adversarial example. In addition, since a targeted word might have various meanings under different contexts, we introduce a bilingual dictionary to ensure that all possible reference translations of this targeted word are not shown in the translation output. Moreover, since the semantic meaning of a targeted word could be destroyed in a meaningless source sentence with messy contents, we require that adversarial examples crafted under our setting should be fluent and meaningful. Noting that no sentence-level references are needed during the attack process, making our setting easily to be scaled up to yield more unbiased results.

Next, under this new attack setup, we design a white-box Targeted Word Gradient adversarial Attack (TWGA) method to generate adversarial examples for NMT systems. On two translation datasets, we use several NMT targeted adversarial attacks including TWGA to attack two popular NMT systems under our proposed setup, and the results illustrate that TWGA can effectively craft high-quality adversarial examples. Additionally, unlike existing works that mainly implement NMT targeted adversarial attacks on small datasets, we conduct experiments on a large-scale dataset for the first time and make in-depth analyses of the attacking results, leading to some insightful findings.

3 The Proposed Attack Setting

3.1 Problem Setup

To address the critical issue mentioned before, we aim to design an appropriate setup for NMT targeted adversarial attacks that could lead to reliable attacking results. Given a source sentence 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x that includes one source side targeted word z𝑧zitalic_z, the goal of an attack under our setting is to craft an adversarial example 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT for 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x that makes the translation of z𝑧zitalic_z not appear in the corresponding translation output 𝒚′superscript𝒚bold-′\boldsymbol{y^{\prime}}bold_italic_y start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT, while making sure that 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT is still a fluent and meaningful sentence. As pointed out by §2.1, the core problem of previous settings is that they fail to hold the label-preserving assumption. To solve this issue, we explicitly constrain that only the non-targeted word tokens in 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x can be perturbed during the attack process. This step ensures z𝑧zitalic_z appears in both 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x and 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT, which enables us to find translation errors on z𝑧zitalic_z by examining whether the reference translation of z𝑧zitalic_z appears in 𝒚′superscript𝒚bold-′\boldsymbol{y^{\prime}}bold_italic_y start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT.

Moreover, since a targeted word z𝑧zitalic_z might have various meanings under different contexts, we further apply the bilingual dictionary in §2.2 to obtain a set {𝐙}𝐙\{\mathbf{Z}\}{ bold_Z } that contains multiple reference translations of z𝑧zitalic_z, and requires a successful attack to make all the translations in {𝐙}𝐙\{\mathbf{Z}\}{ bold_Z } not appear in 𝒚′superscript𝒚bold-′\boldsymbol{y^{\prime}}bold_italic_y start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT. This step is important since it is possible to craft an invalid adversarial example 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT that only makes one translation of z𝑧zitalic_z disappear in 𝒚′superscript𝒚bold-′\boldsymbol{y^{\prime}}bold_italic_y start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT. Consider the left case in Fig. 2, the attack is regarded as successful if we only provide one reference translation “工作” for the targeted word z=𝑧absentz=italic_z = “working”. However, since another translation “作用” of the targeted word z=𝑧absentz=italic_z =“working” appears in 𝒚′superscript𝒚bold-′\boldsymbol{y^{\prime}}bold_italic_y start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT and becomes the new ground-truth translation of “working”, 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT here is actually an invalid adversarial example.

Finally, we require that the adversarial example 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT under our setting should still be a fluent and meaningful sentence, which is important since the semantic meaning of the targeted word z𝑧zitalic_z could be destroyed by messy contents in a meaningless sentence. Hence, it could be correct for the reference translation of z𝑧zitalic_z not appear in the translation of a meaningless adversarial example 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT, thus biases the final evaluation results.

Overall, 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT is an adversarial example of x𝑥\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x in our setting if: 1): it leads to a targeted translation error on the targeted word z𝑧zitalic_z, i.e., all the reference translations of z𝑧zitalic_z in {𝐙}𝐙\{\mathbf{Z}\}{ bold_Z } do not appear in y′superscript𝑦bold-′\boldsymbol{y^{\prime}}bold_italic_y start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT; 2): it only modifies non-targeted word tokens in x𝑥\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x. 3): it is a fluent and meaningful sentence. A valid adversarial example under our setting is 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT in the right case of Fig. 2, where 𝒚′superscript𝒚bold-′\boldsymbol{y^{\prime}}bold_italic_y start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT makes a targeted translation error on z=𝑧absentz=italic_z =“working”. Noting that sentence-level references are not needed in our proposed attack setting, making it easily to be scaled up to provide unbiased attacking results.

3.2 Evaluation Metrics

We introduce three metrics that are suitable for evaluating an NMT targeted adversarial attack method. First, we compute the attack success rate (Succ) defined in §2 to measure the effectiveness of the given attack method, where a higher Succ score means it is more powerful. Second, we assume that a good attack method should generate adversarial examples with few token modifications, which avoids it from being a messy sentence. Therefore, we compute the edit distance (Edit) between 𝒙′superscript𝒙bold-′\boldsymbol{x^{\prime}}bold_italic_x start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT bold_′ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT and 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x, normalized by the sequence length of 𝒙𝒙\boldsymbol{x}bold_italic_x. Finally, we suppose a good attack method should craft adversarial examples efficiently and thus record the average number of times an attack queries the victim NMT system for generating an adversarial example (Query). For the latter two metrics, a lower value refers to better performance.

6 Conclusion

In this work, we indicate a critical limitation in the existing settings of NMT targeted adversarial attacks that could lead to unreliable attacking results. To this end, we propose a new setting for NMT targeted adversarial attack to obtain faithful attacking results. Under the new setting, we design an effective attack TWGA to craft adversarial examples, whose strength is demonstrated by extensive experiments. Experiments on a large dataset further lead to some insightful findings about NMT targeted adversarial attacks.
