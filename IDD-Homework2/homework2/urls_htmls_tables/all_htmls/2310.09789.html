Abstract.

Federated learning (FL) achieves great popularity in broad areas as a powerful interface to offer intelligent services to customers while maintaining data privacy. Nevertheless, FL faces communication and computation bottlenecks due to limited bandwidth and resource constraints of edge devices. To comprehensively address the bottlenecks, the technique of dropout is introduced, where resource-constrained edge devices are allowed to collaboratively train a subset of the global model parameters. However, dropout impedes the learning efficiency of FL under unbalanced local data distributions. As a result, FL requires more rounds to achieve appropriate accuracy, consuming more communication and computation resources.

In this paper, we present FLrce, an efficient FL framework with a relationship-based client selection and early-stopping strategy. FLrce accelerates the FL process by selecting clients with more significant effects, enabling the global model to converge to a high accuracy in fewer rounds. FLrce also leverages an early stopping mechanism to terminate FL in advance to save communication and computation resources. Experiment results show that FLrce increases the communication and computation efficiency by 6% to 73.9% and 20% to 79.5%, respectively, while maintaining competitive accuracy.

1. Introduction

In the era of big data, edge devices have garnered extensive utilization in efficiently capturing and retaining vast volumes of data at the periphery of a network. Owing to privacy concerns and limited network bandwidth, it is desired that edge devices process high-volume and privacy-sensitive data locally rather than upload them to a remote server (Li et al., 2020b). To this end, federated learning (FL) emerges. FL is a distributed machine learning paradigm that allows edge devices to collaboratively train a global model without sharing any private data (McMahan et al., 2017). In an FL process, devices (also named clients) learn a machine learning model locally from their personal datasets, and exchange the model parameters/gradients with the server. Compared with raw data, the communication overheads and the risk of data leakage for transmitting the parameters/gradients become smaller (McMahan et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022b). This capability enables FL to train a universal global model that can provide high-quality services to end users in a wide range of privacy-sensitive applications, such as voice assisting (Leroy et al., 2019), healthcare (Qayyum et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022) and Internet of Things (IoT) (Zhang et al., 2022b).

Even though FL remarkably upholds data privacy, in real-world applications, the effectiveness of FL is compromised by the limited capacities of edge devices and unbalanced local data distributions. Due to the system heterogeneity of edge devices, a high variability exists in edge devices’ communication and computation resources, including bandwidth, processor, memory, and battery life (Imteaj et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2017; Li et al., 2020b), presenting a critical challenge to address communication and computation bottlenecks in FL. First, tremendous communication costs can occur when large-scale deep learning (DL) models, such as large image processing models and language models, are transmitted across edge devices with limited bandwidths. Second, training a complex DL model on resource-constrained devices may result in a long latency, as these devices cannot complete the training in time due to deficient hardware support. Subsequently, the resource-constrained devices need more time to complete the local training, and extensive computation resources are consumed in the long-run training process, like compute units (Horváth et al., 2021) and energy (Imteaj et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021). To address this problem, we expect an efficient FL framework that can effectively reduce computation and communication costs while maintaining accuracy.

Data imbalance is another bottleneck in FL. In real life, edge devices are inherently distributed, generating non-identical independent (non-iid) data that may exhibit unbalanced characteristics and unequal training contributions [2]. On the one hand, some devices are important to FL, as their local data distributions are closer to the global distribution. On the other hand, some devices are non-important or even harmful to FL due to reasons like data poisoning, noise, or local biases. In this scenario, it becomes crucially important to select which devices to participate in the collaborative learning. Selecting the right devices will fully utilize the limited computation and communication resources and make the global model reach a high accuracy quickly while selecting unimportant devices may degrade the global model’s accuracy and waste resources (Li et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Lai et al., 2021).

Status Quo and Limitations. To comprehensively address the computation and communication bottlenecks, the dropout technique has been proposed. Dropout methods (Wen et al., 2022; Caldas et al., 2018; Horváth et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Diao et al., 2021) let resource-constrained devices train a ”sub-model” in local training, which is a subset of the global model parameters. During the upstream and downstream global communications, the resource-constrained devices communicate the sub-model parameters with the server as well. Given a drop rate, dropout methods extract a sub-model by pruning neurons in the global model. For example, studies in (Caldas et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2022) generate a sub-model by randomly pruning neurons from the global model. Studies in (Horváth et al., 2021; Diao et al., 2021) fixedly prune the rightmost neurons in the global model. Study in (Li et al., 2021) prunes the unimportant neurons, which are identified by adding a regularization term to a device’s local objective function. Compared with the full global model, the complexities of training and transmitting a sub-model become smaller, enabling dropout to achieve computation and communication efficiency simultaneously.

However, dropout impedes the learning efficiency under local data imbalance. For one thing, dropout implicitly allocates sub-models to edge devices without considering data importance. When a sub-model is allocated to a device whose data are important to FL, the learning efficiency of FL will be compromised. To be straightforward, a common example is shown in Figure 1. Suppose two edge devices: a mobile phone and a laptop. The phone has fewer resources and has been used to take many photos, the laptop has more resources but stores very few images. In this case, the resource-constrained mobile phone owns the data that are important to FL, but dropout can only acquire limited update information from the data by allocating a sub-model to the phone. For another thing, most dropout methods use a random client selection strategy (Wen et al., 2022; Caldas et al., 2018; Horváth et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021). If unfortunately, the non-important devices are selected too many times, the FL process can easily become slow.

With degraded learning efficiency, whether dropout methods can reduce the total resource consumption is yet to be explored. Even though dropout reduces the communication and computation costs in every single training round, it does not necessarily reduce the total resource consumption. This is because, compared with traditional full-model based training methods (e.g. FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017)), dropout methods may require more rounds to achieve a high accuracy owing to a deteriorating learning speed (Caldas et al., 2018), and more communication and computation resources are required to support the additional rounds of training.

Challenges and solutions. In summary, within a heterogeneous edge network where devices train with unbalanced local datasets, dropout may degrade the learning efficiency of FL. Consequently, FL requires more rounds, and thus more communication and computation resources to train an accurate global model. To address this limitation, we expect an FL framework that can achieve communication and computation efficiency under data imbalance, meanwhile maintaining accuracy. We notice that: (1) Dropout degrades the learning efficiency by allocating sub-models to devices with the important data and a random client selection strategy; (2) With lower learning efficiency, dropout demands more training rounds and thus more communication and computation resources to train a global model with desirable accuracy; (3) Compared with dropout, training with a full model is less vulnerable to data imbalance but consumes more communication and computation resources in every single training round. Based on (1), (2) and (3), we raise the question: If we conduct full-model training on every device and design a client strategy to achieve high accuracy in fewer rounds, ending up with less communication and computation consumption overall, why do we even need dropout?

Solution Overview. Motivated by the question above, we propose a novel efficient FL framework – FLrce (Federated Learning with relationship-based client selection and early stopping). The logical line of FLrce is presented in Figure 2. First, FLrce conducts full-model training on every edge device. This overcomes the vulnerability of dropout to real-world scenarios where local datasets are naturally unbalanced, such as an IoT network (Abeysekara et al., 2021). Second, as full-model training causes higher consumption of communication and computation resources in each round, to reduce the overall consumption, FLrce utilizes an early stopping mechanism inspired by (Prechelt, 2002), which terminates FL in advance to reduce the rounds of training. We notice that when the global model ”oscillates” between edge devices’ local optima, it would be very hard for the model to reach the global optimal solution, making FL extremely inefficient. Accordingly, FLrce detects the oscillation between the devices’ parameter updates using cosine similarity, when a large degree of oscillation happens, i.e. over 50% of the update pairs have a negative cosine similarity, the early stopping mechanism will be triggered. Subsequently, the overall resource consumption can be significantly reduced, including computation resources (e.g. energy) and communication resources (e.g. bandwidth). Finally, to mitigate the accuracy loss emanating from fewer training rounds, inspired by (Li et al., 2022), FLrce leverages an innovative client selection strategy that tends to select the devices that are more ”important” to the collaborative learning to improve the learning efficiency. The importance of a device is the sum of its relationship degree on other devices, where the relationship degree between two devices is computed by the cosine similarity or orthogonal distance between their parameter updates. The client selection strategy enables the global model to converge to high accuracy in fewer rounds, which greatly improves the efficiency of FLrce in resource-constrained application scenarios, such as a mobile edge network with limited communication and computation resources.

System Implementation and Experiment Results. We test the performance of FLrce in four applications: image classification, natural language processing, human activity recognition, and intrusion detection. The experiment platform is built upon Flower (Beutel et al., 2022) and Microsoft Azure virtual machines. For comparison, we implement four baselines: Federated Dropout (Wen et al., 2022; Caldas et al., 2018), Ordered Dropout (Horváth et al., 2021), Personalized Dropout (Li et al., 2021), and PyramidFL (Li et al., 2022). To demonstrate the vulnerability of existing dropout methods, local data are distributed unevenly, with the resource-constrained devices owning the most important data, as illustrated in Figure 1. The experimental results show that:

  • •

    FLrce greatly reduces the number of training rounds, completing federated learning using only 20% to 60% of the maximum rounds, while incurring only a marginal sacrifice in accuracy.

  • •

    FLrce substantially reduces the computation cost. When compared to the baselines, FLrce’s energy consumption, even while achieving equivalent or higher accuracy, is lowered by 20% to 79.5%.

  • •

    FLrce significantly reduces the communication cost. Compared with the baselines, the total message size transmitted in FLrce is reduced by 6% to 73.9%.

Due to the possibility that resource-constrained devices can store the data that are important to FL, dropout methods may compromise the learning efficiency of FL by allocating sub-models to important devices. 2.3. Additional Resource Consumption Resulting from Low Learning Efficiency

In a heterogeneous edge network with a high proportion of resource-constrained devices, dropout decreases the learning efficiency of FL, entailing additional consumption of communication and computation resources. 3.2. Relationship-based Client Selection

Inspired by PyramidFL (Li et al., 2022) that reduces the wall-clock time of FL by selecting clients with larger training loss and lower latency, FLrce presents a novel client selection strategy that tends to select clients who are more important to FL to promote learning efficiency. This design enables the global model to reach a high accuracy with fewer training rounds and less resource consumption. An important client helps improve the global model’s mean accuracy and will maintain a positive relationship with most of the others. Therefore, FLrce utilizes a relationship modeling (RM) algorithm that estimates clients’ importance to FL by observing their pairwise relationships. The RM algorithm consists of two components: synchronous RM and asynchronous RM.

3.3. Early Stopping

Inspired by (Prechelt, 2002) that uses early stopping to prevent overfitting in centralized machine learning, FLrce presents a novel early stopping mechanism that terminates FL ahead of the maximum training round. It aims to reduce the communication and computation resource consumption in federated settings. Generally, as FL goes on, the global model tends to oscillate between the important clients’ local optima. In this case, it becomes much more difficult for the global model to reach the global optimum, which triggers the ES mechanism to save the communication and computation resources.

4. Experiments

In this section, we evaluate the performance of FLrce across several applications to demonstrate its strength in saving computation and communication resources and maintaining accuracy and convergence in the presence of unbalanced local data distribution.

4.1. Applications, Datasets, and Models

FLrce is tested on multiple common tasks in FL/IoT backgrounds, including image classification, natural language processing, intrusion detection, and human activity recognition.

  • •

    Image Classification (IC): a vision task to classify images. We use the FEMNIST dataset (Caldas et al., 2019) and a convolutional neural network (CNN) with two convolutional layers and one fully-connected layer, same as (Horváth et al., 2021).

  • •

    Natural Language Processing (NLP): a text task to predict the next character of a sentence. We use the Shakespeare dataset (Caldas et al., 2019) and a recurrent neural network (RNN) with one embedding layer and two LSTM layers, following (Horváth et al., 2021) as well.

  • •

    Human Activity Recognition (HAR): a classification task aiming to recognize individuals’ activities based on the data collected from their wearable sensors. We use the HAR dataset (Garcia-Gonzalez et al., 2020) and a three-layer fully connected neural network following (Li et al., 2021).

  • •

    Intrusion Detection (ID): a classification task to identify intrusion or suspicious behaviors in a sensor-based network. We use the UNSW-NB15 dataset (Moustafa and Slay, 2015), and a three-layer fully connected neural network same as HAR.

4.2. Experiment setup

Baselines. FLrce is compared with four baselines.

  • •

    Federated dropout (Feddrop) (Wen et al., 2022; Caldas et al., 2018) randomly drops units (neurons or channels) in the local model. Given a drop rate ρ𝜌\rho (0≤ρ<10𝜌10\leq\rho<1), in each layer, random ρ𝜌\rho of the global models’ units are pruned and only the remaining 1−ρ1𝜌1-\rho are updated.

  • •

    Ordered Dropout (OD) (Horváth et al., 2021) drops units in a fixed right-to-left order. Given ρ𝜌\rho, in each layer of the model, always ρ𝜌\rho of the rightmost units will be pruned and the left 1−ρ1𝜌1-\rho will remain.

  • •

    Personalized Dropout (PD) (Li et al., 2021) prunes the unimportant units by adding a L2-Lasso regularization term to the local objective function. With regularization, each client is able to find and prune the unimportant units with the least ρ𝜌\rho magnitudes. The set of unimportant units depends on the distribution of local data, therefore, the way of each client pruning units is unique.

  • •

    PyramidFL (Li et al., 2022) reduces computation overheads by modifying the local training epoch of clients. After training, for communication efficiency, a ranking-based dropout is applied on the client side. The drop rate depends on the client’s gradient norm. Moreover, PyramidFL holds a client selection strategy as well, where the server is inclined to select clients with larger training loss and shorter training time.

System implementation. The experiment is implemented on top of the Flower framework (1.4.0) (Beutel et al., 2022) and Pytorch (2.0.0). We build a virtual network consisting of a server running on a private desktop computer and clients running on Microsoft Azure virtual machines (VM). The server selects clients and aggregates results through the interface of Flower. Each selected client trains locally on the VM and returns the parameter updates to the server.

All VMs use the operating system Ubuntu Server 20.04 LTS - x64 Gen 2, and the processor Intel Xeon E5-2673 v3 @2.40GHz. Each VM holds at most M/5𝑀5M/5 parallel processes, with each process representing one client. Furthermore, FLrce helps improve the convergence of the global model. From Figure 10, we can see that FLrce obtains the best learning curve in most cases, with a shape that is smoother and less variant than others. This is because, in FLrce, all clients share a full model architecture with a fixed epoch. Compared with the baselines where clients work with various sub-models/epochs, the staleness or gaps between clients’ updates are limited in FLrce, meaning that there are much fewer diversities between the local parameter updates. Consequently, the learning process of FLrce is more stable and progressive in most scenarios.

It is worth noting that the performance of Feddrop on IC and NLP is catastrophic. From Figure 10 and Table 2, we can see that Feddrop almost learns nothing and ends up with an accuracy/negative perplexity of 2.9%/-37.0. There are three possible reasons. 1. Feddrop is developed on the assumption that the drop rate is fixed across all clients. Therefore, it might not support adaptive drop rates on heterogeneous clients (Horváth et al., 2021). 2. Feddrop drops units in a random manner. However, not all units in a model contribute equally, training or communicating the unimportant units might significantly degrade the learning efficiency (Li et al., 2022, 2021). 3. When the resource-constrained devices store the data that are important to collaborative learning, training a miniature sub-model on these devices will prohibit the server from acquiring the necessary information to train a solid deep learning model, like CNN or RNN.

Computation Efficiency

Due to the existence of the ES mechanism, FLrce is able to reduce overall energy consumption. As Figure 11 shows, FLrce consumes the least energy for all the tasks except for ID. For ID, the performance of FLrce is second only to PyramidFL. However, PyramidFL does not maintain accuracy, as the validation accuracy of PyramidFL is only 53.9% for ID (Table 2), making the computation efficiency worthless.

Communication Efficiency

In FLrce, clients communicate full parameter updates with the server, resulting in a larger communication overhead in every single communication round. However, by leveraging the ES mechanism, FLrce is able to complete the collaborative learning in fewer rounds, thereby reducing the overall communication costs. As Figure 12 shows, overall FLrce transmits the least amount of messages across the network for all the tasks.

To investigate whether the ES mechanism will cause undesirable accuracy loss, we compare the final accuracy/negative perplexity of FLrce and FLrce without early stopping (FLrce w/o ES). The only difference between FLrce and FLrce w/o ES is that the latter ignores the ES criterion and keeps training until reaching the maximum global iteration. Note that the relationship-based client selection strategy still applies. As Table 3 shows, the ES mechanism does not cause too much loss of accuracy/negative perplexity. On the worst case, the ES mechanism degrades the final accuracy by 8.8% (IC). However, the ES mechanism reduces almost 80% training rounds (101 vs. 500). 5. related work

Efficient Federated Learning. To curtail the excessive communication and computation costs in FL, substantial studies have been conducted. To the best of our knowledge, the prior art can be divided into three categories: (1) message compression, (2) accuracy relaxation, and (3) dropout.

Message compression methods reduce the communication cost by decreasing the volume of transmitted messages, including quantization methods (Alistarh et al., 2017; Dettmers, 2016; Fu et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2017) and compression methods (Sattler et al., 2020; Aji and Heafield, 2017; Dutta et al., 2020). Quantization methods reduce the number of bits of transmitted messages to reduce bandwidth usage. For example, quantized SGD (Alistarh et al., 2017) and TernGrad (Wen et al., 2017) map the continuous elements of a gradient to discrete values without changing the statistical property. (Dettmers, 2016) replaces the 32-bit gradients with 8-bit approximations. Tinyscript (Fu et al., 2020) proposes a distribution-aware quantization framework that minimizes the quantization variance of gradients to mitigate accuracy sacrifice. Compression methods let edge devices only communicate a subset of parameters or gradients with the server. In (Sattler et al., 2020; Aji and Heafield, 2017), elements that are less than a pre-defined threshold are removed. In (Dutta et al., 2020), a model is compressed in a layer-wise manner, within each layer, a certain percentage of neurons are removed before transmission. However, message compression does not address the computation bottleneck. On the worst case, deploying compression on the client side might bring additional computation overheads to resource-constrained devices, entailing extra consumption of computation resources.

Accuracy relaxation methods save computation resources by allocating simpler tasks to edge devices. Fedprox (Li et al., 2020c) and Fedparl (Imteaj and Amini, 2021) allows resource-constrained devices to train a sub-optimal solution during local training, which requires less computation effort. PyramidFL (Li et al., 2022) reduces the local epoch if a device cannot complete the training in time. However, the communication bottleneck is neglected in this kind of approach, as devices still communicate the full model architecture with the server.

Dropout simultaneously realizes communication and computation efficiency by allowing clients to train and transmit light-sized sub-models. There exist multiple ways to extract a sub-model from the original model. Federated dropout (Wen et al., 2022; Caldas et al., 2018) generates sub-models by randomly pruning neurons in the original neural network. Fjord (Horváth et al., 2021) and HeteroFL (Diao et al., 2021) prune neurons in a fixed order. In Hermes (Li et al., 2021), each client prunes the unimportant neurons personally, where the unimportant neurons are determined by a group-lasso-based regularization.

All methods above improve efficiency by reducing the consumption of computation and communication resources in every single iteration. FLrce, however, works from a different perspective. Utilizing an ES mechanism, FLrce reduces the overall resource computation resources by reducing the total rounds of training.

Federated Learning with Local Data Imbalance. Due to the intrinsic statistical heterogeneity of IoT devices, clients contribute unequally to the FL task. Some clients promote FL by contributing important parameter updates, while others may hinder FL by sending malicious or noisy updates. It has been well studied on how to overcome data imbalance and maintain performance. Existing works mainly adopt two ways. One way is to mitigate the negative effects of malicious updates in the aggregation phase. For example, FLtrust (Cao et al., 2020) designs a trust mechanism that assigns higher weights to clients whose gradients are more consistent with the global update. RHFL (Fang and Ye, 2022) minimizes the negative impact of noisy datasets by increasing the weights of clients with larger training loss reduction. (Li et al., 2020a) designs an anomaly-detection-based FL framework that can identify malicious updates through a variation auto-encoder (VAE). All updates are fed into the VAE before aggregation, and those with a large construction error will be considered malicious and removed from the aggregation. DS-FL (Itahara et al., 2023) uses an entropy-reduction aggregation strategy to improve the model’s robustness against low-entropy logits from noisy clients.

Another way is to identify and discard malicious clients during the phase of client selection. Fed-CBS (Zhang et al., 2023) invents a metric named Quadratic Class-Imbalance Degree (QCID) that measures the distance between clients’ local data distribution to an ideal uniform distribution. By selecting clients with the lowest QCID values, FedCBS can improve the model’s accuracy when the class distributions among clients are unbalanced. FLDetector (Zhang et al., 2022a) detects malicious clients based on update inconsistency to defend FL against large-scale model poisoning attacks. Similarly, in FLrce, clients who hold the lowest relationship degrees with others are considered to be unimportant and are unlikely to be selected.

Very few of these works consider the communication and computation bottlenecks. Two exceptions are (Itahara et al., 2023) and (Fang and Ye, 2022) which transmit light-sized logits to realize communication efficiency. Comparatively, FLrce simultaneously achieves computation and communication efficiency.

6. Conclusion

In this paper, we propose FLrce, an FL framework that comprehensively achieves overall communication and computation efficiency, meanwhile maintaining accuracy and convergence under local data imbalance. Experiment results show that FLrce maintains competitive accuracy under unbalanced data distribution and significantly improves communication and computation efficiency. In the future, we target enhancing FLrce with strict theoretical proof and improving efficiency from the perspective of every single client.
