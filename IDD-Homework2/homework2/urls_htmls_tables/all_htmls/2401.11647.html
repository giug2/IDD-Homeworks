Abstract

Many recent studies integrate federated learning (FL) with self-supervised learning (SSL) to take advantage of raw training data distributed across edge devices. However, edge devices often struggle with high computation and communication costs imposed by SSL and FL algorithms. To tackle this hindrance, we propose LW-FedSSL, a layer-wise federated self-supervised learning approach that allows edge devices to incrementally train one layer of the model at a time. LW-FedSSL comprises server-side calibration and representation alignment mechanisms to maintain comparable performance with end-to-end FedSSL while significantly lowering clientsâ€™ resource requirements. The server-side calibration mechanism takes advantage of the resource-rich server in an FL environment to assist in global model training. Meanwhile, the representation alignment mechanism encourages closeness between representations of FL local models and those of the global model. Our experiments show that LW-FedSSL has a 3.3Ã—3.3\times lower memory requirement and a 3.2Ã—3.2\times cheaper communication cost than its end-to-end counterpart. We also explore a progressive training strategy called Prog-FedSSL that outperforms end-to-end training with a similar memory requirement and a 1.8Ã—1.8\times cheaper communication cost.

1 Introduction

A significant portion of real-world data, valuable for practical AI applications, resides on edge devices. Such data is often unlabeled, making the adoption of self-supervised learning (SSL) strategies Chen et al. (2020); He et al. (2020); Chen and He (2021) essential. SSL enables models to learn from raw data by generating their own supervisory signals, eliminating the need for labels. Most SSL methods follow centralized training schemes, often requiring an exhaustive data collection process to build a centrally stored dataset. Although some data, such as natural images, can be conveniently collected, it can raise significant concerns for privacy-sensitive data, such as medical data. Meanwhile, decentralized learning approaches like federated learning (FL) KoneÄná»³ et al. (2016); McMahan et al. (2017) enables privacy-preserving collaborative model training. An FL system functions by collecting trained model parameters from edge devices (a.k.a., clients) rather than data. This mechanism safeguards the confidentiality of private data held by different parties. As a result, federated self-supervised learning (FedSSL) approaches have recently emerged as a potential strategy for leveraging raw data in distributed environments Zhuang et al. (2021); Li et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023).

SSL, especially when involving state-of-the-art models like transformers Vaswani et al. (2017); Dosovitskiy et al. (2021a), can impose substantial computation demands. Unfortunately, clients in an FL environment often operate with limited computation and communication resources. Therefore, it is crucial to explore resource-efficient SSL approaches for FL systems. A straightforward way to reduce computation demands on low-memory devices is to conduct the training with a smaller batch size. However, using a small batch size for SSL approaches can diminish the quality of learned representations and compromise performance Chen et al. (2020); Li et al. (2023). Moreover, simply reducing the batch size does not lower the FL communication cost since clients still need to exchange the full model with the server. On the other hand, using a large batch size would exclude low-memory devices from participating in the FL process.

One promising solution is the layer-wise training approach, where a model is systematically trained one layer at a time, gradually increasing its depth. The term â€œlayerâ€ can refer to either an individual layer or a block comprising multiple layers. In layer-wise training, only a single layer is active at a time for training, significantly reducing computation requirements. Moreover, only the active layer needs to be exchanged with the server, further reducing communication costs. TableÂ 1 shows a significant reduction in a clientâ€™s resource requirements for layer-wise training compared to end-to-end training.

Although layer-wise training significantly reduces resource requirements, it may not be able to match the performance of end-to-end training as shown in TableÂ 1. In each stage of layer-wise training, a new layer is sequentially attached to the model, and all preceding layers are frozen, excluding them from further updates. Given a fixed number of FL rounds for each stage, it can be challenging to train the newly added layer to work seamlessly with the previous layers, especially if the number of rounds is small. Although increasing the number of rounds could potentially alleviate this problem, it would impose extra communication and computation overhead on the clients, canceling the benefits of layer-wise training.

To address these challenges, we propose LW-FedSSL, by introducing two simple, yet effective mechanisms: server-side calibration and representation alignment. Although clients in an FL environment may have limited resources, the central server typically possesses significant computation power. Therefore, in LW-FedSSL, the server-side calibration mechanism leverages the computation resources of the central server to train the global model on an auxiliary dataset. This mechanism solely operates on the central server and incurs no additional overhead on the clients. Additionally, the representation alignment mechanism encourages alignment between local models and the server-side-trained global model during the local training process.

Our contributions can be summarized as follows: (i) We conduct an in-depth exploration of layer-wise training for FedSSL. (ii) We introduce LW-FedSSL, a framework designed to improve the effectiveness of layer-wise training in the context of FedSSL. LW-FedSSL achieves comparable performance to end-to-end training while significantly lowering the resource requirements of FL clients. (iii)Â We also explore a progressive training approach called Prog-FedSSL. Although the progressive approach requires more resources than the layer-wise approach, it still remains more resource-efficient than end-to-end training while significantly outperforming both.

3 Related Work

Layer-wise training was initially introduced in the context of deep belief networks and restricted Boltzmann machines Hinton et al. (2006); Bengio et al. (2006). The original motivation behind its development was to address the challenges associated with training deep neural networks, such as vanishing gradients. While layer-wise training has become less common over the years, it can still be effectively employed as a resource-efficient strategy within FL systems. Training the model incrementally, one layer at a time, offers a distinct resource-saving advantage over end-to-end training. To date, only a limited number of studiesÂ Wang et al. (2022); Guo et al. (2023) have explored layer-wise training in the FL context, and even fewerÂ Guo et al. (2023) have examined its application in the domain of FedSSL.

Some studies Caldas et al. (2018); Horvath et al. (2021) reduce clientsâ€™ computation costs by selectively dropping parts of the model. Federated Dropout Caldas et al. (2018) randomly removes a subset of neurons or filters from a layer to reduce the computation cost while relying on compression techniques to minimize the communication overhead. FjORD Horvath et al. (2021), a similar approach, introduces an ordered dropout mechanism instead of random dropping. Meanwhile, Wang et al.Â Wang et al. (2022) propose a progressive training approach for FL called ProgFed. While similar to layer-wise training in gradually increasing the model depth, ProgFed differs by training all existing layers at each stage. The aforementioned studies primarily focus on federated supervised learning tasks that require labeled data for training.

On the other hand, Huo et al.Â Huo et al. (2021) study layer-wise SSL in the speech domain. While the study was intended for an on-device training scenario, the authors mainly examined it in a centralized setting. Another study by Guo et al.Â Guo et al. (2023) is closely related to our work, as it also explores layer-wise training for FedSSL. The authors propose a depth dropout technique which aims to reduce both computation and communication overhead but may lead to a slight decrease in performance. In contrast to the above studies, our work offers an in-depth exploration of layer-wise training in FedSSL. However, as we have previously demonstrated, layer-wise training alone may not achieve the same level of performance as end-to-end training. To bridge this gap, we introduce LW-FedSSL, a layer-wise training approach that can compete end-to-end training with significantly lower client resource requirements. Moreover, we explore a progressive training approach called Prog-FedSSL, which outperforms end-to-end training while requiring fewer resources.

4.1 LW-FedMoCoV3

Firstly, we discuss the pure layer-wise training baseline, referred to as LW-FedMoCoV3. FigureÂ 1 illustrates the local training process of LW-FedMoCoV3 across different stages. For an encoder Fğ¹F with a total of Sğ‘†S layers, the training process is divided into Sğ‘†S stages. Each stage sâˆˆ[1,S]ğ‘ 1ğ‘†s\in[1,S] runs for a fixed number of FL communication rounds, Rssubscriptğ‘…ğ‘ R_{s}. The training process starts with an empty encoder Fğ¹F, and each stage sğ‘ s sequentially adds a new layer Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s} to Fğ¹F, gradually increasing its depth. During stage sğ‘ s, only the corresponding layer Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s} is updated, while all prior layers L1subscriptğ¿1L_{1} to Lsâˆ’1subscriptğ¿ğ‘ 1L_{s-1} within Fğ¹F are frozen, serving only for inference. Likewise, the server only needs to broadcast the aggregated layer Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s} back to clients. This reduces both upload and download communication costs for clients. 4.2 LW-FedSSL

To address the performance gap between layer-wise and end-to-end training for FedSSL, we propose LW-FedSSL by introducing two crucial mechanisms: server-side calibration, integrated at the server, and representation alignment, integrated into the local training process. FigureÂ 2 illustrates these two mechanisms of LW-FedSSL at stage sğ‘ s.

Server-side calibration: In layer-wise training, only the most recently added layer, Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s}, is actively trained by clients at any given stage sğ‘ s. This may limit prior layersâ€™ ability to adapt to the newly added layer, potentially degrading the modelâ€™s performance. Given a limited number of rounds Rssubscriptğ‘…ğ‘ R_{s} available for each stage, solely training Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s} may prove insufficient to match the training received by the end-to-end strategy. On the other hand, increasing the number of rounds could introduce additional resource demands on clients. To tackle this, we can take advantage of resources available at the FL server. The server can contribute by training the current sub-model on a small auxiliary dataset, Dgsuperscriptğ·ğ‘”D^{g}, at the end of every communication round. Specifically, following the aggregation process, the server can train the aggregated encoder Fğ¹F across all existing layers (i.e., L1subscriptğ¿1L_{1} to Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s}) before broadcasting Fğ¹F back to the clients. The dataset Dgsuperscriptğ·ğ‘”D^{g} may originate from a different domain than that of the clientsâ€™ data, as the server cannot directly access data within clients. Furthermore, Dgsuperscriptğ·ğ‘”D^{g} may typically be unlabeled, requiring server-side training to also utilize an SSL approach. We believe that training across all layers in the current sub-model plays a crucial role in calibrating the layers to function harmoniously. Also, our assumption regarding the auxiliary data at the server can be reasonably met since the server may require a small dataset for various purposes, such as performance monitoring, pre-training, distillation, or other tasks in the FL context Li et al. (2023); Sattler et al. (2021).

Such data heterogeneity can introduce bias to the local training process, negatively affecting the performance of the global model. To mitigate this, some studiesÂ Li et al. (2021) encourage local training processes across different clients to converge toward parameters that are closely aligned with the global parameters. Often, this is achieved by aligning the representations of local models with those of the global model while simultaneously pushing them away from old representations (from old local models of previous FL rounds). In contrast, our representation alignment mechanism does not require old local parameters. As a result, clients no longer need to store and perform inference on old parameters, reducing the overhead. 4.3 Prog-FedSSL

Inspired by Wang et al.Â Wang et al. (2022), we explore progressive training for FedSSL, denoted as Prog-FedSSL. Similar to LW-FedMoCoV3, progressive training also starts with an empty encoder Fğ¹F, appending a new layer Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s} to Fğ¹F at each stage sğ‘ s. However, in contrast to updating only a single layer Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s}, progressive training updates all existing layers (i.e., L1subscriptğ¿1L_{1} to Lssubscriptğ¿ğ‘ L_{s}) during each stage sğ‘ s. This distinction results in higher computation and communication costs for Prog-FedSSL than layer-wise training approaches. Nonetheless, when compared with end-to-end training, which requires clients to train a full model with Sğ‘†S layers (i.e., L1subscriptğ¿1L_{1} to LSsubscriptğ¿ğ‘†L_{S}) at each round, Prog-FedSSL enables clients to train only a sub-model with sğ‘ s layers (sâ‰¤S)ğ‘ ğ‘†(s\leq S). Moreover, clients only need to exchange the sub-model with the server. In essence, Prog-FedSSL saves both computation and communication costs for (Sâˆ’s)ğ‘†ğ‘ (S-s) layers at each stage sğ‘ s compared to end-to-end training. As we will demonstrate in SectionÂ 5.2, progressive training outperforms both layer-wise and end-to-end training approaches. Nevertheless, it is important to note that the memory requirement of progressive training gradually increases as the stages progress, becoming equivalent to that of end-to-end training at the final stage (i.e., when s=Sğ‘ ğ‘†s=S). 5.3 Ablation Study

In FigureÂ 4, we compare LW-FedSSL with the baseline LW-FedMoCoV3 and its two variants, which solely incorporate either server-side calibration or representation alignment. Incorporating server-side calibration improves performance on the CIFAR-10 dataset (from which the auxiliary data is sampled), but leads to a decline on other datasets. This may result from training on auxiliary data, whose update direction conflicts with that of local training. Compared to the baseline, incorporating representation alignment obtains better performance on the STL-10 dataset, with comparable performance on the others. Meanwhile, LW-FedSSL, which incorporates both mechanisms, consistently outperforms the baseline across all datasets. One plausible explanation for this is that representation alignment encourages local representations to stay close to global representations during local training. This in turn helps preserve the knowledge acquired from local data during the server-side calibration process. FigureÂ 4 suggests that instead of acting independently, server-side calibration and representation alignment are complementary for improving the performance of layer-wise training.

For the supervised baseline, an end-to-end classification model is trained with vanilla FL using labeled data, and then only the encoder part is kept for linear evaluation. 5.6 Number of Clients

To investigate the impact of the number of clients Nğ‘N, we conduct experiments with Nğ‘N set to 5, 10, 30, and 45. For each setting, we uniformly partition the unlabeled data portion of the STL-10 dataset into Nğ‘N local datasets. Therefore, the total number of data samples remains constant across all settings. Linear evaluation results with varying Nğ‘N values are shown in FigureÂ 7b. We can observe that all approaches, except LW-FedSSL, experience a drop in performance as the number of clients increases. Integration of representation alignment mechanism enables LW-FedSSL to maintain good performance even in scenarios with a large number of clients Li et al. (2021).

5.7 Learning Rate Strategy

By default, we use cosine decay scheduling for the learning rate. We explore different learning rate strategies for LW-FedMoCoV3, LW-FedSSL, and Prog-FedSSL, including a fixed learning rate and cosine decay scheduling for each training stage, which we refer to as â€œcyclicâ€. FigureÂ 8 illustrates how the learning rate changes at each local epoch for different strategies, and the results are presented in FigureÂ 6. For LW-FedMoCoV3, using a fixed learning rate achieves the best performance, and also for both LW-FedSSL and Prog-FedSSL, simply using a fixed learning rate can be a decent choice. A fixed learning rate may allow layers in later stages to receive effective updates, whereas in cosine decay scheduling, later layers undergo minimal updates due to a small learning rate. While the cyclic learning rate pattern proves to work decently for Prog-FedSSL, it may not be suitable for LW-FedMoCoV3 and LW-FedSSL. As seen in FigureÂ 8, the learning rate for cyclic strategy drops quickly in each stage when only a limited number of rounds are available. This can impact layer-wise training, where a layer receives training only in its corresponding stage. (For LW-FedSSL with a fixed learning rate, we do not use weight transfer, as it demonstrates improved performance.)

6 Conclusion

Edge devices in distributed environments struggle to meet the computation and communication requirements of FedSSL. In response to these challenges, we propose a layer-wise training approach called LW-FedSSL, enabling FL clients to reduce their resource requirements. Our experiments demonstrate that LW-FedSSL has a 3.3Ã—3.3\times lower memory requirement, 2.1Ã—2.1\times lower total FLOPs consumption, and 3.2Ã—3.2\times cheaper total transmission cost (download + upload), while achieving comparable performance to its end-to-end training counterpart. Furthermore, we explore a progressive training approach called Prog-FedSSL, which remains more resource-efficient than end-to-end training, with significantly improved performance.
