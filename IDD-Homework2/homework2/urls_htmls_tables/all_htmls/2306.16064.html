Abstract

Existing federated learning solutions focus on transmitting features, parameters or gadients between clients and server, which suffer from serious low-efficiency and privacy-leakage problems. Thanks to the emerging foundation generative models, we propose a novel federated learning framework, namely Federated Generative Learning, that transmits prompts associated with distributed training data between clients and server. The informative training data can be synthesized remotely based on received prompts containing little privacy and the foundation generative models. The new framework possesses multiple advantages, including improved communication efficiency, better resilience to distribution shift, substantial performance gains, and enhanced privacy protection, which are verified in extensive experiments on ImageNet and DomainNet datasets111The implementation is available at https://github.com/zj-jayzhang/Federated_Generative_Learning..

Recently, significant progresses has been achieved in many learning fields by scaling up to large models, i.e., BERTÂ Devlin etÂ al. (2018), GPT3Â Brown etÂ al. (2020), ViTÂ Dosovitskiy etÂ al. Typically, the large models are first pre-trained with massive low-quality web data for basic capability, then fine-tuned with a small number of high-quality data, especially manually-labeled data, for evoking the desired capability. Though the web data are easy to access, the high-quality training data are still scarce, since high-quality datasets are usually private or unsuitable to release. For example, most medical data are expensive to labelling and sensitive to release due to safety and privacy problems. In addition, the raw data is usually the key assets of many companies, which is impossible to be distributed. Therefore, the demand for collaborative machine learningÂ Gong etÂ al. In recent years, Federated Learning (FL)Â McMahan etÂ al. (2017); Kairouz etÂ al. (2021b); Bonawitz etÂ al. (2019); KoneÄná»³ etÂ al. (2016); Silva etÂ al. (2019); Zhao etÂ al. The classic federated learning framework consists of model initialization, local training, model aggregation, and global updating. In practice, FL systems encounter several challenges that impede their real-world application:

  1. 1.

    High communication cost. Existing FL solutions have to transmit the model parameters or gradients between clients and server. However, in large-model era, model parameters are usually billions to trillions. The transmission of model parameters or gradients requires high communication cost which is impractical even unaffordable. Furthermore, the communication cost will significantly increase in federated continual learning scenario, since more commnication rounds are required for learning from continuously received training data.

  2. 2.

    Data heterogeneity. In FL, a fundamental challenge arises from the presence of statistical heterogeneity among local data distributions across distinct clients. This leads to the problem of objective inconsistency for model-averaging-based FL methods, as the global model converges to a stationary point of a mismatched objective Wang etÂ al. (2020); Zhang etÂ al. (2022b), thus causing remarkable performance degradation.

  3. 3.

    Privacy and security risks. The most important concern of FL systems is data privacy and security. The popular solutions transmit model parameters / gradientsÂ McMahan etÂ al. (2017), or synthetic dataÂ Zhou etÂ al. (2020) that all suffer from serious privacy leakageÂ Zhao etÂ al. (2020). How to train models efficiently and privately is long-standing problem.

The recent advancements in foundation generative models, i.e., Stable diffusion Â Rombach etÂ al. (2022b), DALL-E2 Â Ramesh etÂ al. (2022), Imagen Â Saharia etÂ al. (2022), and GLIDE Â Nichol etÂ al. (2021), have provided a high-quality text conditional image synthesis which can be used to train models. Especially, these foundation models are off-the-shelf and publicly available. Besides, the privacy investigationÂ Carlini etÂ al. (2023) shows that Stable Diffusion synthesized images will contain 109 near-copies of training images when synthesizing 175 million images, which means extremely low privacy leakage risk. Thus, we pose the following question:

Can Federated Learning be accomplished by transmitting the compact knowledge with little privacy of local training data based on the foundation generative models?

In this paper, we propose a novel framework called Federated Generative Learning (FGL), which leverages the powerful foundation generative models, e.g., Stable Diffusion, to synthesize high-quality substitute training data in the server based on the prompts from clients. Specifically, our method only requires each client to upload the prompts corresponding to their local training data. After collecting all prompts from the clients, the server performs prompt aggregation and synthesize a high-quality substitute training dataset. Then, a global model can be trained on the synthetic dataset. There are multiple benefits of the proposed framework:

  1. 1.

    Low Communication Cost. Compared to previous methods that rely on multi-round communication of weights or gradients, our method requires only one communication round between clients and the server. As shown in FigureÂ 1(b), during the initial communication between any client and the server, the parameter transmission in FedAvg needs 42.7MB for the small network ResNet18, while our method only sends 229 Bytes prompts. In addition, this advantage will be enlarged when training large models with billions of parameters and implementing federated continua learning in which requires more communication rounds for learning from new data.

  2. 2.

    Robust to Data Heterogeneity. Since our method only requires clients to upload prompts corresponding to their local training data, it allows the server to collect prompts from all clients and synthesize all training data in one communication round. As a result, our method exhibits insensitivity to data distribution. FigureÂ 1(b) compares our method to FedAvg under three different data distributions. Ours (one-shot), consistently achieves good performance across all distributions. Furthermore, our method can significantly improve the performance and even outperform the centralized training by implementing five-round communication.

  3. 3.

    Better privacy-preserving: Traditional FL involves the transmission of model parameters or gradients between clients and server. However, once these model parameters are leaked, the attackers can carry out model extraction attacksÂ Li etÂ al. (2023b), member inference attacksÂ Li etÂ al. (2023a), and other malicious activitiesÂ Zhang etÂ al. (2023b); Zhu etÂ al. (2019), posing significant security threats to the FL systemÂ Nguyen & Thai (2022); Liu etÂ al. (2022). In contrast, foundation generative model based FL can better protect data privacy by transmitting prompts. Our method can implement FL in few round communication which significantly reduces the privacy leakage risk.

2 Related Work

2.1 Federated Learning

The rising concerns on data privacy and security have caused the data silo problem that prevents the development of artificial general intelligence. Federated learning (FL) Â McMahan etÂ al. (2017) has been a promising solution to this problem which allows multiple clients to collaboratively train a global model in the server without exposing their private training data. Typically, each client trains a local model on its private data and periodically shares its latest model parameters with the server. Then, the server will deliver the global models to clients. FL has been widely used in real-world applications, including healthcare Liu etÂ al. (2018); Silva etÂ al. (2019); Cui etÂ al. (2021); Liu etÂ al. (2021a); Chen etÂ al. (2021), recommendation systems Â Liang etÂ al. (2021); Liu etÂ al. (2021b), finance Suzumura etÂ al. (2019); Liu etÂ al. (2020); Long etÂ al. (2020) and internet of things Chen etÂ al. (2020); Hu etÂ al. (2020); Savazzi etÂ al. (2020). Nevertheless, federated learning methods suffer from the problems of high communication cost, data heterogeneity, privacy and security risks and significant performance decrease.

Many studiesÂ Zhou etÂ al. (2020); Dennis etÂ al. (2021); Zhang etÂ al. (2022a) attempt to reduce communication overhead in federated learning by designing one-shot communication methods. For example, Zhu et al.Â Zhou etÂ al. (2020) use dataset distillation Â Wang etÂ al. (2018) to implement one-shot federated learning. But the distilled dataset has potential privacy leakage problem. Zhang et al. Â Zhang etÂ al. (2022a) propose a two-stage data-free one-shot federated learning method. However, compared to few-shot federated learning, the performance of this method decreases significantly. Different from them, our method can realize one-shot federated learning with little communication overhead and achieve high-performance while little privacy leakage by transmitting prompts.

To address the data heterogeneity problem, data-based methods pre-process client data before training, such as using data sharing or data augmentation techniquesÂ Zhao etÂ al. (2018); deÂ Luca etÂ al. (2022). Algorithm-based methods employ learning techniques, such as knowledge distillation, multi-task learning, and personalized layers, while system-based methods use client-based clustering methods to better understand the heterogeneous data environment. However, these methods do not fundamentally solve the problem of imbalanced data distribution. In contrast, our method can solve the data heterogeneity problem by synthesizing all training data at once in the server based on the prompts sent from all clients.

2.2 Foundation Models

Foundation Generative Models.

Large generative models, such as Stable Diffusion Â Rombach etÂ al. (2022b), DALL-E2 Â Ramesh etÂ al. (2022), Imagen Â Saharia etÂ al. (2022), and GLIDE Â Nichol etÂ al. (2021), have recently emerged as an off-the-shelf tool for high-quality and real-looking image generation conditioned on text prompts. A few works have been exploring the usage of synthetic images as training data. For example, He et al. Â He etÂ al. (2022) show that synthetic data generated by diffusion models can improve pre-training, zero-shot, and few-shot image classification performance. Li et al. Â Li etÂ al. (2023d) demonstrate that synthetic data generated by conditional diffusion models can be used for knowledge distillation without access to original data. Zhou et al.Zhou etÂ al. (2023) synthesize better images for model training with stable diffusion by implementing diffusion inversion. Though good performance has been achieved, inverting every image is expensive and will leak privacy.

Foundation Models in FL.

The foundation generative models are still under-explored in federated learning, though there exist a few related works that study foundation models in federated learning. The most similar one is Yang etÂ al. (2023) which leverage diffusion model in the server to synthesize training samples that complied to the distributions of domain-specific features from clients. Yu et al. Yu etÂ al. (2023) introduce federated learning into foundation model training for training foundation models collaboratively with private data. Like traditional FL methods, they also transmit model parameters between servers and clients. Based on the shared CLIP model, Guo et al. Guo etÂ al. (2022) transmit the small number of updated parameters of prompt learner from clients to server for reducing communication cost. Our study is different from them in multiple aspects: 1) Their methods transmit features or parameters, while our federated generative learning framework transmits prompts which require less communication while are better at privacy-preserving. 2) Our framework is based on shared foundation generative models, while theirs are based on the shared foundation classification models.

4 Federated Generative Learning

The overall framework of the proposed Federated Generative Learning framework is illustrated in Figure Â 2. Detecting Content Replication and Memorization.

Membership Inference Attack (MIA).

The objective of MIA is to examine whether a specific data point belongs to the training set used to train a machine learning model. Due to the fact that our method does not directly use real training data, intuitively it should be able to defend against membership inference attacks. To verify this, we employ popular metrics in MIA, such as confidenceÂ Salem etÂ al. (2018), loss Â Sablayrolles etÂ al. (2019); Yeom etÂ al. (2018), entropy Â Salem etÂ al. The greater similarity between the distributions of the modelâ€™s output metrics (e.g., loss and entropy) for training members and non-members, the more membership privacy the model can guarantee. We calculate the average metric values for training members and non-members, respectively, and then compare the difference between the two averages. The results are presented in TableÂ 3, which demonstrates that our method can reduce the discrepancy between training members and non-members on most metrics compared to FedAvg. 6 Conclusion

In this work, we introduce a pioneering framework for Federated Learning, named Federated Generative Learning, which transmits prompts associated with distributed training data between clients and the server. By leveraging foundation generative models, informative training data can be synthesized remotely using received prompts that contain minimal privacy. The proposed framework exhibits several noteworthy advantages, including improved communication efficiency, better resilience to distribution shift, substantial performance gains, and enhanced privacy protection. We hope this work can inspire researchers in the field of Federated Learning to shift from transmitting model parameters/gradients to the new paradigm of transmitting prompts.
