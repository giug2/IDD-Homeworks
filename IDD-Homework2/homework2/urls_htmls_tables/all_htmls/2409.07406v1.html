Abstract

While personal characteristics influence people’s snapshot trust towards autonomous systems, their relationships with trust dynamics remain poorly understood. We conducted a human-subject experiment with 130 participants performing a simulated surveillance task aided by an automated threat detector. A comprehensive pre-experimental survey collected data on participants’ personal characteristics across 12 constructs and 28 dimensions. Based on data collected in the experiment, we clustered participants’ trust dynamics into three types and assessed differences among the three clusters in terms of personal characteristics, behaviors, performance, and post-experiment ratings. Participants were clustered into three groups, namely Bayesian decision makers, disbelievers, and oscillators. Results showed that the clusters differ significantly in seven personal characteristics: masculinity, positive affect, extraversion, neuroticism, intellect, performance expectancy, and high expectations. The disbelievers tend to have high neuroticism, and low performance expectancy. The oscillators tend to have higher scores in masculinity, positive affect, extraversion and intellect. We also found significant differences in the behaviors and post-experiment ratings among the three groups. The disbelievers are the least likely to blindly follow the recommendations made by the automated threat detector. Based on the significant personal characteristics, we developed a decision tree model to predict cluster types with an accuracy of 70%.

I Introduction

The increasing adoption of automated and autonomous systems in our society has led to a growing interest in understanding how trust develops between humans and these systems. Early work on trust in automation/autonomy is focused on identifying antecedents of trust. A range of factors have been identified and summarized in several literature review articles [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. In these early works, a person’s trust in automation is typically measured at specific time points, i.e., snapshot trust, predominately at the end of an experiment after a series of interactions and/or sometimes at the beginning of an experiment.

More recently, acknowledging that a person’s trust can change dynamically while interacting with autonomous technologies, there is a shift of research focus from snapshot trust to trust dynamics – how humans’ trust in autonomy forms and evolves due to moment-to-moment interaction with autonomous technologies [6, 7, 8]. Real-time trust prediction models [9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15] are developed and distinct type of trust dynamics are revealed [10, 13, 11, 14]. McMahon et al. [13] identified two distinct types of trust dynamics depending on the trust-dependent behavior exhibited by the participants: followers and preservers. Followers have a high propensity to trust and a greater likelihood of complying with recommendations, while preservers display lower levels of trust and are less likely to follow recommendations. In another study, Liu et al. [14] revealed two groups of trust dynamics: confident and skeptical [14]. Compared to the skeptial group, the confident group exhibited higher initial trust, slower trust erosion following trust violations, and higher trust recovery. Guo and Yang [10] found three types of trust dynamics in an episodic task setting: Bayesian decision maker, oscillator, and disbeliever. In a follow-up study, the same three types were revealed in a sequential task setting [11]. The Bayesian decision makers update their trust in a Bayesian manner, the oscillators’ trust fluctuates dramatically, and the disbelievers display very low trust in autonomy.

Despite existing research efforts on trust dynamics, one significant research gap remains. Prior research measuring snapshot trust reveals significant associations between certain personal characteristics and post-experimental trust [16, 17, 18, 19], and between personal characteristics and a person’s trust propensity (i.e., an individual’s inherent tendency to trust automation) [20, 21, 22]. While the importance of investigating trust dynamics over snapshot trust has been recognized [23, 6, 8], we know little about the relationships between trust dynamics and personal characteristics. This gap makes it challenging to comprehend which personal characteristic factors impact trust dynamics the most, how they do so, and how diverse individuals exhibit distinct trust dynamics.

To address this gap, in this study, we conducted a human-subject experiment involving a total of 130 participants performing a simulated surveillance task aided by an imperfect automated threat detector. Based on key literature review studies on trust in automation, we constructed a comprehensive list of 12 personal characteristics constructs and 28 dimensions that could potentially influence a person’s trust dynamics when interacting with autonomous agents. The survey was administered before the experiment. During the experiment, participants’ dynamic trust when interacting with the automated threat detector was recorded. With the experimental data, we first performed clustering analysis and obtained three clusters of trust dynamics: Bayesian decision maker, disbeliever, and oscillator. Subsequently, Analyses of Variance (ANOVAs) were conducted to explore differences across the three trust dynamics clusters. The clusters differed in seven personal characteristics, including masculinity, positive affect, extraversion, neuroticism, intellect, performance expectancy, and high expectations, as well as in participants’ behaviors and post-experiment ratings. Lastly, we developed a decision tree model for predicting which cluster a participant belongs to based on the participant’s personal characteristics. The seven significant personal characteristics were used as the input features, and the trust dynamics cluster as the prediction labels. The decision tree achieved an overall accuracy of 70.0%.

II Related Work

This section reviews three bodies of research that motivated our study. We begin by describing the shift of research focus from snapshot trust to trust dynamics. We then review the associations between personal characteristics and snapshot trust. At last, we present the limited amount of research on the clustering of trust dynamics.

II-A From trust to trust dynamics

Trust in automation/autonomy has been receiving a growing amount of research attention. Early works on this topic predominantly embraced a static perspective on trust, gauging it through pre- or post-experimental surveys. Numerous factors have been identified as antecedents of post-experimental trust. See [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 24] for a full list of factors.

More recently, acknowledging that a person’s trust can change dynamically while interacting with automated and autonomous technologies, there is a shift of research focus from snapshot trust to trust dynamics - how humans’ trust in autonomy forms and evolves due to moment-to-moment interaction with automated/autonomous technologies [6, 7, 8, 24]. One body of research on trust dynamics explores how trust is developed, violated, and recovered during the course of interaction [25, 26, 7, 6, 27]. Trust is established and developed as humans learn from the outcomes of their interactions, but trust violations can also happen after negative experiences [27, 28]. In cases where trust violations occur, efforts can be made to restore trust, and these efforts often involve various actions/strategies such as apologies [29, 30, 31], denials [31], explanations [32, 33, 30], and promises [34].

The second body of research is focused on developing real-time trust prediction models [35, 10, 11, 12]. Examples of trust prediction models include the online probabilistic trust inference model (OPTIMo) by [35], the Beta random variable model by [10], and the Bayesian model combining Gaussian processes and recurrent neural networks by [36]. For a detailed review, please refer to [37].

II-B Associations between personal characteristics and post-experimental trust

Extensive research has investigated personal factors that influence (snapshot) trust in automation, predominately measured at the end of an experiment after a series of interactions. Results show that human-related factors are significantly related to post-experiment trust [2, 3]. Broadly speaking, the factors can be classified into characteristics-based and ability-based variables [2]. The characteristics-based factors refer to the traits of an individual user that are associated with how they recognize themselves [2].

There are diverse dimensions, including personality, culture, mood, attitudes toward automation, propensity to trust, risk propensity, and decision-making style. Among them, the effects of personality traits on snapshot trust have been extensively investigated (e.g., [16, 17, 18]). For instance, personality traits like extraversion are known to positively correlate with trust [38, 17], while neuroticism shows a negative relationship with trust [17, 18]. In terms of cultural characteristics, Huang et al. [21] found that people with more beliefs in horizontal collectivism and individualism are inclined to have higher trust in automation, and Chien et al. [39] also identified similar positive correlations between individualism and trust. Regarding mood, Stokes et al. [40] have shown that positive moods have been linked to higher trust. Attitudes toward automation and propensity to trust are also known to serve as important antecedents of trust. Participants with positive attitudes and higher trust propensity tend to give higher ratings to the post-experiment trust scale [41, 11, 16]. Risk propensity, an individual’s tendency to take risks [42], is also proposed to have associations with their trust attitude as well as the resulting behaviors of whether they try to follow the automation or not [43]. In addition, decision-making style would also correlate with trust, as intuitive people were found to exhibit more reliance on automated decision aids [44].

The ability-based variables encompass factors associated with a user’s skills and competence, including factors like attentional capacity and self-confidence [2]. For attentional capacity, empirical studies have shown a negative relationship between it and trust. For instance, Chen and Terrence [45] showed that people with higher perceived attentional control had lower trust in automation and were less likely to rely on automation. Likewise, self-confidence is known to have a negative association; people with relatively higher self-confidence are likely to have lower trust and thus prefer to exert full control over a given task rather than following the automation [25, 46].

In summary, many human-related variables have significant associations with post-experimental trust. The insights derived from these prior research studies have prompted our focus on examining the associations between personal characteristics and trust dynamics.

II-C Clusters of trust dynamics

Notably, recent studies have revealed the existence of different types of trust dynamics [10, 13, 11, 14]. McMahon et al. [13] identified two distinct clusters, namely followers and preservers, based on participants’ trust-dependent behaviors. Followers were characterized by a high propensity to trust and a greater likelihood of complying with recommendations, while preservers displayed lower levels of trust and were less likely to follow recommendations. Liu et al. [14] categorized participants into skeptical and confident groups using variables derived from 12 different features. The confident group exhibited higher initial trust, slower trust erosion following trust violations, and higher trust recovery, in contrast to the skeptical group. Guo and Yang [10] identified three types of trust dynamics in an episodic task setting: Bayesian decision maker, oscillator and disbeliever. The Bayesian decision makers update their trust in a Bayesian manner, the oscillators’ trust fluctuates dramatically, and the disbelievers display very low trust in autonomy. Bhat et al. [11] found the same three types in a sequential task setting. This body of clustering research underscores the suitability of unsupervised clustering methods for revealing unique trust dynamics groups.

It’s important to note that some of these studies [13, 14] incorporated behavior-related variables (i.e., compliance with agent recommendations, reliance on automation) into clustering. While it might seem reasonable to include these metrics to classify individuals’ reliance behaviors, it is essential to differentiate between trust as an attitude and trusting behaviors [47]. Considering trust as a behavior could introduce potential confounders, such as workload, situational awareness, and operator self-confidence [47, 25]. Therefore, our study placed a primary focus on trust attitudes. We aimed to identify trust dynamics clusters based solely on users’ trust attitudes and to investigate whether these distinct trust dynamics clusters exhibit unique patterns of reliance and compliance behaviors.

II-D Associations between personal characteristics and trust dynamics

Despite growing research on identifying distinct clusters of trust dynamics, limited attention has been directed toward exploring the associations between personal characteristics and trust dynamics. To the best of our knowledge, one study [11] attempted this but only covered a very limited subset of personal characteristics, including personality, perfect automation schema, and propensity to trust. In comparison to the extensive body of empirical research evaluating the impacts of various personal factors on snapshot trust at the end of an experiment, a significant research gap is apparent. To address this, our study assembled an exhaustive list of personal characteristics that were previously shown to affect snapshot trust and, therefore could potentially influence one’s trust dynamics. Our focus is to determine whether different trust dynamics groups can be characterized by specific personal characteristic variables.

III METHOD

We conducted a human-subject experiment where participants engaged in a simulated surveillance task with the help of an imperfect automated threat detector. The research studies complied with the American Psychological Association code of ethics and were approved by the institutional review board.

III-A Participants

We gathered data from 130 individuals (average age=22.6 years, SD=3.5). All participants had normal or corrected-to-normal vision. None of them had previously taken part in a similar study or had any prior experience with the specific testbed used in the current research. Participants were compensated with $20 upon completion of the experiment. Additionally, there was an opportunity to receive a performance-based bonus ranging from $2.5 to $10.

III-B Apparatus and stimuli

During the experiment, the participants worked with a swarm of drones to perform a surveillance task at 100100100100 sites (i.e. 100 trials). Each trial lasted for 10 seconds. During a trial, the participants had to perform two tasks simultaneously, including a compensatory tracking task and a detection task to spot potential threats from the photo feeds captured by the drones (Figure 1). Participants had access to either the tracking task or the detection task display at any given time and needed to toggle between the two displays.

Tracking task. The tracking task was constructed based on the PEBL compensatory tracker task (http://pebl.sourceforge.net/battery.html). Using a joystick, participants had to control the movement of a green circle, which randomly drifted on the screen. The goal was to maneuver the circle towards a crosshair positioned at the center of the display (see Figure 1(a)). The performance of the tracking task was assessed based on the average distance between the circle’s position and the center. This measurement captured how effectively participants could control the circle and maintain its proximity to the crosshair during the 10-second duration of each trial.

Detection task. In addition to the tracking task, participants were responsible for detecting threats. Each trial involved participants receiving a new set of four static images from the drones for threat detection. The images were presented as shown in Figure 1(b). The threat being detected was represented by a person and only one threat appeared among the four images. The distribution of threats across the four images followed a uniform distribution, ensuring randomness.

An imperfect automated threat detector assisted the participants in performing the threat detection task. If the detector identified a threat, an alert in both visual and auditory form would go off. The visual alert was displayed on the tracking display (Figure 1(a)), while the auditory notification was delivered through sound. Participants were expected to accurately and promptly identify the presence of the threat by pressing the “Report” button on the joystick. Participants were not required to report the absence of a threat; their expected action was to take no action when there was no threat.

Toggle between two displays. Every trial started on the tracking display. Participants were allowed access to only one display at a time and were required to switch between the tracking and detection displays using a “Switch” button on the joystick. A 0.5-second time delay was introduced each time they toggled between displays, simulating computer processing and loading. The program automatically recorded the time stamp and the number of times participants pressed the “Switch” button during the experiment.

Scoring system. During the experimental task, participants simultaneously performed the tracking task and the detection task. They had to make a trade-off decision on which task to focus on at any given time. For instance, if they chose to check the four images, they could potentially earn more points in the detection task but fewer points in the tracking task, and vice versa. Participants could earn 0-10 points for the tracking task and 0-5 points for the detection task in every trial. Thus, the total points achievable in each trial were 15 points. The tracking score was computed using a 10-bin histogram of the Root Mean Square of the tracking errors (RMSE) distribution, which was based on a dataset from a previous study [23]. III-D Measures

In our study, we collected a range of subjective, behavioral and performance data. Prior to the experiment, we collected data on participants’ personal characteristics. During the experiment, we captured participants’ dynamic trust, their behaviors, and their performance. After the experiment, participants filled a post-experiment survey.

To identify relevant factors that affect users’ trust in autonomy, we conducted a review of key literature review and meta-analysis papers focusing on summarizing factors influencing trust, including [1, 2, 3, 5, 4]. From these papers, we compiled all personal characteristics previously shown to be associated with snapshot trust in autonomy. As a result, we had a list of 12 distinct constructs encompassing a total of 28 dimensions.

For each construct, we utilized well-established scales. Table II tabulates the 12 personal characteristics constructs and the measurement scale used in our study. III-E Experimental procedure

Upon arrival, participants were asked to provide informed consent and fill out a pre-experiment survey, including the demographics and the personal characteristics surveys. After that, participants received a training and practice session on the tasks. The practice session consisted of 30 trials with only the tracking task and eight trials combining both the tracking and detection tasks. The eight trials involved equal numbers of hits, misses, false alarms, and correct rejections, enabling participants to experience all possible cases. Participants were informed that the automated threat detector used during the practice was for illustration purposes.

The actual experiment consisted of a total of 100 trials, with a five-minute break after the 50⁢t⁢h50𝑡ℎ50th50 italic_t italic_h trial. After completing all trials, participants filled in the post-experiment survey, which gathered their overall trust and general subjective evaluations. Finally, they were compensated for their participation and received a bonus based on their total scores.

IV DATA ANALYSIS AND RESULTS

In this study, we performed trust prediction for the 130 participants, followed by a clustering analysis. After assigning each participant to specific cluster groups based on the clustering analysis, we conducted ANOVA to investigate if there were any significant differences between the clusters. In cases where there were significant differences, post-hoc tests with Bonferroni corrections were conducted.

IV-C Decision tree model

To predict the type of trust dynamics a user would exhibit based on their personal characteristics before they interact with an autonomous system, we developed a decision tree model using the seven personal characteristics identified in Section IV-B. The input variables to the decision tree model were scores on masculinity, positive affect, extraversion, neuroticism, intellect, performance expectancy, PAS-high expectations.

We first randomly divided the dataset into a training and validation, and test dataset in an 80: 20 ratio. Subsequently, within the training and validation dataset, we conducted hyperparameter tuning using the five-fold cross-validation method (i.e., 64% and 16% of total data points). Our goal was to identify the parameters that would produce the highest average weighted F1 score for the validation datasets. We chose the weighted F1 score as the main criterion considering that the distribution of the cluster groups is imbalanced. Consequently, we determined the hyperparameters of a maximum depth to be 6 and a minimum number of leaves in a node to be 1 and the splitting algorithm to be entropy. With these hyperparameters, we configured a decision tree model and tested it with the test dataset. The tree achieved an accuracy of 70.0%. Figure 8 shows the decision tree, and Figure 9 shows the confusion matrix.

From the decision tree, we could see that some meaningful characteristics were used as decision criteria. Most of the oscillators were classified on the right-hand side of the tree because they tended to have higher ratings in characteristics such as intellect and masculinity. The decision tree also demonstrated fairly good classification performance, particularly in terms of recalling the minority groups, disbelievers, and oscillators.

V DISCUSSION

V-A Clustering trust dynamics

In contrast to prior research focusing on snapshot trust, our study focused on the dynamics of trust and employed a large participant sample (n=130𝑛130n=130italic_n = 130). We first predicted a person’s trust in real-time employing the Beta random variable model and clustered the trust dynamics into distinct groups. Compared to other studies that utilized features associated with participants’ behaviors for clustering trust dynamics [14, 13], our approach focused solely on metrics derived from participants’ subjective trust ratings. This emphasis aligns with the definition of trust as “an attitude that an agent will help achieve an individual’s goals in a situation characterized by uncertainty and vulnerability [47]”. Consequently, our goal was to center the analysis on this attitude itself. This led to the identification of three distinct cluster groups, BDM, disbeliever, and oscillator, each exhibiting unique trust dynamics characteristics. It’s worth noting that the clustering outcomes and their distribution exhibited similarities with those reported in [11] and [10]. Our study benefits from a larger sample size (n=130𝑛130n=130italic_n = 130) and further proves the generalizability of the three clusters of trust dynamics across multiple datasets.

V-B Associations between trust dynamics, personal characteristics, behaviors

Our study successfully identified meaningful associations between an individual’s characteristics and their trust dynamics and behavioral metrics. By thoroughly examining a wide array of personal characteristics variables known to influence (snapshot) trust, our study contributes to a comprehensive understanding of this intricate relationship. Seven personal characteristics dimensions, including masculinity, positive affect, extraversion, neuroticism, intellect, performance expectancy, and PAS-high expectations, differed significantly among the three clusters. Our results highlight distinct characteristics of the disbeliever and oscillator groups.

The disbelievers are characterized by high scores for neuroticism and low scores for performance expectancy and PAS-high expectations. In addition, they display low post-experiment trust and the least amount of blind-following behaviors. Neuroticism refers to the tendency to experience negative emotions such as anxiety, sadness, anger, or emotional instability [53]. In prior research, the neuroticism personality trait has been shown to have a negative relationship with post-experiment trust [17, 18]. In addition, individuals with high neuroticism tend to rely more on their own judgment instead of on the automation [17]. The alignment between our results and prior research supports a coherent picture that disbelievers, being highly neurotic, have the lowest post-experiment trust and cross-check the automated threat detector the most. The consistency also provides validity for clustering the disbelievers as a distinct group. The performance expectancy and PAS-high expectations dimensions both measure an individual’s expectation of an automated/autonomous system’s performance [57, 60]. Prior research examining the relationship between expectant and post-experiment trust, and between perfect automation schema (PAS) and post-experiment trust revealed a positive relationship between expectancy and trust [66], as well as between the high expectations facet of PAS and trust [67]. Likewise, the results of our study imply that disbelievers have low prior expectations of automation, and their trust levels are likely to remain low throughout their interaction with the automation.

Regarding the oscillator group, they displayed higher scores in masculinity, positive affect, extraversion and intellect. These results have interesting implications for previous research studies. In fact, positive affect [40], extraversion [38, 17], and intellect [68] are factors that have been proposed to have a positive correlation with trust. On the other hand, the cultural dimension of masculinity has been suggested to be related to making the transfer of trust across entities difficult [69]. However, in our study, all of these dimensions were more prominent for the oscillator group, whose trust neither remained consistently high nor low but fluctuated significantly. Still, since the number of oscillators in this study was 14, further research focused on exploring the characteristics of the oscillator group could greatly enhance our understanding of their trust dynamics.

V-C Predicting trust dynamics based on personal characteristics

We developed a decision tree model using key personal characteristics to classify the type of trust dynamics. The accuracy of the decision tree demonstrates the potential to predict the type of trust dynamics to which a person belongs based on their personal characteristics. Being able to predict a person’s trust dynamics type before their interaction with autonomous technology could lead to specialized trust prediction algorithms tailored to each trust dynamics type, enabling the design of trust-aware agents.

In particular, such a classification model would be helpful in identifying individuals who would behave very differently from a Bayesian decision maker. For instance, the study shed light on the existence of a group of individuals whose trust exhibits significant fluctuations over time (oscillators). Their trust dynamics made it very difficult to accurately predict their trust using the Bayesian inference model. This finding, in conjunction with the classification model, opens up a future research avenue, suggesting the need for a more robust trust prediction model specifically designed to address the unique behavior of the oscillator group.

VI CONCLUSION

The objective of the current study was to explore the relationships between personal characteristics and trust dynamics. To achieve this, we conducted a human-subject experiment to collect trust dynamics data from 130 participants as they engaged in a simulated surveillance task aided by an automated threat detector. Additionally, we gathered comprehensive personal characteristic data from participants through a survey, along with post-experiment subjective ratings. The analysis of the trust dynamics data revealed three distinct trust dynamics clusters: Bayesian decision maker (BDM), disbeliever, and oscillator. Subsequently, we conducted ANOVA and found seven personal characteristics that were significantly different across the three groups: masculinity from the measures of culture, positive affect from the measures of mood, extraversion, neuroticism and intellect from the personality measures, performance expectancy from the measures of expectancy towards autonomous systems, high expectations from the measures of perfect automation schema. Furthermore, utilizing these significant variables, we developed a decision tree model capable of classifying users into one of the three trust dynamics clusters. Overall, our study contributes to a comprehensive understanding of how diverse users exhibit varying trust dynamics and underscores the role of specific personal characteristics as significant antecedents of trust dynamics.

Despite having a relatively larger sample size (n=130𝑛130n=130italic_n = 130), the numbers of disbelievers and oscillators remain limited. To gain clearer insights into the distinct characteristics of these two groups, a larger sample size would be beneficial. Future investigations could potentially leverage our findings by implementing pre-screening surveys to identify and recruit participants who are more likely to exhibit disbelievers’ or oscillators’ behaviors. Replicating this study with a larger number of disbelievers and oscillators would enable us to develop a more robust and generalizable model to predict a user’s trust dynamics.
