Abstract

Federated Learning (FL) is a collaborative machine learning (ML) approach, where multiple clients participate in training an ML model without exposing the private data. Fair and accurate assessment of client contributions is an important problem in FL to facilitate incentive allocation and encouraging diverse clients to participate in a unified model training. Existing methods for assessing client contribution adopts co-operative game-theoretic concepts, such as Shapley values, but under simplified assumptions. I Introduction

Over the last decade, Federated Learning (FL)Â [1, 2, 3] has emerged as the de facto standard for collaborative Machine Learning (ML) without exposing the private data. FL facilitates the involvement of multiple data owners or local clients in training a global ML model without the need to share any raw dataÂ [4, 5, 6], and thus, aligning with the standard data protection policies like GDPRÂ [7]. As the success of FL depends on the participation of a diverse pool of data contributors (aka clients), a fair and accurate assessment of the client contributions is important to facilitate incentive allocationÂ [8, 9], resolve free rider issuesÂ [10, 11], and encourage diverse clients to participate in an effective model training through FLÂ [12, 13]. Thus, assessing client contributions in FL has become a question of growing interestÂ [14, 15, 16].

Client Contribution in FL. The existing client contribution assessment techniques in FL can be categorized in two ways: individual and coalition assessment. In individual assessment, a local client is evaluated based on its similarity with the global model, the local relative accuracy, or the reputation of the clientÂ [12, 17]. In coalition assessment, the marginal effect of a local client is accounted for when it joins a set of other clients in training processÂ [18, 14, 19]. Coalition assessment is known to be more effective than individual assessment in the heterogeneous data setting, where a client only has a partial coverage of the feature space but the global model needs to cover the whole feature spaceÂ [16]. But a coalition-based assessment can depend on the order in which the clients join in the training and are being evaluated.

Shapley Values as Client Contributions. To address these issues of coalition assessment, tools from cooperative game theory, such as Shapley valuesÂ [20], are proposed for assessing client contributionsÂ [18, 14, 21, 16, 19]. Computing an exact Shapley value has an exponential runtime complexity due to the exhaustive enumeration of all permutations of clients. Several approximations are proposed based on Monte Carlo samplingÂ [22] and its variantsÂ [23, 21, 14], and by evaluating complementary contributionÂ [24]. While applying Shapley value in FL for client contribution, majority of the methods consider simplified assumptions or rely on heuristic definitions of client contribution. For example, [19, 25, 15, 16] considered all clients participate in each epoch. [25] heuristically focused on the Shapley value in recent epochs to quantify the contribution index of clients. [26] computed client contributions of non-participating clients with restricted assumptions, such as Lipschitz continuous model loss and all clients participating in at least one epoch. Finally, [16] heuristically defined client contribution of non-participating clients without providing any theoretical justification. Therefore, we study the following research question:

Can we develop a framework for assessing client contributions in a centralized server federated learning, where a subset of clients participate in each training epoch?

II Related Work

Researchers have proposed several approaches to apply Shapley value to assess client contributions in FL. Notably, [25] utilized the gradients of local clients and proposed an one epoch evaluation (OR) and a multi-epoch evaluation (MR) for client contribution. [15] extended MR and proposed a truncated multi-epoch (TMR) evaluation to eliminate an entire epoch based on a pre-defined threshold. [16] improved Shapley value computation via random permutation sampling and group testing. [19] further improved computation by eliminating unnecessary epochs based on the incremental utility and proposed an improved permutation ordering for an effective client contribution assessment. In all methods, all clients participate in each training epoch â€“ we aim relax the assumption in this paper.

To our best knowledge, two methods considered the non-participating clients setting like ours:Â [16] andÂ [26]. For non-participating clients, Â [16] heuristically defined a value for contribution without providing any theoretical justification â€“ which we address in our work. In contrast, Â [26] aimed to guess missing utility values of client combinations where a subset of clients do not participate in training and proposed a low rank matrix factorization to assess contributions. The said method has several limitations: low rank matrix factorization requires a suitable characteristic of the utility function and a restricted assumption of client participation. Furthermore, matrix factorization is a one-shot approach â€“ assessing contribution in a new epoch requires re-computation of the whole matrix, which is infeasible in practice. As such, Â [26] not only requires high computational time on increasing epochs but also incurs high estimation error. In our study, we aim to design a client contribution assessment framework that achieves not only efficiency in computation but also accuracy in estimation while considering the practical scenario of dynamic participation and non-participation of clients over epochs.

IV-C A Schedule for a Faster Contribution Assessment

We discuss a scheduling procedure to assess client contribution efficiently. The key idea is to consider a computational budget, such as the maximum number of epochs to compute Shapley value, and derive an optimal subset of epochs based on the solution of a constrained optimization problem.

In formalizing the optimization problem, we focus on the following aspects. (i) Server-sided fairness. The server optimizes the utility coverage by computing Shapley value in an optimal subset of epochs where the sum of incremental utilities is higher. In addition, the server prioritizes epochs where a higher number of clients participate in training. (ii) Client-sided fairness. Since a client does not necessarily participate in all epochs, the clients aim to minimize the absolute pair-wise difference of exposure probabilities. A client is said to be exposed in an epoch if it participates in training in that epoch and Shapley value is computed in the same epoch. Now, we discuss two mixed integer linear programs (MILP) to achieve (i) only server-sided fairness and (ii) two-sided fairnessÂ [31], where both server-sided and client-sided fairnesses are considered.
