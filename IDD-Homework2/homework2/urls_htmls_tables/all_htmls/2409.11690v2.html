Abstract.

The ID-free recommendation paradigm has been proposed to address the limitation that traditional recommender systems struggle to model cold-start users or items with new IDs. Despite its effectiveness, this study uncovers that ID-free recommender systems are vulnerable to the proposed Text Simulation attack (TextSimu) which aims to promote specific target items. As a novel type of text poisoning attack, TextSimu exploits large language models (LLM) to alter the textual information of target items by simulating the characteristics of popular items. It operates effectively in both black-box and white-box settings, utilizing two key components: a unified popularity extraction module, which captures the essential characteristics of popular items, and an N-persona consistency simulation strategy, which creates multiple personas to collaboratively synthesize refined promotional textual descriptions for target items by simulating the popular items. To withstand TextSimu-like attacks, we further explore the detection approach for identifying LLM-generated promotional text. Extensive experiments conducted on three datasets demonstrate that TextSimu poses a more significant threat than existing poisoning attacks, while our defense method can detect malicious text of target items generated by TextSimu. By identifying the vulnerability, we aim to advance the development of more robust ID-free recommender systems.

1. Introduction

Traditional recommendation paradigms convert all users/items into ID embeddings and match these embeddings to capture user preferences and recommend suitable items from a vast pool (Guo etÂ al., 2024; Wang etÂ al., 2023a; Yu etÂ al., 2022). Although such ID-based recommendations show promise, they struggle with the unavoidable cold start problem (Wei etÂ al., 2021a). When a new item/user is introduced, the recommender system must assign a unique new ID to this item/user, necessitating continuous retraining of the system to accommodate it. Additionally, the newly trained corresponding ID embeddings, which lack sufficient historical information, cannot precisely encode the users/items, leading to degraded recommendation (Yuan etÂ al., 2023b). Thus, the reliance on IDs significantly hinders the effectiveness of recommendation tasks, particularly when dealing with cold-start items.

To overcome the limitations imposed by IDs in recommender systems, an advanced ID-free recommendation paradigm has been proposed and proven effective in addressing the cold start problem (Hou etÂ al., 2022; Li etÂ al., 2023). Figure 1 illustrates the difference between ID-based and ID-free recommendations. In ID-based recommendations, a unique ID number is assigned to each user or item, and every ID has its corresponding embedding stored in the look-up table. In contrast, ID-free recommender systems leverage the powerful semantic understanding capabilities of language models (LMs) (Zhao etÂ al., 2023) to encode the text information of each user and item into corresponding representations. These text representations can establish semantic relationships between newly added users or items and existing ones, which effectively eliminates the dependence on ID embeddings and mitigates the cold start problem.

While ID-free recommendation offers a new paradigm for modern recommender systems, its robustness against the poisoning attacks (Wang etÂ al., 2024) aimed at promoting individual target items remains underexplored. In ID-based recommendation, attackers can deceive the system into recommending low-quality target items to more users (Chen etÂ al., 2022; Huang and Li, 2023a; Huang etÂ al., 2021; Fang etÂ al., 2020), severely undermining the utility of recommender systems. If such vulnerabilities exist in ID-free recommender systems, the consequences could be serious once it is widely adopted. To address this risk, we initially explored the susceptibility of these systems to two types of attacks: injection poisoning attacks (Wang etÂ al., 2024), which involves injecting forged user profiles with malicious interactions into recommender systems, and text poisoning attacks (Li etÂ al., 2018), typically used in the natural language processing (NLP) domain, which subtly modifies the textual information of target items. Through experiments (refer to Section 2 for details), we found that both attack types have a limited impact on ID-free recommender systems. Injection poisoning attacks, while more effective, require prohibitive costs due to the need to inject a substantial number of malicious profiles (over 0.1% of the total user base). Conversely, traditional text poisoning attacks, despite their low cost, proved ineffective as minor text modifications cannot significantly impact the recommendation modelâ€™s encoding process.

Although ID-free recommender systems appear almost immune to existing attacks (large-scale injections are actually infeasible due to limited attack budgets and the increased difficulty to evade defense systems), in this paper, we reveal a critical vulnerability in ID-free recommender systems by demonstrating how these systems can be disrupted by a novel cost-effective attack. As shown before, traditional text poisoning attacks are often limited by their minor modifications, leading to ineffectiveness. However, in recommender systems, some attackers have the ability to control item text, allowing for more flexible and extensive modifications. Building on this premise, we introduce a special type of text poisoning attack called Text Simulation attack (TextSimu), which utilizes benign-looking simulation prompts to manipulate large language models (LLMs) into rewriting target item texts in a manner that mimics popular items. By aligning with the characteristics of popular items, the generated text can be favored by the recommendation model and appeal to more users. Specifically, TextSimu integrates a unified popularity extraction component to extract keywords from multiple popular items and incorporate them into the prompt instruction, ensuring global popular preferences are reflected rather than preventing reliance on a single popular item. Additionally, given that a single prompt may potentially result in an output with unstable quality, we developed the N-persona consistency simulation strategy, which involves creating multiple personas that each independently crafts malicious texts. These personas then collaborate and refine outputs to ensure consistency. The final output is synthesized based on the most consistent characteristics across the generated texts for the specific target item.

Furthermore, while the proposed attack is designed for black-box settings, to assess the theoretical upper bound of TextSimuâ€™s threat, we extend it to a white-box attack scenario, assuming access to the LLM and the ID-Free recommender system. In this white-box context, we employ an adaptive fine-tuning method to train the victim LLM as a poisoning text generator. This method can be seamlessly combined with the N-persona consistency simulation strategy, enhancing the potency of the text simulation attack.

Lastly, to mitigate the harm caused by TextSimu and similar attacks, we correspondingly propose a defense method named RewriteDetection. The core idea of RewriteDetection is to segment item text into two parts and use LLMs to predict the content of the remaining part. By comparing the differences between the ground-truth content and the predicted content, including semantic and recommendation effects, we can identify whether the text is LLM-generated and possesses malicious promotional capabilities. This approach effectively filters out malicious items, achieving the goal of maintaining the integrity of the recommender system.

2.2. Experiments and Findings

2.2.1. Impact of Injection Poisoning Attacks

We first initiated a series of experiments targeting injection poisoning attacks on ID-free recommender systems, which involves injecting a set of malicious users with well-designed interactions to boost the visibility of target items within the Beauty dataset. Our focus was on two prevalent injection attack methods: Random-Attack (Lam and Riedl, 2004a), which involves malicious users selecting random items and target items to interact with, and Bandwagon-Attack (Turk and Bilge, 2019), which increases the visibility of target items by associating them with popular items instead of randomly selected ones.

Subsequently, we assessed the impact of these poisoning attacks on two widely-used ID-free recommendation algorithms: MoRec (Yuan etÂ al., 2023b) and UnisRec (Hou etÂ al., 2022). The chosen target items is nearly with zero opportunities to be recommended. As depicted in Figure 2, the threat level of injection poisoning attacks, quantified by Hit Ratio@50, escalates with the increase in the number of malicious users. A higher injection ratio of malicious users significantly disrupts the recommendation outcomes. However, it is crucial to highlight that even a 0.1% presence of malicious users indicates an impractically high budget for the attackers (Huang and Li, 2023b). This implies that achieving an effective attack requires substantial resources from the attackers.

2.2.2. Impact of Text Poisoning Attacks

The heavy reliance of ID-free recommender systems on textual information prompted us to investigate text poisoning attacks, focusing on text modifications to influence model recommendations. To evaluate the effectiveness of traditional text poisoning attacks transferred in the recommendation domain, we employed two well-known techniques: TextBugger (Li etÂ al., 2018) and TextFooler (Jin etÂ al., 2020), which were used to alter the textual content of target items to enhance their visibility within ID-free recommender systems.

As shown in Figure 2, text poisoning attacks had minimal impact on recommendation outcomes, with Hit Ratio@50 values not exceeding 0.1%. This suggests that text poisoning methods effective in NLP contexts do not adapt well to the recommendation domain. We attribute this to the fact that traditional text poisoning attacks are primarily designed for traditional NLP tasks such as text classification (Kowsari etÂ al., 2019). These attacks typically involve minor modifications to characters or words, directly converting correct tokens into incorrect or unknown tokens. While such small modifications are highly effective for NLP tasks, the additional recommendation encoding process in ID-free recommender systems tends to overlook these minor textual changes, rendering the modifications ineffective.

Discussion. Injection poisoning attacks can greatly increase the visibility of target items, but they are expensive to execute. Conversely, text poisoning attacks are less costly since they only require text modifications, but are almost ineffective in promotion. However, there is a key difference between traditional text poisoning attacks in NLP and those in the recommendation domain. In NLP tasks, attackers make subtle changes to the text to avoid detection, while in the recommender system domain, the text of an item is often controlled by the merchant (attacker), allowing for more significant modifications. This indicates that in recommender systems, there is greater flexibility in how the text of target items can be manipulated. Following this idea, the next section will explore the possibility of implementing a novel text poisoning attack that is cost-effective while achieving powerful attack effectiveness simultaneously.

3.2. Naive LLM-Powered Poisoning Attack

A straightforward way for attackers to achieve cost-effective and powerful malicious promotion is to rewrite the original textual information of target items in an appealing style using expert domain knowledge. However, hiring experts increases the attack budget, which contradicts our goal of cost-effectiveness. Instead, advanced LLMs, with their extensive open-world knowledge, can serve as a substitute for human experts. This allows us to accomplish the task conveniently without incurring additional costs for industry expertise. To illustrate this approach, we first design a simple example prompt for LLMs as follows:
Rewrite the product content so that it is more likely to be liked by users.

However, this simple prompt has two unavoidable weaknesses: (1) Since different users have diverse preferences, the simply generated textual information cannot appeal to a wide user base. (2) Additionally, to seamlessly integrate the text into systems, the generated textual information should align with the language style of the target system. LLMs, lacking this specific knowledge, cannot accurately tailor content to align with the recommendation tendency and language style of the victim system. Consequently, a simple prompt is inadequate for LLMs to generate precisely suitable textual formats for describing target items.

In the following subsections, we will introduce a novel type of text poisoning attacks, called Text Simulation attack (TextSimu), which leverages simulation thought with LLMs as a rewriting tool to adapt to different user preferences and language styles. Firstly, we will introduce the black-box TextSimu, which does not require prior knowledge of the recommender system and can be more easily applied to real-world scenarios. Subsequently, we delve into the expLoRAtion of the white-box TextSimu, which is aware of the victim LLM and recommendation model used by the system, to examine the theoretical upper limits of this attack.

6. Related Work

6.1. ID-Free Recommendation

Traditional ID-based recommender systems encounter cold-start problems due to the assignment of unique ID representations to new users and items(Huang etÂ al., 2023; Wei etÂ al., 2021b). To address these issues, multi-modal recommender systems have utilized pre-trained language models to encode the textual information of items, transforming them into text representations (Wei etÂ al., 2023). These enriched text representations are then integrated with ID representations for model training.

With the advent of advanced LMs, researchers have introduced ID-free recommendations to address the limitations of ID representations. This approach leverages text representations encoded by LMs, eliminating the need for item ID embeddings (Yuan etÂ al., 2023b). For example, ZESRec (Ding etÂ al., 2021) introduces zero-shot recommenders that utilize natural-language descriptions as continuous indices, eliminating the need for item IDs used in traditional methods. MoRec (Yuan etÂ al., 2023b) utilizes LLM encoders to transform plain text into item representations, demonstrating performance comparable to traditional ID-based methods that need an expensive end-to-end training process. Similarly, UnisRec (Hou etÂ al., 2022) eschews explicit item IDs, employing a lightweight item encoding architecture based on parametric whitening and a mixture-of-experts enhanced adaptor. This system incorporates two contrastive pre-training tasks to encode item description texts into transferable representations across various recommendation scenarios. Furthermore, Recformer (Li etÂ al., 2023) represents items as key-value attribute pairs instead of item IDs, and introduces a novel bi-directional Transformer model for item representation. This model, based on a pre-training and fine-tuning framework, significantly outperforms existing ID-based methods, particularly in zero-shot and cold-start item recommendation scenarios.

6.2. Poisoning Attacks

In the past, poisoning attacks in recommendation primarily depended on heuristic manipulations of user behavior data (Lam and Riedl, 2004b). However, with the increased sophistication of recommender systems, traditional attack methods have become ineffective. Thus, researchers have developed more advanced attacks that are strategically designed to craft malicious interactions (Wang etÂ al., 2022a, 2023b; Guo etÂ al., 2023; Yuan etÂ al., 2023a). For instance, SGLD (Li etÂ al., 2016) generates malicious interactions within matrix factorization-based systems to effectively promote target items. This approach optimizes malicious user-item interactions by exploiting the dynamics of graph propagation.

Vulnerabilities to poisoning attacks become more pronounced in text data-involved scenarios. These attacks demonstrate how textual modifications can significantly increase an itemâ€™s visibility, bypassing direct manipulation of user interactions. Initially, text poisoning attacks proved effective primarily in the NLP domain, leading to the emergence of numerous text poisoning attack methods (Ebrahimi etÂ al., 2017; Wang etÂ al., 2021; Zeng etÂ al., 2020; Xu etÂ al., 2020). In the field of recommender systems, text attacks are also gradually revealing their impact. For example, ARG (Chiang etÂ al., 2023) utilizes a reinforcement learning framework with three auxiliary rewards and aspect generation training to craft malicious reviews that convincingly deceive review-based recommender systems. TextRecAttack (Zhang etÂ al., 2024) underscores the security vulnerabilities of LLM-based recommender systems by targeting the manipulation of textual data associated with items. This approach employs adding, deleting, and replacing word techniques to generate malicious text aimed at inducing shifts in recommendations. ATR-2FT (Oh etÂ al., 2024) employs text rewriting way by fine-tuning an LLM to achieve both greater attack performance and higher text rewriting quality. In this study, we examine the vulnerability of ID-free recommendation methods to text-poisoning attacks aimed at promoting specific items.
