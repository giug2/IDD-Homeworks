Abstract

In this article, we propose an approach for federated domain adaptation, a setting where distributional shift exists among clients and some have unlabeled data. The proposed framework, FedDaDiL, tackles the resulting challenge through dictionary learning of empirical distributions. In our setting, clients’ distributions represent particular domains, and FedDaDiL collectively trains a federated dictionary of empirical distributions. In particular, we build upon the Dataset Dictionary Learning framework by designing collaborative communication protocols and aggregation operations. The chosen protocols keep clients’ data private, thus enhancing overall privacy compared to its centralized counterpart. We empirically demonstrate that our approach successfully generates labeled data on the target domain with extensive experiments on (i) Caltech-Office, (ii) TEP, and (iii) CWRU benchmarks. Furthermore, we compare our method to its centralized counterpart and other benchmarks in federated domain adaptation.

1 Introduction

In Machine Learning (ML), Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a training paradigm for distributing the training of large scale models over a network of devices [1]. Nonetheless, this paradigm is substantially challenged when the clients, i.e., the data holders, follow different probability distributions, a case known as non-i.i.d. FL.

Further, in Domain Adaptation (DA), a sub-problem within transfer learning [2], a labeled source domain adapts towards an unlabeled target domain. These domains are characterized by their different probability distributions. Multi-Source DA (MSDA), a later generalization, considers multiple heterogeneous source distributions [3]. Naturally, there is a parallel between DA and non-i.i.d. FL, as both learning problems deal with distributional shifts. In this paper we propose to view each domain in MSDA as a client in FL.

As a solution to client heterogeneity in MSDA, we propose to adapt the Dataset Dictionary Learning (DaDiL) framework of [4] to a federated setting. Especially, we show that the DaDiL variables, i.e., the atoms and barycentric coordinates, are decoupled, so that training is done without the need for clients to communicate their data between themselves.

Section 2 presents the overall background to our method. Section 3 presents our main contribution, i.e. Federated DaDiL (FedDaDiL). Section 4 presents and discusses our experiments, and finally section 5 concludes this paper.

2.2 Federated Learning

This setting was first formalized by the algorithm FedAVG [1], in which a server averages clients’ models at each round of communication. Yet, in real-world scenarios clients generate opinion-based and personal data from different environments. Hence, the massively distributed data can be non-i.i.d. between different clients participating in the global training. As a result, the global performance of a federated model is degraded [9]. Numerous methods seek to tackle client heterogeneity through the perspective of model parameters or optimization trajectory [10, 11]. However, these methods assume that all clients can access labeled data, which might not hold in practice.

2.3 Domain Adaptation

DA is a sub-problem within transfer learning [2]. In this context, a domain is a pair (𝒳,Q)𝒳𝑄(\mathcal{X},Q) of a feature space and a feature marginal distribution. In DA, one has different but related domains over the same feature space. The goal is to adapt models learned on a labeled source distribution QSsubscript𝑄𝑆Q_{S} to an unlabeled target distribution QTsubscript𝑄𝑇Q_{T}. We focus on MSDA [3], which tackles the learning problem under heterogeneous source distributions. Data comes from multiple distributions 𝒬S={Q^Sℓ}ℓ=1NSsubscript𝒬𝑆superscriptsubscriptsubscript^𝑄subscript𝑆ℓℓ1subscript𝑁𝑆\mathcal{Q}_{S}=\{\hat{Q}_{S_{\ell}}\}_{\ell=1}^{N_{S}}, where Q^Si≠QSjsubscript^𝑄subscript𝑆𝑖subscript𝑄subscript𝑆𝑗\hat{Q}_{S_{i}}\neq Q_{S_{j}} for i≠j𝑖𝑗i\neq j.

OT previously contributed to MSDA in various ways. For instance, [12, 13] use Wasserstein barycenters for aggregating the heterogeneous sources into a common distribution, whereas [4] proposed a novel method unifying MSDA and Dictionary Learning (DiL) through Wasserstein barycenters. However, none of these methods consider a federated adaptation setting. In this sense, other MSDA frameworks were considered for Federated DA (FedDA). For instance, [14] employs feature disentanglement and adversarial training for aligning source clients with target data held by a server. In addition, [15] employs knowledge distillation on models learned privately on source domain data, while reducing the amount of communication rounds needed for convergence.

3 Methodology

Problem Statement. In the following, we assume that each domain in MSDA is a client in FL. This setting puts our algorithm in the non-i.i.d. scenario [9]. Among the existing clients, one client, called target, has unlabeled data. Our goal is to provide labeled data that follows this client’s probability distribution, so that a classifier can reliably predict on such domain. Our proposed FedDaDiL learns a dictionary of public empirical distributions 𝒫𝒫\mathcal{P}, called atoms, and private barycentric coordinates 𝒜𝒜\mathcal{A}. We illustrate this idea in figure 1.

3.2 Federated Dataset Dictionary Learning

FedDaDiL (Algorithm 1). On one hand, FedDaDiL follows [4, Alg. 2] in using SGD. On the other hand, the optimization of (𝒫,𝒜)𝒫𝒜(\mathcal{P},\mathcal{A}) is distributed among the clients. FedDaDiL works as follows. (1) The server initializes a global dictionary 𝒫g(0)superscriptsubscript𝒫𝑔0\mathcal{P}_{g}^{(0)}. (2) At round r𝑟r, the server communicates a mini-batch from atoms to each client. (3) The client clones this mini-batch into a local version, i.e., 𝒫ℓ(0)←𝒫g(r)←superscriptsubscript𝒫ℓ0superscriptsubscript𝒫𝑔𝑟\mathcal{P}_{\ell}^{(0)}\leftarrow\mathcal{P}_{g}^{(r)}, and optimizes 𝒫ℓsubscript𝒫ℓ\mathcal{P}_{\ell}, as well as its own barycentric coordinates αℓsubscript𝛼ℓ\alpha_{\ell} through E𝐸E local epochs (cf. algorithm 2). Due to the decoupling introduced by FedDaDiL, the client’s barycentric coordinates can remain private. Finally, at the end of the communication round, the server aggregates the different dictionary versions generated by the clients.

Updating Client Parameters (Algorithm 2). Analogously to FedAVG [1], each client participating in FedDaDiL needs to locally update parameters w.r.t. its own data. This is described in algorithm 2. In a nutshell the client calculates its own loss fℓsubscript𝑓ℓf_{\ell} with mini-batches, and updates 𝒫ℓsubscript𝒫ℓ\mathcal{P}_{\ell} and αℓsubscript𝛼ℓ\alpha_{\ell} internally via gradient descent. The barycentric coordinates αℓsubscript𝛼ℓ\alpha_{\ell} need to be projected into the simplex, as in [4, Sec. 4.2].

Aggregating Dictionary Versions. The last ingredient in FedDaDiL is aggregating the multiple versions {𝒫ℓ(r)}ℓ∈𝒞subscriptsuperscriptsubscript𝒫ℓ𝑟ℓ𝒞\{\mathcal{P}_{\ell}^{(r)}\}_{\ell\in\mathcal{C}}. Overall, equation 3 includes heterogeneous terms. For instance, the target domain objective does not include labels. For that reason, averaging dictionary versions in analogy with FedAVG is not feasible. Therefore, we seek to mimic the reasoning behind DaDiL, who optimize a different mini-batch of atoms for each domain. As a consequence, we propose to randomly select one version from the pool of client’s local dictionaries. As we show in our experiments, this still yields good solutions for DaDiL.

For comparison, assume a reasonably large batch size of nb=128subscript𝑛𝑏128n_{b}=128, and d=2048𝑑2048d=2048. This implies communicating nb×d=218subscript𝑛𝑏𝑑superscript218n_{b}\times d=2^{18} bytes of information, which is considerably smaller than modern deep neural nets.

4 Experiments and Discussion

We evaluate FedDaDiL on 3 benchmarks: (1) Tennessee Eastmann Process (TEP) [16], (2) Case Western Reserve University (CWRU) and (3) Caltech-Office 10 [17]. While the first two are concerned with fault diagnosis from time series, the third is a standard benchmark in visual DA. TEP consists of signals coming from the simulation of a large-scale complex chemical process. We reproduce the experimental setting of [16], which includes deep DA methods. CWRU consists of vibration signals collected from rotating machinery, for which, we reproduce the experimental setting of [4]. For TEP and CWRU, we use features extracted from a neural network trained with source-domain data. For Caltech-Office, we use convolutional features of a DeCAF netowrk [18].

We compare FedDaDiL with centralized DaDiL [4]. For completeness, we report WJDOT (Weighted Joint Distribution OT) [19] and Wasserstein Barycenter Transport (WBT) [13] on the Caltech-Office 10 benchmark. Our main point of comparison are other federated baselines: (1) FedAVG [1], which is the federated implementation of the Source-Only baseline. On TEP and CWRU, we also compare with (2) FedMMD, (3) FedDANN, (4) FedWDGRL, and (5) KD3A [15]. (2-4) are adaptations of the methods of [20, 21, 22]. We also considered FADA [14], but their neural net architecture could not be directly adapted to time series data.

4.2 Federated Domain Adaptation

The considered benchmarks, CWRU, Caltech-Office and TEP, pose an increasing challenge w.r.t. the federated setting. Indeed, these benchmarks have 3, 4, and 6 clients respectively. We present our results in table 2(c).

First, in FedDA, methods explicitly know from which domain data comes from. This is different from usual evaluation of single-source algorithms in the MSDA setting, where algorithms concatenate source-domain data toghether. As such, some methods improve over their centralized baselines (e.g., DANN vs. FedDANN on CWRU). Nonetheless, for an increasing number of clients, and an increasing degree of heterogeneity, one can expect some performance degradation (e.g. FedMMD on TEP).

Second, FedDaDiL outperforms other FedDA methods. Indeed, a few aspects favor FedDaDiL: (i) our method does not need to update the neural net encoder, which is computationally complex. (ii) As DaDiL, our proposed method interpolates distributional shift. As such, it is able to exploit regularities in the distributional shift between clients’ distributions. Nonetheless, the federated setting is more challenging than centralized DaDiL, as evidenced in Fig. 2(a), and the performance degradation w.r.t. centralized DaDiL.

5 Conclusion

We present an innovative approach to address MSDA within a FL context. We introduce a federate strategy to train DaDiL [4], without clients needing to directly communicate their data. As such, clients collaboratively build the atoms of a shared dictionary 𝒫={𝒫^1,…,𝒫^k}𝒫subscript^𝒫1…subscript^𝒫𝑘\mathcal{P}=\{{\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{1},...,\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{k}}\}, which models the distributional shift occurring between domains. Concurrently, clients also locally optimize weights that enable them to model their data distribution as an interpolation of atoms in the Wasserstein space. Clients’ privacy is thus reinforced, as their weights are not shared.

In the FedDA setting, samples in the target domain are predicted either by using the Wasserstein barycenter of the target domain (FedDaDiL-R) or by using an ensemble of classifiers trained on the atoms (FedDadDiL-E). Our experimental results improve classification performance by 11.60%, 17.98% and 2.27% in signal, and image processing DA tasks. Further works includes analyzing our method through the lens of differential privacy, and extending our setting to the partitioning of domains throughout multiple clients.
