Abstract.

Click-Through Rate (CTR) prediction holds a paramount position in recommender systems. The prevailing ID-based paradigm underperforms in cold-start scenarios due to the skewed distribution of feature frequency. Additionally, the utilization of a single modality fails to exploit the knowledge contained within textual features. Recent efforts have sought to mitigate these challenges by integrating Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs). They design hard prompts to structure raw features into text for each interaction and then apply PLMs for text processing. With external knowledge and reasoning capabilities, PLMs extract valuable information even in cases of sparse interactions. Nevertheless, compared to ID-based models, pure text modeling degrades the efficacy of collaborative filtering, as well as feature scalability and efficiency during both training and inference. To address these issues, we propose Cost-Efficient Language Model Alignment (CELA) for CTR prediction. CELA incorporates textual features and language models while preserving the collaborative filtering capabilities of ID-based models. This model-agnostic framework can be equipped with plug-and-play textual features, with item-level alignment enhancing the utilization of external information while maintaining training and inference efficiency. Through extensive offline experiments, CELA demonstrates superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. Furthermore, an online A/B test conducted on an industrial App recommender system showcases its practical effectiveness, solidifying the potential for real-world applications of CELA.

1. Introduction

Recommender systems deliver personalized items to users by analyzing user browsing history and demographics, presenting users with items tailored to their preferences. Click-through Rate (CTR) prediction plays a crucial role in this process by estimating the likelihood that the user will interact with a specific item. This ensures that the displayed items are those most likely to bolster user engagement and revenue.

The ID-based paradigm has garnered considerable success. Notable examples include DeepFM (Guo et al., 2017), PNN (Qu et al., 2016), and DCN (Wang et al., 2017), which focus on high-order explicit feature crossing, while DIN (Zhou et al., 2018) and DIEN (Zhou et al., 2019) are geared towards mining behavior sequences. This paradigm, illustrated on the left side of Figure 1, entails encoding user and item features into sparse one-hot vectors, subsequently transformed into dense embedding vectors via a lookup process. Latent co-occurrences among features are discerned via a feature interaction layer. Over the past decade, the advancement of these models has centered on developing more sophisticated and scenario-adapted feature interaction layers as a means to enhance the overall performance. Despite their prevalence, ID-based models exhibit certain limitations: 1) Dependence on historical data: This dependence can result in suboptimal modeling in scenarios such as cold-start or long-tail elements, where historical interactions for items or features are limited or absent, leading to inadequate learning of embeddings. 2) Limited feature scope: Primarily focusing on categorical and numerical features, ID-based models overlook other modalities like text, which may harbor untapped knowledge that could potentially enhance the model’s effectiveness.

To alleviate these limitations, works such as PTab (Liu et al., 2022) and CTRL (Li et al., 2023) have integrated Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) into recommender systems. These models employ hard prompts to structure raw features for each interaction, subsequently processed through PLMs for recommendation purposes, as shown on the right side of the Figure 1. By adopting natural language to represent features instead of one-hot vectors, this approach preserves the semantic integrity of features. This enables PLMs to apply their external knowledge and reasoning capabilities, effectively capturing valuable information even in cases of sparse interactions. For example, ID-based models struggle to learn embeddings for infrequently interacted items like bread, whereas PLMs utilize external knowledge to classify bread as a type of food, akin to toast. Concurrently, this natural language representation facilitates the integration of textual features, such as item descriptions, thereby augmenting the recommender system’s effectiveness.

However, these text-enhanced models face inherent limitations that hinder their practical deployment, also shown in Figure 1. First, modeling based exclusively on pure text may not be ideally suited for capturing collaborative signals, which often results in reduced performance (Li et al., 2023). Second, scalability issues emerge as the number of features increases, potentially leading to constructed text exceeding the language model’s maximum token limit and thus losing valuable information, particularly when the sequence of user behaviors is extensively long. Furthermore, it becomes impractical to delineate user behaviors in a detailed manner, including the specification of item features for each behavior. Last but not least, to bridge the gap between PLM outputs and recommendation tasks, PLMs are usually fine-tuned at the interaction-level (Liu et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Muhamed et al., 2021), yet this process is notably time-intensive. Such inefficiency inherent in PLM text processing extends to inference as well (Liu et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2023).

To overcome these challenges, we propose the Cost-Efficient Language Model Alignment (CELA) for CTR prediction. CELA leverages textual features and PLMs to mitigate the cold-start issue and expand the feature scope. It integrates ID-based models to preserve the collaborative filtering effectiveness, and shifts away from the hard prompt mode, thereby addressing scalability concerns. Through item-level alignment, the PLM is fine-tuned effectively and oriented towards recommendation tasks. CELA is structured as a three-phase paradigm: 1) domain-adaptive pre-training stage, the PLM is further trained on a corpus of textual features, tailoring it to the specific dataset, while also training an ID-based model; 2) recommendation-oriented modal alignment stage, contrastive learning is employed to align the item text representations with the item feature embeddings of the ID-based model. This alignment is at the item level, ensuring minimal training overhead. Post-alignment, text representations of each item are cached, facilitating efficient access via item identifiers, thereby reducing inference costs; and 3) multi-modal feature fusion stage, these aligned text representations are integrated into a new ID-based model, along with non-textual features, enabling their interplay to identify potential click patterns. The final two stages are structured for alternate execution, ensuring ongoing alignment of the PLM with the progressively refined embedding table.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

  • •

    We propose a novel paradigm for integrating textual features with ID-based models. This approach is model-agnostic and necessitates minimal modifications to the existing network architecture, rendering it conveniently applicable.

  • •

    We develop an item-level alignment strategy to direct the capabilities of the PLM for recommendation tasks, thereby reducing training time overhead and achieving performance enhancements. Moreover, the cached item text representations are indexed by item identifiers during inference, ensuring low latency.

  • •

    Comprehensive offline experiments on public and industrial datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of CELA. An online A/B test within an App recommender system further validates CELA’s real-world efficacy and feasibility.

2. Related Works

2.1. ID-Based Models for CTR Prediction

ID-based models have been predominant in industry recommendations over the past decade. They capture the characteristics of the items and users by one-hot vectors and learn the collaborative signals to predict the likelihood of a user clicking on an item (Lian et al., 2020). Their architectures are meticulously crafted, evolving into increasingly intricate ones. FM (Rendle, 2010) stands out as one of the earliest machine learning models to incorporate second-order feature interactions. Both WideDeep (Cheng et al., 2016) and DeepFM (Guo et al., 2017) incorporate deep components to learn high-level feature interactions. PNN (Qu et al., 2016) extends the feature interaction function and tightly couples the interaction of first-order and second-order features. AutoInt (Song et al., 2019) and DESTINE (Xu et al., 2021) incorporate self-attention mechanisms to weight different feature interactions. Fi-GNN (Li et al., 2019) leverages graph structures to model feature interactions. DCN (Wang et al., 2017), DCNv2 (Wang et al., 2021), and EDCN (Chen et al., 2021) capture meaningful feature crossings automatically, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering or exhaustive searching. DIN (Zhou et al., 2018) and DIEN (Zhou et al., 2019) model user behavior sequences, aiming to capture the relationships between the target and historical behaviors.

2.2. Text-Enhanced Models for CTR Prediction

Language models have made remarkable strides, achieving unprecedented natural language understanding and generation. These models are pre-trained on massive datasets and adopt advanced architectures like Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), enabling diverse applications such as chatbots (Du et al., 2022), translation (Zhang et al., 2022), and content creation (Chung et al., 2022). Highlighting their potential in semantic comprehension, language models are considered promising for recommendation. Analyzing extra-textual elements such as ad text, search queries, or user reviews, these models may have the capability to discern user intent and subtle nuances between items, thereby enhancing ID-based ones. P5 (Geng et al., 2022) represents a paradigm that redefines various tasks into natural language processing (NLP) tasks. By structuring interactions and metadata in natural language and inputting them into T5 (Raffel et al., 2020), it can generate the target response. CTR-BERT (Muhamed et al., 2021) concatenates all features along with their respective feature names. It undergoes pre-training and distillation with BERT (Devlin et al., 2018). Subsequently, BERT encodes the text of users and items, generating representations that serve as inputs for a late fusion MLP. PTab (Liu et al., 2022) is pre-trained on BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) with a language head using the ”field: value” corpus, which is derived from tabular data. It is then fine-tuned for the CTR task, incorporating a classifier head. UniSRec (Hou et al., 2022) employs a frozen BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) as a text encoder and utilizes a Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture to model user behaviors. CTRL (Li et al., 2023) uses contrastive learning for representation alignment between collaborative and semantic models at the interaction level. The collaborative model, now enriched with semantic information, is fine-tuned and can be deployed without the semantic model.

4. Methodology

The proposed framework is depicted in Figure 2, encompassing three stages:

  • •

    Domain-Adapted Pre-training (DAP). It involves further pre-training of a PLM on a corpus comprising item-side text specific to the recommendation dataset, to better adapt the PLM to the unique characteristics of the dataset. Concurrently, a traditional ID-based model is trained.

  • •

    Recommendation-Oriented Modal Alignment (ROMA). It focuses on aligning the text representations of items from the PLM with the item-side feature embeddings from the ID-Based model in the latent space. Such alignment is essential for tailoring the text representations to recommendation tasks.

  • •

    Multi-Modal Feature Fusion (MF2). The aligned item text representations are treated as a common feature, which is then integrated with non-textual feature embeddings. Subsequently, a new ID-based model is trained to leverage both the aligned text representations and other features, thereby enhancing the overall effectiveness of the recommender system.

Notably, the last two stages are designed to be executed alternatively, where the PLM is continually aligned to the more advanced embedding table.

Despite the profound domain understanding that the PLM achieves through pretraining, a significant gap persists between their output space and that of collaborative filtering. This discrepancy arises because the capabilities of the PLM extend well beyond what recommendation tasks require. Without targeted training and constraints, the outputs of the PLM may not align with the needs of recommender systems. Consequently, it is crucial to refine and orient the PLM’s outputs towards recommendation tasks, a process referred to as alignment. This essential alignment, which aims to provide a representation of the text feature that can be seamlessly integrated with other features for the next stage, is illustrated in the central portion of Figure 2. The substantial number of parameters in the PLM and the extensive volume of interactions in the dataset render interaction-level alignment inefficient (Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2022). 4.4. Alternate Training

The last two stages, as indicated by the green dotted line in Figure 2, are designed to be executed in an alternate fashion. Following the completion of the embedding table training in the MF2 stage, we can revert to the ROMA stage. Here, the item text representation is realigned to the newly trained embedding table. Subsequently, this realigned language model is utilized to generate a new text embedding table. This updated table is then employed in the subsequent iteration of the MF2 stage for further training. This alternate approach allows for iterative refinement and enhancement of both the alignment and fusion processes, thereby progressively improving the overall performance of the model.

5.1. Experimental Settings

5.1.1. Datasets

We conduct offline experiments on three datasets that include textual features. The statistics for these datasets are provided in Table 1.

Amazon Dataset 111https://cseweb.ucsd.edu/∼similar-to\sim∼jmcauley/datasets/amazon_v2/ includes reviews and product metadata from the Sports subset on Amazon, which is widely used as a benchmark dataset (Zhou et al., 2018, 2019; Li et al., 2023). Following (Zhou et al., 2018), negatives are sampled at a 1:1 ratio. The task is to predict the likelihood of a user reviewing the next item, considering user behaviors and the item descriptions as the textual feature, with the behavior sequence up to 20.

MovieLens Dataset 222https://grouplens.org/datasets/movielens/latest/ collectes the rating activity from MovieLens 333http://movielens.org/. Due to the necessity to extract movie summaries as the textual feature from provided hyperlinks, we sample 10 million interactions through the random sampling of users. The task is to predict whether a user will like the next item, with a preference indicated by a rating of 3 or higher, based on a sequence of up to 20 user behaviors.

AppGallery Dataset comprises application exposure and download records sampled from Huawei AppGallery over eight days. Data from the first seven days constituted the training set, while data from the subsequent day served as the test set. The task is to predict whether the user will download the next application, with application description as the textual feature and a sequence of up to 14 user behaviors.

5.1.2. Baselines

Our method is benchmarked against contemporary state-of-the-art models, encompassing both ID-based and text-enhanced models. Classic ID-based models, such as AutoInt (Song et al., 2019), DCN (Wang et al., 2017), DCNv2 (Wang et al., 2021), FiBiNET (Huang et al., 2019), PNN (Qu et al., 2016) , WideDeep (Cheng et al., 2016), DeepFM (Guo et al., 2017), and xDeepFM (Lian et al., 2018), focus on modeling high-order feature interactions. In contrast, models like DIN (Zhou et al., 2018) and DIEN (Zhou et al., 2019) specialize in fine-grained user behavior modeling. Text-enhanced models, including P5 (Geng et al., 2022) and PTab (Liu et al., 2022), aim to describe data in natural language to elicit responses from language models. CTR-BERT (Muhamed et al., 2021) integrates traditional and textual features for fusion in a MLP, UniSRec (Hou et al., 2022) utilizes a frozen BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) as its text encoder, and CTRL (Li et al., 2023) aligns text and tabular data representations to infuse the ID-based model with semantic information.

5.1.3. Evaluation Metrics

In this research, Area Under the Curve (AUC) and logarithmic loss (Logloss) are adopted as evaluation metrics, both widely acknowledged in the assessment of CTR models (Song et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Qu et al., 2016; Guo et al., 2017). AUC evaluates the model’s capacity to accurately rank a positive instance above a negative one, thereby indicating its ranking efficacy. Logloss, on the other hand, quantifies the degree of correspondence between the predicted and actual CTR, offering a metric for the model’s predictive accuracy.

In our offline experiments, models are implemented using RecStudio (Lian et al., 2023) and Transformers (Wolf et al., 2020). We integrate RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) to enhance the optimal ID-based model. AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017) optimizer facilitates all training stages. In the ROMA stage, τ2subscript𝜏2\tau_{2}italic_τ start_POSTSUBSCRIPT 2 end_POSTSUBSCRIPT is 1, batch size is 128, the language model’s learning rate is 5e-5, and projection units are [128, fi⁢dsubscript𝑓𝑖𝑑f_{i}ditalic_f start_POSTSUBSCRIPT italic_i end_POSTSUBSCRIPT italic_d], with fisubscript𝑓𝑖f_{i}italic_f start_POSTSUBSCRIPT italic_i end_POSTSUBSCRIPT as item-side feature count and d𝑑ditalic_d set at 16.   • •

  • •

    DIN + RoBERTa yields fewer performance improvements compared to CELA, suggesting that the mere inclusion of textual feature is insufficient for effective information extraction and utilization in recommendation tasks. The extent of the performance impact varies with the degree of overlap between the PLM’s corpus and the textual features in the dataset.

  • •

    Removing the DAP stage from CELA leads to a reduction in performance, as it deprives the PLM of the opportunity to comprehend domain-specific knowledge, and the anisotropy issue compromises the PLM’s output expressiveness. Nevertheless, the subsequent ROMA stage aligns the PLM output with the recommendation domain, enabling the recommender system to understand PLM outputs. Although the PLM’s knowledge is not perfectly tailored for the domain, it results in performance that still surpasses that of DIN.

  • •

    The removal of the ROMA stage from CELA also leads to diminished performance.   • •

    Removing the SimCSE loss from DAP leads to a performance decline, as it overlooks the anisotropy issue present in PLMs, thereby diminishing expressiveness. Incorporating SimCSE loss is advisable to enhance the quality of text representations.

  • •

    Removing the MLM loss from DAP results in diminished performance. This is because MLM, serving as a widely adopted pretraining loss, facilitates the swift adaptation of language models to specialized domains. DAP w/o MLM performs even worse than CELA w/o DAP on Amazon dataset, suggesting that using SimCSE loss only in pretraining is not ideal due to the inconsistency with the objective of PLMs’ training. Originally, SimCSE loss was proposed as an auxiliary objective in pretraining, aiming to regularize pre-trained embeddings’ anisotropic space to be more uniform.

  • •

    Removing the PLM significantly reduces performance due to less semantic information preservation when using ROMA directly on the text embedding table. Directly mapping the text embedding table to the ID-based model’s output space, with only a basic projection head, leads to substantial semantic information loss.

  • •

    Replacing the dot product with cosine in ROMA results in reduced performance in AppGallery dataset. So we leverage dot product as the similarity function, and it’s widely used in recommendation system, such as SASRec (Kang and McAuley, 2018), UniSRec (Hou et al., 2022).

  • •

    The performance of MF2 w/o text is comparable to that of DIN, but significantly lags behind CELA. This indicates that CELA’s performance improvement benefits from semantic information.

These observations underscore the importance of both the DAP and ROMA stages in CELA for maximizing the PLM’s potential in recommendation systems, highlighting the critical role of adapting the PLM to domain-specific knowledge and tailoring it for task-specific objectives.

5.6. Further Analysis

5.6.1. Compatibility for language models.

We compare the performance of CELA equipped with PLMs of various sizes: BERTTiny (14.5M) (Jiao et al., 2019), BERTSmall (29.1M) (Bhargava et al., 2021), BERTMedium (41.7M) (Turc et al., 2019), RoBERTaBase (110M) (Liu et al., 2019), BERTLarge (336M) (Devlin et al., 2018), and OPT (1.3B) (Zhang et al., 2022). For the OPT model, Causal Language Modeling (CLM) pre-training loss and LoRA fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2022) are implemented. Experimental results are illustrated in Figure 4, leading to the following insights:

  • •

    CELA, when enhanced with a PLM, no matter its size, exhibits significant performance gains over DIN, validating that the use of PLMs can extract knowledge from textual features and benefit CTR prediction.

  • •

    As the complexity of the comprehensive PLM escalates, the marginal gains in CELA’s performance exhibit a plateauing trend. Notably, BERTLarge underperforms relative to RoBERTaBase, attributable to the former’s significantly larger parameter set, which does not fine-tune as effectively with limited items. This observation implies that beyond a specific threshold, the addition of parameters ceases to yield proportional improvements, suggesting that a medium-sized PLM is sufficient to attain optimal performance.

5.6.3. Alignment of Popular Items Only

ID-based models exhibit poor performance in cold-start scenarios, suggesting inadequacies in learning item embeddings for cold-start items. We aim to ascertain whether aligning poorly learned item embeddings during the ROMA stage might lead to a decline in performance. To this end, we design an experiment in which CELA’s ROMA stage aligns only the top 20%, 40%, 60%, 80%, and 100% of items by popularity. Figure 5 indicate that CELA’s performance reaches its apex when aligning 80% of the most popular items. This suggests that limiting the alignment to a subset of popular items facilitates a more accurate mapping of item text representations to the collaborative filtering space, while aligning poorly learned embeddings will lead to performance degradation.

5.7. Online A/B Testing

To evaluate the effect of CELA on real-world scenarios, we deploy CELA and conduct an online A/B test in an industrial Android AppGallery, which is one of most popular App stores of China.

5.7.1. Deployment

The online deployment process is outlined as:

  • •

    Data Preprocessing. Utilizing a LLM, we refine the collected app descriptions to eliminate extraneous details, such as contact information, thereby enhancing conciseness. The refined text is indexed by the item identifier for low-cost storage.

  • •

    Update Process. Given the infrequent changes in app descriptions, weekly training is conducted during the DAP stage. Thanks to the efficiency of the ROMA stage, it is feasible to align the PLM daily with the item embeddings from the previous day’s recommendation model. This alignment supplies the MF2 stage with the text embedding table, minimizing additional training overhead. Accordingly, updates to the MF2 stage also occur daily.

  • •

    Online Services. The online recommendation model retrieves text representations directly from the pre-computed text embedding table via item identifiers, circumventing the need for real-time PLM text processing.

This deployment facilitates iterative and daily enhancements of both the ROMA and MF2 stages, while the efficient and effective online serving ensures optimal service delivery.

6. Conclusion

In this study, we introduced a novel cost-efficient framework for CTR prediction that leverages the strengths of PLMs and textual features alongside ID-based models. To tailor the capabilities of PLMs for recommendation, we implemented domain-adaptive pre-training and item-level alignment, concurrently addressing the inefficiencies associated with PLM training at the interaction level. By abolishing hard prompts and employing PLMs solely as text encoders, we intricately describe user behaviors while preventing token overflow due to the increase in features. Our framework is model-agnostic and industry-friendly, with low training overhead and serving latency. Comprehensive experiments conducted on both public and industrial datasets validate CELA’s notable enhancements in performance.
