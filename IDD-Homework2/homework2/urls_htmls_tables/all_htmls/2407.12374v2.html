Abstract.

Cross-domain recommendation (CDR) extends conventional recommender systems by leveraging user-item interactions from dense domains to mitigate data sparsity and the cold start problem. While CDR offers substantial potential for enhancing recommendation performance, most existing CDR methods suffer from sensitivity to the ratio of overlapping users and intrinsic discrepancy between source and target domains. To overcome these limitations, in this work, we explore the application of graph signal processing (GSP) in CDR scenarios. We propose \proposed, a unified CDR framework based on GSP, which employs a cross-domain similarity graph constructed by flexibly combining target-only similarity and source-bridged similarity. By processing personalized graph signals computed for users from either the source or target domain, our framework effectively supports both inter-domain and intra-domain recommendations. Our empirical evaluation demonstrates that \proposedconsistently outperforms various encoder-based CDR approaches in both inter-domain and intra-domain recommendation scenarios, especially when the ratio of overlapping users is low, highlighting its significant practical implication in real-world applications.

1. Introduction

Cross-domain recommendation (CDR) has emerged as a promising approach in recommender systems (Zang et al., 2022). By leveraging additional information sources, it has addressed the long-standing challenges of data sparsity and the cold-start problem. Data sparsity is a prevalent problem in recommender systems that arises because users interact with only a limited subset of the total available items, making it challenging to accurately learn user preferences. Furthermore, recommender systems have difficulty in providing reliable recommendations to newly joined users, so-called cold-start users, due to the absence of historical interaction data (Man et al., 2017). The core idea behind CDR is to leverage user data from a dense source domain to enhance recommendation performance in a sparse target domain.

There are two main scenarios in CDR, which are intra-domain and inter-domain recommendation (Figure 1). Intra-domain recommendation focuses on recommending items to users within the same domain, while leveraging shared user preferences or interaction patterns to mitigate the data sparsity problem of the target domain. There have been various attempts to tailor domain adaptation methods for facilitating this scenario (Hu et al., 2018; Li and Tuzhilin, 2020; Cao et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2023). On the other hand, inter-domain recommendation aims to recommend items to users in a different domain, where there exist discrepancies between the domains. In this sense, transfer learning methods have been developed to map and align features between the source and target domains (Zhu et al., 2021; Man et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2022; Kang et al., 2019). Note that most previous studies on CDR methods have only focused on either an intra-domain or inter-domain recommendation scenario.

Despite their own advantages, existing CDR approaches face several critical challenges. First, the final performance of existing methods largely depends on the ratio of overlapping users, which indicates how many users are shared between the source and target domains, as they train a model by utilizing overlapping users as a bridge to link the two domains. In general, a low overlap ratio impedes the effective transfer of source domain knowledge to the target domain (Chen et al., 2023). Second, most existing methods overlook that two different domains may have fundamentally distinct characteristics and user behaviors, referred to as domain sensitivity, leading to limited performance improvement for CDR scenarios. To maximize the performance gain, the potential helpfulness of source domain knowledge to the target domain should be further investigated.

To tackle these challenges, in this work, we focus on graph signal processing (GSP) as an innovative approach to CDR. Several recent studies (Shen et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023a; Xia et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2023) have demonstrated the effectiveness of GSP for collaborative filtering (CF), by seamlessly aligning the concept of GSP with that of CF. Particularly, Graph Fourier Transform (GFT) enables the comprehensive representation of users/items in the spectral domain, offering a powerful method for uncovering underlying patterns in complex graph structures. The existing GSP-based CF methods, such as GF-CF (Shen et al., 2021) and PGSP (Liu et al., 2023a), build a similarity graph for users and items, and then make it smooth with various graph filtering strategies, including linear filter and ideal low-pass filter. By utilizing users-items and users-users similarity information as personalized graph signals, GSP can provide item recommendations to users based on the predicted (i.e., smoothed) signals. Built upon the unique benefits of GSP-based CF, our proposed approach to CDR does not rely on encoder training, making its performance much robust to the ratio of overlapping users between source and target domains.

This paper proposes \proposed, a novel and unified CDR framework based on Graph Signal Processing. The key difference of \proposedover previous GSP-based CF methods is to fuse preference knowledge of both domains into a cross-domain similarity graph, by flexibly combining a target-only similarity graph and a source-bridged similarity graph. To be precise, target-only similarities refer to information inherent to the target domain, while source-bridged similarities enhance the target domain similarities by using the source domain information through inter-domain similarities between the two domains. In particular, for further personalized signal processing, \proposedadopts various strategies to augment the cross-domain similarity graph and produces personalized input signals according to the augmentation strategy. In the end, filtering users’ signals on the similarity graph, where the signals are separately defined for source domain users and target domain users, \proposedcan serve as a unified recommendation framework for both CDR scenarios.

Our extensive experiments demonstrate that \proposedmostly shows the best accuracy among a range of baseline methods, including encoder-based, GSP-based CF methods, and the state-of-the-art CDR methods, in both scenarios. Specifically, our \proposedframework working on the cross-domain similarity graph outperforms other GSP-based CF methods working on a single domain or unified domain similarity graph; this indicates that \proposedmore effectively utilizes source domain knowledge to enhance target domain recommendation. Notably, for the Amazon dataset known for its high sparsity and low overlap ratio, \proposedachieves a remarkable performance gain of over 20% compared to the best baseline results. Furthermore, \proposedis validated to be less sensitive to the ratio of overlapping users, resulting in enhanced robustness for CDR. For reproducibility, our codes are publicly available at https://github.com/jeongeun/CGSP.

Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

  • •

2. Related Work

2.1. Cross domain recommendation

The existing CDR studies can be categorized according to the target CDR scenarios: intra-domain and inter-domain recommendation.

For intra-domain recommendation (Li and Tuzhilin, 2020; Cao et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018), many studies have mitigated the data sparsity problem in the target domain by leveraging information from the source domain. DDSAN (Chen et al., 2018) proposes a new domain-similarity regularization to promote domain invariance by constraining the model parameters from different domains to be close to each other. DCDCSR (Zhu et al., 2018) leverages the source domain information along with the sparsity degrees of individual users and items. CoNet (Hu et al., 2018) enables feature learning across domains by incorporating cross-connections in its neural network architecture, which improves the transfer of information between domains. DARec (Yuan et al., 2019) emphasizes the inherent structure of the rating matrix to transfer rating patterns without requiring auxiliary information. CDAML (Xu et al., 2023) utilizes clustering alongside meta-learning for domain adaptation, aiming to improve recommendations in sparse target domains. CAT-ART (Li et al., 2023) proposes a contrastive autoencoder to generate a global user embedding based on information from all domains. UniCDR (Cao et al., 2023) employs domain-specific and domain-shared embeddings along with aggregation schemes, which also allows it to handle both two CDR scenarios.

The inter-domain recommendation (Liu et al., 2023b, 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2019, 2020; Sahu and Dwivedi, 2020; Man et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2022b; Zhu et al., 2022) aims to provide recommendations to cold-start users who have not interacted with the target domain, by leveraging their information in the source domain. Many studies have focused on transfer learning based on shared user features or mapping functions. EMCDR (Man et al., 2017) uses latent representations and mapping functions for knowledge transfer, and SSCDR (Kang et al., 2019) further proposes a semi-supervised learning method to effectively train the mapping function. CDRIB (Cao et al., 2022b) introduces new regularizers based on the information bottleneck principle to build user-item correlations across domains. PTUPCDR (Zhu et al., 2022) proposes a meta-network to generate personalized mapping functions. UniCDR (Cao et al., 2023) can also be applied to recommend inter-domain recommendations using domain-shared embeddings.

Though effective, the existing CDR methods are often largely dependent on the ratio of overlapping users, as they train a model by utilizing overlapping users as a bridge to link the two domains. Also, most CDR methods lack the capability of explicitly adjusting the impacts of the source domain, which makes them ineffective when two different domains have large intrinsic discrepancies in terms of user behavior.

2.2. GSP-based Collaborative Filtering

Recently, Graph Signal Processing (GSP) has gained significant attention as a promising direction for recommender systems (Shen et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022a, 2023a; Xia et al., 2024, 2022; Liu et al., 2022b; Choi et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024), as its primary concept aligns with the goal of collaborative filtering, which ensures that similar users share similar embeddings; this would be further explained in Section 3.2.

Shen et al. (2021) demonstrates that graph filtering methods are closely connected to existing CF methods. Motivated by the role of linear filter in LGCN (He et al., 2020), they suggested LGCN-IDE, which stands for LightGCN with Infinitely Dimensional Embedding. They also proposed GF-CF, a unified graph convolution framework that employs a mixed-frequency graph filter combining linear and ideal low-pass filter. Building on this mixed filter approach, FIRE (Xia et al., 2022) improves real-time recommendation efficiency and model updating speeds, thereby addressing dynamic interactions and the cold-start problem. PGSP (Liu et al., 2023a) further develops this concept concept by incorporating an augmented similarity graph with personalized graph signals. HiGSP (Xia et al., 2024) advanced the framework with a module consisting of cluster-wise and globally-aware filters.

While GSP has proven effective in single-domain recommendations, its effectiveness in CDR remains unexplored in the literature. We believe this presents a promising research opportunity, as applying GSP to CDR could provide novel solutions to inherent challenges of previous CDR methods.

3.2. GSP-based Collaborative Filtering

Recently, there have been several attempts to employ GSP for collaborative filtering (CF) (Shen et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023a). The main concept of GSP is smoothness, which refers to the property of a signal on a graph where connected nodes (representing users or items in CF) have similar signal values. A smooth signal on the graph suggests that users and items connected by a short path within the graph are likely to interact with each other, indicating shared preferences. For instance, the linear filter leverages the spectral properties of the graph, particularly focusing on the lower spectrum of the Laplacian matrix, which corresponds to the more smoothly varying components across the graph (Yu and Qin, 2020). This concept aligns with the goal of CF, which aims to exploit similarities between users and items for recommendation.

Existing GSP-based CF methods involve three steps:

  1. i)

    Constructing a similarity graph of users and items

  2. ii)

    Defining a graph filter for the similarity graph

  3. iii)

    Generating and filtering personalized graph signals

The first step is to build a similarity graph for users and items based on their interactions. The similarity graph encodes the underlying relationships among users, among items, and between users and items. Next, the second step is to apply a graph filter. Existing methods (Shen et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023a; Xia et al., 2022, 2024) apply a mixed frequency filter that combines a linear filter and an ideal low-pass filter to capture both locally smooth and globally rough signals, thereby offering a comprehensive understanding of user interactions and preferences.

The last step generates an input signal representing each user and then processes it through the filtered similarity graph, in order to capture meaningful patterns across items; this results in predicted (i.e., smoothed) scores for all items, which are used for recommendation in the end.

4.1.2. GSP-based CDR framework

The most straightforward solution for utilizing user-item interactions in both domains for GSP is to employ a unified graph that merges two graphs with overlapping users. This solution can be effective when the source and target domains share a sufficient number of users. However, when there is minimal user overlap, the limited anchor information severely restricts effective knowledge transfer across domains; this diminishes the benefits of utilizing the source domain. Therefore, we concentrate on developing a GSP-based CDR framework that can adeptly incorporate cross-domain information, enhancing the effectiveness of GSP even when direct user overlap is low.

Inspired by the concept of random walks (Perozzi et al., 2014), where nodes that are closely connected by a few steps are considered similar, we suggest that items from source and target domains could be seen as similar if there’s a path connecting them through users common to both domains. This connection implies that these users have similar preferences or interests in both domains, which could signify similarity between the items they interact with. Building on this, our approach starts from capturing similarity between source domain items and target domain items and then explores the deeper connections between items (as well as between users) across different domains.

The first step of our \proposedframework is to construct a cross-domain similarity graph, denoted by 𝐆𝐆\mathbf{G}bold_G; this distills the compressed relationships between users and items across the two domains. It effectively addresses the data sparsity problem by constructing an interconnected graph that integrates direct similarities within the target domain with supportive similarities derived from the source domain. This cross-domain approach enables a richer representation of user preferences, which eventually enhances personalized recommendations. 5.1. Experimental Settings

5.1.1. Datasets and domain setup

We employ two widely-used multi-domain datasets: Douban and Amazon (Zhu et al., 2020, 2019; Kang et al., 2019). Douban is a popular service in China that features user ratings across various content categories. Following (Zhu et al., 2018), we use Movie as the source domain, and Music and Book as the target domains. Amazon dataset also encompasses a wide range of products and user ratings. Following recent work (Zhu et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023), we select Movie as the source domain and Music as the target domain. Additionally, Sports and Clothes are chosen as the source and target domains. The statistics and domain setup are summarized in Table 1  and Table 2.

It is worth noting that the two datasets have distinct characteristics. Amazon dataset is highly sparse, which makes recommendation tasks challenging. On the other hand, Douban dataset has a very high ratio of overlapping users 222This ratio is calculated as the proportion of target-domain users who are also present in the source domain., almost reaching 1; this indicates that most of the users in the Music or Book domains are active in the Movie domain as well.

5.1.2. Evaluation setup

Our experiments include both intra-domain and inter-domain recommendation scenarios. For the intra-domain scenario, from the target domain, 80% of each user’s historical interactions are used for training and the remaining 20% for testing (Shen et al., 2021). Additionally, 20% of the training interactions are randomly selected to form a validation set for optimizing the hyperparameters. For the inter-domain scenario, we designate 50% of overlapping users as test users. Their interactions in the source domain are used for training, and those in the target domain for testing. Similar to the intra-domain recommendation scenario, 20% of the training interactions are used as a validation set.

We focus on the top-N𝑁Nitalic_N recommendation task for implicit feedback, adopting two commonly used ranking metrics: Recall@N𝑁Nitalic_N and NDCG@N𝑁Nitalic_N (Kang et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2023). It is noted that we opt for a full-rank evaluation instead of a sampling-based evaluation, such as the leave-one-out protocol, for a more thorough assessment.

5.1.3. Baselines

We consider different sets of baseline methods for intra-domain and inter-domain CDR scenarios, respectively.

For evaluation in intra-domain CDR scenarios, we execute basic CF methods using user-item interactions only in a target domain (single domain), and those in both domains (unified domain):

  • •

    GF-CF (Shen et al., 2021), LGCN-IDE (Shen et al., 2021), PGSP (Zhu et al., 2022): These are GSP-based methods specifically for CF. In terms of GF-CF and PGSP, they initially utilize a mixed-frequency filter combining a linear filter and an ideal low-pass filter. To compare the results with ours, we conduct experiments using both the linear filter and the mixed-frequency filter, thus reporting the superior performance.

  • •

    BPR (Rendle et al., 2012), LGCN (He et al., 2020): These are encoder-based methods that respectively train user/item embeddings and graph encoder through optimization of pairwise ranking loss.

We also compare CDR methods for the intra-domain scenario:

  • •

    DCDCSR (Zhu et al., 2018) , CoNet (Hu et al., 2018): These are encoder-based methods utilizing the dual-network architecture to transfer information through cross-connections by leveraging shared features between the source and target domains.
