Abstract

Semi-supervised learning (SSL) can utilize unlabeled data to enhance model performance. In recent years, with increasingly powerful generative models becoming available, a large number of synthetic images have been uploaded to public image sets. Therefore, when collecting unlabeled data from these sources, the inclusion of synthetic images is inevitable. This prompts us to consider the impact of unlabeled data mixed with real and synthetic images on SSL. In this paper, we set up a new task, Real and Synthetic hybrid SSL (RS-SSL), to investigate this problem. We discover that current SSL methods are unable to fully utilize synthetic data and are sometimes negatively affected. Then, by analyzing the issues caused by synthetic images, we propose a new SSL method, RSMatch, to tackle the RS-SSL problem. Extensive experimental results show that RSMatch can better utilize the synthetic data in unlabeled images to improve the SSL performance. The effectiveness is further verified through ablation studies and visualization.

1 Introduction

Semi-supervised learning (SSL) methodsÂ [31; 40; 43] can leverage unlabeled data to enhance model performance, thereby reducing the need for extensive data labeling. For example, standard SSL for image classification tasks requires a small number of labeled images and an extensive number of unlabeled images. The unlabeled images are typically assumed to be collected from publicly available sources such as the Internet. SSL methods employ various strategies to enable the model to learn from both labeled and unlabeled data, achieving significantly better performance than supervised learning using only the labeled portion.

However, along with these advances, a new problem has arisen in recent years. A large number of powerful generative modelsÂ [12; 19] is emerging rapidly, allowing more users to enjoy the convenience of synthesizing and sharing AI-generated images with just a click on their devices. As a consequence, an increasing amount of synthetic data is being uploaded to public image sets, e.g. the Internet. This results in the unlabeled data we collect from public image sets will contain increasingly synthetic data. Although these synthetic images look remarkably similar to real images to the human eye, they may pose a potential threat to model learning because the feature distributions of these generated images deviate from those of natural images.

This trend brings a new challenge to SSL tasks, as such a practical scenario will become increasingly common: the unlabeled data we collect from public image sets will contain more and more synthetic data. While some worksÂ [9; 10] have been done on investigating the impact of labeled synthetic images for supervised learning, in SSL tasks, the model dynamically selects unlabeled data for learning based on model knowledge during training. The impact that unlabeled synthetic data may have on SSL during this process has not been studied. Therefore, we propose and attempt to address the following questions:

  • â€¢

    Are existing SSL methods ready for synthetic unlabeled data?

  • â€¢

    How should we handle the synthetic data for SSL?

To answer these questions, in this paper, we set up a new task named Real and Synthetic hybrid SSL (RS-SSL), which means that a portion of synthetic images is mixed into the unlabeled data for SSL, as illustrated in FigureÂ 1. We established a new RS-SSL benchmark with commonly used SSL datasets and mainstream generative models. Then, we evaluated the performance of recent representative SSL methods on our benchmark. Unfortunately, our finding is that current SSL methods are not able to utilize synthetic data efficiently: the introduction of synthetic images into unlabeled data usually provides no performance improvement and sometimes has a significant negative impact.

This result prompts us to propose RSMatch to address the RS-SSL task. We follow an intuitive process to design RSMatch. Firstly, we try to distinguish the synthetic images in the unlabeled data. This is challenging since we have no labeled synthetic images and only limited real images, which makes it impossible to train a synthetic image identification model by standard supervised learning. Our RSMatch achieves this by the proposed class-wise synthetic data queue along with corresponding updating and training strategy. Secondly, we attempt to explore the potential training value of the detected synthetic images. And we managed to further improve SSL performance by properly utilizing the synthetic images. This is achieved by our proposed synthetic proxy head structure for training.

With RSMatch, synthetic data not only avoids becoming a burden but can actually be used to improve the SSL performance. We show the effectiveness of our RSMatch on various benchmarks. Overall, our contributions can be summarized as follows:

  • â€¢

    We set up a new task, RS-SSL, which is a practical and challenging task with the emergence of synthetic images.

  • â€¢

    We evaluated recent SSL methods on our established benchmarks for RS-SSL and found they are not ready for dealing with synthetic images in unlabeled data.

  • â€¢

    We propose the class-wise synthetic data queue with corresponding updating and training strategy to distinguish the synthetic images under the condition that no labeled synthetic image and only limited labeled real images are given.

  • â€¢

    We propose the real and fake head structure for further utilizing the identified synthetic data to improve SSL performance. These proposed components are combined into RSMatch, a new SSL method to tackle the RS-SSL problem.

Our source code and the synthesized data will be made publicly available upon acceptance.

2 Related work

2.1 Semi-supervised learning

Semi-supervised learning (SSL) aims to utilize unlabeled data to improve the performance of the model supervised by limited labeled data, thus reducing the cost of labeling additional data. Typically, SSL methods apply consistency regularizationÂ [1] for self-training to utilize unlabeled data. FixMatchÂ [31] is one of the most influential SSL methods recently, as it effectively integrates some of the techniques from earlier worksÂ [3; 36; 25; 2], becoming a foundational framework for subsequent works. The following works improved the effectiveness of SSL in various aspects, such as using class-wise dynamical thresholdsÂ [40; 35], applying contrastive learning strategiesÂ [15; 43]. These methods have also propelled the development of SSL for downstream tasksÂ [32; 20]. Meanwhile, there are also SSL methods considering the potential issues in unlabeled data, such as class imbalanceÂ [38; 8], domain differenceÂ [4; 7], and open-set problemsÂ [16; 28]. As far as we know, we are the first to tackle the problem of SSL with generative unlabeled data. While MP-SSLÂ [37] considered a problem named generative SSL, their setting is to guide the generative model to generate specific data for training. Thus, it is entirely different from ours, as we aim to mitigate the impact caused by already collected generated data mixed into the unlabeled data.

2.2 Training with synthetic images

Utilizing synthetic data from training has never ceased, as this kind of data is easier to obtain. With the rapid emergence of powerful generative models, some generated images are almost indistinguishable from real ones. Meanwhile, more and more synthetic data are being uploaded to the public image sources. Thus, how these generated images impact training has become an unavoidable problem. Early worksÂ [5; 42; 13] explored training with images generated by GAN-based models. Recent works focus on generated data from more powerful Diffusion ModelsÂ [30; 12]. Hataya et al.Â [9] evaluated the effect of generated data from the stable diffusion modelÂ [26] on several supervised vision tasks and found that the generated data can have negative effects. He et al.Â [10] found the generated data from the GLIDE modelÂ [19] is helpful for zero-shot and few-shot classification on the pre-trained CLIP modelÂ [22]. These works, however, only focus on investigating the impact of labeled synthetic data on supervision tasks, where the prompt is available and serves as the classification label. In contrast, we consider another practical scenario for SSL, in which the mixture of collected real and synthetic images are all unlabeled.

2.3 Text-to-image diffusion model

Diffusion modelsÂ [30; 12] learn to reverse the forward diffusion process to recover the original data from the added noise. Due to the high generation quality, diffusion models have become the mainstream generative models in recent years. Meanwhile, diffusion models can be guided by multimodal conditions to synthesize imagesÂ [18; 41]. Text-to-image generation takes the natural language description as the condition and is supported by many diffusion model-based works like GLIDEÂ [19], stable diffusionÂ [26] with its variantsÂ [21; 17], ImagenÂ [27], DALL-EÂ [24], etc. These works made high-quality generative models accessible to ordinary users on common devices, leading to a large number of images generated by these models being uploaded to public image sources. In this work, we use a combination of these models to construct benchmarks that are closer to real-world scenarios.

3 RS-SSL benchmark

We base on the characteristics of synthetic images uploaded to public image sets to construct our benchmark. The synthetic images are usually generated by 1) diverse prompts and 2) different generative models. Thus, we follow the following process to generate images for our task:

Prompts. While some workÂ [9] uses only one type of description, like â€™A photo of â€¦â€™, as the prompt to simulate the public synthetic images, we consider a more practical scenario in which users tend to use more diverse descriptions to generate and upload images. Therefore, we follow He et al.Â [10] to leverage an off-the-shelf word-to-sentence model T5Â [23] to generate description texts. Specifically, we feed the model with class names from the real datasets and generate various descriptions. For example, the generated description for the class name â€™catâ€™ could be â€™A beautiful white cat laying on a rockâ€™.

Generative models. Some workÂ [11; 9] uses one specific generative model for synthesizing. However, users will be more likely to use different generative models. Thus, we apply three different generative models, GLIDEÂ [19], Stable Diffusion v1.4 (SD14)Â [26], and SDXL-LighteningÂ [17], to represent the commonly used generative models published at different times. We input the prompts for each class above to these models to get synthetic images.

Dataset organization. We generate images for the classes of commonly used SSL datasets. To align with the original data scale of existing SSL methods, we randomly replace some of the unlabeled real images with our synthetic images. This is achieved by introducing a replace ratio to control the proportion of unlabeled data replaced with synthetic data. For example, a synthetic ratio of 0.5 means half of the real images in unlabeled data are replaced by synthetic data. We maintain class balance based on the ground truth during replacement to focus on the issues introduced by synthetic data.

4 Semi-supervised learning on RS-SSL

4.1 Are SSL methods ready for RS-SSL?

Evaluation on existing SSL methods. We use our benchmarks for evaluating the existing SSL methodsÂ [31; 40; 35; 43]. To explore the impact of synthetic data, we mainly compare the SSL results of each SSL method on 1) the entire RS-SSL benchmarks and 2) RS-SSL benchmarks removing the synthetic part (i.e. only the real unlabeled data). The results in TableÂ 1, Â 2 show that the inclusion of synthetic images did not provide improvement and even had negative effects at times. Such results confirmed the challenging nature of RS-SSL and prompted us to further analyze and address the issues brought by synthetic data for SSL.

Analysis of synthetic data. Due to the massive emergence of generative models, the issues with synthetic data are being summarized. ResearchersÂ [9; 29] have primarily observed these issues: 1) unnatural details, e.g. animals with an abnormal number of limbs; 2) semantic errors, which means the ambiguous prompts or multiple objects in the prompt result in synthetic images that do not primarily represent the target class. 3) lack of diversity: the images of the same class from a specific generative model tend to have the same shapes, backgrounds, etc. The cause can be attributed to the distribution mismatch of synthetic and real data. The T-SNEÂ [33] visualization result in FigureÂ 2 also demonstrates the distribution mismatch of the SSL method, FixMatch, training on CIFAR-10 with a replace ratio of 0.5. Due to the inclusion of synthetic data, the learned real data distribution for the target task will drift during SSL and lead to a negative effect.

Despite these issues, we experimentally confirm that synthetic data does not always degrade accuracy in our setting. Furthermore, several studies have shown that carefully selected synthetic images can improve the performance of models in supervised learning. Therefore, we believe that synthetic images have a potentially positive effect and try to find a way to properly utilize these synthetic images.

4.4 SSL with both real and synthetic data

The success of identifying synthetic images in unlabeled data enables us to further consider how to utilize these data. With our analysis above, we believe that 1) the synthetic images, which have a distribution mismatch with real images, should not hurt the classification head of the target task since the parameters in the classification head are related to the learned class-wise prototype of the target task; 2) the synthetic images also introduces high-quality content details, some of which are even not present in the real data, thereby enabling the encoder to extract more comprehensive features.

Following this solution, we propose to use synthetic images to train the encoder of the classifier but avoid affecting the target classification head. To achieve this, we introduce an additional head, i.e. the fake head, to the classifier for tasking synthetic data classification. Thus, the impact of synthetic data on the original classification head (i.e. the real head) can be diverted to the fake head. Meanwhile, the gradients from synthetic data can also be backpropagated to the feature encoder. The real images and identified synthetic images are used to train with real and fake heads separately.

Comparison on RS-SSL benchmarks. We evaluate the selected SSL methods and RSMatch on our RS-SSL benchmark. We use various replaced ratios and the number of labeled images for extensive evaluation. Here, FixMatch (real only) denotes that we manually remove the synthetic images based on the ground-truth. Thus, it can be referenced as an ideal scenario in which we can perfectly identify and remove all the synthetic data.

The results in TableÂ 1 show the results on different replaced ratios, which means how many real images are replaced by synthetic images in the unlabeled data, as mentioned before. Here, we use 25 labeled images per class. We can conclude that 1) current SSL methods still face the risk of including synthetic data in the unlabeled dataset. For example, FixMatch dropped around 1.42% accuracy by introducing 50% of synthetic images on CIFAR-10. 2) When the replace ratio is relatively small, synthetic images are more likely to have a detrimental effect on SSL performance. This emphasizes the necessity of the RS-SSL task, as in the real world, the ratio of synthetic data tends to be small. Meanwhile, the negative effects of introducing synthetic data are less pronounced when the replace ratio is relatively high, as the lack of data caused by removing synthetic images becomes a more serious issue. 3) With RSMatch, the synthetic images can be utilized to further improve the SSL performance. For example, RSMatch made an improvement of 4.27% accuracy on CIFAR-100 with a replace ratio of 0.3. The same conclusion is drawn in TableÂ 2, where we fixed a replaced ratio of 0.5 and selected various numbers of labeled images. For example, RSMatch made an improvement of 1.47% on CIFAR-10 with 100 labeled images per class.

Limitations and future works. In RSMatch, we did not consider further selecting the identified synthetic data for training, while some workÂ [29] has found that the synthetic images generated by carefully designed prompts are better training samples. This suggests that different synthetic images have different values for training. Thus, further evaluating the training value of these images and utilizing them distinctively has the potential to improve the SSL performance, which is also a promising future direction.

6 Conclusion

In this paper, we propose and address a new challenging task: Synthetic and Real hybrid Semi-supervised Learning (RS-SSL), to investigate the impact of the increasingly producing and uploading of synthetic data on SSL. We first set up RS-SSL benchmarks to evaluate the performance of existing SSL methods on this task and found the current SSL methods are not ready to face the challenge of the inclusion of synthetic images. Then, we propose RSMatch, a new SSL method that can 1) identify the synthetic images in the unlabeled data without any provided synthetic labels and 2) further utilize the synthetic images to improve SSL performance. With RSMatch, there is no need to specifically consider the inclusion of synthetic data when collecting unlabeled data from public image sets like the Internet. Therefore, our research further advances the practicality of semi-supervised learning.
