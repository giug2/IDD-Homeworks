Abstract

Predicting the physical interaction of proteins is a cornerstone problem in computational biology. New classes of learning-based algorithms are actively being developed, and are typically trained end-to-end on protein complex structures extracted from the Protein Data Bank. These training datasets tend to be large and difficult to use for prototyping and, unlike image or natural language datasets, they are not easily interpretable by non-experts. We present Dock2D-IP and Dock2D-IF, two â€œtoyâ€ datasets that can be used to select algorithms predicting protein-protein interactionsâ€”or any other type of molecular interactions. Using two-dimensional shapes as input, each example from Dock2D-IP (â€œinteraction poseâ€) describes the interaction pose of two shapes known to interact and each example from Dock2D-IF (â€œinteraction factâ€) describes whether two shapes form a stable complex or not. We propose a number of baseline solutions to the problem and show that the same underlying energy function can be learned either by solving the interaction pose task (formulated as an energy-minimization â€œdockingâ€ problem) or the fact-of-interaction task (formulated as a binding free energy estimation problem).

1 Introduction

One of the long-term goals of computational structural biology is to explain and predict the phenotype of an organism from the basic physical and chemical properties of its molecular constituents. Given the key role of proteins in living processes, a central problem in the field is to predict the structure of a protein from its amino acid sequenceÂ [1, 2]. Considerable progress on that problem has recently been made by algorithms such as AlphaFold2Â [3] and RoseTTAFoldÂ [4], using multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) as additional inputs, and by more recent models replacing MSAs with sequence embeddings from large language modelsÂ [5, 6, 7, 8]. The outstanding performance of AlphaFold2 at the 2020 CASPÂ 14 competition suggests that such architectures can solve a large portion of the protein structure prediction problemÂ [9, 10, 11].

Because proteins typically perform their functions by coming into contact with other proteins (as well as with nucleic acids, carbohydrates, and a variety of small molecules), it is also critical to be able to predict protein-protein interactions (PPIs) and the structures of the complexes they form. While this problem can be viewed simply as a more general version of the protein structure prediction problemâ€”involving two protein chains instead of oneÂ [12]â€”, many of the data sources used in state-of-the-art protein structure prediction algorithms are not as easy to leverage for the prediction of PPIs. There are fewer structures of protein complexes than of single proteins and, more importantly, the co-evolution signal between physically interacting proteins is weaker and harder to extract than that between interacting regions of a single proteinÂ [13, 14, 15]. For that reason, PPI prediction models are expected to benefit greatly from strong priors based on molecular structure and energyÂ [16].

In light of the success of AlphaFold2, it has become clear that learning-based algorithms will be crucial in solving many of the remaining key problems in computational structural biology. From the last decade of progress in computer vision and natural language processing, we expect that the design of these new learning-based algorithms will be driven by the development of standard performance metrics and well-curated datasets. One such example from the field of computer vision is the ImageNet datasetÂ [17], which enabled the development of the AlexNetÂ [18] and ResNetÂ [19] architectures, still considered the gold standard for computer vision applications. Many of these learning algorithms have risen to prominence by first showing outstanding performance on smaller, simpler datasets such as MNISTÂ [20]. These datasets allow for fast prototyping and are generally more interpretable than the large, complex datasets ultimately used for training.

In the case of PPI prediction, learning-based algorithms are typically trained on structures extracted from the Protein Data BankÂ [21] and tested on datasets such as the Docking BenchmarkÂ [22] and the CASP/CAPRI targetsÂ [23, 24]. However, these datasets are impractical for prototyping and are not easily interpretable by humans, which makes it harder to analyze the advantages and drawbacks of any new algorithm.

This paper presents Dock2D-IP and Dock2D-IF, two simple, small-scale datasets that can be used to select algorithms predicting protein-protein interactions. Our aim is to provide an equivalent of the MNIST dataset for the molecular recognition problem, formulated here as an energy-based shape recognition problem. While we are designing the Dock2D datasets with protein-protein interactions in mind, they are generic enough to apply to any type of receptor-ligand interactions.

Protein-protein interaction data fall into two broad categoriesÂ [25, 26]: 1) structural data obtained from X-ray crystallography or high-resolution electron microscopy and 2) fact-of-interaction data obtained using proteomics methods. In this work we use a single energy function to generate datasets for both categories, so that prediction models can be trained from either type of data. To make the problem more easily tractable and interpretable, we formulate it in two dimensions instead of three and use a simple shape-based interaction potential similar to the one proposed by Katchalski-Katzir et al.Â [27].

The paper is divided as follows: the â€œDatasetsâ€ section describes the generating function and the Dock2D datasets, the â€œModelsâ€ section describes algorithms for the pose prediction problem and for the fact-of-interaction prediction problem, and the â€œResultsâ€ section reports the performance of the proposed models and discusses their relative merits and their potential for transfer learning.

3 Models

As stated in the Introduction, the main goal of the Dock2D datasets is to facilitate the development of models that can learn from either â€œinteraction poseâ€ (IP) data or â€œinteraction factâ€ (IF) data. Here we propose two types of models that set a standard of algorithmic performance on each dataset: a â€œbrute-forceâ€ model, that generates a prediction by calculating the energy function for all translations and rotations, and an â€œeconomicalâ€ model, that learns the energy function without calculating the full partition function Zğ‘Z. While the â€œbrute-forceâ€ models essentially solve both the IP and IF prediction tasks, they are impractical solutions for the three-dimensional version of the problem, for which the number of states is much larger than in the two-dimensional version. By contrast, the â€œeconomicalâ€ models aim to provide tractable solutions in higher dimensions.

3.1 Overall architecture

All models learn a two-feature representation of each shape (receptor and ligand) using a Siamese SE(2)-equivariant CNNÂ [35] (see FigureÂ 4) and use these features to compute an energy of the formÂ (2), with weights a00subscriptğ‘00a_{00}, a10subscriptğ‘10a_{10}, a01subscriptğ‘01a_{01}, and a11subscriptğ‘11a_{11} treated as learnable parameters. Spatial correlationsÂ (3) are computed using fast Fourier transform (FFT)Â [36].

The Siamese SE(2)-equivariant CNN module has only two convolutional layers, each using a fixed kernel size of 5Ã—5555\times 5. The first layer maps the input shape onto one scalar and four vectors and the second layer maps those back to one scalar and one vector, representing the bulk and boundary features used in Eq.Â (2). Each convolutional layer is followed by a ReLU applied to the norm of the featuresÂ [35], to preserve the equivariance and allow the scalar and the vectors to mix. The final vector feature is reduced to scalar by taking an element-wise norm.

5 Conclusion

We have proposed the Dock2D datasets (Dock2D-IP and Dock2D-IF) and several representation-learning â€œbaselineâ€ models demonstrating end-to-end learning of a simplified, 2D version of the molecular recognition problem. The representation-learning baselines effectively solved the interaction pose (IP) prediction problem in two dimensions (see TableÂ 1), whether they were trained on the full task of predicting optimal pose (ğ­0,Ï•0)subscriptğ­0subscriptitalic-Ï•0(\mathbf{t}_{0},\phi_{0}) or on the simplified task of predicting optimal translation ğ­0subscriptğ­0\mathbf{t}_{0} given the ground-truth orientation Ï•0subscriptitalic-Ï•0\phi_{0}. Training on the IP task (whether â€œbrute-forceâ€ or â€œsimplifiedâ€) was extremely efficient, requiring only 100 epochs and completing in minutes.

The baselines also solved the fact-of-interaction (IF) prediction problem (see TableÂ 2), whether the predicted Î”â€‹FÎ”ğ¹\Delta F values used in the â„’IFsubscriptâ„’IF\cal{L}_{\mathrm{IF}} loss were computed explicitly using Eq.Â (11) or sampled using Eq.Â (13). While training models on the IF task took significantly longerâ€”requiring 1000 epochs and sometimes taking days to completeâ€”, training time can be drastically reduced by starting from the energy function learned from the IP task (see FigureÂ A.8).

One of the goals of this work is to pave the way for algorithms using fact-of-interaction data to infer structural details of protein-protein complexes. Our results suggest that, provided a model implements an energy function Eğ¸E from which both an optimal pose and a binding free energy Fğ¹F can be computed, the representations learned from training that model on the IP task are compatible with those learned from the IF task, and that a two-headed model could be jointly trained on a mixture of structural data and fact-of-interaction data. In the context of the Dock2D toy data, the transferability of learned features is due to the fact that the same energy function was used to generate both the Dock2D-IP and Dock2D-IF datasets. While it is unclear to what extent the same approach will work on experimental data, since the connection between configurational energy Eğ¸E and binding free energy Fğ¹F cannot be exactly defined and since what constitutes a positive interaction depends on the experimental assay usedÂ [39], we expect many of the strategies developed in this work to be applicable.
