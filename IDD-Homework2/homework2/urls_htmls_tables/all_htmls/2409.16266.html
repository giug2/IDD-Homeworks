REBEL: Rule-based and Experience-enhanced Learning with LLMs for Initial Task Allocation in Multi-Human Multi-Robot Teams

Abstract

Multi-human multi-robot teams combine the complementary strengths of humans and robots to tackle complex tasks across diverse applications. However, the inherent heterogeneity of these teams presents significant challenges in initial task allocation (ITA), which involves assigning the most suitable tasks to each team member based on their individual capabilities before task execution. While current learning-based methods have shown promising results, they are often computationally expensive to train, and lack the flexibility to incorporate user preferences in multi-objective optimization and adapt to last-minute changes in real-world dynamic environments. To address these issues, we propose REBEL, an LLM-based ITA framework that integrates rule-based and experience-enhanced learning. By leveraging Retrieval-Augmented Generation, REBEL dynamically retrieves relevant rules and past experiences, enhancing reasoning efficiency. Additionally, REBEL can complement pre-trained RL-based ITA policies, improving situational awareness and overall team performance. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of our approach across various settings. More details are available at https://sites.google.com/view/ita-rebel.

I Introduction

Multi-human multi-robot (MH-MR) teams are dynamic and versatile units where multiple humans and robots, each with unique capabilities and characteristics, collaborate to complete tasks with varying requirements [1]. Unlike conventional human-robot teams, which consist of a single type of agent or just one human, MH-MR teams leverage the distinct yet latently complementary strengths of both humans and robots. This synergy has enabled their successful deployment in diverse applications, including search and rescue operations, military missions, warehouse logistics, and manufacturing assembly lines [2].

Despite the advantages of MH-MR teams, a primary challenge stems from the inherent heterogeneity of the team, which necessitates ITA strategies that effectively match individual capabilities with specific tasks before task execution [3]. Poor ITA decisions can result in suboptimal performance, which is difficult to adjust or optimize after the task has begun [4, 5].

Existing literature on ITA can be categorized into two main approaches: model-based and learning-based. Model-based methods involve the development of mathematical models, often using large-scale mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11], to serve as allocation rules. Despite their strong mathematical foundations, these methods are highly dependent on parameter selection and extensive model engineering, which limits their generalizability and often prevents a one-size-fits-all solution.

On the other hand, learning-based approaches show better potential by learning through experimentation with different task requirements and team configurations. For example, [3] formulates the ITA problem as a contextual MDP and proposes an attention-based RL approach. Additionally, a hierarchical RL approach for especially large-size teams is introduced in [12]. While these methods achieve state-of-the-art performance compared to model-based approaches, three key limitations remain.

First, training these RL models is time-consuming and expensive, making real-world human-robot interaction (HRI) scenarios less practical [12]. Second, these methods often lack the flexibility to account for multi-objective optimization (MOO), such as user preferences related to mission time, human workload, or noise levels [11]. In real-world applications, users may have diverse objectives beyond team performance, and current RL approaches are typically designed for Single Objective Optimization (SOO), limiting their ability to reflect such preferences. Lastly, RL models often require fixed inputs, meaning they must be retrained to accommodate changes in team composition, such as a robot malfunctioning or new humans joining the team. This lack of adaptability to last-minute changes is a significant drawback in real-world dynamic environments.

Meanwhile, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant reasoning capabilities and flexibility in robotics applications [13, 14, 15]. In multi-agent systems, LLMs have been applied to enhance agent cooperation by improving interactions and collaborative skills [16, 17, 18]; however, these methods focus on intra-agent communication and do not address task allocation in heterogeneous teams. Additionally, prior works [13] have leveraged LLMs for multi-robot task allocation without considering human-robot collaboration, making them inapplicable to MH-MR teams. Despite the growing use of LLMs in robotics, their application to task allocation in MH-MR teams remains largely unexplored, and existing methods may not fully address the complexities of human-robot interactions and MOO.

To address the above challenges, we propose REBEL, an LLM-based method that introduces a rule-based learning approach to enhance ITA, as illustrated in Fig.¬†1. LLMs have been utilized to generate and refine user-centric guidelines across various domains, producing driving policies aligned with human styles [19, 14] and patient-specific treatment plans in healthcare [20]. In our framework, LLMs generate and iteratively update allocation rules for each single objective specified by interacting with the environment and testing different ITA strategies, while storing experiences for future reference. We then utilize Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) to dynamically retrieve instructional rules and prior experiential data for either single-objective optimization (SOO) or MOO as additional contextual information. REBEL benefits from this learning-retrieval pattern, enabling it to handle unseen scenarios, reflect human preferences, and accommodate last-minute changes in team composition. II Problem Formulation

ITA in MH-MR teams involves assigning tasks to team members with varying capabilities before mission execution [3]. In these teams, the heterogeneity of members is a defining characteristic. Each team member, whether human or robot, has unique attributes, such as physical capabilities, cognitive skills, speed, and endurance. Similarly, tasks vary in complexity and requirements, demanding specific skill sets or resources for successful completion. The challenge is to match these diverse agents to the right tasks to maximize individual capabilities.

Unlike traditional approaches [3, 12] that focus solely on maximizing team performance as a SOO problem, we address ITA as a MOO problem. In real-world MH-MR settings, performance alone does not capture the full scope of mission success; factors like mission time and human workload are equally important. User preferences also play a crucial role, as different users, such as mission planners, may prioritize objectives differently, valuing either quick completion or minimizing cognitive load. Additionally, real-world ITA faces the challenge of last-minute changes in team composition due to robot malfunctions, battery depletion, or the unavailability of human members. The introduction of new team members mid-mission further adds complexity.

In this work, we tackle these ITA challenges by focusing on multi-objective optimization based on user preferences and adaptability to last-minute changes in team composition, leveraging LLM agents with experience-enhanced rule-based learning.

III Methodology

III-A Overview

As shown in Fig.¬†2, the REBEL framework for ITA in MH-MR teams consists of three stages: Rule Generation, Experience Generation, and Inferencing. The first two stages comprise the Knowledge Acquisition (KA) phase, where objective-specific rules are created and refined. Additionally, experience data is generated by recording the simulation outputs of an LLM agent‚Äôs ITA plans. This acquired knowledge serves as a valuable resource for decision-making. In the Inferencing stage, the same LLM agent, or a new one adapted to specific scenarios provided by the user, dynamically leverages this knowledge using RAG [21]. This enhances decision-making, enabling better SOO performance, adaptation to user preferences in MOO, and an improvement in the system‚Äôs ability to respond to situational changes in team composition.

III-B Stage 1: Rule Generation

The Rule Generation stage is the cornerstone of the KA phase. This stage aims to generate prescriptive objective-specific rules that guide the ITA process within MH-MR teams. Aside from providing background information, G,Bùê∫ùêµG,Bitalic_G , italic_B in Lines 1, 2 in Algorithm¬†1, rules are created through zero-shot prompting of the LLM agent, which draws upon its pre-trained knowledge to generate task-specific rules based on mission objectives. Following rule generation, the LLM engages in self-reflection by assessing the outcomes from the Experience Generation stage, allowing it to refine and improve the rules iteratively.

In MH-MR teams, which comprise a mix of human and robotic agents with diverse capabilities, such as the cognitive flexibility of humans and the precision or endurance of robots, the process of matching tasks to appropriate agents requires clear and actionable rules. To ensure the LLM can handle the complexity of these diverse systems, we introduce a Structured Prompting Format (SPF). This format, informed by prompt engineering research [22, 13], organizes information into five distinct sections: Mission Description, Goals, User Preferences, Rules, and Prior Experiences. By structuring the prompt in this way, we provide the LLM with critical contextual information before generating the rules. Once generated, the rules are stored in raw text and as sentence embeddings in a vector database for fast retrieval during the Inferencing stage.

III-C Stage 2: Experience Generation

The Experience Generation stage is where the LLM agent acquires practical knowledge by simulating various MH-MR scenarios. Each simulation introduces distinct mission objectives and dynamic environmental conditions, providing a rich context for the LLM to refine its decision-making capabilities. The primary goal of this stage is to enable the LLM to gain hands-on experience in applying the rules generated in Stage 1, as well as adapting to real-world complexities such as task difficulty, changes in team composition, and unforeseen operational challenges. During each simulation, the LLM is tasked with executing SOO for a specific objective. The rules RùëÖRitalic_R serve as a guide, helping the LLM make decisions about task allocation. As the simulations progress, the LLM collects performance data Dùê∑Ditalic_D‚Äîincluding task completion times, resource utilization, and agent workload, which are stored in both raw and vectorized formats. This collected data form a repository of past experiences that the LLM can reference during future decision-making processes when inferencing. By learning from these experiences, the LLM is able to adjust its strategies to improve task allocation efficiency in dynamic and unpredictable environments.

The Inferencing stage evaluates the LLM‚Äôs ITA performance once it has been equipped with both the rules and experience data from the previous stages, as seen in Algorithm¬†1. The Ensemble Retriever combines Sparse and Dense retrieval methods to capture both keyword-based and semantic similarities, ensuring the most contextually appropriate rules are retrieved.

The second workflow, handled by the Experience RAG module, focuses on retrieving relevant experience data. The system identifies the most similar missions from the experience vector database to the user‚Äôs mission description. To enhance this process, we implement a custom algorithm that embeds each component of the mission description independently and then uses Cosine-Similarity to compare the components sequentially. The similarity scores are summed after comparing each component, providing a final similarity score between the user‚Äôs mission and the prior experience. By integrating both rule-based learning and experiential data, the LLM is able to adapt to new scenarios with improved situational awareness and task allocation performance.

IV Case Study and Experiments

IV-A Task Scenario and Configuration

We designed a case study involving an extensive environmental surveillance task centered on monitoring pollution leaking from a warehouse. Pollutants originating from the warehouse require precise observation and identification by MH-MR team. The mission begins when a satellite system detects and catalogs various points of interest (POIs). The team is tasked with two primary objectives: 1) Moving to each POI to capture images and 2) Analyzing the acquired images to determine whether a POI represents an actual hazard. We use the same human factors, robot characteristics, and task specifications from [12], and they are summarized as: i) Human Factors[24]: Cognitive ability, Operational skill level; ii) Robot Characteristics: Mobility (speed), Sensory capabilities (camera quality) in UGVs and UAVs, Operation mode (fully autonomous or under collaborative control with human operators); iii) Task Specifications: Locations of the POIs, Intrinsic complexity levels.

IV-B Simulation Environment

For the experiments, we utilized a benchmark simulation environment for ITA in MH-MR teams [12]. Each POI corresponds to a building, and the color of each building indicates its inherent complexity level for hazard evaluation. A visual representation of this environment is provided in Fig.¬†3.

IV-B1 Robot Model

Both the speed of the robots and the quality of the images they capture depend on the control mode. When robots navigate autonomously to a POI, they use default parameters. In shared control mode with human, these parameters are adjusted based on the operator‚Äôs level of expertise. Additionally, the probability of successful onboard hazard classification by the robot is influenced by both the captured image quality and the inherent difficulty of the hazard. The specific attributes of the robots are the same as those in the settings of [12].

IV-B2 Human Model

We model human operators as sequential event handlers, where their performance in hazard classification is influenced by fatigue, workload, and task complexity. These factors are moderated by individual cognitive abilities and operational skill levels, as described in [25]. The probability of accurate hazard classification is determined nonlinearly, combining these elements according to the model in [26]. Factors such as fatigue and workload are modeled with decreasing performance over time, while task complexity is captured using a sigmoid function to reflect its nonlinear impact on performance [27]. Cognitive capacity and operational expertise, which affect performance adjustments, are categorized into three tiers (low, medium, high), with values randomly assigned within these ranges for each experiment [26, 11]. The detailed equations for this human model can be found in [12].

IV-C Experimental Setting

IV-C1 Single Objective Optimization

We first evaluated REBEL‚Äôs performance on elementary SOO tasks. During Experience Generation, ten missions are simulated for each objective and their performance data is stored in the Experience Vector Database. Following few-shot learning paradigms, we limit the number of simulated missions per objective to ten. The three objectives we tested independently were Maximizing Task Performance, Minimizing Mission Time, and Minimizing Human Workload, as these objectives are frequently encountered in real-world MH-MR applications [26]. For comparison, we used two baseline methods: the cross-attribute attention RL-based approach, AtRL [3], and a model-based approach, MILP [11]. Additionally, we evaluated two ablations of REBEL: one without experience data and another without both rules and experience data. For the LLM methods, switching between objectives simply required changing the prompt, whereas the baselines required reward shaping. We simulated an MH-MR team consisting of 5 humans, 7 robots, and 30 POIs for all models. For each objective, we conducted 100 trials and reported the average performance.

IV-C2 Multi-Objective Optimization

Next, we carried out extensive MOO experiments, with the same three objectives from SOO, to assess each method‚Äôs ability to comprehend complex user preferences and generate appropriate ITA plans. In contrast to SOO, these experiments better reflect real-world MH-MR scenarios, where multiple, often competing, objectives must be simultaneously considered. We compared REBEL with the AtRL baseline and the same two ablations. In each test, one objective was given higher priority over the other two, and we assigned weights to emphasize these preferences (e.g., 0.5 for the highest-priority objective and 0.25 for the others). Finally, we recorded the average performance across all three objectives to analyze their behavior under varying user-preference weights.

IV-C3 Situational Awareness

We further tested REBEL‚Äôs ability to demonstrate situational awareness in response to last-minute changes in team composition. We simulated scenarios with both fewer and additional team members chosen randomly to see if the rules and experience data from the original setting still provided benefits in these altered configurations. Unlike AtRL, which has fixed input dimensions and cannot handle such changes directly, we developed REBEL+AtRL by adjusting the ITA actions of the pre-trained AtRL model in the original setting with our method. This adaptation allows us to explore REBEL‚Äôs capacity to enhance the situational awareness of pre-trained RL models under dynamic team configurations.

V Results and Analysis

V-A Results of Single Objective Optimization

Table I summarizes the results from the SOO experiments. REBEL consistently outperforms its two ablations (Zero-Shot and REBEL-No Exp), underscoring the importance of our Experience-enhanced Rule-based Learning approach. Without these components, the LLM lacks critical external knowledge, reducing its effectiveness in making ITA decisions. This highlights the inherent complexity of the ITA task, even when only optimizing for a single objective. REBEL also consistently outperforms MILP, which relies on tuned parameters, unlike REBEL‚Äôs dynamic iterative learning. REBEL performs better than AtRL in minimizing Mission Time, but it lags behind in Task Performance and Human Workload. Despite this, the performance gap is relatively small, with AtRL achieving only 9.28% better Mission Performance and 3.5% lower Human Workload.

These results demonstrate that REBEL can achieve near state-of-the-art performance within 10% of advanced RL methods, even without formal training. The structured integration of relevant rules and experience data due to RAG allows the LLM to perform effective ICL, resulting in improved ITA plans. REBEL is also more sample-efficient than AtRL, requiring only 10 examples of prior mission experience data compared to AtRL‚Äôs extensive training requirements, positioning REBEL as a strong candidate for complex ITA tasks in scenarios where training data or computational resources are limited.

V-B Results of Multi-Objective Optimization

Fig.¬†4 showcases the performance of different methods in MOO settings, with normalized values for each objective, task performance (TP), mission time (MT), and human workload (HW), to illustrate preference alignment. The blue, orange, and green lines represent scenarios where TP, MT, and HW were prioritized by users, respectively. The actual prioritized objectives for each method are highlighted in a dotted red box.

From the results, we observe that REBEL exhibits superior sensitivity to user-specified objective preferences compared to the baselines. When a particular objective is prioritized, REBEL consistently aligns the performance of that objective with the user‚Äôs preferences, as demonstrated by the highest normalized performance value (1.0) in the blue, orange, and green lines for TP, MT, and HW, respectively. This alignment is particularly clear in the red boxed regions in Fig.¬†4, where REBEL demonstrates its ability to meet user-defined goals effectively.

In contrast, AtRL only successfully prioritizes MT and HW, but struggles with TP, as indicated by the lower values for this objective. Zero-Shot performs well only for MT, while REBEL-No Exp shows inconsistent preference alignment across all three objectives. This discrepancy highlights the value of incorporating experience data, particularly in more complex multi-objective scenarios, where LLMs like REBEL benefit from having additional context and history to interpret and prioritize conflicting objectives.

Moreover, the results reveal that REBEL is more adept at making trade-offs among objectives based on user preferences, compared to the other methods, which exhibit less flexibility in adjusting to shifting priorities. This ability to dynamically reallocate resources and effort to align with user preferences is a critical advantage in real-world MH-MR tasks, where multiple objectives often need to be balanced simultaneously.

V-C Results of Situational Awareness

As shown in Table II, REBEL demonstrates greater robustness under team composition changes, whether increasing or decreasing the number of members, compared to the Zero-Shot LLM model. Although performance drops slightly from the static setting, REBEL still delivers strong results, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed rule-based learning and experience-enhanced approach. This adaptability is not only beneficial for SOO and MOO, but also proves advantageous in last-minute changes to team composition.

More importantly, REBEL+AtRL shows promising outcomes, illustrating REBEL‚Äôs potential as a test-time adaptation module for pre-trained RL-based ITA policies. This combination of REBEL‚Äôs adaptability with AtRL‚Äôs reinforcement learning strengths enhances overall performance and flexibility, making it a powerful approach for handling real-time team composition changes in dynamic environments.

VI Conclusion and Future Work

In this work, we introduced REBEL, a novel LLM-based framework for ITA in MH-MR teams. By integrating rule-based learning with experience-enhanced strategies using ICL and RAG, REBEL addresses several critical challenges, including MOO, user preference adaptation, and situational awareness in dynamic and changing team environments. Our extensive experiments validate REBEL‚Äôs effectiveness across SOO, MOO, and user-preference alignment. Additionally, we demonstrated REBEL‚Äôs potential as a test-time adaptation module for pre-trained reinforcement learning-based ITA policies, further enhancing its flexibility and robustness in real-world scenarios.

Future work will explore extending REBEL‚Äôs capabilities to more complex multi-agent environments with larger teams and increased heterogeneity. Additionally, integrating REBEL into real-world systems and refining its user preference modeling will provide deeper insights into its practical applications, scalability, and adaptability in diverse, dynamic settings.
