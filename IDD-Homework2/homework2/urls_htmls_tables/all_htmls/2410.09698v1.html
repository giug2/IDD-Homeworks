Abstract

Online platforms have transformed the formal job market but continue to struggle with effectively engaging passive candidates—individuals not actively seeking employment but open to compelling opportunities. We introduce the Independent Halting Cascade (IHC) model, a novel framework that integrates complex network diffusion dynamics with economic game theory to address this challenge. Unlike traditional models that focus solely on information propagation, the IHC model empowers network agents to either disseminate a job posting or halt its spread by applying for the position themselves. By embedding economic incentives into agent decision-making processes, the model creates a dynamic interplay between maximizing information spread and promoting application. Our analysis uncovers distinct behavioral regimes within the IHC model, characterized by critical thresholds in recommendation and application probabilities. Extensive simulations on both synthetic and real-world network topologies demonstrate that the IHC model significantly outperforms traditional direct-recommendation systems in recruiting suitable passive candidates. Specifically, the model achieves up to a 30% higher hiring success rate compared to baseline methods. These findings offer strategic insights into leveraging economic incentives and network structures to enhance recruitment efficiency. The IHC model thus provides a robust framework for modernizing recruitment strategies, particularly in engaging the vast pool of passive candidates in the job market.

1 Introduction

The digital revolution, spearheaded by online platforms, has dramatically reshaped the formal job market, leading to a surge in digital recruitment platforms and job-matching algorithms [1]. Despite these advances, recruiting passive candidates—individuals not actively seeking a job but willing to consider attractive offers—remains a pressing challenge [2]. According to LinkedIn, about 70% of potential candidates are categorized as passive; among them, 85% are open to new job opportunities if approached with a compelling offer [3]. While effective in matching job openings with suitable candidates, existing job recommendation systems struggle to convert passive candidates due to their disinterest in job-seeking [4]. This results in high matching rates but low conversion to actual hires, particularly in sectors like Information and Communications Technology and Data Science. Therefore, it is crucial to develop effective mechanisms to engage this cohort. This research is driven by the interest of a company in solving this real-world problem.

Propagation through social networks has been studied in several domains [5]. A fundamental problem in viral marketing strategies is to select the most influential nodes to trigger a large cascade of adoptions within a social network [6, 7]. One of the simplest models of this kind called Independent Cascade (IC) model, was investigated in [8, 9]: an initial seed of nodes receive the information, and then, in discrete steps, active nodes try to influence its inactive neighbors. Their success, therefore, depends on the propagation probability of their ties.

In [10], the authors provide the first provable approximation guarantees for efficient algorithms for this problem. Through computational experiments and theoretical analysis based on submodular functions, the researchers demonstrate that a natural greedy strategy achieves solutions that are provably within 63% of optimal for several classes of models. They show that their algorithms significantly outperform node-selection heuristics based on degree and distance centrality, providing practical and effective solutions for maximizing influence spread in social networks. In [11], the authors take inspiration from the IC model and focus on finding the optimal sequence of nodes to activate for maximizing diffusion. They investigate the computational aspects of strategic diffusion through complex contagion, particularly in the context of seed selection, and show that greedy and majority solutions cannot have better than a logarithmic approximation guarantee.

In this work, we build on the theory of IC models for strategic diffusion in social networks and propose an extension called the Independent Halting Cascade (IHC) model. Distinct from traditional models that focus solely on information propagation, the IHC model allows network agents to make a binary choice: either propagate the information further or try to halt its spread. In the job recruitment scenario, candidates can choose to propagate the job posting within their network, or to apply, thus halting the information cascade. This introduces a dynamic tension between two forces: one striving for maximum information dissemination and the other aiming to arrest it.

A major novelty of this proposal lies in its incorporation of economic incentives to guide agent behavior, inspired by the literature on recursive incentive mechanisms [12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17]. In this context, agents are not just passive information carriers; they act as rational decision-makers who weigh the potential economic rewards of either propagating job information or applying for a position themselves. This approach aligns with the economic game theory literature, where agents maximize their utility based on perceived incentives.

By structuring incentives hierarchically, as in the DARPA recursive incentive scheme, we create a dynamic model that effectively motivates participation and optimizes recruitment outcomes. Agents receive incremental rewards, which not only drive them to spread information, but also to engage passive candidates, those who are open to opportunities but not actively searching. This incentive structure is particularly crucial in modern recruitment strategies, as it addresses the challenge of converting passive job seekers, a group that traditional digital recruitment platforms often fail to engage effectively [1, 2, 4].

Thus, our research advances the field of network diffusion by integrating economic incentives with agent-based modeling, providing a novel framework that enhances digital recruitment efficiency. This framework bridges the domains of complex networks, economic incentives, and job market dynamics, offering a robust tool for modern recruitment strategies that aim to efficiently engage a broader pool of potential candidates.

2 Methods

We introduce the Independent Halting Cascade (IHC) model. This novel diffusion agent-based model builds on the established Independent Cascade (IC) model [10, 11], where network agents traditionally focus solely on propagating information. In contrast, our IHC model grants agents the dual ability to continue the propagation or otherwise halt the diffusion process (e.g., by applying for a job posting) and, with a certain probability, successfully terminate it (e.g., by securing the job position). Further, an economic incentive mechanism encourages individuals to propagate job postings until a successful hire is made.

2.2 Economic incentives for spreading maximization

In the IHC model, economic incentives fulfill two roles: they engage passive job candidates and encourage agent-to-agent job recommendations. We incorporate the distribution of incentives according to the DARPA recursive incentive scheme. This framework effectively maximizes both information dissemination and task completion, offering a balanced and efficient strategy for modern recruitment.

However, several challenges arise in real-world applications of such incentive mechanisms [20, 21]. One significant issue is the difficulty in maintaining engagement over longer recruitment chains. As the reward diminishes exponentially for distant nodes in the network, agents at the periphery may lack sufficient motivation to recommend candidates or propagate the job posting. This phenomenon can result in incomplete cascades, where information about job opportunities fails to reach a broader audience. Additionally, modeling heterogeneous behaviors—where agents have varying levels of responsiveness to incentives—adds complexity to ensuring effective propagation.

Another challenge is balancing the trade-off between maximizing information spread and incentivizing application. The IHC model introduces a dynamic tension between these two objectives: while economic rewards motivate agents to continue spreading job information, they also encourage early termination of the chain when an agent applies. In practice, the optimal balance of these forces depends on the structure of the network and the relative scarcity of highly qualified candidates. If the application probability is too high, the cascade may halt prematurely, limiting the model’s effectiveness in reaching passive candidates deep within the network.

We remark that, while the economic incentives mechanism motivates the construction of our model, it does not play a role in its dynamics. We assume, for simplicity, that the budget B𝐵Bitalic_B does not affect the agents’ recommendation and application probabilities. Future work may explore how varying incentive structures or personalized rewards could be used to further optimize recruitment outcomes, particularly in networks with heterogeneous agent behaviors.

3.4 The Oracle direct-recommendation system

We developed the IHC model to provide a mathematical framework for a digital job recruitment system based on social incentives that can compete with direct recommendation systems, such as LinkedIn. Direct recommendation systems often struggle to engage passive workers in applying for job vacancies, particularly for roles requiring highly skilled individuals [1, 27]. This failure may have several explanations, such as the lack of a suitable population with accounts or activity on these platforms or passive working not having enough incentive to consider attractive job vacancies. The IHC model addresses these challenges by introducing economic incentives for agents to apply for job vacancies or recommend them to peers. The social recommendation mechanism facilitates the free flow of information throughout a social network, reaching individuals beyond the initially observed population. In contrast to direct-recommendation systems, which are constrained to their platform’s user base, incentive-based systems have the potential to reach people outside the initial observed population through their recommendation mechanism.

This section introduces direct-recommendation systems within the IHC model framework. 4 Discussion

In this study, we present the Independent Halting Cascade model, a framework that integrates network diffusion mechanics and economic incentives to address the issue of recruiting passive job candidates. The IHC model extends the Independent Cascade Model [10] by allowing agents to make a binary decision: to either propagate information or halt its spread by applying for a job, thereby capturing the real-world dynamics where individuals balance between spreading opportunities and taking action themselves. Our approach leverages insights from recursive incentive mechanisms [12], structuring rewards hierarchically to motivate agents effectively within the network. This extension of diffusion modeling introduces the capability of halting cascades, an essential feature for recruitment scenarios.

The analyses reveal several behavior-rich regimes in the IHC model that differ from traditional diffusion models. For instance, we observe distinct operational settings where the diffusion either maximizes the economic surplus or optimizes the likelihood of successful hires. These regimes depend on the tuning of model parameters such as application and recommendation probabilities, which can be inferred from real-world data traces [23]. Importantly, our results demonstrate that while social systems (utilizing peer recommendations) are effective in larger, heterogeneous networks (e.g., the Twitter network), Oracle systems, which rely on direct recommendations, struggle significantly when job vacancies are highly specific. The IHC model, however, remains robust across various conditions, efficiently matching job vacancies to candidates even as specificity increases.

Our findings also underscore the broader implications of the IHC model beyond digital recruitment. By effectively modeling the balance between information propagation and cascade halting, the IHC framework offers valuable applications in domains such as marketing campaigns, where incentivized actions must be managed within networks to optimize outcomes. This approach provides a novel lens through which recursive economic incentives and agent-based modeling can be applied to influence and optimize network interactions [32, 33, 34, 35].

Furthermore, the IHC model is designed to be empirically adaptable. Future work should focus on parameter inference from diverse real-world networks, leveraging large datasets to refine the calibration of application and recommendation probabilities. Such efforts will enhance the model’s performance and provide more precise guidelines for practitioners aiming to implement incentivized recruitment systems. In addition, exploring heterogeneous topologies (e.g., scale-free and small-world networks) will further validate the robustness of the IHC framework across different social and economic contexts, ensuring its applicability to real-world systems.

In summary, the IHC model contributes both theoretically and practically to the understanding of incentivized dynamics in social networks. It stands at the intersection of complex networks and economic game theory, providing a powerful framework for addressing challenges in modern recruitment and beyond. By bridging empirical data and theoretical modeling, the IHC framework lays the foundation for more efficient and economically sound recruitment strategies, particularly for engaging passive candidates—an area where existing job-recommendation systems often fall short.
